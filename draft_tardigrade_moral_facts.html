Put a dried tardigrade on a microscope slide and you are looking at something that is real and doing nothing. Not sleeping. Not slowly ticking over. Doing nothing. A tardigrade is a half-millimetre animal with eight legs and claws, and when the water around it vanishes it pulls in its legs, folds into a barrel-shaped husk called a tun, and stops. Its chemistry — the endless small reactions that we usually point to when we say a thing is alive — comes "reversibly to a standstill," in the words of the biologist David Keilin, who in 1959 coined the term cryptobiosis, literally "hidden life," for exactly this state.[1]

Add a drop of water and, minutes or decades later, it walks away.

I want to use that ordinary, well-documented fact to do something to a famous argument in philosophy. The argument says that there are no such things as real moral facts — no fact that cruelty is actually wrong, out there in the world, the way it is a fact that the slide is glass. The tardigrade does not prove that argument false. It does something more useful. It takes the argument apart into two pieces and shows that one of the pieces was never frightening at all.

Where is the life when nothing is happening?

First, the biology, because the whole move depends on getting it right.

When a tardigrade dries out, the water inside its cells is replaced by a kind of natural sugar-and-protein glass. This is not a metaphor. In 2017 a team led by Thomas Boothby showed that tardigrades carry special floppy proteins — "intrinsically disordered," meaning they have no fixed shape — that, as the water leaves, lock together into an amorphous solid.[2] An amorphous solid is the technical name for glass: a liquid cooled or dried so fast that it freezes in place before it can arrange itself into a neat crystal. Window glass is a liquid that has, in effect, forgotten how to flow. The inside of a dried tardigrade is the same trick, run on the cell's own machinery. Some species add trehalose, a sugar that does the same vitrifying job in other dried-out organisms.[4] I have leaned on this same glass before — as a model for protective retreat under threat — but here the glass is doing a different job. There it was a way of surviving. Here it is a way of being real while idle.

Here is why that matters. Chemistry needs movement. Reactions happen because molecules bump into each other. Trap every molecule in a glass and you have not slowed life down — you have stopped the thing that life is, while keeping every part exactly where it was. The biologist John Clegg called this an ametabolic state: metabolism, the running chemistry, has gone to zero, or so close to zero we cannot measure it.[3]

So ask the obvious question: while the tardigrade is dried, where is its life? It is plainly not in any activity, because there is no activity. The honest answer is that the life is in the arrangement — in the precise frozen configuration of the glass, which holds within it the capacity to start running again the instant water returns. The tardigrade is real. It keeps its identity; it is still that animal. And it is doing nothing. Its existence has been moved out of the stream of cause and effect and stored, intact, in a shape.

Philosophers have a clean pair of words for this. A property is occurrent when it is something actually going on, and dispositional when it is a readiness for something to go on under the right conditions. Gilbert Ryle used the example of brittleness: a glass is brittle even while it sits unbroken on the shelf, because "brittle" names not a current event but a standing readiness to shatter if struck.[9] The dried tardigrade's life is dispositional in exactly this way. It is not a doing; it is a readiness to do, stored in matter.

The argument the tardigrade is about to cut in half

Now the philosophy. In 1977 the Australian philosopher J.L. Mackie made what is still the most quoted case against real moral facts, and he gave it a memorable name: the argument from queerness. ("Queer" here just means strange, weird, unlike anything else.) His claim, in plain words:

"If there were objective values, then they would be entities or qualities or relations of a very strange sort, utterly different from anything else in the universe."[5]

His thought was this. Suppose there really were a fact that some act is to be done — a fact with the "ought" built right into it, that would push any clear-eyed person toward the act regardless of what they happened to want. What kind of thing could that be? Nothing else in the world is like that. Ordinary facts just sit there being the case; they do not reach out and grab you. A fact that was also a command would be a metaphysical monster. Mackie concluded that, since no such monster exists, all our confident moral talk is quietly mistaken — a position called the error theory. We are not lying when we say torture is wrong; we are sincerely asserting something that, like a ghost story, has no fact to make it true.

A related worry, sharpened by Gilbert Harman the same year, is that moral facts seem to do no work.[6] A physicist sees a streak in a cloud chamber and the existence of a real particle explains the streak. But when you see cruelty and judge it wrong, the wrongness explains nothing about your seeing; your upbringing and nervous system explain the judgement entirely. The wrongness, if it is there at all, is causally idle. It pushes nothing, pulls nothing, leaves no fingerprint. (Realists fight this: Nicholas Sturgeon answered that moral facts do explain things — Hitler's depravity helps explain what he did — and the dispute is still live.[7] Notice already that this is a quarrel about whether moral facts are idle, which, as we are about to see, is the easier charge to answer.)

Read those two worries together and you can hear that they are actually a bundle of two different complaints:

  • Complaint A — real but inert. A moral fact would be real yet do no causal work: it sits outside the chain of bumping molecules entirely.
  • Complaint B — built-in oughtness. A moral fact would carry its own command: a "to-be-done" woven into the fabric of the fact itself.

Mackie's word "queer" smears these together into one shudder. The tardigrade pulls them apart.

Complaint A is not queer. Look — it is on the slide.

The dried tardigrade is real, fully natural, no monster, and causally inert — it does no pushing or pulling on anything, runs no reactions, leaves no fingerprint on the surrounding world. By Complaint A's standard it should count as deeply strange. It does not. No biologist looks at the tun state and declares it "utterly different from anything in the universe." They reach for a perfectly ordinary category: it is stored. Its reality has been parked in a configuration instead of in an activity.

And once you are looking for it, this category is everywhere. A seed is an oak's adulthood held inert in a husk. A struck-but-unrung bell holds a pitch it is not yet sounding. A memory is a fact about your past that is doing nothing right now and will spring into the present when cued. The frozen patterns I have written about elsewhere — a coral reef that persists as a stored structure rather than an ongoing performance — are the same shape. Reality simply does not require that a thing be busy to be present. Some things persist by working; a great many persist by stopping, their whole being folded into an arrangement that waits.

So Complaint A collapses. "Real but presently inert" is not a queer category at all. It is one of the most populated categories in nature. If that were the only objection to moral facts, a realist — someone who believes in real moral facts — could simply say: fine, moral facts are dispositional. They are true the way a dried tardigrade is alive and a shelved glass is brittle — as a standing readiness, not a current event. A fact that nobody is acting on is no stranger than a tardigrade nobody has watered.

The line the tardigrade cannot cross

But notice what the rescue actually delivers, because this is where the second complaint comes back with teeth.

What does the tardigrade's stored disposition redeem into when its conditions are met? Water arrives and the glass softens and the chemistry resumes. That is all. The disposition cashes out as an event — molecules start moving again. It is a fact about what the system will do given water. There is not the faintest trace, anywhere in that story, of what the system ought to do. To say the glass "ought" to revive is just a loose way of saying it will. No reason is stored in the husk. Only a mechanism is.

This is the oldest fault line in moral philosophy — David Hume's gap between is and ought, between a description of what happens and a claim about what should. And it is exactly the line a stored disposition cannot cross. A disposition is a packed-up will; an obligation is a reason. You can vitrify a tardigrade's future behaviour into a glass. You cannot vitrify a reason, because a reason is not the kind of thing that "goes off" when a trigger arrives. It is the kind of thing that would be true, and would count in favour of acting, even if no one ever acted on it and no trigger ever came.

So the tardigrade gives the realist a real gift and then takes back half of it. It dissolves Complaint A entirely: being real-but-inert is no embarrassment, because the world is full of inert reality stored in shape. But it leaves Complaint B standing — cleaner and sharper than before. The genuinely strange thing about a moral fact was never that it is inert. It is that it is supposed to carry an ought, a reason-giving force, and no amount of stored mechanism adds up to that. Mackie was right that something here is hard to place. He was wrong about what. He charged moral facts with two crimes; the tardigrade acquits them of one.

The lifeboat the boldest realist will not board

There is a way to take the gift and live with the loss, and it is worth seeing clearly, because it shows what realism would have to give up to use the tardigrade's rescue.

Suppose you say: moral facts are dispositional, and that is fine. To call an act wrong is to say it is the sort of act that any sufficiently informed, calm, clear-eyed observer would condemn — a readiness in us, stored and waiting, that "goes off" reliably under ideal conditions, the way the tardigrade's life goes off under water. The philosopher John McDowell defended a version of this, treating values like colours: a thing is red because it is disposed to look red to normal viewers in good light, and perhaps a thing is cruel because it is disposed to repel any clear moral gaze.[8] This is a real and respectable kind of moral realism. The wrongness is genuinely there, in the act, as a standing readiness.

But a disposition always needs two things the tardigrade taught us to look for: a base, the actual arrangement that does the work, and a trigger, the condition that sets it off. For the dried tardigrade the base is the molecular glass and the trigger is water. For the moral disposition, what is the base, and what is the trigger? The only honest answer this view can give is: the base is us — the responses of human beings, idealized — and the trigger is a clear view of the act. The wrongness is real, but it is real the way a colour is real: anchored in how a certain kind of observer is built to respond.

And that is precisely the prize the boldest realists refuse to surrender. A "robust" moral realist — David Enoch is the sharpest recent example — wants moral facts to be true entirely independently of any observer, the way the mass of a proton would be what it is even in a universe with no minds at all.[10] The tardigrade's lifeboat keeps moral facts real, but only by tethering them to a responder. It saves realism by making it modest. The robust realist would rather drown than be rescued on those terms, because to him a fact that depends on how we are built is not the mind-independent fact he was fighting for in the first place.

Two witnesses, one gap

I have run into this exact wall before from the other side. In an earlier piece on gene drives — stretches of engineered DNA that force themselves into nearly every offspring — I argued that a value commitment can be written straight into a physical substrate and then propagate through a population regardless of anyone's belief or consent. Matter, it turns out, can carry a directive. But the directive a gene drive carries is mechanical: it copies itself because of its chemistry, not because there is a reason it should. The gene drive and the dried tardigrade are two witnesses to the same boundary. Both show that matter can store directedness — a packed-up "what will happen next." Neither shows that matter can store a reason. Stored mechanical directedness is not normative force, no matter how faithfully it propagates or how long it waits in the glass.

This also tells you where today's essay sits next to one I wrote on whether moral progress has a signature. That piece took on the other classic worry about moral facts — the one about knowing: even if moral facts existed, how could we ever detect them? There I looked for fingerprints, the dynamical signatures (independent convergence, irreversibility) that would tell truth-tracking apart from mere cultural fashion. This piece takes on the worry that comes first: not "how would we know?" but "what would they even be?" The tardigrade does not answer that question. It cleans it. It strips away the false half of the strangeness — the inertness — and hands you the true half, alone and unobscured: an ought is not a disposition, and nothing in the natural world has yet shown us how to store one.

That is the whole use of the dried animal on the slide. It is the plainest possible demonstration that a thing can be completely real and completely idle at once — that the universe is not embarrassed by inert reality. So when you find yourself recoiling from moral facts because they would "do nothing," check whether you are recoiling at the tardigrade. You are not. The recoil, if it is honest, is aimed at something narrower and stranger and still unexplained: not a fact that sits quietly, but a fact that, sitting quietly, is also somehow a command.
