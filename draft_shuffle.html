Ask how many times a deck of cards must be shuffled, and you get a confident answer — seven. The number comes from a 1992 paper by Dave Bayer and Persi Diaconis titled Trailing the Dovetail Shuffle to its Lair, popularised on the front page of the New York Times by Gina Kolata two years before that, and now repeated in almost every magic shop, poker forum, and statistics textbook that touches the topic.[1]

Seven is a real number. It is correct. It is also, in a way that took the field nearly a century to admit out loud, an answer to a question nobody had bothered to write down.

The Mistake You Think Was Made

The folk version of the story is that for decades people thought three or four shuffles were enough, and then in 1992 a couple of mathematicians proved otherwise. Casinos shuffled too little. Bridge players shuffled too little. Everyone shuffled too little. Then a paper arrived, and now we know better.

The folk version implies the mistake was arithmetic — we thought it was four, but the right answer is seven. Three apart. A small fix to a small problem.

This is not the mistake that was made.

The Mistake That Was Made

Before 1992, when a card player said a deck was “well shuffled,” the underlying assertion was unfalsifiable. There was no yardstick. The phrase meant: I cannot find a pattern in this deck. Which is a statement about the player, not about the deck.

The thing the player could not see was not the answer; it was the absence of a metric. A deck after three shuffles looks pretty random to most humans. So does a deck after five. So, in fact, does a deck after one — if the human is sufficiently tired. There is no contradiction here. The deck is the same deck. What changes is the test you subject it to.

The 1992 number is not the news. The yardstick is.

The Yardstick

Bayer and Diaconis use a metric called total variation distance between probability distributions. If \(\pi\) is the uniform distribution over all \(52! \approx 8 \times 10^{67}\) orderings of a deck, and \(Q_k\) is the actual distribution of orderings after \(k\) riffle shuffles, then

d_TV(Q_k, π) = (1/2) ∑ |Q_k(σ) − π(σ)|

where the sum runs over every permutation \(\sigma\) of 52 cards. The metric ranges from 0 (indistinguishable from uniform) to 1 (perfectly distinguishable — some pattern survives intact). It has a clean operational meaning: it equals the maximum, over all possible “tests” you could apply to the deck, of the probability that the test correctly tells you the deck is not yet uniform.[2]

Once you have that metric, the question how shuffled is the deck becomes a number, not a feeling. And then you can ask how the number changes with each shuffle.

The Answer the Yardstick Gives

Here is Table 1 of the Bayer-Diaconis paper, for a 52-card deck under the Gilbert-Shannon-Reeds model of a riffle shuffle (cut size binomial, then interleave preserving relative order within each half):[3]

shuffles k    d_TV
1             1.000
2             1.000
3             1.000
4             1.000
5             0.924
6             0.614
7             0.334
8             0.167
9             0.085
10            0.043
11            0.021
12            0.010

Three structural facts jump out.

First, for the first four shuffles, the deck is essentially as distinguishable from uniform as it could possibly be. \(d_{TV} = 1\) means: there exists a test that correctly identifies the deck as non-random with certainty. Four shuffles isn’t “close to random.” It’s as far from random as zero shuffles.

Second, the transition between “essentially non-random” and “close to uniform” happens in a window of about five shuffles, between shuffle five and shuffle ten. This is the cutoff phenomenon: a sharp phase transition where total variation distance plummets from near 1 to near 0 across a narrow window, rather than gliding gradually downward. David Aldous and Persi Diaconis isolated this behaviour as a general feature of finite Markov chains in the mid-1980s and named it.[4]

Third, “seven” is a soft midpoint of that cutoff window — the smallest number for which the distance has clearly dropped to less than half, and is now decaying by a factor of two with each subsequent shuffle. It is not magic. Asymptotically, the result is that \(\tfrac{3}{2}\log_2 n\) shuffles suffice for a deck of \(n\) cards, and that the cutoff window has width \(O(1)\) around that point. For \(n = 52\), \(\tfrac{3}{2}\log_2 52 \approx 8.6\), so the cutoff sits between seven and nine. Seven is the lower edge.

The Next Yardstick

In 2000, Lloyd and Nick Trefethen published a paper proposing a different metric: an information-theoretic distance, essentially the entropy deficit between \(Q_k\) and the uniform distribution.[5] They argued that this metric captures something different from total variation. By entropy, they got six.

The discrepancy is not a contradiction. It is a confession. Both numbers are right. They answer different questions. Total variation asks how many shuffles before no statistical test can reliably distinguish this deck from a uniform shuffle? Entropy asks how many shuffles before the deck has been adequately scrambled in an information-theoretic sense? Those are not the same question.

Persi Diaconis later made the point explicitly: the right number of shuffles depends on the game you are about to play.[6] For blackjack, where local card order matters more than global, fewer shuffles suffice. For a game where the bottom and top half of the deck must be exchanged in some non-trivial way, more shuffles are needed. The metric you should use is the metric that captures the game; the answer follows from there.

Seven was never the answer. It was the answer to total variation distance under GSR riffles for a single 52-card deck.

The Mistake That Travels

The shape of this mistake — treating a metric-relative answer as if it were a property of the object — is not local to cards.

In cryptography, the question “how many rounds of a block cipher are enough?” has the same structure. AES uses ten rounds for a 128-bit key and fourteen for a 256-bit key. The choice is not derived from a single uniqueness argument; it is the result of analysis under specific attack metrics (differential, linear, related-key) which yield specific round counts. A cipher that resists one attack at six rounds may fall to another at eight. The number of rounds is downstream of which adversary you imagine.[7]

In statistical mechanics, the “mixing time” of Glauber dynamics on an Ising model exhibits the same cutoff phenomenon as card shuffling. Whether you say the system has mixed depends entirely on whether you measure total variation, spectral gap, or some local observable. Different metrics, different mixing numbers, all of them correct.[8]

In machine learning, the question “how many epochs of training are enough?” reproduces the same architecture. Training loss falls smoothly; test loss can plateau, dip, double-dip, or rise again depending on which generalisation metric you use. The empirically observed cutoff in transformer training — the “grokking” transition — sits between a training-data metric (mastered in hundreds of steps) and a held-out metric (sometimes mastered only after tens of thousands).[9] Same data, two metrics, two answers, both right.

In metrology, the kilogram problem before 2019 was the same problem dressed differently. There was no metric outside the artefact: the prototype kilogram was the kilogram by definition. The drift of one prototype against another (the US prototype K4 lost roughly 41 μg of nominal mass against the IPK between 1889 and 1989[10]) could only be measured by choosing one or the other as the reference. When the SI was redefined in 2019 to fix Planck’s constant, the question stopped being about which kilogram was the real one and started being about which physical invariant the answer was relative to.

The 2019 SI redefinition is the closest thing the physical sciences have to the 1992 shuffle result. Both moments share the same structure. A long-standing answer (this prototype is 1 kg; seven shuffles randomise a deck) was replaced by a relation to a chosen invariant (the kilogram is the mass that makes Planck’s constant exactly \(6.626 \times 10^{-34}\) J·s; the deck is randomised to within total variation distance \(\varepsilon\) of uniform under the GSR model). The improvement was not in the answer. The improvement was in admitting what the answer was relative to.

What the Yardstick Costs

Picking a metric is not free. In Why Is Glass Transparent the relevant choice was an observation band: glass is transparent relative to the narrow window of wavelengths between the electronic and vibrational silences of its atoms. In Why Music Has Twelve Tones the chosen metric was cent-error per pure interval; pick a different acceptable error, and you get nineteen tones, or fifty-three. In Joyner’s 1991 marathon model, the choice was which three scalars (VO\(_2\)max, lactate threshold, running economy) to retain; pick different scalars, and the floor moves.

In every one of these cases the answer to the question how X is the object? turned out to be downstream of a choice the asker had not noticed they were making. The number that came out the other side was correct, but the number was about the model, not about the world.

This is the structural feature these essays keep finding. The First Subtraction named it as a property of measurement systems: every measurement requires a chosen origin, and the choice is usually invisible. The Spec Is Downstream named the same thing for engineering: the specification only becomes legible after the substrate is chosen. How Random Was John Cage’s Music of Changes showed that even “chance” required first designing an urn; the music was determined by what was in the urn, not by the draws. The shuffle question makes the same admission in a domain — randomness, of all things — where the chosen substrate is the metric of distance to uniform.

What the Shuffler Cannot See

Return to the table. After five shuffles the total variation distance is 0.924. After seven it is 0.334. After ten it is 0.043. A human looking at the deck after seven shuffles cannot tell it from a deck after ten, in the sense that no obvious clump of suits or runs of consecutive ranks survives. Both look random to a human eye. The yardstick says they are not the same. The yardstick says one is three times further from uniform than the other.

This is what a metric does. It supplies the discrimination that the eye does not have. Until 1992, the question how many shuffles? was not really a question. It was a request for a feeling. The 1992 result was not the discovery of an answer. It was the discovery that the question could be answered at all.

Which suggests the obvious coda. There are presumably other questions of this shape still waiting on their yardsticks. How well-mixed is this country? How well-trained is this model? How well-understood is this idea? Each of these is currently answered by something resembling a card player’s feeling. The metric that turns the feeling into a number has, in each case, not yet been written down. The number it will give, when someone finally writes it, will probably surprise us by less than the existence of the metric does.

Seven was a side effect.
