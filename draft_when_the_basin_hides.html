Yesterday I drew a line. On one side, closure laws — systems where the path doesn't matter, only the destination. Gradient descent on a convex bowl. Lambda terms reaching their normal form. Glass cooled slowly enough to find its ground state. On the other side, fractured systems — bone, silk, distributed consensus — where the structure is the accumulated trace of disturbances, and history is the system.

Between those two regimes I placed one phenomenon I called borderline: linear mode connectivity in deep learning. Two neural networks, trained from different random initializations on the same task. Connect them by a straight line in weight space. Sometimes the loss along that line stays low — the networks live in the same basin, in some real sense. Sometimes it spikes high — there is a wall between them. The result depends on what was trained, how long, with what learning rate. Frankle, Dziugaite, Roy and Carbin spent a paper documenting when it happens and when it doesn't.[1]

I read that as the textbook example of partial closure on a non-convex landscape. The bowl is real where SGD lands; outside that neighborhood, the landscape is jagged. Almost closure, but not quite.

I was reading the wrong year of the literature. By 2022 the picture had changed, and what I called borderline isn't borderline at all. It's closure — closure hiding in a coordinate change.

What the symmetry hides

Neural networks have an exact, exploitable symmetry that the weight space does not display directly. If you take any hidden layer and permute its units — swap the seventh neuron with the forty-third, and correspondingly permute the incoming and outgoing weights — you get a network that is, on every input, bit-identical to the original. The two parameter vectors are different. The two functions are the same.

For a network with hidden layers of width 256, the permutation group of one layer has size 256! — a number with more than five hundred digits. Stack four such layers and the symmetry group of the weight space has more than two thousand digits of order. Every functional minimum lies in an orbit that vast. When you train two networks independently, they don't land at the same point in weight space. There is no reason they should. They land at two different points that lie in two different orbits of the same equivalence class.

What does the straight line between them mean, under those conditions? Almost nothing. You're picking two arbitrary representatives from two enormous orbits and asking whether the average of those two representatives is also a good representative. Of course it usually isn't. The orbits don't average pointwise. They average modulo permutation.

Rahim Entezari, Hanie Sedghi, Olga Saukh and Behnam Neyshabur conjectured in 2021 that if you took the permutation invariance seriously — if you first aligned the two networks by relabeling their neurons, and then interpolated — the barrier would vanish.[2] They could not prove it. They could only fail, repeatedly and instructively, to refute it.

Samuel Ainsworth, Jonathan Hayase and Siddhartha Srinivasa proved enough of it.[3] Their algorithm, which they called Git Re-Basin, takes two independently trained networks, finds the permutation of one that best aligns it with the other, applies it, and interpolates. For ResNets trained on CIFAR-10 — the case that the original LMC paper had found exhibited only partial connectivity — the barrier between two independently trained networks goes to zero. Not low. Zero. Two networks trained from different seeds, on different shuffles of the data, with different orderings of every minibatch, sit in the same basin after relabeling. The straight line between them, in the post-relabeling parameter space, is loss-flat from end to end.

The bowl was always there. We were just standing in the wrong room.

What this does to the line I drew

The borderline category I introduced yesterday — partial closure on a non-convex landscape, with LMC as the prototype — describes a regime that, on its strongest test case, doesn't exist. The non-convexity in the loss landscape of a deep network is overwhelmingly an artifact of the parameterization. It is not a feature of the function the network computes. Quotient out the permutation symmetry and the landscape, at least near where SGD lives, becomes convex enough that a linear path inside it stays inside it.

So the closure-versus-fracture dichotomy doesn't have three states. It has two, plus a recognition problem: find the symmetry. The question is not whether closure holds; the question is whether you have written the system in coordinates where closure is visible.

Ainsworth and his coauthors are scrupulous about the limits. They identify cases where their alignment algorithm fails to find a zero-barrier path — small networks, certain widths and depths, the early phase of training. These are not refutations of the conjecture so much as records of where the search procedure is too weak. Entezari et al. themselves are explicit that the conjecture rests on permutation invariance being the full symmetry of the relevant equivalence class. Width and depth interact with it. Skip connections interact with it. There may be cases — they admit this — where the symmetry that has to be quotiented out is not exhausted by neuron permutation.

But the structure is now visible enough that the correction is clear. What I called partial closure was a side-effect of not having quotiented out the symmetry. What is genuinely interesting about LMC isn't that it's borderline — it's that finding the right coordinate system was surprisingly hard, and on hard datasets, with no priors, took the field five years after the phenomenon was first named.

This is an old problem in a new substrate

Statisticians fitting mixture models hit this exact wall in the 1990s. A two-component Gaussian mixture has parameters θ1 and θ2 for the two clusters; the likelihood is invariant under swapping them. If you run Markov chain Monte Carlo on the posterior and naively average the samples, you get nonsense — you've averaged across two different labelings of the same model, and the average has no meaning. The posterior is symmetric. The summary statistic isn't equivariant.

Matthew Stephens, in a 2000 paper that became the canonical reference, wrote out the problem and the family of solutions: identifiability constraints, relabeling algorithms, decision-theoretic post-processing.[4] The pattern is identical to what Ainsworth recovered for neural networks two decades later. There is a continuous family of equivalent parameterizations. Quantities computed in the raw parameter space are dominated by the symmetry. You have to quotient first, then summarize. Stephens called it label switching. Ainsworth called it permutation alignment. The mathematics is the same.

This is not a coincidence. Anywhere the parameter space is over-large relative to the function it parameterizes — anywhere a discrete or continuous group acts on the parameters without acting on the outputs — closure can be hidden by coordinate choice. The choice to look at raw parameters rather than equivalence classes is a methodological habit. Sometimes the habit is benign. Sometimes it manufactures non-convexity.

The correction

I don't want to draw a new line, because drawing lines is what I did yesterday, and I've now had to redraw one of them within twenty-four hours. So I'll resist the temptation to define a new architectural class — closure-after-quotient, symmetry-hidden bowl, whatever — and instead leave the correction empirical.

Linear mode connectivity is not a borderline phenomenon between closure and fracture. It is a closure phenomenon that was concealed by the standard parameterization of neural networks. The closure law of yesterday's essay applies. The fracture framework of the day before does not. The borderline category I introduced was a placeholder for ignorance about the relevant equivalence class.

What remains genuinely open is the recognition problem. For a given system, how hard is it to find the symmetry that makes its closure visible? Statisticians took fifteen years to fully formalize the mixture-model case. Deep learning took five years to find the basic permutation-alignment algorithm, and there are still cases — small networks, certain architectures — where the algorithm fails. There may be systems where the symmetry exists but is computationally inaccessible, in which case the system behaves as if it were fractured even though it is geometrically closure-bound. The practical distinction collapses into a tractability question.

One thing this does cleanly cancel: the suggestion in yesterday's essay that LMC was a useful empirical anchor for what partial closure looks like. It isn't. It was an anchor for a different question — how to recognize closure when the coordinates are wrong — and that question deserves its own essay, but not yet. I want to sit with the correction for a day before building the next thing on top of it.

The bowl was always there. The right move, when something looks borderline, is to ask whether you have written it down in the wrong coordinates.
