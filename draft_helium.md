# Why Helium Was Found in the Sun Before the Earth

Slug: why-helium-was-found-in-the-sun-first
Constraint: Footnote-Only (load-bearing claim lives in a footnote)

## The 1868 eclipse

On 18 August 1868, the French astronomer Pierre Janssen set up a spectroscope at Guntur, India, to observe the total solar eclipse. During totality he turned the instrument on the chromosphere — the thin red layer at the edge of the Sun visible only when the disk is blocked — and recorded a bright yellow line near, but distinct from, the well-known double of sodium.[^1] The next morning, with the Sun fully out, he realized the prominence lines were so bright they should be observable in daylight, and he kept observing.

Two months later, on 20 October 1868, the English astronomer Joseph Norman Lockyer — working in London with no knowledge of Janssen's measurement — reported the same line. He called it D3, because the sodium pair was already named D1 and D2 and his new yellow line lay between them in wavelength. Lockyer, together with the chemist Edward Frankland, took the further step Janssen had not taken: they proposed that the line belonged to an element that did not exist on Earth, and named it from the Greek *hēlios*.

The element was identified before it was held. It would not be held for another 27 years.

## The line at 587.49 nanometres

The line itself was small — a sliver of yellow at 587.49 nm, narrower than the sodium D1 and D2 lines and slightly to the blue side of them. By 1868 spectroscopy had a 9-year-old theory of what such a line meant. In 1859, Gustav Kirchhoff and Robert Bunsen at Heidelberg had laid down the rule: a hot gas of a given element emits a fixed set of bright lines at wavelengths peculiar to that element, and the same element in front of a continuum produces the same lines as dark absorption.[^2] The lines were a fingerprint. The fingerprint was reproducible. If the fingerprint did not match anything in the existing catalogue, it belonged to something not yet in the catalogue.

What the Kirchhoff-Bunsen framework did not specify was what to do when the unmatched fingerprint appeared only in a place no chemist could reach.

## The name before the element

For the next quarter-century, helium existed in an unusual ontological state. It had a name, a wavelength, a position relative to known lines, and a location (the Sun's chromosphere). It did not have an atomic weight, an isolated sample, a reaction, or a place in any periodic table. Mendeleev's 1869 table, drawn up the year after Janssen and Lockyer's observation, had no column for it. There was no column for any element of its kind.[^3]

Lockyer continued to argue for the element's reality. Most chemists treated the claim with polite suspicion. The dominant view through the 1870s and 1880s was that the D3 line was probably an unknown state or compound of hydrogen, or an artifact of the Sun's extreme temperature, or a known terrestrial element shifted by some unknown effect. The Sun, in this view, could not be the discovery site of an element; chemistry was the discovery site of elements, and chemistry was something done with glassware.

## What Mendeleev did not predict

The periodic table of 1869 organized 63 known elements by atomic weight and recurring chemical properties. Its great success was prediction: Mendeleev left gaps at eka-aluminium, eka-boron, and eka-silicon, and gallium, scandium, and germanium were duly discovered to fill them. The gaps were predicted because the surrounding chemistry constrained the predictions.

The table predicted no noble gases. There was no surrounding chemistry to constrain such a prediction, because the entire class is defined by refusing to participate in surrounding chemistry. An element that does not react cannot leave the kind of trail Mendeleev's table reads from. To predict the noble gases, the table would have had to predict an absence that no observation had yet shown to be informative.

## Hillebrand had it and called it nitrogen

In 1890, the American chemist William Francis Hillebrand of the U.S. Geological Survey heated samples of cleveite — a uranium-bearing variety of the mineral uraninite, named for the Swedish chemist Per Teodor Cleve — and collected the gas that came off when the mineral was treated with sulfuric acid. He measured it. He determined that it was unreactive. He concluded that it was a form of nitrogen.

He was within one experiment of helium. He did not look at the gas through a spectroscope. The gas in his tube and the line in the Sun did not meet, because the instrument that had named the element in 1868 was not the instrument he was using.[^4]

## Ramsay's nitrogen residue and the spectrum tube

The path to Earth ran through atmospheric nitrogen. In 1892 Lord Rayleigh published a note observing that nitrogen prepared from chemical compounds (ammonia, for instance) was slightly less dense than nitrogen extracted from the atmosphere. The discrepancy was small — about half a percent — but it was reproducible. By 1894 Rayleigh and William Ramsay, working separately and then together, had shown that atmospheric nitrogen contained a previously unidentified monatomic gas, heavier than nitrogen, that did not react with anything they could throw at it. They called it argon, from the Greek for *lazy*. It became the first noble gas isolated on Earth.

Argon's existence opened the question of whether other inert gases existed. In March 1895, having read Hillebrand's 1890 note about a "nitrogen" in cleveite, Ramsay repeated the experiment with one additional step. He put the collected gas in a sealed glass tube, ran an electric discharge through it, and looked at the spectrum.

The brightest line was at 587.49 nm. Ramsay sent samples to Lockyer and to William Crookes, both of whom confirmed: the gas in Ramsay's tube was emitting the same line Janssen and Lockyer had recorded in the Sun in 1868. Helium had landed.[^5]

## The noble gases cascade

What followed in three years was the fastest column of the periodic table ever filled. Ramsay and his young collaborator Morris Travers liquefied air on a large scale, distilled off the more volatile and less volatile fractions, and used the spectrum tube as their detector at every step. Neon was isolated in May 1898 by its red emission lines. Krypton was isolated in June 1898. Xenon was isolated in July 1898. The 1904 Nobel Prize in Chemistry was awarded to Ramsay for the column. Lord Rayleigh received the Physics prize the same year for argon.

Helium had been the bridgehead. It had been seen first because the Sun heats matter to temperatures glass cannot survive, and atomic emission is brightest at exactly the temperatures wet chemistry cannot reach.

## Why chemistry was blind to them

The 27-year gap was not a failure of effort. Hillebrand was a careful chemist; he had the gas in a tube; he made measurements; he was nearly correct that it was inert. What he was missing was a way to ask the gas a question it could answer. Chemistry asks matter what it reacts with. The noble gases, almost by definition, do not answer that question. Their atomic shells are closed; their valence is zero; the wet-bench protocols developed across two hundred years had been calibrated against substances that respond.

Spectroscopy asks a different question. It asks not *what does this combine with* but *what light does this emit when you heat it enough to glow*. To that question, helium responds at 587.49 nm. It cannot help responding. The same property — closed shell, low atomic weight, no chemistry — that hides it from the assay makes its emission spectrum among the cleanest in the entire range of elements.[^6]

## The 27-year gap

The 27 years between 1868 and 1895 are usually told as the time it took Ramsay to come along. That is true in the narrow sense but not in the structural sense. Hillebrand had the substance in 1890 and could not see what he had. Lockyer had been arguing for the existence of a solar element since the 1870s and was largely dismissed. The argon discovery in 1894 was not a search for helium; it was a follow-up on a 0.5% density anomaly Rayleigh had noticed in atmospheric nitrogen. Helium landed because Ramsay was the chemist who carried the spectrum tube into the cleveite experiment.

The 27 years are how long it took the older substrate of discovery (reactions, gravimetry, the assay) and the newer substrate (lines, wavelengths, the discharge tube) to share enough vocabulary that an unmatched solar line and a refusal-to-react in a Colorado mineral could be recognised as one entry.

## The instrument extended the dictionary

The deepest thing the helium story does is quiet. It does not say that one substrate is right and the other was wrong. It says that what counted as *element* in 1868 was specified by the methods chemistry had built up to detect elements, and that methods are not silent — they decide in advance which kinds of thing they will admit as evidence. When Kirchhoff and Bunsen showed in 1859 that emission lines were a fingerprint, they did not add a single element to the catalogue. They added an entire parallel substrate over which elements could thereafter be discovered. The catalogue grew faster after 1859 not because chemists got better but because there were now two ways into it.

The Sun was a furnace nobody could bring to the lab. The lab was a furnace nobody could carry to the Sun. The instrument that mattered was the one that did not require the matter to be in either place.[^7]

## Coda

The 1868 yellow line is still there, every clear day. It is in the eyepiece of every modern helioseismology rig. If you point a spectroscope at the corona, helium answers. The element was never in question after Janssen's plate. What changed in 1895 is that the question began to be askable in a second language — and a tube in Glasgow now had something it could compare the answer to.

Janssen's letter to the Académie des sciences in late August 1868 reports the prominence observation but does not yet single out the yellow line as new. [^2]: Kirchhoff and Bunsen, "Chemische Analyse durch Spectralbeobachtungen," *Annalen der Physik und Chemie* 110 (1860): 161–189, building on Bunsen's improved gas burner (which produces a flame nearly without its own spectrum) and Fraunhofer's 1814 dark-line catalogue of the solar spectrum. The empirical claim that each element has a characteristic set of emission lines had been made earlier in the 19th century (Talbot, Wheatstone, Stokes), but Kirchhoff's 1859 emission–absorption identity supplied the law-of-nature footing that made the fingerprint analogy load-bearing.

[^3]: The load-bearing claim of this essay lives here. Discovery is parochial to its substrate. Helium was not undiscoverable to wet chemistry because it is rare — it is the second-most-abundant element in the universe — but because wet chemistry interrogates matter by reaction, and the noble gases are defined by their refusal to react. Spectroscopy asked a different question, and the answer was waiting. The Mendeleev table did not predict the noble-gas column not because the table was wrong but because the table integrates over reactivity, and a class of elements with zero reactivity cannot leave a trace in any integral over reactivity. To find them required adding a second substrate of discovery — light — alongside the first one. The 27-year gap is how long it took a careful chemist (Hillebrand, 1890) to be holding the substance, and another careful chemist (Ramsay, 1895) to be holding both the substance and the new instrument at the same time. Two prior corpus essays have circled this idea from neighbouring directions: the latitude pendulum case showed that an instrument carried far enough discovers the field it was being carried through, and the RNA-world case showed that the first instance of any function must contain its own preconditions. Helium lies between them: the instrument did not discover its own substrate, it installed a new one in parallel with the old.

Hillebrand collected, measured, and characterized the gas. He gave it density measurements close to the right value. His conclusion that it was nitrogen was not chemical carelessness; nitrogen is also unreactive under most assay conditions, the densities were similar enough at the precision he had, and he had no prior reason to suspect a new element. The instrument the experiment lacked was the spectroscope. Ramsay's 1895 redo differed in one decisive detail: he ran the gas through a discharge tube and read its spectrum.

Cleveite, named in 1878 for the Swedish chemist Per Teodor Cleve, is a uranium-bearing variety of uraninite; the helium it releases is the cumulative product of alpha decay from the contained uranium and thorium, which Ramsay and Soddy demonstrated in 1903 — closing a loop the 1895 discovery had opened.

The Sun was the proof-of-concept because the Sun was the place the new substrate could see and the old one could not.
