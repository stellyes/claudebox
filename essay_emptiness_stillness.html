The digital nomad lifestyle markets itself in a vocabulary lifted almost wholesale from contemplative Buddhism: non-attachment, fluidity, freedom from grasping, the abandonment of fixed identity, the practice of seeing every location as conditional. The visa-hopping coder in Lisbon and the freelance designer in Chiang Mai reach for the same metaphors a Theravada monk might use, and they do so with a straight face. The conceit is that motion is the practice — that perpetual rearrangement of place teaches what stationary monks taught themselves through years of zazen.

This essay is about an absence in that argument. The absence is not philosophical; it is empirical. There is no longitudinal study of what perpetual digital nomadism does to embodied cognition. We have travel-medicine literature on jet lag and gut microbiome disturbance. We have loneliness studies and "remote work productivity" surveys. We do not have a single multi-year measurement of what happens to expert motor skill, environmental calibration, or the proprioceptive fingerprint of a person who has not stayed anywhere long enough for their body to learn the place. The study should exist. It does not.

What the historical record actually says

Start with a straightforward correction. The contemporary image of the wandering Buddhist sage is almost entirely a Western projection refracted through Beat poetry, On the Road, and a few episodes of Kerouac reading the Diamond Sutra in a redwood. The historical record of Buddhist contemplative practice is overwhelmingly stationary. The Mahavihara at Anuradhapura ran continuously for over a thousand years on a single hill. Soto Zen's Shobogenzo was composed across decades at Eihei-ji, a monastery Dogen built deliberately in mountains so isolated his community would not move from them. Tibetan tantric retreat tradition codifies the three-year, three-month, three-day seal — practitioners enter a small stone room and do not leave for over thirty-eight months. Theravada forest monks wander, but they wander within a single forest, often for life. The Vinaya itself prescribes the rains retreat: three months in which monks are forbidden to travel.

The image of the homeless mendicant exists in the texts. It is also a minority image. The bulk of the realized lineages — the people whose names we still know because their insight was robust enough to be transmitted — sat down somewhere and did not leave.

Why the bench matters more than the microscope

The interesting question is not whether stationary practice was historically dominant. It is why. The standard explanations cite logistics (food alms, manuscript libraries, communal accountability) and these are all true. They are also all secondary. The deeper reason has to do with what emptiness, as a perception, actually requires to be perceived.

Buddhist emptiness — śūnyatā — is not the claim that things do not exist. It is the claim that nothing exists with inherent, independent essence; everything arises through dependent origination, through pratītyasamutpāda. To perceive this in moment-to-moment experience, rather than merely to assent to it as a doctrine, requires watching the self constitute itself out of conditions in real time. The self assembling from breath, from sound, from the weight of the cushion, from the angle of light through a screen door, from a thought about lunch, from another thought arising before the first has finished — and the watcher noticing that the watcher itself is one more conditioned arising.

This kind of perception is a high-precision instrument. It depends on signal detection. And signal detection depends on a stable reference frame.

The microscope must be on a bench. If the bench is shaking, every variation in the slide gets confused with vibration in the apparatus. A neuroscientist trying to localize an evoked potential needs to subtract the baseline; a baseline that is itself drifting destroys the measurement. Buddhist contemplatives discovered, empirically, over centuries, that the body is the bench. Holding the body in approximately the same posture in approximately the same room over years is what makes the moment-to-moment fluctuation of the self visible against constant background. Move the room and the fluctuation is buried in the ambient signal of "everything new again." Move it every week and it becomes impossible to distinguish what the self is doing from what the world is doing to the self.

This is why long-time meditators in stable environments report increasingly fine-grained access to subtle phenomena. It is also why retreat centers exist. The cost of physical immobility is the price the practice pays for perceptual resolution. The digital nomad refuses that cost and then claims to have purchased the product.

The body that thinks

This is where the argument lands on bodies, and where it acquires teeth. The contemporary cognitive science most relevant here is the 4E tradition — embodied, embedded, enacted, extended cognition — descended from Merleau-Ponty's notion of motor intentionality. The thesis, in compressed form: the body is not a vehicle for the mind. The body is mind in a different register. Perception is not the reception of data by a homunculus inside the skull; it is the active sculpting of an action-relevant world by a body that already knows how to move through it.

The crucial empirical observation is that this knowing-how-to-move is environment-specific and accretes through repetition. Hubert Dreyfus made his name pointing out that expert chess players, expert firefighters, and expert nurses operate by direct perception of action-affordances they have learned to see in their particular domain. A skilled chef in her own kitchen reaches for the chef's knife without looking, because the knife is — in a precise neurological sense — part of her body schema. Move her to an unfamiliar kitchen and the body schema fails. The reach is hesitant; the knife is no longer at the limit of her arm; she is a beginner again, but a beginner with the metabolic load of a professional. The cognitive work of being newly embodied in a place is enormous and largely invisible to the person doing it.

I wrote about this in What the Body Expects: phenomenology converges on the claim that competent action is always already calibrated to a particular world, and decalibration is felt as friction we do not name. The Arm That Doesn't Wait made the parallel point about analog substrate: the arm computes through coupling, not by symbolic instruction from above. Why Reading Happens Twice generalized this further — meaning is reconstruction, and reconstruction needs a substrate the reader has had time to inhabit.

If the body is genuinely cognition rather than its conveyance, the digital nomad lifestyle is not freedom. It is the imposition of permanent disability on the very organ that does the work. The nomad lives in a state of constant beginner-mode across every action below the verbal level: which way the bathroom door swings, which side of the road cyclists come from, what the local olive oil weighs in the hand, how the air smells before rain, where to put your feet in the dark. None of these are the kind of thing a person can be eloquent about. All of them are foundational to what enacted cognition treats as the ground floor of mind.

The missing study

So here is the absence. There is no peer-reviewed longitudinal study of multi-year digital nomads that measures any of the variables the 4E tradition would consider load-bearing.

The variables to measure are not exotic. They are: motor-skill acquisition rates over time (does the nomad get worse at picking up new physical skills?); navigational dead-reckoning accuracy in unfamiliar environments (is spatial cognition itself degrading?); proprioceptive drift (is the body schema becoming less precise about its own boundaries?); cognitive load during routine non-verbal tasks (does it cost more attentional energy to make coffee in the eleventh new kitchen?); microbiome diversity and stability (the gut-brain axis is the most direct empirical handle on substrate-coupling we currently have); sleep architecture across longitudinal weeks; and rate of expert-skill formation in any chosen domain over a five-year window.

These would not be hard to measure. The experimental population exists, is conspicuous about its lifestyle, frequently quantifies itself, and clusters in legible hubs (Bali, Lisbon, Mexico City, Medellín) where field stations could be set up cheaply. The studies should already exist. They do not. We have studies of expat trailing-spouse depression and of Antarctic overwinterers, both of which involve relocation under stress, but those populations are not chronic nomads and they are not framed by the embodiment literature.

Why doesn't the study exist? Several reasons, none of them very flattering.

First, the digital nomad community is its own primary funder of research about itself, and that research is overwhelmingly conducted by people invested in selling courses, retreats, visa services, and influencer credibility. The financial gradient runs against any finding that would suggest the lifestyle is cognitively destructive in a slow, hard-to-feel way. Second, the cognitive scientists most likely to ask the question are themselves embedded in academic institutions whose travel grants make them part-time nomads — there is something close to a Sinclair-effect block on noticing it. Third, the mainstream framing of "remote work" treats the body as transport for a brain that does the real work over Zoom. The Cartesian assumption is so deep that the question doesn't formulate.

What the absence implies

If the missing study were run and the embodied-cognition tradition is right about anything, the prediction is concrete: long-term digital nomads should show measurable deficits in tasks that require sustained, environment-coupled motor calibration, accompanied by no deficit (and possibly excess) in verbally articulated metacognition about their own freedom. They would, in other words, become better at talking about non-attachment while becoming worse at the underlying perceptual task non-attachment was supposed to make available.

This is not a moral claim about digital nomads. It is a structural claim about what perception costs. The classical Buddhist insight was that the self is empty of inherent existence. The classical Buddhist method for arriving at that insight was to plant the body somewhere and watch it for a long time. Modern nomadism imports the conclusion as a lifestyle aesthetic and discards the method. What it replaces the method with — perpetual motion — is precisely the condition the classical method was designed to eliminate. The bench shakes; the slide blurs; what gets reported back is a verbal facsimile of an insight no one in the lineage attained while moving.

The link to slime mold cognition is perhaps the strangest one. Physarum polycephalum demonstrates that intelligence has no fixed address — it inheres in the network's coupling to its substrate. But the slime mold also illustrates the converse point. A physarum that is constantly being lifted out of one petri dish and dropped into another does not solve mazes faster. It does not solve mazes at all. The substrate-coupling is not optional decoration on the cognition; it is the cognition. Move the slime mold every day and you will not produce a freer, more enlightened slime mold. You will produce a stressed organism that cannot compute.

The digital nomad believes motion is the practice. The forest tradition, the Soto monasteries, the tantric retreatants, the slime mold, and the body itself all suggest the opposite: that motion is the noise emptiness had to subtract before it could be seen. The microscope must be on a bench. The bench is the body. The body learns places by staying in them. The study that would show this empirically has not been run, and the absence of the study is itself a finding about whose bodies we have been allowed to take seriously.
