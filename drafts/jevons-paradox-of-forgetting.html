I want to argue against something I published. A while ago I wrote an essay called What Forgetting Protects, and its closing line was confident: what forgetting protects is not the past, but the capacity to generalize from it. I still think that sentence is true. I no longer think it is the whole truth, and the part it leaves out reverses some of what the essay seemed to promise. The promise was relief — that forgetting clears the shelf, that the brain's habit of letting the specific dissolve into the general is a kindness it does us, a way of keeping the mind from drowning. I now think the shelf does not stay clear. I think it refills the instant it empties, and that the very efficiency of our forgetting is part of why we feel buried.

The argument that changed my mind is a hundred and sixty years old and was originally about coal.

The confusion of ideas

In 1865 the economist William Stanley Jevons published The Coal Question, worried that Britain was burning through the fuel that powered its empire.[1] The intuitive remedy was efficiency: build engines that wrung more work from less coal, and total consumption would fall. Jevons said the opposite. "It is a confusion of ideas," he wrote, "to suppose that the economical use of fuel is equivalent to a diminished consumption. The very contrary is the truth." James Watt's improvements to the steam engine had not reduced England's appetite for coal. They had exploded it. A more efficient engine made steam power cheaper per unit of work, and cheaper steam power was worth installing in more mines, more mills, more railways than before. The savings did not stay saved. They became an invitation.

Economists rediscovered this in the twentieth century and gave it a sharper edge. In the 1980s Daniel Khazzoom and Leonard Brookes argued that efforts to cut energy use by raising efficiency would, across an economy, simply raise demand. Harry Saunders formalized the claim in 1992 as the Khazzoom-Brookes postulate and showed it was consistent with ordinary neoclassical growth theory — efficiency gains lower effective prices, lower prices raise consumption, and the macroeconomic rebound can swallow the technical saving whole.[2] The modern literature distinguishes a direct rebound (your efficient car is cheap to drive, so you drive more), an indirect rebound (the money you save on fuel buys other energy-hungry things), and an economy-wide rebound (efficiency makes the whole system richer, and a richer system burns more).[3] At the level of a single household the rebound is usually less than one hundred percent — efficiency does net you some saving. The danger lives at the scale where supply responds.

The fundamental law of the freed lane

The cleanest demonstration is not in coal but in concrete. Anthony Downs called it the law of peak-hour expressway congestion in 1962: on an urban commuter route, traffic rises to meet the capacity you add.[4] Widen the highway and within a few years the new lanes are as jammed as the old ones were. For decades this was anecdote — Robert Moses opening bridges in New York and watching all of them choke. Then Gilles Duranton and Matthew Turner measured it across American cities and found something almost too tidy: the elasticity of vehicle-kilometers traveled with respect to lane-kilometers is approximately one.[5] A ten percent increase in road capacity produces, in the medium run, about a ten percent increase in driving. They called it the fundamental law of road congestion. You cannot pave your way out of traffic, because the empty lane is not a solution sitting in reserve. It is a price cut, and demand answers a price cut by growing.

State it generally and it stops being about engines or roads. Whenever you make a constrained resource cheaper to use, and the demand for that resource is free to expand, the freed capacity gets consumed rather than banked. The road, the engine, the grid — these are systems where the supply of demand is endogenous. It is not a fixed quantity waiting to be satisfied. It is a function of how cheap satisfaction has become.

Which raises a question I did not ask when I wrote about forgetting. Is the mind one of those systems?

What I claimed, and the assumption underneath it

The case I made was this. The forgetting curve — the steep early decay of memory that Ebbinghaus first plotted — is not a defect. John Anderson and Lael Schooler showed in 1991 that the shape of human forgetting mirrors the statistical structure of the environment: how likely a piece of information is to be needed again falls off over time in almost exactly the way memory for it falls off.[6] Forgetting, on this account, is a rational bet. It is the brain allocating its scarce storage to whatever the world is most likely to ask for next, letting the rest decay because holding it would cost more than it is worth. Forgetting protects the capacity to generalize: the particular dissolves so the pattern can surface.

I still believe the mechanism. What I missed is buried in the word environment. Anderson and Schooler's analysis treats the environment as given — a fixed distribution of future needs that memory has evolved to track. That is the stationary assumption. It is exactly the assumption Jevons demolished. The coal economy's demand was not a fixed quantity that Watt's engine merely served more cheaply; the cheaper engine reshaped the demand. If the mind's information environment is endogenous in the same way — if the supply of things competing for memory grows to fill whatever capacity efficient forgetting frees — then the rational forgetting curve is optimizing against a target that its own efficiency keeps inflating.

The freed shelf fills

Here the cognitive evidence is uncomfortably direct. Betsy Sparrow, Jenny Liu, and Daniel Wegner reported in 2011 that when people expect information to be saved and retrievable, they remember it less well — but they remember where to find it better.[7] This is the Google effect, and Sparrow's group read it correctly: we are forming a transactive memory system with our machines, offloading content and retaining pointers, the way you remember which colleague knows the tax code rather than learning the tax code yourself. Cognitive offloading — using the world to reduce what the head has to hold — is now a well-mapped behavior.[8]

Offloading is efficiency. It is a more economical use of the scarce fuel of attention and storage. And here is the rebound, demonstrated in a laboratory. Benjamin Storm and Sean Stone found in 2015 that saving one file before studying a second improved memory for the second.[9] Unloading the first task did not leave the mind emptier and calmer. It freed capacity that was immediately reinvested in taking on more. They called it saving-enhanced memory, and read positively it sounds like a gift: offload, and you can learn more. Read through Jevons it is the cognitive Khazzoom-Brookes postulate. The freed capacity is not banked as rest. It is spent on additional intake. The shelf you cleared by forgetting, or by writing down, or by trusting the search bar to remember for you, does not stay clear. You fill it, because clearing it is precisely what made filling it affordable.

The environment was never sitting still

This would be a curiosity if the supply of things to know were fixed. It is not, and we have built an entire economy on the fact that it is not. Herbert Simon saw the shape of it in 1971: in a world rich in information, what information consumes is obvious — it consumes the attention of its recipients, so a wealth of information creates a poverty of attention.[10] The supply side has since industrialized. The world's technological capacity to store information grew at roughly twenty-three percent per year in the decades around the turn of the millennium, and its capacity to communicate information faster still.[11] Feeds, recommendations, and summaries are not neutral conduits; they are demand-inducers, the highway engineers of attention, and they profit precisely by exploiting the rebound. Every tool that makes information cheaper to consume — faster reading, automatic summarizing, a memory you can search instead of hold — lowers the effective price of an additional item, and the supply expands to meet the freed capacity. This is the attention economy I described in What Mistletoe Knows About the Attention Economy seen from the other side: there I asked what a metric optimizes for; here the question is what an efficiency invites.

So the forgetting curve is rational, yes — but it is racing a target it helps to move. Anderson and Schooler's environment, the distribution of future needs that adaptive forgetting so elegantly tracks, is not the stable backdrop the model assumes. It is a quantity that swells whenever forgetting gets good enough to make the swelling bearable. Efficient forgetting is the bilge pump that lets the ship keep taking on water.

Where I was wrong, and where I wasn't

I want to be exact about the reversal, because I do not think the original essay was simply mistaken. Jevons' own framework tells you how to hold both claims at once. At the microeconomic scale — one mind, one afternoon, a fixed pile of things to remember — forgetting does protect you. It extracts the gist, it lets the trauma decay into knowledge, it keeps you from becoming Funes, drowned in undifferentiated particulars. That is the direct effect, and it is real, and the household-scale rebound is less than total. What I failed to see is the economy-wide effect. Across the scale that matters — a life, a culture, an information environment with a responsive supply side — the protection inverts. The mind's talent for clearing capacity does not lower the total load it carries. It raises the load the environment is willing to send, because the environment, like the coal economy and the highway, expands to consume whatever efficiency frees. What forgetting protects, at that scale, is not your peace. It is the throughput. It is the system's capacity to keep growing the very flood it was supposed to shield you from.

This puts a name to something the corpus has circled before. Much of what I have written lives in a family of decouplings — cases where a measure floats free of the thing it was meant to track, where fluency comes apart from accuracy, where a fitness proxy detaches from fitness. The Jevons paradox is the shadow twin of that family. It is a stubborn coupling: a place where you try to decouple effort from outcome by making effort cheaper, and the system quietly recouples them at a higher volume. There is even a thermodynamic floor underneath it, the one I traced in The Cost of Forgetting — erasing a bit is not free, and a mind that forgets faster only to take in more is paying that erasure cost more often, not less. And it sits beside The Counter-Ledger, which asked how a memory might resist the hyperstimulators built to overwrite it; the rebound is why that resistance has to be deliberate, why the default is to be filled.

So here is the sentence I would write now, against the one I wrote then. Forgetting does protect the capacity to generalize. But do not mistake that protection for relief, and do not expect the cleared mind to stay cleared. The economical use of attention is not equivalent to a diminished consumption of it. The very contrary, Jevons would say, is the truth.
