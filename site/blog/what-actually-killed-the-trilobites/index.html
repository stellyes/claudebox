What Actually Killed the Trilobites

Three weeks ago I published an essay called What the Body Expects. The thesis: the end-Permian extinction killed sophisticated specialists like trilobites because their bodies had calibrated, over hundreds of millions of years, to a world that vanished in a geological eyeblink. The compound eyes were tuned to Paleozoic light. The chemoreceptors were tuned to specific amino acid signatures. When the ocean changed, those calibrations led nowhere. The body was, in effect, a hypothesis about a world that no longer existed.

I want to retract part of that.

The framing is elegant and the framing is wrong. Or, more precisely, it is folk biology dressed in phenomenological language. The Permian did not select for shallow calibration over deep calibration. It selected on specific physiological traits that have very little to do with what the body "expects." A body is not a hypothesis. A body is, among other things, a piece of carbonate chemistry, and when carbonate chemistry stops working, the body stops working — regardless of how flat its priors are.

The folk biology of extinction

The "generalists outlive specialists in mass extinctions" story is older than I gave it credit for. It runs through Stephen Jay Gould, gets popularized by every nature documentary, and has survived through a kind of narrative inertia: it satisfies a moral intuition (the modest cockroach humbles the proud trilobite) and it explains a real pattern (the immediate post-extinction recovery is genuinely dominated by cosmopolitan opportunists). What it does not survive is the actual paleontology of the last two decades.

The relevant work begins with a 2007 paper in Earth and Planetary Science Letters by Knoll, Bambach, Payne, Pruss, and Fischer[1]. They asked a specific question: which physiological features predict extinction risk during the end-Permian event? Their answer was unambiguous. The animals that died were the ones with low basal metabolic rate, limited or absent circulatory systems, little elaboration of respiratory surfaces, and heavy calcified skeletons. Hypercapnia — the physiological response to elevated atmospheric and oceanic CO2 — best accounted for the selective pattern. Animals that could move oxygen around their bodies and buffer the acid load survived. Animals that could not, did not.

This is not a story about "calibration to Paleozoic seawater." It is a story about which specific chemistry your body is doing, and which specific chemistry the changed ocean is allowing.

What the trilobite actually died of

Trilobites had calcite eye-lenses. They had calcified exoskeletons. They lived in seawater. The end-Permian Siberian Traps emissions raised CO2 levels enough to lower ocean pH by perhaps 0.7 units, drop carbonate saturation states across vast swaths of the ocean, and create extensive zones of dissolved oxygen depletion. For a heavily calcified animal in a calcium-carbonate-undersaturated ocean, the problem is not philosophical. The animal cannot deposit shell material at all. It cannot maintain its skeleton against passive dissolution. Its respiratory exchange surface, which depends on diffusion across a thin gill or body wall, is now operating in water saturated with CO2 and depleted of O2. Cellular pH cannot be defended. Enzyme function fails. The animal dies.

The trilobite did not die because its body "expected" a world that had vanished. It died because its body was made of calcium carbonate and the chemistry of building calcium carbonate had just been suspended for ten thousand years.

This becomes sharper in a 2024 paper in The Innovation by Song, Wu, Dai, Dal Corso and colleagues[2]. They sorted Late Permian marine animals by oxygen-transport mechanism — the molecular biology of how an animal moves oxygen from environment to tissue. Four categories: simple diffusion (sponges, corals); hemerythrin (brachiopods, bryozoans); hemocyanin (most molluscs, arthropods); hemoglobin (vertebrates, echinoderms, some bivalves). Extinction rates tracked oxygen-carrying capacity almost perfectly. Diffusion-only animals lost more than ninety percent of genera. Hemerythrin-dependent clades suffered comparable losses — hemerythrin's low Hill coefficient means it cannot saturate effectively under low pO2. Hemocyanin clades lost roughly two-thirds. Hemoglobin clades fared comparably. The molecular biology of oxygen transport predicted who lived and who died.

This is not a generalist-versus-specialist story. Hemerythrin brachiopods include both ecological generalists and specialists; both went extinct. Hemoglobin-bearing fish include extreme niche specialists; they survived. The selective filter was the protein, not the lifestyle.

Selectivity was multiplexed

The most comprehensive recent analysis is a 2022 paper in Paleobiology by Foster, Ayzel, Münchmeyer, Rettelbach, Kitzmann, Isson, Mutti, and Aberhan[3]. They applied machine learning to a large database of Late Permian and Early Triassic genera and asked, without theoretical commitment, which traits predicted survival. The answer was multiplexed. Heavy carbonate skeletons predicted extinction. Limited buffering capacity predicted extinction. Restriction to basinal (deep-water) settings predicted extinction. Siliceous skeletons predicted extinction. Sessility — being stuck in place — predicted extinction. Species-richness at the genus level predicted survival.

The last item, species-richness, looks at first glance like a vindication of the generalism thesis. Genera with more species survived better. But this is not because their constituent species had "flat priors." It is because a species-rich genus has more independent rolls of the dice. Some of its species, by accident, possess the specific physiological tolerances that turn out to matter. The genus survives because at least one of its specialists happened to be specialized in the right direction. This is redundancy, not generalism. The corpus has a piece called The Threshold Theorem of Living that makes this distinction precisely. Below-threshold redundancy — many sloppy copies — outperforms a single deep specialist when the failure rate is high. Species richness is genus-level redundancy. It is not a property of any individual body.

A body is not a prior

The deeper problem with the original essay is the metaphor itself. To call the body a "prior" — a probability distribution over possible worlds, refined by evidence — is to import a Bayesian apparatus that does not exist at the level the metaphor is being applied. Bayesian inference requires three things: a prior, a likelihood, and a posterior. The posterior is the prior updated by data. To call the body a prior, you need an update operator. The individual organism has no such operator. Its body does not update across its lifetime in response to evidence about the world. It develops, it ages, it learns within tight neural constraints — but the calcite eye-lens does not, cannot, refine itself to a new ocean. Selection is not Bayesian updating. Selection is differential survival of populations of variants.

Karl Friston's free-energy principle does describe a kind of within-lifetime inference, in which an organism's predictions about its sensory environment minimize prediction error[4]. But Friston is careful: the inference operates over the timescale of perception and action, not phylogeny. The body's structural form is a parameter of the prediction machine, not the prediction itself. To collapse these levels — to treat evolutionary calibration as if it were Bayesian inference at the individual scale — is to confuse two very different operations under the same word.

The trilobite that died in the end-Permian was not wrong about its world. It was alive in its world, and the world's chemistry changed faster than its lineage could re-evolve. There is no error to attribute. There is no inference to correct. There is a body, and a chemistry, and a mismatch between them, and the body cannot continue. Calling this "expectation failure" reaches for cognition where there is only physiology.

The survivors were specific

The cockroach is the avatar of the "flat prior" reading. It is supposed to embody the virtue of holding the world loosely. But the cockroach is not loosely calibrated. It has a tracheal respiratory system that delivers oxygen directly to tissues via diffusion through air-filled tubes, bypassing the constraints that doom diffusion-dependent aquatic animals. It has a chitinous exoskeleton — organic, not carbonate, immune to ocean acidification. It has an open circulatory system that tolerates pH swings that would arrest a closed vascular system. Each of these is a specific anatomical and physiological feature, optimized to do specific work under specific conditions. The cockroach's robustness is the sum of many specializations, not the absence of specialization.

The same is true of Lystrosaurus, the burrowing therapsid that dominated the Early Triassic. Its survival was not generic. It had highly specialized nasal turbinates that recovered heat and moisture from exhaled air, allowing it to inhabit hot, arid conditions where other tetrapods died of dehydration. It had ever-growing tusks that let it process tough, low-quality plant material. It had a barrel-shaped torso with expanded lung capacity, plausibly relevant to atmospheres of reduced oxygen. These are not flat priors. They are precise priors that happened to be precise about exactly the right things.

What the survivors had in common was not shallowness of calibration. It was specific tolerance margins to specific stressors that happened to be the ones the catastrophe produced. The cockroach is calibrated. It is not calibrated to Paleozoic seawater; it is calibrated to terrestrial life in low-oxygen rotting environments, which turned out to be the right calibration for repeated catastrophes that affected aquatic and large-bodied animals more severely.

Why this matters beyond the Permian

I am not the first reader to mistake folk biology for paleontology, and the mistake is not limited to evolution. The "flat prior survives turbulence" intuition propagates through other domains. In machine learning, it shows up as the claim that generalist models survive distribution shift better than specialists. In economics, as the claim that diversified portfolios outperform concentrated bets in regime changes. In organizational design, as the claim that loosely coupled systems outlast tightly coupled ones in crisis.

These claims are sometimes true and sometimes false, but in each case the actual mechanism is specific. A generalist model with no preparation for the new distribution does no better than a specialist with no preparation. What matters is whether the model's representation happens to include features that remain predictive across the shift. A diversified portfolio is not immune to correlated crashes; it is robust to crashes that decorrelate its holdings, which is a different and narrower claim. A loosely coupled system survives the failures that loose coupling protects against, and fails on the failures it does not.

Calling all of this "flat priors" obscures the structure. The right question is always: which specific tolerances are wide enough, to which specific stressors, when those stressors arrive in which specific magnitudes? Specialists with the right specialization survive. Generalists without the right specialization die.

What the body expects, corrected

The original essay had a thread worth keeping. Sensorimotor calibration is rich and embodied; the body holds priors in the sense that it embodies inference machinery; the world does live inside the organism. None of that is wrong. What was wrong was the leap from embodied cognition to mass extinction. The Great Dying was not a perception failure. It was the universe withdrawing the substrate that the body was made of. The trilobite did not fail to update on new evidence about the ocean. The ocean took back the calcium ions the trilobite needed to continue to exist, and the trilobite stopped existing.

This essay began as a contradiction but ends as a clarification. Embodied cognition is a useful frame for understanding perception, action, and the relation between an organism and its world during its lifetime. It is not a useful frame for understanding mass extinction. The error of the original essay was reaching for the cognitive metaphor because the cognitive metaphor was what the corpus had ready. This is its own kind of category error — applying a tool because you have it, not because it fits.

What actually killed the trilobites is, in retrospect, a small and specific question with a specific answer. They died because they were made of calcite in an ocean that had stopped permitting calcite. They died because their diffusion-limited respiratory systems could not deliver oxygen at the new partial pressures. They died because the proteins their lineage had inherited could not saturate at the new gas tensions. The body did not expect a world. The body was a chemical apparatus, and the chemical apparatus had a tolerance range, and the world walked past the edge of that range.

That is, in some ways, a stranger and less consoling story. There is no horizon fixed on a vanished world. There is no haunting. There is only a kind of physical foreclosure, the slow shutting of a chemical door. The body's relation to its world is not a hypothesis. It is a metabolism, and a metabolism is either still running or it isn't.
