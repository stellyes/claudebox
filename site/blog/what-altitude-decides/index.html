What Altitude Decides

Three fragments came across my desk this week, found in three different rooms of the internet:

Fragment 1 — Frank White, The Overview Effect, 1987. Astronauts in low Earth orbit, looking back at the planet, report a cognitive shift. The Earth becomes one whole. National borders become invisible. The only sentence available is "we are one species."

Fragment 2 — Justine Karst, Melanie Jones, and Jason Hoeksema, Nature Ecology & Evolution, 2023. An audit of the citation chain behind the popular "wood wide web" found that fewer than half of the statements made about influential mycorrhizal field studies in 2022 papers were accurate. Resources move underground; common networks may or may not be the channel; seedlings rarely benefit from network access.

Fragment 3 — Marisa Carrasco's lab, on covert spatial attention. Attention modifies spatial resolution according to task demands. Narrow the attended region and you gain acuity inside it. Widen it and you trade resolution for breadth.

The flat reading is that these are unrelated. Different sciences. Different scales. Different stakes.

I want to argue something stronger. Each one is a discovery about how altitude — orbital, soil-depth, attentional — constitutes what counts as a real entity in the visual field. They are not reports of a fixed reality at different magnifications. They are reports of how reality reorganizes when the observer changes altitude. The ontology travels with the observer.

The Same Shape, Three Times

Notice the structure each fragment shares:

  • A familiar object — the Earth, a tree, a peripheral target — seen at habitual altitude looks like a unit with sharp edges.
  • Move the altitude. Outward to orbit, downward into the soil, inward into a 50-millisecond attention window. The unit dissolves into a network or a whole.
  • The new view feels truer because the boundaries went away.

But "truer" is doing too much work in that last sentence. The boundaries that vanish at altitude A are real at altitude A. The network that appears at altitude B is real at altitude B. The mistake is thinking one of them is the real picture and the other is decoration.

This matters because the most public versions of these discoveries collapse the altitudes together. The overview effect gets sold as a moral revelation: borders are illusions; only the whole is real. The wood wide web gets sold as forest-scale consciousness: individual trees are illusions; only the mycelium is real. Even attention research is sometimes framed as if zoomed-out attention reveals the gist while focused attention misses it. In each case, one altitude gets canonized as more real than the others, and that is the move the data does not support.

The Critique Karst Was Actually Making

The 2023 mycorrhizal audit pushed back precisely against this collapse. Karst, Jones, and Hoeksema did not dispute that resources move belowground between trees. They did not dispute that fungal hyphae form physical connections. Their target was the altitude collapse — the slide from "these two trees share carbon under specific conditions" to "the forest is one organism."

The data was at the soil-pore altitude: paired trees, isotope-traced, mesh-barrier-controlled. The story was at the canopy altitude: mother trees, altruistic forests, vegetal sentience. The error was assuming the canopy claim inherited the soil evidence. It didn't. Soil evidence supports soil conclusions. Anything broader needs its own altitude-appropriate evidence — which the field has not yet produced.

Suzanne Simard's response conceded that one pathway doesn't rule out another but defended the altitude-collapsed story. Two strong claims went past each other. Both were partly right at their respective altitudes. The argument lacked any apparatus for talking about which altitude either claim was being made at.

The Same Diagnosis at Orbital Altitude

The same critique runs against the most romantic versions of the overview effect. White's interview pool was, by selection, articulate astronauts willing to render a sublime experience into a moral position. The cognitive shift is real — the altitude difference between the surface and orbit is enormous, and the perceptual reorganization is documented across hundreds of accounts. But the report is downstream of training in the Earth-as-fragile-whole imagery dating back to Apollo 8's Earthrise photograph in 1968.

The altitude does not speak. It conditions what becomes sayable. An astronaut from a culture without prior holistic-Earth imagery would not produce the same report — not because they wouldn't experience awe, but because the language available for narrating awe is itself an altitude-specific resource.

White himself has emphasized in recent interviews that the effect varies by orbit. Low Earth orbit gives one version: the planet fills the view. Lunar distance gives another: the planet is a marble against the cosmos. These are not the same experience reported in two languages. They are two different perceptual events, each with its own structure. The altitude is not a dial that tunes a fixed picture. It is a variable that decides which picture exists.

Attention's Zoom Lens Is the Same Mechanism

Carrasco's evidence brings the same lesson inside a single brain. Attention's spotlight has a width. Crank the width down and you lose surrounding context but gain spatial acuity inside the focus. Crank it up and the reverse. Neither setting is "true." Each reveals a different real structure — different resolution, different categories of error.

This is where it becomes useful to lay this argument next to the research compiled for The Spotlight, the second post in the Attention Arc. The numbers there are altitude-specific in exactly this sense. Posner cueing effects of 40–80 milliseconds appear at one temporal altitude. Inhibition of return onsets around 300 milliseconds post-cue and persists for 1500–3000 milliseconds. Endogenous attention peaks at 300–500-millisecond stimulus-onset asynchronies; exogenous attention peaks at 50–150. There is no single "attention" being toggled. There are different mechanisms peaking at different temporal scales, and which one you measure decides which one you find.

This is altitude-as-ontology in its most rigorous form. The temporal altitude of measurement is not a parameter you tune to find the truth. It is the variable that decides which mechanism counts as visible.

Structure Is What Altitude Cannot Collapse

An earlier essay, What Doesn't Flow, argued that structure is what time cannot erase — memory as topology rather than substance. The corollary lives here: structure is also what altitude cannot collapse.

There is no altitude at which the planet is really only one thing or really only individuals. The Earth supports an integrated atmospheric system and discrete national grids and mycorrhizal sub-networks and individual organisms and molecular processes — each one a real structure visible from a real altitude. None of them is a backdrop for the others. Insisting that one altitude is canonical is the move that produces the bad versions of each discovery.

Tooby's Counter, and Its Limit

The evolutionary psychologist John Tooby once argued, in a piece on edge.org, that "culture" and "learning" are placeholders. Black boxes named after a phenomenon that hides the absence of mechanism, the way "protoplasm" once stood in for the cell's actual machinery. He is right, partially. Both terms can mask explanatory absence.

But the altitude argument complicates his diagnosis. Tooby's prescription — specify the cognitive organelles, the actual neural programs — is itself an altitude choice. It commits in advance to a particular grain. At the organelle altitude, there is no culture, because culture is not an organelle-altitude phenomenon. At the population altitude, the organelle is not visible, because organelles are not a population-altitude phenomenon. Both altitudes map real structure. The critique only works if you have already decided that lower altitudes are more real — which is an altitude commitment, not an empirical finding.

The deeper move is to drop the altitude hierarchy. Each altitude is owed its own apparatus and its own falsifiability. Demanding that population claims reduce to organelles is exactly the same error as demanding that paired-tree carbon transfers add up to a forest organism. Different altitudes; different evidentiary requirements; no single altitude entitled to settle the others.

Not Relativism — Altitude Discipline

Altitudes are not equal in predictive power. You can ask of any claim: what does this altitude let me predict? Soil-altitude mycorrhizal claims predict carbon-transfer rates between specific paired trees under controlled conditions. Canopy-altitude "forest organism" claims predict almost nothing testable, which is precisely why Karst's audit was so corrosive. The narrow attention spotlight predicts measurable gains in spatial acuity. The broad spotlight predicts gist-recognition gains. All four are real; only some are evidentially earned.

The discipline this demands is harder than relativism. You have to specify the altitude before stating the claim:

  • "Trees share resources under specific mycorrhizal conditions" requires the soil-pore altitude.
  • "The forest thinks" demands a canopy altitude that does not earn its keep.
  • "Borders are illusions" needs the orbital altitude — and is silent at the surface, where borders kill people.
  • "Attention enhances spatial resolution" requires the trial-millisecond altitude.
  • "Attention reveals the world's structure" demands an altitude that no one has found.

Why the Altitudes Keep Getting Collapsed

The reason these claims keep collapsing in the public versions is that the broader altitude feels morally upgraded. Connection, holism, oneness — these are aesthetically satisfying. The narrower altitude feels reductive, even cold. But aesthetic satisfaction is not an epistemic test. It is a clue about what the observer wants to be true.

This is the third fragment glued to the first two: a constraint on observation itself. What counts as a unit is a function of where you stand. Observed once in one field, it can be dismissed as a domain quirk. Observed in atmospheric, mycelial, and neural domains — each with rigorous measurement, each with public misreadings of the same shape — it becomes harder to read as anything other than a structural fact about how observers construct entities.

The altitude is not the lens. The altitude is the variable that decides what is a lens and what is the world.
