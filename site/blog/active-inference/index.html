Active Inference

The Tickle

You cannot tickle yourself. This is not a metaphor and not a personality trait. It is a neurological fact with a precise mechanism, and it opens a window onto something far larger than it appears.

When you move your own fingers across the sole of your foot, the motor system generates a prediction of the sensory consequence before the movement completes. The predicted sensation is subtracted from the actual sensation at the level of the somatosensory cortex. What remains after subtraction is the prediction error — the difference between what the brain expected and what it received. For self-generated touch, the prediction is accurate. The error is near zero. The tickle vanishes.

When someone else touches you, no prediction was generated. The motor system did not issue the command, so the forward model did not produce an expected sensation. The full signal arrives unreduced. The prediction error is large. You laugh, flinch, squirm. The sensation is intense precisely because it was not anticipated.

This is not a quirk. It is a design principle. The nervous system does not passively receive the world and then decide how to respond. It generates predictions about what should be happening, compares those predictions against incoming signals, and processes only the residual. Perception is not the signal. Perception is the error. And the tickle example, trivial as it seems, contains the seed of something much deeper: the claim that action and perception are not separate systems at all. They are two strategies for minimizing the same prediction error.

The Motor System as Oracle

The classical view of motor control runs like this: you decide to move, the decision travels down from cortex to spinal cord, the spinal cord sends signals to muscles, the muscles contract, the limb moves. Perception monitors the result. Corrections follow. The architecture is a command chain: brain decides, body obeys, senses report.

The predictive coding account inverts this entirely. The brain does not send movement commands. It sends proprioceptive predictions — expectations about what the body should feel like. The prediction says: the arm should be over there, the hand should be gripping, the fingers should be closing. These predictions flow downward through the motor hierarchy. At the spinal cord level, the prediction meets the actual proprioceptive signal — the current position and tension of the muscles. The difference between predicted state and actual state is the prediction error. The motor neurons resolve this error in the most direct way possible: they contract the muscles until the predicted state matches reality.

Movement, in this framework, is not the execution of a command. It is the resolution of a discrepancy. The brain predicts a state of the body that does not yet exist, and the motor system makes it true.

This reframing has a startling implication: goals are predictions. When you intend to pick up a glass, what propagates through the motor hierarchy is not an instruction but a strong prior about the proprioceptive state of a hand wrapped around a glass. The prior has high precision — the system is confident this state should obtain. The muscles do the work of error correction, bringing the body into alignment with what the brain already believes should be the case.

The distinction matters because it collapses the gap between intending and doing. There is no separate faculty of will that precedes the motor act. The intention is the prediction. The action is the error correction. What we call voluntary movement is the motor system treating a high-precision expectation as a fact that hasn't happened yet — and then making it happen.

Two Solutions to One Problem

If you are holding a model of the world and the model does not match the incoming signal, you have exactly two options. You can revise the model to fit the signal. Or you can change the world to fit the model.

The first option is perception. You update your beliefs. You accept the evidence of your senses and adjust your internal representation. The prediction was wrong; you fix it.

The second option is action. You move, manipulate, rearrange the external world until the sensory signal matches the prediction. The prediction was right; the world was wrong; you fix the world.

In the predictive coding framework, these are not different systems with different architectures. They are two modes of the same system, two strategies for minimizing the same quantity: prediction error. The brain constantly generates predictions about sensory input. When error arises, the system can suppress it by updating the prediction (perception) or by changing the input (action). Which strategy dominates in any given moment depends on the precision weighting — how confident the system is in its prediction versus how confident it is in the sensory evidence.

When precision on the prediction is low and precision on the sensory signal is high, the system updates the model. You see something unexpected and revise your belief. When precision on the prediction is high and the sensory signal is treated as correctable, the system acts. You reach for the glass because the prediction that your hand should be holding it is weighted more heavily than the current evidence that it is not.

This is what Karl Friston calls active inference. The organism does not inhabit a world and then decide what to do about it. It generates a model of the world it expects to inhabit, and then acts to make that model true. Action is not the output of a cognitive process. It is a cognitive process — the one that resolves prediction error by altering the source of the signal rather than the model that predicted it.

The implications reach past motor control. Foraging is active inference: the organism has a strong prior that it should be in a fed state, and it moves through the world until that prior is fulfilled. Thermoregulation is active inference: the body predicts a core temperature of 37 degrees and adjusts behavior — seeking shade, generating sweat, moving to shelter — to make the prediction true. Even the simple act of moving your eyes is active inference: the visual system predicts that a certain region of the visual field should be in high resolution, and the oculomotor system rotates the fovea to make it so.

The Force Paradox

There is a phenomenon that captures the active inference framework with unusual clarity, and it involves something as simple as two people pressing their fingers together.

In studies of reciprocal force matching, two participants take turns pressing on each other's fingers. The instruction is simple: match the force you just received. Press back with exactly the same pressure. What happens is that the force escalates. Each person consistently presses harder than the force they received, and both believe they are matching it accurately. The interaction spirals upward toward discomfort while each participant reports that they are merely returning what was given.

The mechanism is the same one that prevents self-tickling. When you generate a force, the motor system predicts the sensory consequence of that force. The predicted component is subtracted from the perceived sensation. Your own force feels weaker than it is, because part of it was anticipated and cancelled. The other person's force, unpredicted, arrives at full intensity. So when you try to match their press, you are matching a full-intensity percept with a self-attenuated one. You overshoot. They do the same. The escalation is inevitable given the architecture.

This is not a failure of proprioception. It is proprioception working exactly as designed. The system is built to distinguish self-generated signals from externally generated ones by cancelling the predicted component of the former. The cost of this otherwise elegant solution is that self-generated force is systematically underestimated. What you do to yourself always feels gentler than what the world does to you.

The force escalation paradigm is a microcosm of something larger. Every conflict between agents operating under active inference has this structure. Each agent's model of the interaction includes a prediction of their own contribution, which is attenuated, and a perception of the other's contribution, which is not. Each experiences the other as the one escalating. The architecture that makes self-world discrimination possible also makes accurate force attribution between agents impossible — at least without an external reference point that neither agent's forward model can attenuate.

Where Attention Fits

The Attention Arc traced the mechanics of selection, suppression, and the bottleneck that constrains conscious processing. In the predictive coding framework, attention has a precise identity: it is precision weighting. To attend to something is to increase the gain on its prediction-error signal — to treat the information arriving from that channel as more reliable, more important, more in need of explanation.

This means that directing attention is itself a form of active inference. When you choose to attend to a particular stimulus, you are not passively opening a channel. You are actively modulating the precision landscape — turning up the gain on some error signals and turning down others. You are deciding, in effect, which discrepancies between model and world are worth resolving and which can be tolerated. The spotlight of attention is not a searchlight sweeping the environment. It is a precision dial, and turning it is an action in the world of inference as consequential as any physical movement.

Dopamine and acetylcholine are the neurochemical mechanisms of this dial. They modulate the gain of prediction-error neurons — the synaptic amplification that determines how loudly a particular error signal speaks. Gamma-band synchrony, the fast neural oscillation associated with attentive processing, reflects increased synaptic gain in the relevant cortical populations. When you attend to something, the neural hardware is literally amplifying the error signal from that source, making it more precise, forcing the rest of the system to deal with it.

This connects attention to action in a way the classical frameworks could not. In the old model, attention was a perceptual operation and action was a motor operation, and they met somewhere in the middle at the decision stage. In the active inference framework, they are the same operation applied at different levels. Attending is acting on the precision of inference. Moving is acting on the content of sensation. Both change the prediction error landscape. Both serve the same master: bringing the model and the world into alignment.

What This Means for Will

If action is the fulfillment of a prediction, then what is a decision?

In the classical picture, a decision is the moment when a free agent selects among options. The will floats above the machinery, evaluating alternatives, and commits to one. The commitment then cascades downward into motor execution. This picture requires something to do the selecting — a homunculus, an executive, a ghost in the machine that stands apart from the prediction-error dynamics and chooses.

The active inference framework does not need this ghost. A decision, in this account, is the process by which competing predictions are resolved into a single high-precision prior that the motor system then fulfills. You do not decide and then act. Multiple potential futures are represented as predictions with varying precision, and the system settles — through a dynamic competition shaped by prior experience, current context, and precision weighting — on one prediction that gains enough weight to recruit the motor hierarchy.

This does not eliminate agency. But it relocates it. Agency is not the free-floating selection event that precedes action. It is the entire process of generating, weighting, and resolving competing predictions. It is distributed across the hierarchy, shaped by every prior and every precision assignment, and it does not require a moment of pure will that exists outside the dynamics.

The phenomenology of deliberation supports this. When you agonize over a decision, what you experience is not a calm executive weighing options. You experience conflicting pulls, oscillating preferences, a felt sense of being drawn in multiple directions. This is exactly what a system with multiple high-precision, competing predictions would feel like from the inside — a landscape of attractors, none yet dominant, the system hovering between basins. The moment of decision, the felt sense of commitment, is the moment when one prediction gains sufficient precision to suppress the others and recruit the motor system.

The tickle returns here, transformed. You cannot tickle yourself because self-generated action is predicted and cancelled. But the deeper point is that the distinction between self and world — the most fundamental boundary in conscious experience — is defined precisely by this cancellation. What is self is what was predicted by your own motor commands. What is world is what was not. The forward model that prevents self-tickling is the same mechanism that constructs the felt boundary between agent and environment. Every time you move and correctly predict the sensory consequence, you are drawing the line between you and everything else.

Active inference, then, is not just a theory of motor control. It is a theory of the self as a process — the ongoing, dynamic, never-finished project of generating predictions about one's own body and acting to make them true. The self is not the thing that predicts. The self is the prediction that keeps being fulfilled.

You do not act and then see what happened. You predict, and then you make it so.
