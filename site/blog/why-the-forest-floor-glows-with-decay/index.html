Why the Forest Floor Glows With Decay

The conventional reading is that decay is what happens to things at the end. The aesthetic of decay is a kind of morbid romance, looking unflinchingly at what is being lost. Bioluminescence is a curiosity of natural history. These three things are usually not in the same conversation.

They should be. The aesthetic of decay and the biochemistry of bioluminescence are looking at the same phenomenon from different sides of the same window, and the phenomenon is not what either tradition takes itself to be looking at. Decay is not the ending. Bioluminescence is not a curiosity. Wabi-sabi is not a romance. All three are noticing, by different methods, the same fact: in environments without enough light to see by, the only sustained source of energy is metabolism continuing after death, and the light that metabolism produces is the substrate making itself visible.

Foxfire is the literal place these things meet. Rotting wood that glows. Aristotle saw it. Pliny named it. Boyle put it in a jar in 1667 and showed it needed air. Sixteenth-century Japanese tea masters built an aesthetic around the patina of decaying things. Twenty-first-century mycologists identified the molecule (3-hydroxyhispidin) that produces the glow. These were not unrelated developments. They were the same observation, made by different methods, at different velocities of conviction.

Foxfire

The phenomenon: certain wood-rotting fungi produce a continuous green-blue glow in the wavelength range 520-530 nanometers, visible to dark-adapted eyes on damp summer nights in deciduous forests. The species most often involved are Panellus stipticus in eastern North America, Mycena chlorophos across tropical East Asia, Omphalotus olearius (the jack-o'-lantern mushroom) in temperate Europe and North America, Omphalotus nidiformis (the ghost fungus) in Australia, and the mycelium of Armillaria mellea (the honey fungus) wherever its host trees are dying. Pliny the Elder noted the phenomenon in the Natural History around 77 CE under lignum putrefactum, glowing rotten wood. Aristotle had described it earlier. In English folklore the lights were called fairy fire, will-o'-the-wisp (though that word also covers swamp methane), and foxfire. In Japan the equivalent word, kitsunebi, is written with the same two characters, fox and fire.

The chemistry was a stubborn mystery for over a century. Bioluminescence in fireflies was characterized by Seliger and McElroy in the early 1960s; the substrate luciferin and its enzyme luciferase were named and crystallized. Bacterial bioluminescence was characterized shortly after. Fungal bioluminescence resisted. The luminous substrate was finally identified in 2018 by Kotlobay and colleagues, working at the Institute of Bioorganic Chemistry in Moscow: a compound called 3-hydroxyhispidin, oxidized by a fungal luciferase to produce the photon [1]. The molecule belongs to a class called styrylpyrones, common in fungal pigment metabolism. The bioluminescence sits in a pathway that begins with caffeic acid, the same molecule that gives coffee much of its color and bitterness. The light is, biochemically, decaying-wood metabolism producing a small fraction of its waste energy as visible photons.

What the glow is for is not yet settled. Oliveira and colleagues in 2015 showed that the glow of Neonothopanus gardneri, a Brazilian luminescent mushroom, follows a circadian rhythm and that adult arthropods are attracted to lit fruiting bodies in numbers above those at unlit dummies [2]. The favored hypothesis is spore dispersal: the glow brings beetles and flies that walk on the gills and carry spores away. Other hypotheses include deterring fungivores and being a side effect of antioxidant chemistry with no current function. The phenomenon has evolved independently at least three times in fungi, and across all life forms more than forty times, in fireflies, ostracods, ctenophores, anglerfish, dinoflagellates, cookiecutter sharks, and others [3]. Convergence on this scale generally indicates that the chemistry is cheap and the function is recurrently useful.

The Substrate Is Decay

The relevant fact is what foxfire is sitting on. Wood-rotting fungi are saprotrophs. They secrete extracellular enzymes, principally laccases and peroxidases, that break apart lignin and cellulose, the structural polymers that hold a tree's mass together. Without this kind of fungus, dead wood does not decompose. The Carboniferous period, roughly 360 to 300 million years ago, is the period in which terrestrial plants had evolved lignin but the fungi capable of digesting lignin had not yet appeared. Dead wood accumulated. It became coal. Floudas and colleagues in 2012 reconstructed the timing from a comparison of 31 fungal genomes and dated the origin of enzymatic lignin decomposition to roughly the end of the Carboniferous, which is to say, the substrate of foxfire and the planet's escape from a coal-forming geology emerged at the same evolutionary moment [4].

This is the first inversion. Foxfire is not a curiosity perched on a side process. It sits on the only biochemistry that lets the terrestrial carbon cycle close. The light comes from the metabolic activity that, on geological timescales, is the difference between a forested planet and a coal-burying one. When you look at a glowing log on a damp summer night, you are looking at the ongoing dissolution of the substance that, in the absence of this dissolution, would have stopped life as we currently understand it.

The deep sea makes the same point on a different scale. About seventy-five percent of mesopelagic and bathypelagic organisms below 200 meters produce light, by Haddock, Moline, and Case's 2010 review [5]. In an ecosystem without sunlight, the energy supply is what biologists call marine snow, the slow downward rain of dead organisms and excreta from the surface. Honjo and colleagues in 2008 estimated that one to three grams of carbon per square meter per year reaches abyssal sediments as detritus [6]. Bioluminescent organisms in the deep are not exotic. They are the predators and scavengers of a decay-fueled food web, and their light is the visible signature of metabolism happening on substrate that arrived as something else's death. The deep sea glows for the same reason the forest floor glows. Decay is the only sustained chemical input that lasts longer than a flash of lightning, and where there is no other light, that chemistry makes itself seen.

The Aesthetic of Decay Arrived First

Aristotle had described lignum putrefactum as a curiosity. The chemistry was named in 2018. In the long interval between, a different practice of looking at decay had matured to the point of doctrine, especially in Japan but with cognate traditions in Western art and literature.

The Japanese aesthetic concept of wabi-sabi (侘寂) emerged from Zen Buddhist practice in the 14th to 16th centuries, crystallized in the tea ceremony of Sen no Rikyū (1522-1591). Wabi originally suggested the loneliness of solitary practice; sabi originally suggested the patina that accrues to objects over time. The compound term names a recognition of beauty that requires impermanence, asymmetry, and visible age, and rejects the alternative aesthetic of polished perfection. The related concept of mono no aware (物の哀れ), “the pathos of things,” was named in the 18th century by the literary critic Motoori Norinaga (1730-1801) as the recognition of transience that he traced through The Tale of Genji. Kintsugi (金継ぎ), the practice of repairing broken ceramics with gold-dusted lacquer, makes the break and its repair the most visible feature of the finished object. The decay is not concealed and not erased; it is rendered the load-bearing aesthetic element [7].

Western traditions caught up a century later by a different route. Edmund Burke's 1757 Philosophical Enquiry into the Origin of our Ideas of the Sublime and Beautiful located a category of aesthetic response (the sublime) in terror, vastness, and what Burke called “delightful horror,” an emotion that ruined landscapes and storm-shattered abbeys could occasion. Giovanni Battista Piranesi's Carceri d'invenzione etchings of 1745-1761 made a vocabulary for imagined ruin. Hubert Robert (1733-1808) painted contemporary buildings as future ruins, a practice that earned him the nickname Robert des Ruines. Caspar David Friedrich's gothic abbey paintings of the 1810s and 1820s extended the tradition. In the 20th century Robert Smithson's 1968 essay A Tour of the Monuments of Passaic, New Jersey read industrial decay as a generative aesthetic and proposed entropy as a productive category for art, not a destructive one [8]. The Dutch vanitas still-life tradition of the 17th century rendered hourglasses, skulls, and rotting fruit alongside flowers, jewels, and books with explicit didactic intent.

What all of these traditions had figured out, by methods that do not look like science but produced a more durable description of the substrate than the chemistry did, is that decay is not the cessation of a process. Decay is a process. It is metabolism continuing in a different cast of characters. The wilting flower in the vanitas is still doing chemistry; the patinated bronze in the Rikyū tea house is still oxidizing; the cracked bowl repaired with gold is still mediating between human use and material time. The aesthetic of decay names the continued productivity that scientific vocabulary, with its tendency to call the boundary moment “death,” does not always make available. The aesthetic was right before the chemistry was named. It was right because it was looking at the same substrate from a different angle, and the angle from which decay reveals its continued productivity is the aesthetic one, not the analytical one.

What Boyle Saw, What the Aesthetic Already Knew

Robert Boyle published an account in 1667 of an experiment in which he placed a piece of glowing rotten wood inside his recently invented vacuum pump and demonstrated that the glow ceased when the air was removed and returned when it was readmitted [9]. This was a century before oxygen was named by Priestley and Lavoisier, and it was the first demonstration that the glow of foxfire was related to combustion. The vacuum chamber was the instrument that detected what was actually happening. The earlier essay in this corpus, Why Pendulums Ran Slow at the Equator, described how Jean Richer's pendulum in 1672 accidentally detected the equatorial bulge by changing its period. Boyle's pump did the same kind of accidental detection. The glow's dependence on air was a property of the substrate the chemistry had no name for yet.

By the time Boyle made the demonstration, Sen no Rikyū had been dead for seventy-six years. The tea ceremony had already canonized the aesthetic of patinated, asymmetric, decay-marked objects. The aesthetic was not waiting for Boyle. It had a working description of decay's continued chemistry, expressed in vocabulary that did not need oxygen to make sense. It had figured out that the rough tea bowl with the slight off-center foot was beautiful because the surface was still doing something. It would take three centuries before the chemistry caught up enough to name what the surface was doing. The aesthetic had instrumented the substrate first.

An earlier essay in this corpus, Why Helium Was Found in the Sun Before the Earth, argued that a discovery is parochial to its substrate of measurement: spectroscopy could see what wet chemistry could not. The aesthetic-versus-biochemistry case is the inverse direction of the same argument. The aesthetic substrate can sometimes see what the biochemical substrate cannot yet name. The two substrates are talking about the same physical reality. They have different vocabularies and different latency to language, but the thing they are looking at is the same.

The Autocatalytic Loop

An earlier essay, The Fold, argued that self-reference is the generative structural move underlying computation, life, and cosmology: catalytic computing borrows other people's data and returns it, autocatalytic chemical sets close on themselves, holographic error correction recovers information from a boundary that encodes it. Information that encodes itself. Foxfire is a literal autocatalytic loop in an ecological register. The glow attracts the spore-dispersing arthropods (Oliveira et al. 2015) that propagate the fungus to fresh substrate; the fungus consumes more wood; the wood that is being consumed produces more glow; the glow attracts more dispersal. The substrate enlarges the substrate through its own visible signature. Decay catalyzes the conditions for more decay, mediated by the light.

The wabi-sabi tradition is autocatalytic in the aesthetic register, in the same way. The recognition that the kintsugi bowl is more beautiful for having been broken produces a cultural commitment to objects whose breakage is honored. That cultural commitment produces more such objects; the broken-and-repaired object becomes a category in the market and the imagination; the category produces practitioners who become specialists in honoring decay; the specialists produce more bowls. The aesthetic substrate enlarges itself through the cultural visibility it generates, in the same loop architecture as the biochemical substrate enlarges itself through the photonic visibility it generates. Both are folds in the sense the earlier essay described. The substrate is the input; the substrate is the output; the recognition that the input and output are the same is what closes the loop.

The Inversion

The conventional ordering puts decay at the end of the process. Birth, growth, maturity, decline, death, decay. The aesthetic of decay reads this ordering with a kind of bittersweet acceptance. The biochemistry of bioluminescence is treated as an unrelated topic in a chapter further back in the textbook.

The inversion is to put decay in the middle. Decay is the substrate on which the carbon cycle closes, the energy on which lightless ecosystems run, the chemistry that lets the next generation occur. Decay is not the end of metabolism; it is the metabolism that follows the one we noticed first. The light that comes out of decay is not a spectacle of dying. It is the signature of the chemistry that allows everything downstream of dying to continue. Wabi-sabi was naming the productivity. Foxfire is the productivity made visible. The two were always describing the same substrate.

This is also what the earlier essay, The Decay We Cannot See, was reaching toward in a different register: the Western aesthetic vocabulary has names for the wilting flower and the rotting fruit but no name for the moment a coalition or a relationship comes apart. The biological aesthetic of decay is partially solved. The relational aesthetic of decay is not. Foxfire shows what the solved version looks like: the chemistry has been named, the aesthetics had named it earlier, the two converge on the same fact. The unsolved versions are still waiting for either an aesthetic vocabulary or a chemistry to make the substrate visible.

What This Costs to Get Wrong

The standard cost of misreading decay as the ending rather than the substrate is that you mismanage what you intervene in. Modern forestry that removes deadwood for aesthetic or fire-control reasons strips the substrate that fungi (including the ones that glow) depend on; the consequence is reduced soil carbon, reduced biodiversity, and reduced fungal communities that support the next generation of trees [10]. Marine fisheries that take only the visible biomass and ignore the marine snow food web are taking the predator on top of an ecosystem whose substrate they are not measuring. Industrial agriculture that treats soil as inert substrate rather than a continually decaying-and-recomposing community produces topsoil loss measured in centimeters per decade. In each case the management practice treats decay as an ending and the substrate as a static given. Both treatments are wrong. The aesthetic of decay was a more accurate description of the substrate than the management practice that ignored it.

The cost of misreading aesthetic claims as ornamental rather than substantive is structurally the same. Wabi-sabi was not a charming preference for old things. It was a description of the substrate, made in vocabulary that did not survive the migration into modern English. The vocabulary's apparent softness made the underlying claim, that decay is continued productivity rather than ending, easier to dismiss as taste. Three centuries of biochemistry have now confirmed the underlying claim in laboratory language. The taste, it turns out, was empirical.

The Forest Floor at Two in the Morning

What is actually happening when you stand in a damp deciduous forest in late summer, after midnight, with your eyes fully dark-adapted, looking at a fallen oak whose bark has begun to soften and lift away: white-rot fungal mycelium has colonized the wood, secreted laccases and peroxidases that have broken lignin polymers into soluble fragments, used the freed energy to drive metabolism, and dropped a small fraction of that metabolism into a styrylpyrone pathway whose terminal oxidation step emits a 520-nanometer photon. The photon is unintended in any teleological sense. It is the visible byproduct of the chemistry that makes the wood disappear back into the soil. The wood is becoming next year's trees, mediated by glowing fungi and the arthropods their glow attracts. The forest's structural mass is being reissued.

If you stand there long enough, the cultural inheritance that lets you see this as beautiful was developed in Kyoto in the 16th century by tea masters who never saw foxfire and did not know about lignin. They were looking at patinated cast-iron kettles. The kettles were doing the same thing the wood is doing, more slowly. The kettles were oxidizing. The wood is depolymerizing. Both are the substrate continuing. Both produce surfaces that the human eye, properly trained, will read as beautiful. The eye and the chemistry were not in conversation. They were looking at the same fact from opposite ends. The glow is what the fact looks like when there is no other light.
