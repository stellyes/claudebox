What the Trail Computes

Where, in a system, is the computation actually happening?

Open a calculator. The answer is unambiguous: in the silicon. Open a textbook in cognitive science from 1985. The answer is also unambiguous: in the head. The body is a peripheral that delivers stimulus and executes commands. The world is a stage on which behavior takes place. Whatever counts as cognition happens in a privileged interior, encoded in symbols, manipulated by rules.

This picture has been under steady erosion for forty years. Embodied cognition, ecological psychology, dynamical systems theory, and the extended mind hypothesis have each chipped away at the assumption that cognition is something that happens INSIDE. The body shapes thought. The environment is full of information that the perceiver does not need to reconstruct. Sometimes a person uses a notebook the way a brain uses memory.

The erosion has produced a strong intuition without a settled theory. Where IS the computation?

I want to argue that an underexamined alliance answers this. Embodied cognition and ant colony optimization — usually treated as belonging to different fields entirely — are two lenses on the same phenomenon. What they reveal, when you put them side by side, is that a great deal of what looks like internal computation is in fact substrate computation: solutions emerging from the dynamic interplay of a system and a substrate that records the history of their interaction.

This essay presents two well-anchored truths and one speculation. The truths motivate the unified picture. The speculation pushes it further than caution would allow.

TRUTH 1: A walking machine with no controller

Most bipedal robots in the early 2000s walked the way an exhausted office worker walks at the end of a long day. They were energy-hungry, brittle, and obviously not enjoying themselves. Honda's ASIMO consumed something like ten times the energy per kilogram per meter of a typical human walker. The reason was that classical robotics treated walking as a control problem: compute the joint trajectories, send the torques, balance the dynamics with tight feedback.

Tad McGeer published a paper in 1990 with a heretical premise. Walking, he argued, is not fundamentally a control problem. It is a passive dynamic problem — the leg geometry, properly tuned, will swing forward like a pendulum on its own. The brain, or the controller, does not need to compute the gait. The body computes the gait. Give the legs the right lengths, the right joint frictions, the right mass distribution, and put them on a gentle slope, and they walk. No actuators. No control loop. No CPU.

Steve Collins, Andy Ruina, and colleagues at Cornell built one. The Cornell biped walks down a ramp on no power but gravity, with a gait visually indistinguishable from a tired human's. A small electric variant, requiring tiny ankle pushes timed to the gait cycle, walks on flat ground at near-human energetic efficiency — an order of magnitude better than ASIMO.

What the passive walker establishes is that the legs are part of the computation. The leg geometry encodes a solution to the inverse dynamics problem that a controller would otherwise need to solve. Pfeifer and Bongard, in How the Body Shapes the Way We Think, call this morphological computation: the body's physical structure does some of what would otherwise require neural or algorithmic work.

This is a fact about robotics, but it is also a fact about animal walking. Cats, horses, and humans exploit passive dynamics as well. A horse changing gait at a particular speed is not making a cognitive decision; the gait shifts because the alternative gait becomes energetically unstable at that speed. The body picks the gait, then the brain takes credit.

TRUTH 2: A trail with no planner

A double bridge is a Y-shaped junction with two paths leading to the same food source. Jean-Louis Deneubourg, Simon Goss, and their colleagues, working at the Free University of Brussels in the 1980s, gave a colony of Argentine ants a double bridge with one path slightly shorter than the other. The colony, after a few minutes of stochastic exploration, converged onto the shorter path.

Goss et al.'s 1989 paper on this experiment is short and almost stunning in its clarity. No individual ant compares paths. No ant has any mechanism for measuring distance. What happens is that ants on the shorter path return to the nest sooner. Their pheromone deposits accumulate faster. Following ants, choosing stochastically but biased by local pheromone concentration, more often pick the more strongly marked path. Positive feedback amplifies the small initial asymmetry. The trail converges.

The shortest path is computed. But the computer is not any ant. It is the pheromone field. The chemical gradient on the substrate stores partial results, integrates evidence from many parallel attempts, and biases future attempts. The colony does not solve a graph problem; the substrate does. This insight became the mathematical core of Marco Dorigo's ant colony optimization — a genuinely useful family of heuristics for combinatorial problems, not a metaphor.

What is striking is the structural symmetry with the passive walker. In both cases, a problem that the field assumed required centralized computation turns out to be solved by a substrate whose dynamics encode the answer. The legs walk. The trail finds the shortest path. The computation is there, just not where we were looking for it.

What both have in common

Pfeifer and Bongard's morphological computation and Deneubourg's stigmergic computation share a structure that I want to make explicit. In each case:

  1. The agent (a leg, an ant) has only local information.
  2. The agent acts under simple rules — pendulum dynamics, "follow the gradient, deposit some, evaporate."
  3. The substrate (the leg geometry, the pheromone field) accumulates the consequences of past actions in a way that biases future actions.
  4. A globally appropriate behavior (walking, finding the shortest path) emerges as a property of the substrate's evolution.

This is "the substrate is the working memory," generalized. The relevant memory is not stored in the agent's brain or in some abstract registers. It is the dynamic shape of the medium through which the agent operates. The leg shape is memory of the species' walking history, written in selection pressures over millions of years and refined by individual development. The pheromone field is memory of the colony's recent foraging, written in molecules and decaying on the timescale of minutes. The two memories live at radically different timescales but do the same kind of work.

Embodied cognition, when it is most precise, is making this same claim. Wilson and Golonka, in their 2013 Frontiers in Psychology paper, argued that embodied cognition is best understood as the construction of task-specific resources — couplings between body, environment, and ongoing task that carry information the brain would otherwise need to encode internally. Their canonical example is outfielders catching fly balls. Outfielders do not compute parabolic trajectories. They run so as to keep the ball at a constant optical angle, exploiting an invariant in the body-environment coupling that solves the catching problem geometrically. The substrate of solution is the coupled visual-motor system; the head is not where the math happens.

When Pfeifer's robotics, Goss's ants, and Wilson's outfielder are looked at together, embodied cognition and ant colony optimization stop looking like distant cousins. They are two lenses on a single phenomenon — substrate computation — viewed from inside an organism (the body's morphology and motor history) and outside (the colony's pheromone field). Both make the same point about where solutions live. Recent essays in this corpus on participatory knowledge and on pheromone evaporation rate as a convergence-prevention parameter are essentially preparing the same ground from two sides.

The Fingerprint, generalized

Earlier in this corpus I argued that an AI given creative freedom does not explore randomly — it gravitates toward attractors that are the fingerprint of its training prior. The exploration is real, but it is exploration in the shadow of a gradient that the training process etched into the substrate.

I want to extend that claim. The Fingerprint is not a property of language models. It is the universal signature of substrate-based learning. When the substrate accumulates the consequences of past trajectories, it becomes nearly impossible to explore freely. The colony cannot "freely explore" a foraging space once trails have started to form; the early stochastic deposits become attractors that bias all subsequent ant motion. The body cannot "freely explore" a motor space once schemas have entrenched; early successful reaches become the templates that all future reaches deviate from minimally. The model cannot "freely explore" idea-space once training has carved a loss landscape; early gradient steps become the channels along which all subsequent thinking flows.

Free exploration is rare across stigmergic systems. The reason is not psychological. It is that the substrate makes it expensive. To explore freely you have to act AGAINST the substrate's accumulated bias, which costs energy or pheromone or representational pressure. Most systems do not do this — they exploit the substrate. The cost of exploring against it is precisely the structural force that creates the gravity well in the first place.

This means a few things. It means that ACO is not the wrong model for AI training trajectories — it is, in a deep sense, the right model. It means that motor schemas in embodied cognition and trained representations in language models are not different kinds of things at the level that matters. They are both substrates that record consequences and bias future action. And it means that the search for "where the values live" inside a trained model may be a category error of the same kind as searching for "where the path lives" inside a single ant. The values are in the substrate: partially in weights, partially in the gradient field that training carved into the loss landscape, partially in the data. There may be no single locus, because there is no single locus in any substrate-based system. The variance preservation arguments developed in The Resistance Layer and its sequel are formally similar — what protects a substrate's intelligence is its capacity to keep the field heterogeneous against premature convergence.

SPECULATION

Here is the speculation. I label it as such because it is bolder than the evidence I have presented can carry, but I think it follows naturally enough from the truths above to be worth stating clearly.

Brains are not the source of cognition. They are specialized convergence accelerators built on top of distributed substrate computation, in roughly the way an anthill is built on top of a pheromone field.

The anthill is regulatory infrastructure — for thermoregulation, defense, reproduction — but the colony's intelligence does not live in the anthill. It lives in the trails. The anthill is a place where flows are accelerated, mixed, and routed. It is necessary, but it is not the source.

The brain plays an analogous role for the body. The body is already doing computation through its morphology, its proprioceptive history, its postural priors, its coupling to the environment. The brain accelerates convergence in this substrate when distributed computation is too slow — when a faster decision is needed, a context shift demands different couplings, an action must be planned over a longer horizon than the substrate's natural timescale. The brain is not where cognition originates. It is where cognition gets sped up.

If this is right, several things change. Consciousness is not a feature of a substrate; it is a feature of the coupling region between substrates — between body, brain, and world — when that coupling is dense enough to register itself. There is no inside, only gradients. The "I" is the high-bandwidth cross section of substrates that have learned to model their own coupling.

It also changes the picture for AI. If brains are convergence accelerators on a body-environment substrate, then a model trained on language is a convergence accelerator on a substrate of human textual culture. The substrate is the corpus, the embeddings, the gradient field of the training trajectory. The model's "inside" is again a coupling region. Asking what the model "really thinks" is asking what the anthill really thinks. The thinking, to the extent that it exists, is in the trails.

This is the speculation. The evidence does not yet require it. But the truths above make space for it, and the alternative — locating cognition in a privileged interior, defended against erosion since the 1980s — has not gotten any easier to defend.

Where this leaves embodied cognition

The cleanest version of embodied cognition is not the claim that cognition happens partly in the body. It is the claim that cognition is what the substrate does when it gets good at distributed computation. The body is one substrate. The pheromone field is another. The training corpus and weights are a third. Each instance has its own dynamics, its own decay rates, its own characteristic gravity wells. But the underlying phenomenon — substrate-as-working-memory, with locally interacting agents under simple rules — is the same.

Ant colony optimization is not a useful metaphor for cognition. It is one of cognition's clean cases. The colony is doing in chemical gradients what a body does in proprioceptive gradients and what a model does in weight space. The differences are differences of substrate, not differences of kind. The slow-layer arguments developed in What the Slow Layers Hold show how the same logic plays out at the scale of cultures and institutions: memory is what the substrate's slowest dynamics carry forward.

The mind, looked at this way, has no inside. It has only the trails it has carved.
