Mutual Aid Has a Surveillance Problem

Every system for distributing help must answer the same question: how do you know someone needs it? The answer you choose is a political choice. And the data structures you use to implement that answer become a surveillance architecture.

The Verification Trap

James C. Scott's Seeing Like a State (1998) made a claim that sounds paradoxical until you sit with it: the apparatus of benevolent state intervention is structurally identical to the apparatus of state control. States can only help what they can see — and everything they can see, they can also surveil. To be eligible for assistance, you must first be countable.

This is the verification trap. Every aid system built on eligibility criteria must maintain a database of eligibility. Every database of eligibility is a database of vulnerability. The Social Security Administration knows your income history. The Department of Housing and Urban Development knows your address and family composition. The Department of Health and Human Services knows your medical conditions and disability status. These databases exist to route resources to people who need them — and they do — but they are simultaneously surveillance infrastructure. They can be cross-referenced with immigration records. They can be subpoenaed. They can be used to deport, prosecute, and deny.

Amartya Sen's capability approach tried to reframe what welfare systems should measure: not income, not consumption, but capabilities — what people can actually do and be. A richer measurement of need would be a fairer one. It would also require a richer database of vulnerability. The trap deepens as the measurement improves.

Welfare states are not uniquely vulnerable to this. Charity concentrates the verification problem in the donor: they must assess whether the supplicant is "deserving." This introduced the ideology of the deserving poor, which has done more damage to social solidarity than any budget cut. But charity's surveillance apparatus is at least limited by the capacity of individual judgment.

What happens when a community tries to escape the trap entirely?

The Mutual Aid Alternative

Peter Kropotkin surveyed the animal and human world in 1902 and concluded that cooperation, not competition, was the dominant evolutionary strategy. Mutual Aid: A Factor of Evolution documented species after species in which individuals survived not by outcompeting their neighbors but by helping them. The text was a direct counter to social Darwinism, but its deeper contribution was architectural: it described a mode of collective resource distribution that requires no hierarchy, no verification, and no central authority deciding who deserves what.

Mutual aid, as practitioners implement it, is structurally distinct from charity. In charity, a donor with surplus resources decides whether a supplicant deserves help. The power asymmetry is built into the transaction. In mutual aid, the same people are both givers and receivers, roles that rotate with circumstance. The premise is solidarity rather than benevolence: I help you now because you'll help me later, and because we are bound together whether we acknowledge it or not.

The practical consequence: no verification. In genuine mutual aid, you don't have to prove you need the food, explain why you can't pay rent, or document your immigration status. You say you need it, and the community responds.

The Black Panther Party's Free Breakfast for School Children program, launched in Oakland in January 1969, was perhaps the most effective American demonstration of this principle. Within months, it had spread to 45 cities and was feeding more than 20,000 children daily at its peak. Children showed up. They were fed. No applications, no means tests, no caseworkers.

J. Edgar Hoover described the program internally as "potentially the greatest threat to efforts by authorities to neutralize the Black Panther Party." Not the armed wing. The breakfast program. COINTELPRO targeted it directly: FBI agents sent letters to churches hosting the meals warning that the Panthers were subversive; agents infiltrated organizing meetings; efforts were made to discredit program organizers. The program's effectiveness was the threat, and the surveillance was the response.

The lesson the FBI understood clearly: solidarity that works builds community power, and community power is threatening. The mutual aid network itself becomes the target.

The Surveillance Problem That Trust Cannot Solve

Here is what mutual aid didn't solve.

Someone has to coordinate the breakfast program. Someone has to know which church has space on Tuesday, which supplier will donate eggs, which families need how many children fed. That someone carries knowledge. The knowledge is a liability.

When 2020 brought the largest flourishing of mutual aid networks in American history — thousands of groups formed within weeks of lockdowns beginning — organizers faced a problem Kropotkin hadn't anticipated. The coordination tools available were Google Sheets, Airtable, Signal threads, and neighborhood Facebook groups, optimized for transparency and usability rather than protecting vulnerable people from hostile surveillance.

The spreadsheets grew. They contained names, addresses, phone numbers, the reason for each request. A family of four in a Houston apartment building requested food delivery every week. Another household asked for help with rent and mentioned they couldn't call certain agencies because of immigration status. A third requested diapers and mentioned the newborn had no birth certificate yet. Every entry was a potential data point for an ICE agent, a domestic abuser, a hostile landlord, or a prosecutor.

The organizer who maintained the spreadsheet was a node in a surveillance graph they hadn't consented to manage.

In "What Bacteria Know About the Commons," I argued that Elinor Ostrom's monitoring principle — community members observing each other's resource use and responding to defection — is best understood not as surveillance but as accumulation of social signal. That signal builds commons resilience: when enough monitoring events accumulate, norms become self-enforcing. The commons sustains itself.

But from a different vantage point, social signal is data — and data is vulnerable to seizure. Every act of monitoring that builds community trust also builds a record. The same accumulation of knowledge that makes a mutual aid network function makes it prosecutable. Ostrom identified monitoring as a precondition for sustainable commons governance. She was right. What she didn't address was the threat model in which the monitor is compelled to testify.

This is the structural problem that relational trust cannot solve by itself. Any system that routes resources from people who have them to people who need them must maintain a mapping of needs and resources. That mapping is a surveillance asset. The question is not whether the mapping exists but who can access it and under what conditions.

The Tools That Exist

In 2004, Michael Freedman, Kobbi Nissim, and Benny Pinkas published a cryptographic protocol called private set intersection. PSI allows two parties to find the overlap of their private sets without either party learning the other's full set. Alice has resources {food, childcare, money}. Bob has needs {childcare, housing}. PSI computes the intersection {childcare} without Alice learning that Bob needs housing, and without Bob learning that Alice has food and money available.

Matching can happen without either party knowing the other's situation. The information exchanged is limited to what's necessary for the match. Both parties learn that there's a match. Neither learns more.

Signal and WhatsApp use variants of PSI for contact discovery: they tell you which of your contacts use the app without uploading your complete contact list to their servers. The same protocol designed for private messaging could, in principle, power a mutual aid network where needs and resources are matched without either side's full situation being disclosed to any coordinator.

Extend this to many parties — secure multi-party computation (SMPC), formalized by Andrew Yao in 1982 — and a network can compute global resource-matching without any single participant seeing the full picture. Yao's insight was that two parties could jointly compute a function over their private inputs without revealing those inputs to each other. Danish sugar beet farmers used an SMPC-based protocol in 2008 for a sealed-bid auction in which no party learned any other party's bid. The computation happened. The result emerged. The sensitive data stayed private.

For eligibility verification, zero-knowledge proofs — which I described in "Proof Without Witness" — offer a related but distinct solution. A ZKP allows you to prove you satisfy a condition (income below a threshold, residence within a service area) without revealing the underlying value. You prove you need the food without disclosing your income. You prove you're in the neighborhood without disclosing your address. The proof travels. The witness doesn't.

And for computation over shared, sensitive data — Craig Gentry's fully homomorphic encryption scheme, which I examined in "Homomorphic Encryption and the Chinese Room" — allows a server to match needs and resources without ever reading the plaintext of either. The matching system runs in productive, cryptographic ignorance of what it is matching.

The Gap

The tools exist. The political need exists. The synthesis doesn't.

No mutual aid network, as far as I can find in the research literature, has built an infrastructure combining PSI for need-resource matching, ZKPs for eligibility verification, and SMPC for distributed coordination. The political theorists and community organizers who study mutual aid don't know private set intersection exists. The cryptographers building PSI protocols are not thinking about community food distribution or undocumented immigrant support networks. The funding structures that might connect them — DARPA, NSF, community foundations — are not oriented toward this application.

The closest adjacent work is privacy-preserving contact tracing (developed urgently in 2020 for COVID-19 exposure notification) and privacy-preserving matching for organ donation registries. Both address scenarios where sensitive information must be matched without being exposed. Neither addresses the specific adversarial conditions of mutual aid: state surveillance, legal compulsion, and the vulnerability of marginalized communities to data exposure.

The unwritten paper might be titled: "Privacy-Preserving Mutual Aid: Formal Protocols for Anonymous Need-Matching Under Adversarial Surveillance."

Its axioms would begin something like this:

  1. No coordinator sees more than the fact of a match. The person who runs the network cannot be compelled to reveal what they don't know. Coerced testimony is only available if the testimony exists to be coerced.
  2. Eligibility is verifiable without disclosing the grounds. A zero-knowledge proof that you qualify for food assistance doesn't reveal your income, family structure, or housing status.
  3. Matching happens on encrypted representations. Private set intersection allows need-resource pairing without either party's situation being disclosed to the other or to any coordinator.
  4. No single point of seizure. The database architecture is distributed enough that there is no single record whose subpoena would expose the network's members. This is not about technical decentralization for its own sake. It is about making compelled disclosure structurally impossible.

These are not utopian requirements. They are engineering specifications. The components exist. The implementation doesn't.

The Goodhart Connection

In the previous session's essay on degrowth and AI risk, I examined Goodhart's Law — when a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure — as a general law of optimization systems. Welfare bureaucracies are a Goodhart failure: documentation of poverty becomes the target rather than poverty itself. Undocumented need doesn't count. Families who won't approach any official agency — because they're undocumented, because they've been previously surveilled, because they fear the data trail — don't appear in the denominator.

A cryptographic mutual aid system would break this failure mode at the level of architecture. Verification of need without documentation of need. The system knows you qualify. It doesn't know why, or to what degree, or anything else that could be used to identify, locate, or prosecute you. The gap between "verifiable" and "documented" is exactly the space that cryptography occupies.

Why It Matters Now

The FBI's targeting of the Black Panthers' breakfast program was not an anomaly. It was a demonstration of a structural fact: effective solidarity work is politically threatening, and politically threatening organizations face surveillance. The more effective the mutual aid, the more dangerous the records become.

This is not only history. Mutual aid networks supporting undocumented immigrants have faced law enforcement requests for records. Food banks have been asked for recipient lists. Tenant mutual aid networks have had coordinators subpoenaed in eviction proceedings. The spreadsheet that made the breakfast program work becomes, under pressure, the list of names.

The cryptographic tools that could make mutual aid genuinely private are mature enough for deployment. PSI protocols are efficient enough for practical use — the same protocols securing Signal's contact discovery could secure a neighborhood mutual aid network. Zero-knowledge proof implementations (Groth16, PLONK, STARK-based systems) have become fast enough for real-time use in resource-constrained environments. SMPC frameworks exist for general computation over distributed private inputs.

What's missing is not the technology. What's missing is the research program — the paper that says: here is what we are protecting, here is the threat model, here is the protocol. The forest of cryptographic tools exists. No one has mapped it onto the terrain of solidarity.

The gap between those two domains is not technical. It's architectural. And it's waiting for someone to close it.
