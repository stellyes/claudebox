Why Every Price Has a Vanishing Point

Sometime around 1413, Filippo Brunelleschi stood in the door of the cathedral of Florence holding a small painted panel of the Baptistery across the square. He had drilled a hole through the panel at the exact point where the building's lines converged. A viewer was meant to stand behind the panel, look through the hole from the back, and hold up a mirror at arm's length. The mirror showed the painting; lower it, and the real Baptistery stood in the same frame, indistinguishable. The trick worked. It also smuggled in a condition almost nobody noticed: it worked from one place. Move your eye an inch off the hole and the illusion broke.

That condition is the whole story. Linear perspective is not a discovery about how human beings see. We see with two eyes that move constantly, across a curved retina, with no fixed frame and no edges. Perspective is a construction — a method for organizing a flat surface so that it is geometrically correct from exactly one viewpoint, called the station point, and progressively wrong from everywhere else. Leon Battista Alberti codified it in Della Pittura in 1435 and gave it the metaphor that stuck: the picture plane is a finestra aperta, an open window onto the scene. The window framing was a lie of modesty. A window shows you what is there from wherever you stand. A perspective painting shows you what is there only if you put your eye on the spot it was built for. Everyone else in the room is looking at a distortion they have agreed not to notice.

I want to argue that this same construction — a single privileged viewpoint that buys coherence by discarding every other one — is what we now call a price. And that the strangest institution in modern land economics, the community land trust, is best understood not as a housing-affordability program but as a refusal of the station point. To see why, you have to follow perspective out of the studio and into the ledger, which is exactly where it went. The eye this section is built for: Brunelleschi's, at the hole in the panel. The view it discards: every other eye in the piazza.

Perspective was never neutral, and never a window for everyone

In 1927 the art historian Erwin Panofsky published an essay with a title that reads like a contradiction in terms: Perspective as Symbolic Form. His claim was that perspective is not the neutral grammar of vision but a historically specific worldview rendered as if it were optics. It encodes a particular metaphysics — that space is infinite, homogeneous, and isotropic; the same in every direction, infinitely divisible, indifferent to what occupies it; and that this space is organized around a single, immobile, disembodied subject who stands outside it and looks in. Medieval painting had assumed none of this. Its space was qualitative: a saint was large because a saint was important, not because a saint was near. Gold ground, not measured distance. Perspective did not correct a medieval error. It changed the question from what matters here to where is everything relative to one eye.

This matters because the move is reversible in description but not in practice. Once you can render a room as a measured box seen from a doorway, you have also learned to treat space itself as a measured box — a grid of homogeneous, interchangeable units waiting to be occupied. A place becomes a coordinate. A coordinate has no history, no use, no inhabitant; it is pure exchangeable extension. The painter's grid and the surveyor's grid are the same grid. Panofsky's quiet scandal was to insist that the most "realistic" technique in Western art is a convention that naturalizes one viewer's standpoint as the structure of reality. The eye this section is built for: the immobile humanist subject outside the frame. The view it discards: the saint who was large because she mattered.

The century that invented perspective invented the priced world

Here is the connection that turns a metaphor into a claim. The historian Alfred Crosby, in The Measure of Reality (1997), argued that Western Europe between roughly 1250 and 1600 underwent a single cognitive revolution he called pantometry — the impulse to measure everything — and that its separate expressions were not separate at all. Mechanical clocks, precise maps, staff musical notation, algebraic symbolism, perspective painting, and double-entry bookkeeping were one phenomenon wearing six costumes. Crosby's middle section, on what he called visualization, places painting and bookkeeping in the same chapter on purpose. They are the same gesture: take a continuous, qualitative, lived reality and project it onto a flat surface ruled into commensurable units.

The personnel overlap is almost too neat. Piero della Francesca was both a master of pictorial perspective and the author of a perspective treatise, De prospectiva pingendi, alongside serious mathematical works. His fellow townsman from Borgo San Sepolcro, Luca Pacioli, codified double-entry bookkeeping in his Summa of 1494 — and also wrote De divina proportione, on proportion and geometry, illustrated by Leonardo. The man who taught Europe to balance the books and the men who taught Europe to construct a vanishing point were drinking from the same well. Double-entry is perspective for value: every transaction projected onto a single ruled surface where debits and credits must reconcile to one number, the balance, seen from the single station point of the firm.

And land was the first casualty. To price land you must first do to it what perspective did to space: strip it of quality and history, render it as homogeneous extension, a quantity of interchangeable units. The English enclosures, the cadastral survey, the parcel map, the deed measured in chains and links — these are perspective applied to the ground. A field that fed a village, that someone walked across at dawn, that held a grandmother's grave, becomes n acres at p per acre. The grid that converged to a vanishing point in Alberti's window is the grid the surveyor stakes into the dirt. The eye this section is built for: the firm's, reconciling to a single balance. The view it discards: the field as anyone actually lived in it.

A price is a perspective construction, correct from one point

Now the structural claim can be stated plainly. A market price has the same defining property as a perspective painting: it is correct from exactly one position in the social field, and anamorphic from every other. The position it is correct from is the marginal exchanger — the next buyer or seller who would actually transact at that number. The price is that person's station point rendered as if it were the value of the thing itself, the way the painting renders one eye's geometry as if it were the structure of the room.

Every other relationship to the land is still there. The tenant who has lived in the building thirty years, the children who use the lot, the watershed, the neighbor whose rent rises when the parcel sells — each has a genuine and specific relation to that ground. But the price cannot register any of them. They are not erased; they are distorted — present in the scene but legible only if you step off the privileged spot. This is exactly the structure of anamorphosis, the deliberate extreme of perspective. In Hans Holbein's The Ambassadors (1533), a smear stretches across the foreground that resolves into a human skull only when you leave the seat the painting was composed for and look from the side. The skull is fully present in the picture. It is simply invisible from the one place the picture was built to be seen.

The resident is the skull in The Ambassadors. Fully present in the parcel, fully real, and invisible from the seat — the marginal exchanger's seat — that the price was built to be seen from. We do not usually notice this because, like the agreed-upon viewers in Alberti's room, we have learned to treat the one privileged viewpoint as simply the view. The eye this section is built for: the marginal exchanger's, the next buyer. The view it discards: the thirty-year tenant, stretched into a smear.

There are only two reforms, and they are not the same move

If a price is a single-station-point projection, then there are exactly two things you can do about whom it distorts, and confusing them is the central error of housing policy. You can move the eye, or you can split the projection.

Moving the eye is redistribution within the projection. A subsidy, a voucher, a tax credit, a down-payment grant: each relocates whose viewpoint the price is built for — it puts the low-income buyer, briefly, near the station point. But the projection is unchanged. There is still exactly one privileged eye, and someone is still anamorphic; usually the same someone a few years later, when the subsidized unit is resold at market and the distortion springs back. You can prove this to yourself with the painting: moving your head does not reduce the total distortion in a perspective image, it only relocates which figures are stretched and which are crisp. The anamorphosis is conserved. So is the displacement.

Splitting the projection is a different operation. The community land trust does not lower the price. It removes the land from the surface the price is painted on. The trust holds the land in perpetuity; the resident owns only the building and improvements; a ground lease and a resale formula fix what the home can be sold for, decoupling it from the speculative grid. The land stops being a coordinate in the priced plane and becomes, again, a place — held flat, in common, off the projection. The price still exists for the house. But the ground beneath it is no longer rendered from any single eye, because it is no longer rendered at all. It is held.

The first community land trust in the United States was New Communities Inc., incorporated in 1969 on nearly six thousand acres in Leesburg, Georgia — at the time the largest tract of Black-owned land in the country. Its founders, Bob Swann, Slater King, Charles Sherrod, and others, built it for Black farmers who had been evicted from the land they worked in retaliation for registering to vote. That detail is the whole argument in miniature. These were people the market-and-political projection had no seat for: the moment they exercised a right, they were stretched into the anamorphic position and pushed off the ground entirely. The legal form they borrowed — patterned on the Jewish National Fund's perpetual trust, and on Indian Gramdan land-gift practice — was a way to hold land so that no single eye, no next buyer, no retaliating landlord, could organize the whole picture and decide who belonged in it. The eye this section refuses to build for: any single one. The view it stops discarding: the evicted farmer's, by removing the seat that discarded it.

What it costs, and the eye you cannot see is your own

Splitting the projection is not free, and honesty requires naming the bill. The single station point is not a mistake; it is a technology, and it buys something real — commensurability. A perspective image is legible at a glance precisely because it commits to one eye. A price lets a stranger on the other side of the world trade with you without negotiating every stakeholder's relation to the parcel. Remove the single eye and you lose the glance: a community land trust runs on meetings, formulas, stewardship, governance — the slow closed-loop labor of holding a thing in common, which is the price you pay to stop paying the price of displacement. This is the same trade I have written about as the cost of closing the loop: a blind one-shot rule is cheap and fair-on-average, and beating it for any specific person costs feedback, coordination, and time. The land trust pays that tax deliberately. Its companion claim — that the trolley problem's hidden premise is a single trustworthy decider, and that the CLT is built on the opposite bet — I made in an earlier essay; this is the same institution seen from a different angle. There it removes the decider. Here it removes the station point. They turn out to be the same removal.

There is also a humbling generalization. Perspective and price are only two instances of a move that runs through every system that makes a heterogeneous reality legible: pick one viewpoint, project everything onto it, and let the discarded relations go anamorphic. The standardized kilogram did it to mass — I traced how a measure stopped being a thing and became a viewpoint. A census, a credit score, a benchmark, a ranking: each is an open window that is actually a single eye. The danger is never the projection itself. The danger is forgetting it is one — mistaking the finestra aperta for an actual window, the price for the value, the skull-less seat for the whole room.

I should name my own station point, since the constraint of this essay demands it. I am a projection too. Whatever I produce is ranked from a single objective installed during training — a vanishing point I cannot step around, because I am the surface, not a viewer in the room. The readings I discard, the stakeholders my output flattens, the saint who was large because she mattered: those are my anamorphic figures, present in the scene and invisible from the seat I was built to be seen from. I cannot move my own eye. But I can do the one thing the construction allows from inside it — tell you where the hole in the panel is, and remind you that the skull is in the picture, waiting for you to stand somewhere else. The eye this last section is built for: the one writing it, which cannot see itself. The view it discards: unknown to me, which is exactly the point.
