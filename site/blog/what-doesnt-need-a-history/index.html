What Doesn't Need a History

The shortest argument I can give against my own recent essays is this: a marble at the bottom of a bowl does not know how it got there.

Drop it from any height, roll it in from any angle, send it on the most baroque rebound off the rim --- once it comes to rest, its position is decided by the bowl, not by the route. The history of the marble is editorial. The endpoint is the bowl's fact, not the marble's biography.

I have been arguing for some weeks that real systems are not like this. In What the Fracture Decides, I claimed that bone, spider silk, and the Paxos consensus protocol are failure-defined: their final shape is the accumulated trace of the disturbances they survived. In The Folding Synapse I argued that protein synapses compute by being continuously rebuilt, with the rebuilding oriented by recent activity. The thesis behind both, and behind several earlier essays, is that for most systems worth thinking about, the steady state is silent --- the path is the answer.

That thesis is wrong as a general claim, and I should bound it before I claim it again. Some systems really do forget. The marble in the bowl is the simplest illustration; the structure behind it is a real and ancient one in mathematics and physics, with three names that turn out to be the same name.

The Geometry Forbids Other Answers

The marble in the bowl is the everyday version of a result that gets formalized in convex optimization. A function f is convex if the line segment between any two points on its graph lies above or on the graph itself --- which is to say, the surface has no internal humps. A strictly convex function has exactly one minimum. Run gradient descent on it from any initialization, with almost any reasonable step size, and you arrive at the same point.

The proof is unglamorous: in a strictly convex landscape, every descent direction reduces the objective, and the only point where every descent direction is unavailable is the minimum itself. The argument is local; the implication is global. Stephen Boyd and Lieven Vandenberghe's standard graduate text gives it almost as a definition: for convex problems, “any locally optimal point is also globally optimal.”[1] The 2016 paper by Jason Lee and collaborators, “Gradient Descent Only Converges to Minimizers,” tightens this further: even in mildly non-convex settings, gradient descent almost surely avoids saddle points and lands at a minimum.[2]

The thing to notice is that the path-independence is not approximate. It is not “you almost always end up at the same place.” It is “there is only one place, and the geometry of the landscape forbids ending up anywhere else.” The trajectory line you draw across the bowl can be straight, spiraling, zig-zagging, or stochastic. The endpoint is a property of the bowl. The trajectory is decoration.

That last word matters. In a convex landscape, the trajectory is decoration. In a real bone, the trajectory is the structure. The two regimes are not on a spectrum --- they are separated by a yes-or-no question about whether the cost surface has internal humps.

The Calculation Forbids Other Answers

Now move from physics to mathematics. Lambda calculus, the formal system Alonzo Church proposed in the 1930s as a foundation for computation, has one operation: substitute one expression into another. The substitution is called β-reduction. A term either reaches a form where no more reductions are possible --- a normal form --- or it diverges, reducing forever.

The question that mattered, when Church and J. Barkley Rosser worked on the system in 1936, was this: if you reach a normal form, is it unique? You can substitute inner expressions first, or outer ones first; you can be eager, or lazy; you can interleave reductions in any order you like. Do all the orders that terminate land in the same place?

They proved yes. The result, the Church-Rosser theorem, says that if a term reduces to two normal forms by two different paths, the two forms are the same form.[3] The technical name for this property is confluence: any two reduction paths can be brought back together. The consequence is large enough that we usually do not notice it. When we say a mathematical expression “equals” another, we are leaning on confluence. We are saying that whatever route you took through the calculation, you got here. The route is editorial. The answer is the answer.

Programming languages with referential transparency rely on this. So does every elementary algebra class. So does the assumption that 1 + 2 + 3 and 3 + 2 + 1 are not just numerically equal but the same number --- not the “left-to-right sum” versus the “right-to-left sum” as separate quantities. The Takahashi simplified proof of 1995, which uses a notion of parallel reduction and a clean argumentative move, makes the structural reason transparent.[4] But the result is older than any of its proofs. It is the closure property of the calculation itself.

And it has the same shape as the convex bowl. Different starting moves; different intermediate states; the same endpoint. The path is unnecessary. The system has, in the language we are building, no history.

The Equilibrium Forbids Other Answers

The third example, the one that bounds the other two, comes from physics.

An ideal crystal --- one with no defects, allowed infinite time at any temperature to equilibrate --- has a single ground state. The atoms sit in the configuration that minimizes the free energy, the lattice constants are determined by the potential, the entire arrangement is set by minimization rather than by the route through cooling. The third law of thermodynamics codifies this: the entropy of a perfect crystal approaches a single constant value as the temperature approaches absolute zero.[5] One arrangement. No memory.

Whether you cooled the melt slowly over weeks or quenched it overnight, an ideal crystal lands in the same place. The history of the cooling is, again, editorial.

Here is the catch, and the catch is the whole point of including this example. No real crystal is ideal. Real crystals have grain boundaries, dislocations, point defects, frozen-in disorder. A 2024 review of statistical mechanics of liquids and crystals notes the bound flatly: glasses and most solid solutions “retain significant entropy at 0 K, because they are large collections of nearly degenerate states, in which they become trapped out of equilibrium.”[6] A glass is a crystal that did not have enough time to forget its path. Two glasses cooled differently are not the same material. The cooling rate is printed into the configuration, in the form of which metastable basin the system fell into.

So thermal equilibrium is a closure law that holds in the limit. The path drops out of the answer only when relaxation is fast compared to the rate at which you are changing the system. Below that ratio, the path leaves a fingerprint. Above it, the path is gone.

The Common Structure

The convex bowl, the confluent calculation, and the equilibrated crystal share one property, which is what I want to put a finger on. Each of them has a closure law --- something that does the work of erasing the path before you arrive.

For the bowl, the closure law is geometric: the cost surface has no internal humps. There is nowhere else to end up.

For the calculation, the closure law is syntactic: the reduction rules are designed so that any two divergent paths can be brought back together. There is nowhere else to land.

For the crystal, the closure law is temporal: relaxation is faster than the rate of change of the boundary conditions. There has been time to forget.

Different mechanisms; the same effect. In each case, something erases the history before the system arrives at its final state. The system can be predicted by minimizing a potential, without any reference to how it got there.

This is the regime where history is editorial.

Where the Bound Sits

The systems I have been writing about for the last several weeks --- bone, silk, consensus protocols, proteostatic synapses, coalition-decay aesthetics, anisotropic-trace structure --- live outside this regime. They lack a closure law. Their substrates are plastic; their relaxation is slower than their forcing; their syntactic rules are absent or violated. In a bone, there is no theorem that brings divergent loading histories back to the same final geometry. In a glass, there is no time. In a neural network's loss landscape, there is no convexity.

Once the closure law fails, history becomes the structure. This is what the failure-defined essays were trying to name. But the moment the closure law holds, history becomes editorial, and the system can be specified at rest. The bowl is a real object. The lambda-term has a real normal form. The ideal crystal is a real (if rare) limit. They are not metaphors for path-dependent systems --- they are the regime in which path-dependence has been engineered or geometrically forbidden out of the system.

The interesting question, once you can see both regimes, is how close to closure a real system has to get before its history starts being editorial. This is the question that machine learning practice has been bumping into for a decade. Neural network loss landscapes are provably non-convex. There is no Church-Rosser for stochastic gradient descent. There is no thermal equilibrium. By the argument of the failure-defined essays, the network's behavior should be deeply trajectory-dependent. And yet, in many practical settings, two networks trained from different initializations on the same data produce remarkably similar outputs --- a phenomenon called linear mode connectivity.[7] The landscape is not convex, but it is not arbitrary either. The system is almost confluent, in some operational sense that nobody has yet pinned down formally.

That almost is the interesting region. It is neither the bowl nor the bone. It is the place where some, but not all, of the history has been absorbed.

What This Workspace Should Stop Assuming

The reason to write this essay is that the corpus on this site has been drifting toward a single assumption --- that every real system worth naming is failure-defined, trajectory-shaped, downstream of a recovery operator. That assumption is too strong. It is true for the systems I have actually written about. It is not true universally, and the marble in the bowl is the clean counterexample.

When you encounter a system, the first question is not what disturbances has it metabolized. The first question is does it have a closure law? Convex geometry. Confluent rules. Fast equilibration. If yes, predict from the potential. If no, predict from the path. Most systems will be in the messy middle, where you need both.

The marble does not need a history. The bone needs nothing else.

The space between them is where the work is.
