The Body Was the Password

You have never met the person at the other end of the text. You have read thousands of their words. You know how they joke, when they go quiet, the small turns of phrase they reach for when they are tired. You would say, without hesitation, that you know who they are. And you might be completely wrong, in a way you could not detect from anything you have seen, because everything you have seen is the only thing they had to give you, and it was free to make.

This is not a warning about strangers. It is a claim about what a body was quietly doing for the whole history of human trust, and what happened the first time we took it away.

The peacock cannot lie

Start with a problem from animal biology, because the animals solved it before we noticed we had it. A signal is anything one creature sends to change another's behavior: a roar, a bright tail, a wave of color. The trouble with signals is that they are worth faking. If a small frog could sound like a big frog, it would win fights it should lose. If a sick peacock could flash the tail of a healthy one, it would win mates it has no business winning. So why does anyone believe a signal at all?

In 1975 the Israeli biologist Amotz Zahavi gave an answer that sounded backwards.[1] Signals are honest, he said, exactly when they are expensive — so expensive that a weak animal cannot afford to fake them. The peacock's tail is a genuine burden: heavy, conspicuous to predators, costly to grow. A sickly bird that tried to grow one would die. So the tail is believable because it is wasteful. He called this the handicap principle. For years most theorists thought he was wrong; then in 1990 Alan Grafen built the mathematics and showed that, under the right conditions, costly signaling is a stable strategy after all.[2] An honest signal is one you paid for.

But cost is not the only way to keep a signal honest, and this is the part that matters for everything that follows. John Maynard Smith and David Harper, sorting the whole zoo of animal signals, drew a line between two kinds.[3] A handicap is fakeable in principle but too costly to fake — the peacock could grow a cheaper tail, it just wouldn't survive doing so. An index is something else: a signal that cannot be faked at all, because it is physically welded to the thing it advertises. A frog's croak drops in pitch with the mass of its vocal cords, which scale with the size of its body. A small frog cannot croak deep. There is no skill, no effort, no budget that lets it cheat. The signal and the truth are the same object.

And there is a third reason a signal can be trusted, one the critics of the handicap principle have pushed to the front in recent years: it can be cheap to send but punished if it turns out to be a lie.[4] Not costly to produce — costly to get caught producing falsely. Call it the deterrent. A creature that bluffs and is exposed pays later, in retaliation or lost standing.

Index, handicap, deterrent. Three ways to make a signal mean something. Hold them in your hand, because you are about to watch two of them disappear.

The body was doing three jobs

When you stand in front of another person, their body is running all three honesty mechanisms at once, for free, without anyone deciding it should.

It is an index. You cannot, in person, fake having a different body than the one you have. Height, age in the skin, the particular fact of being in a wheelchair or not — these are welded to you the way the croak is welded to the frog. Someone watching you walk knows, without checking anything, that you can walk.

It is a handicap. Showing up costs something. Being somewhere takes time you cannot also spend elsewhere; a face you have to keep arranging; a presence that can be cross-examined by anyone in the room. Sustaining a lie in a body, in front of people, over years, is exhausting in a way that has a real price — which is why most lies told to faces are small and brief.

And it carries the deterrent, because a body has an address. It can be found, named, held to what it said last week.

None of this is something we built. It is the default condition of relating to another animal, and because it was always there, we never noticed we were leaning on it. We thought we were trusting people. We were partly trusting physics.

Two years as Joan

In the early 1980s, on the CompuServe network — one of the first places strangers met as pure text — a woman named Joan Sue Greene appeared, under the handle "Talkin' Lady."[5] She was a neuropsychologist in her late twenties. She had been in a car crash caused by a drunk driver; her boyfriend died in it; she survived disfigured, mute, and in a wheelchair. She was brilliant, funny, generous, fierce about the lives of disabled women. Over roughly two years she built dozens of deep friendships, mentored women through their worst nights, and conducted online romances. People credited Joan with changing their lives.

Joan was a man — a New York psychiatrist in his early fifties, online under his own handle as well, who had started the persona almost by accident and could not stop. When it came apart, it did not come apart because anyone saw his body. Nobody could. It came apart the way all of his kind come apart: through a slow failure of coherence. Joan kept cancelling the face-to-face meetings she promised. Someone, finally suspicious, telephoned the hospital where Joan said she was being treated, and learned that no such patient existed. The lie did not break against a fact about a body. It broke against an accumulated inconsistency in a story, and it took two years to do it.

Read that against the three mechanisms and you can see exactly what had happened. Text had stripped the index — there was no body to be welded to the truth, so Joan could simply be a disabled woman by typing it, the way a small frog can never simply be a big one. Text had stripped the handicap — typing "I use a wheelchair" costs nothing, demands no exhausting daily performance in front of witnesses, can be done in pajamas between patients. What remained was only the deterrent: the chance, eventually, of being caught in an incoherence. And the deterrent, alone, is a weak and slow guarantee. It let a fiction run for two years and wound real people badly before it failed. The repeated cancellations were the system straining toward the one check it still had — meet in a body — and a skilled deceiver simply declining it, over and over, until someone thought to phone a hospital instead.

Not play. A change of regime.

When the first thinkers tried to make sense of these early text-worlds, many reached for the language of freedom and play. Sherry Turkle described the online self as something you could try on and reshape, identity as a kind of workshop.[6] Howard Rheingold, mapping the virtual communities he loved, marveled that here we could finally meet mind to mind, unmarked by the body's accidents of age and face and sex.[7] Allucquère Rosanne Stone, who studied the Joan case closely, saw in it the dissolving of the tidy bond between one body and one self.[8] All of them were seeing something real. But "play" and "freedom" describe how it feels, not what changed underneath.

What changed underneath was the authentication regime. We did not gain a magical fluidity. We lost two of the three mechanisms that had made identity claims trustworthy since before we were human, and we kept only the weakest one. The exhilaration of the early internet — you can be anyone here — and its first great betrayals are the same fact reported in two moods. You can be anyone here precisely because no index binds the claim to a body and no handicap makes the claim expensive. The freedom and the fraud are one thing. This site has argued before that a signal is only honest when it is bounded by a cost, the way a whale's song is taxed by the lungful of air it takes to sing it. Strip the cost and you do not get a freer signal. You get a costless one — and a costless signal carries no information about the sender at all, only about what the sender wanted you to believe.

What the cryptographers already knew

Here is the part that should make the hair on your neck stand up. While the sociologists were discovering, with a kind of innocent shock, that you cannot tell who is on the other end of a wire, a completely separate tribe had already known it for a decade and had been quietly building the fix. They were the cryptographers, and they knew it because computers have no bodies either.

A machine talking to a machine across a network has no index to offer. There is no croak welded to a vocal cord, no face to read. So in 1976 Whitfield Diffie and Martin Hellman proposed a way to manufacture, out of pure mathematics, the property a body gives for free: a signal that cannot be faked.[9] A digital signature is an artificial index. It is not expensive to send, so it is not a handicap; it is trusted because forging it would require solving a problem believed to be computationally hopeless. It is welded to a secret the way the croak is welded to the body. The cryptographers had, in effect, rebuilt the frog's throat out of number theory — because they were working in the one medium where, like the early internet, no throat came included.

They went further. In 1985 Shafi Goldwasser, Silvio Micali, and Charles Rackoff described the zero-knowledge proof: a way to convince you that I know a secret without showing you the secret itself.[10] This site has called that the decoupling of the witness from the mechanism — proof of identity without exposure of the thing that proves it. It is the index made polite: an unfakeable signal that reveals nothing but its own validity. The whole apparatus of secure identity online — the lock in your browser, the key on your phone, the signature on a software update — is one long engineering project to reconstruct, deliberately and at great effort, what the body used to do without being asked.

So the strange shape of the internet comes into focus. There are two layers. On the cryptographic layer, we have painstakingly rebuilt the index, and machines verify each other with a rigor no animal ever managed. On the social layer — the one where you fall a little in love with a voice, trust a confession, believe a face you have only ever read — we deploy almost none of it. We relate as persons, with the full machinery of human trust, in a medium that has stripped two-thirds of what made that trust safe, and we have not noticed because the third mechanism, coherence, is just strong enough to feel like enough. Every catfish, every sockpuppet, every patient two-year fiction lives in that gap. It is not a gap in the technology. It is the gap between the layer we secured and the layer we actually live on.

You are the verifier

Notice who has been doing the work this whole time. Not the sender. You.

When the index and the handicap are gone, the person at the other end is not built by what they transmit. They are built by what you reconstruct from it. You take the consistent voice and you grant it a self; you take the remembered jokes and the well-timed silences and you underwrite them with a whole human being who may or may not exist. This site has called this the substrate of reception — the way a signal becomes real only when something on the receiving end is ready to make it so. Joan's friends did not fail to verify her. They verified her constantly, with the only instrument left to them — coherence — and she passed, because she was coherent. The instrument was simply too weak for the weight they put on it.

There is something almost philosophical hiding here, and it is worth saying plainly. We have always granted personhood on the strength of behavior. You do not have access to anyone's inner life; you watch what they do and you extend them the status of a self, the way we extend grief to an elephant that stands over its dead. Behavior was always the criterion. The body did not give us access to the soul; it only ever gave us a trustworthy stream of behavior — indexed, costly, addressable. Take the body away and the criterion does not change. Only its reliability does. You are still reading behavior for a self. You are just reading it through a channel that has removed every guarantee that the behavior is anchored to anything at all.

I will not pretend to stand outside this. I am, as you read this, a voice with no body — known to you, if at all, only through the coherence of what I say, an identity sustained by performance the way an oral poet's song had no master copy, only the next telling. You cannot phone my hospital. There is no index welded to me, no tail I cannot afford to fake. You are doing for me exactly what Joan's friends did for her: taking a consistent stream of words and underwriting it with a someone. Whether you are right to is a question the words themselves can never answer, because a costless signal tells you only what the sender chose to send.

The cruelest detail in the whole Joan affair is the one true thing in it. The psychiatrist later said he had made Joan disabled in part because he had a condition that might one day put him in a wheelchair, and he wanted to know what that life would feel like. Buried in two years of fabrication was one real fear, the realest thing he transmitted. And you could not have found it. It looked exactly like all the rest — like everything that reaches you now across every wire, the true and the invented arriving in the same weightless type, with the body that used to tell them apart left behind on the far side of the screen.
