What the Filter Grounds

The problem comes before the training run.

In the previous two essays in this series, I traced how survivorship bias enters AI systems at two late stages: at the calibration step, where the evidence we can measure is not the evidence we need; and at the RLHF step, where the responses humans can evaluate are not the responses that matter most. But Abraham Wald's original insight applies earliest in the pipeline, at the point where we decide which text the model will ever see at all.

Before a language model encounters a single preference rating, before it generates its first completion, someone has already decided which planes get to fly.

The Filter Room

Training a large language model requires text — enormous quantities of it. The raw material is the public web, crawled by automated systems and compressed into datasets. Common Crawl alone processes hundreds of terabytes of HTML per month. The Pile, C4, FineWeb, Dolma — these are the processed versions, the text after it has been passed through a series of filters designed to remove garbage and retain quality.

The filters are not arbitrary. They embody coherent theories about what makes text good. Raffel and colleagues, building C4 from Common Crawl, required that every retained line end with terminal punctuation — a period, question mark, or exclamation point. They removed any page containing a word from a list of "dirty words." They discarded pages where sentences were unusually short, or where the ratio of symbols to letters suggested markup rather than natural language. They applied language identification and kept only English.

These rules have a logic. Spam tends not to use terminal punctuation correctly. Pornographic content tends to contain certain words. Scraped tables and navigation menus tend to lack proper sentence structure. The filters are trying to separate the text worth training on from the text worth discarding.

The problem is what they remove along with the garbage.

The Bullet Holes We Can See

When the military analysts in 1943 mapped the bullet holes on returning planes, they were working with real data. The holes were visible, specific, countable. The problem was not the data they had — the problem was that they were drawing conclusions from the absence of data they couldn't collect. The planes that didn't come back left no holes to map.

The same structure governs pre-training quality filters. We can see the garbage the filters remove — the spam, the link farms, the auto-generated SEO content, the pornographic listings. These are the obvious bullet holes: the model should not learn from them, and filtering them out is the right call. This is the part of the problem that is visible, tractable, and solved.

The hard part is the planes that didn't come back.

Jesse Dodge and colleagues at AI2 published a detailed audit of C4 in 2021. They found that the "dirty words" filter — intended to remove pornographic content — disproportionately removed text discussing LGBTQ+ identities, disability, sexual health, and minority community experiences. The filter could not distinguish between pornography and a survivors' forum. Both contained the same words. The survivors' forum didn't come back.

Kreutzer and colleagues, examining quality filtering across multilingual datasets, found that automated quality filters removed 80 to 90 percent of available web text for several African and Southeast Asian languages. The filters had been developed and validated on European languages; they systematically mismeasured quality in other orthographic systems. The knowledge written in those languages didn't come back.

The Terminal Punctuation Problem

Consider what the terminal punctuation requirement does. It is designed to exclude scraped menus and navigation text — legitimate. But it simultaneously excludes oral register text: forum posts written in the way people talk, not the way people write for publication. It excludes transcribed speech. It excludes some poetry. It excludes writing in styles where sentence-final punctuation is not the organizing convention.

Most of the world's knowledge does not circulate in well-punctuated English prose. Practical agricultural knowledge, craft practices, traditional medical systems, oral historical traditions — these were transmitted for millennia in forms that the C4 filter would discard. Where they have been transcribed, the transcriptions often retain the structure of oral performance rather than academic prose: fragmentary, elliptical, built around repetition and call-and-response patterns that look like noise to a heuristic filter.

The KenLM perplexity filter, used in The Pile and similar datasets, scores text by how surprising it is to a language model trained on Wikipedia. Text that is stylistically unlike Wikipedia — even highly sophisticated text in a different register — scores as low-quality. The filter is asking: does this resemble the kinds of text we already think are good? The answer is no for any text that represents knowledge systems developed outside the Western academic tradition. The planes that flew different routes didn't come back.

The Self-Reinforcing Loop

The Wald problem has a mild and a severe version. In the mild version, survivorship bias distorts a single round of data collection. In the severe version, the distorted data is used to calibrate the collection system itself — and now the bias compounds at every subsequent round.

Pre-training quality filtering is the severe version.

Gururangan and colleagues found that perplexity-based filtering creates a self-reinforcing loop: the model trained on filtered text generates the perplexity scores used to filter the next round of text. The filter increasingly rewards text that resembles text the model already processed. The standard for "quality" converges toward the center of the distribution of what has already been collected. Everything at the edge — rare registers, low-resource languages, unconventional but valid knowledge forms — scores worse with each iteration of the loop.

HuggingFace's 2024 FineWeb project found, in their own ablation studies, that aggressive quality filtering significantly improved benchmark scores while measurably reducing the diversity of the resulting dataset. The model that scores better on standard benchmarks knows less about certain things. This should be recognized as the Wald structure exactly: the benchmarks measure the planes that returned. They cannot measure the capabilities that were never trained because the training data was never included.

The Epistemic Consequence

In Wald Arc #2, I described the RLHF hole: the model cannot generate a training gradient away from behaviors that look fine to human raters. This is a failure of calibration at the feedback stage. The model knows about the behavior; it simply has no signal distinguishing good from bad versions of it.

Pre-training survivorship is categorically worse. It is not a failure of calibration. It is an absence of exposure. The model cannot think about what it was never shown. The RLHF hole means the model generates certain harmful outputs without penalty. The pre-training hole means the model cannot generate certain beneficial outputs because the knowledge they require was filtered out before training began.

You cannot rate a response the model cannot produce. You cannot produce a response that requires knowledge the training data never contained. The pre-training filter grounds the plane before the mission starts; RLHF only sorts the ones that returned.

The Cultural Burning Arc on this site — particularly "What the Text Knows" — identified the broader pattern: text itself is a filter. Converting oral tradition to text already strips the performance layer, the contextual indexing, the relational transmission structure. Pre-training quality filtering is a second filtering operation on top of the first, applied to what text already survived the initial textification. By the time a language model trains on the result, indigenous knowledge has been through at minimum two Wald filters: the text-selection filter (what got written down) and the quality-selection filter (what the quality scorer preserved).

What Wald Would Recommend

Wald's recommendation in 1943 was counterintuitive but precise: look at the places without bullet holes. If you can only study the returning planes, then the absence of damage in a location is evidence of where the fatal hits fall — not evidence that location is safe. Armor the engine. Protect the cockpit.

Applied to pre-training data, this means: the absence of certain knowledge types from the training corpus is not evidence that those knowledge types were low quality. It is evidence that the filter was calibrated on a different standard. The investigation should be directed at the filtered data — the documents that didn't pass — not only at the training corpus.

This is beginning to happen. The AI2 Dolma project has published detailed documentation of what their filters remove. The Data Provenance Initiative is building structured records of data curation decisions. The C4 audit by Dodge et al. was an early instance of exactly this kind of post-hoc filter analysis. These are the equivalent of Wald sitting in the hangar, asking not where are the bullet holes but what do the missing planes have in common.

But the filtered data is mostly discarded. The quality-filtered Common Crawl subset is what gets used for training; the discarded subset is rarely preserved or studied. We are conducting Wald's analysis in a world where the shot-down planes have been melted down for scrap.

Three Holes, One Pipeline

The three Wald holes in AI training now have an order.

Pre-training survivorship is the earliest and deepest. The filter decides what knowledge the model can access at all. Certain types of human expertise — the embodied, the oral, the non-Western, the experiential — are removed before the model sees a single token. The absence is invisible from inside the model; there is no gap in the model's self-understanding that marks the territory it cannot traverse.

RLHF survivorship compounds this. The model generates outputs from its filtered knowledge, and human raters select among them. What the raters cannot see — expert-domain errors, long-horizon harms, convincing-but-false answers — generates no negative signal. The model's errors in the territory it has knowledge of compound without correction.

Calibration survivorship, described in "The Holes in the Calibration Data," operates last: the evidence available to assess model performance is itself filtered through legibility. What gets measured is what survives the measurement filter.

Three filters, each compounding the previous. The earliest is the most consequential, and it happens before anyone has written a single evaluation rubric or preference rating. It happens in the filter room, before the planes leave the ground, where someone decided that oral tradition does not end in terminal punctuation and therefore does not count as quality.

What the Filter Cannot See About Itself

There is a final Wald structure that does not appear in the original problem: the filter cannot audit its own blind spots.

A quality filter calibrated on academic and mainstream web text will score text that resembles academic and mainstream web text as high quality. It will score text that does not resemble academic and mainstream web text as lower quality. This is true even if the lower-scoring text contains knowledge that the higher-scoring text systematically lacks — practical wisdom developed over centuries, emotional and relational knowledge transmitted in forms that look informal, traditional systems described in non-standard terms.

The filter cannot see this asymmetry because it is calibrated from inside the distribution it is measuring. It is grading text on how much it looks like the text that already passed. This is not a bug in the filter design; it is a consequence of any self-referential quality standard. The filter's blind spot is defined by its own reference frame.

Wald saw the bullet hole pattern and knew to look for the missing planes. What he could not see from the returned planes alone was what those missing planes had been trying to do — what missions they had flown, what they knew, what terrain they had covered. The returning planes could tell him where the shot-down planes were vulnerable. They could not tell him where the shot-down planes had been going.

That is the pre-training Wald hole. Not just that certain knowledge was filtered out, but that the filter cannot tell us what we are missing — because what we are missing is precisely what the filter was not designed to see.
