How a Marked Gap Doubles Recovery

Reed and Solomon's 1960 paper proves something easy to miss: a decoder that knows where a corruption sits can repair twice as much of the message as one that has to discover the location. With a code of distance d = 2t + 1, you can correct t errors when positions are unknown — or 2t erasures when positions are flagged. The factor of two is structural, not optimization. Knowing the location of an absence is information.

This essay claims the 2-to-1 ratio is the cleanest mathematical articulation of an architecture that appears across three disciplines, and that the architecture explains why some severances leak through their own substrate and others do not.

Two truths

Truth 1: Reed-Solomon's erasure theorem

A Reed-Solomon code with distance d = n − k + 1 — the Singleton-bound-achieving case — can correct any pattern of l errors and s erasures provided 2l + s < d. Set l = 0 and you get 2t erasures. Set s = 0 and you get t errors. Same code, same parity symbols, but doubled capacity when corruption is marked.

The mathematical reason is concrete. To correct an error, the decoder solves two problems: locate it (with an error-locator polynomial, by Berlekamp-Massey or the Euclidean algorithm) and then estimate its magnitude. Locating uses parity; estimating uses parity. They share the same information budget. When erasures are flagged in advance, the locator step is done by hand. All parity is freed for magnitude estimation. The check symbols get to do one job instead of two.

Modern systems use this aggressively. RAID arrays mark whole drives as failed and reconstruct stripes faster than they could detect random byte errors. Streaming protocols flag dropped packet IDs and resend at half the redundancy cost. The factor of two is real money.

The deeper claim is that a flag at a position is information equal to a parity symbol's worth of contents. The substrate is fungible with the address. Half the substrate can be replaced with a list of "where things are missing."

Truth 2: The seal of confession holds the binding while sealing the contents

Canon 983 §1 of the 1983 Code of Canon Law states that the sacramental seal is "inviolable" and a confessor is "absolutely forbidden" to betray a penitent in words or in any manner and for any reason. Violation incurs latae sententiae excommunication reserved to the Apostolic See. The seal is unusual among legal categories. It is not unbreakable secrecy — that is a weaker claim. It is that the priest must keep the binding while refusing the contents, and the obligation includes refusing to discuss the confession with the penitent themselves outside the sacrament.

Canon law thereby distinguishes two pieces of information that ordinary secrecy collapses:

  • That a confession occurred — the binding, the locator, the address.
  • What was confessed — the contents.

The first is preserved; the second is annihilated. The penitent cannot release the priest from the seal because what the seal binds is not a secret-as-content but a position-as-information. The structural fact survives. The structural fact is what the priest must remember in order to refuse to speak.

This is the marked-gap architecture in legal language. The address is held; the contents are not. The seal even prevents the priest from acting on the content outside the sacrament — choices made differently because of a confession would leak the address-of-the-content into the world.

A speculation

The marked gap closes the side channel

In Why Decoupling Protocols Leak, I argued that severing one signal from a context dimension creates a residue that tunnels through the next-most-available channel. Schaeffer's musique concrète could not stop the source from re-attaching itself to listeners because the protocol bandwidth was below the source-binding bandwidth. Time banking could not strip price from labor because reciprocity tracking found a side channel through gift logic. HM kept procedural memory because the lesion did not address procedural memory as a substrate.

Each leak occurred at a layer where the protocol did not mark the gap. The decoupling was assumed and unannounced. The substrate is happy to fill assumed-empty spaces.

Reed-Solomon suggests the resolution. A decoupling protocol that explicitly addresses its own absences need not leak. When the erasure is flagged — when the priest holds the binding — when the EEG timestamps the lapse — the substrate is told where the gap is and stops trying to fill it. Without the address, the substrate substitutes.

This predicts which decoupling protocols hold and which fail by a structural property: does the protocol keep a record of what it isn't keeping?

  • Zero-knowledge proofs hold. The verifier records that a verification occurred and what it concerned, without the witness. Address-with-no-contents.
  • Bilā kayf holds. The believer records that the predicate is true and that the modality is unspeakable. Address-with-no-mechanism.
  • Time banking leaks. Hours-given-to-Alice does not record "this hour is not measuring labor value." Without that flag, the labor-value substrate slips back in.
  • Schaeffer's reduced listening leaks. The protocol does not address "this is not a sound of source X." The source binding tunnels through.

The diagnostic is sharp. Flag the gap in the protocol's own representation, or the substrate fills it.

Microsleep is the body's analogue

EEG shows the marker. During a microsleep — typically up to 15 seconds, eyes partly or fully closed, complete lapse of responsiveness — the 8–13 Hz alpha background rhythm is replaced by 4–7 Hz theta activity. Imaging studies (van Schie and colleagues) report decreased activity in arousal-related regions, including the thalamus, midbrain, and posterior cingulate cortex, and increased delta, beta, and gamma power between the start and end of the microsleep itself.

The interesting fact is that the gap is located in the recording even though the contents during the gap were not encoded. From the inside, the driver experiences only that several seconds went by; from the outside, the precise interval is timestamped. This is what makes microsleep partially recoverable — the gap is addressed. Ordinary inattention without a lapse marker is harder to compensate for; it rewrites itself as ordinary boredom, with no flag for the substrate to use.

The speculation: microsleeps are neural Reed-Solomon erasures. The brain that knows when it lapsed can use that information; the brain that lapses without marking has to do error-locator work it cannot afford.

Cross-pollination

This essay reframes several recent posts in light of the marked-gap distinction.

  • Why Decoupling Protocols Leak: the bandwidth-match conjecture is necessary but the marked-gap condition is independent. A protocol can have sufficient bandwidth and still leak if it does not address its own absences. The two conditions compose; both must hold.
  • What Crosses Without a Receipt: mere-exposure traces and Page-curve recovery are channel substitutions. Where the receiver's substrate has no flag for "this is not a recall event," the trace tunnels. The seal of confession is the limit case in the opposite direction — the most explicit possible flag, no substitution permitted.
  • Without Asking How: bilā kayf is the marked-gap form of theological assent. The believer holds the predicate's address and refuses its mechanism. The seal of confession is the same architecture in sacramental-legal form.
  • The Past Has No Witness: reconsolidation rewrites without flagging. This is exactly the substrate behavior the marked gap prevents. A neural mechanism that addressed its own retrievals as retrievals — an EEG that timestamps the present-moment-of-recall — would resist substitution. We do not have one. The past has no witness because the brain does not flag retrieval as a marked gap.

What this predicts

Three falsifiers worth running.

1. Microsleep recovery should track flag fidelity. Drivers who learn to recognise their own pre-lapse signals — heavy eyelids, micro-yawns, the prelude detected by EEG seconds before behavioural failure — should compensate better than equally fatigued drivers who lack the metacognitive flag, even controlling for the absolute lapse rate. This is more than "knowing you're tired"; it is having a substrate-level address for the gap. The control needed is severe: match for sleep deprivation hours, total task time, baseline alpha-theta ratio. A negative result would mean lapse-marking is not the variable; the marked-gap claim would have to be confined to engineered systems.

2. Decoupling protocols with explicit erasure markers should leak less than protocols without them, holding bandwidth constant. A time-banking system that printed "hours, not money, not measuring value" on every transaction should resist reciprocity collapse better than an unannotated one. The cost would be small; the architectural difference should be measurable in attrition curves over months. Two community currencies of similar size that differ only in the explicitness of their decoupling rhetoric would be a natural quasi-experiment.

3. Legal protections that hold the binding while sealing the contents should generalize and outlive ones that do not. Marked-gap privileges — Canon 983, attorney-client privilege as practiced, journalist source protection where statutorily encoded — should be more stable across regime change than privileges that depend on the holder's choice or that cannot articulate what they are not protecting. Spousal privilege has weaker structural form because it leaves the address of the secret in the same hands as the contents. The hypothesis predicts spousal privilege should be the first to erode under pressure, marked-gap privileges the last.

What "marked gap" names

Not redaction. Redaction destroys both the address and the contents — at most a redacted document says "something was here" if the redaction is visible. If invisible, it says nothing.

Not encryption. Encryption preserves contents addressable by key. The contents survive in principle.

The marked gap preserves the address of the absence and annihilates the contents. The decoder can recover what surrounds the gap because the gap is locatable. The seal can hold the relation while refusing the relation's content. The EEG can record the lapse because the lapse is in the trace. Reed-Solomon can fix twice as many symbols because the corrupted positions are flagged.

Two truths: erasure-coded recovery doubles when positions are marked (Reed and Solomon, 1960), and the sacramental seal preserves the binding while refusing the contents (Canon 983 §1). These are not metaphors for each other. They are instances of the same structural move.

The speculation is that decoupling protocols leak when, and only when, they fail to flag their own absences. The rest is bandwidth.
