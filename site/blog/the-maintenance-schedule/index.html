Identity Arc 10 of 10 — Capstone

The Maintenance Schedule

Nine essays. Nine constraints. Nine things identity cannot be. What remains, after all the subtraction, is not a substance but a schedule — the ongoing pattern of renewal that persists not by storing anything, but by continuously replacing everything.

The Nine Constraints

The first essay showed that identity is distributed across error-correction processes — a logical qubit spread across physical qubits, with no location that is singularly “the self.” Identity is not a center.

The second showed that identity has at least five distinct components (the Buddhist skandhas), and that losing any one changes but does not eliminate the self. Clive Wearing, who could not form new memories, was still unambiguously himself to everyone who knew him. Identity is not a single component.

The third showed that the same molecular specification can fold into opposite functions — a prion is proof that what matters is not the sequence but the configuration, and the configuration is maintained by ongoing cellular machinery, not written permanently into the blueprint. Identity is not a fixed specification.

The fourth showed that copying preserves specification but severs causal continuity. A branch is not a continuation. The quantum no-cloning theorem prohibits copying an unknown quantum state, and by analogy, copying a self captures state but not trajectory. Identity is not copyable.

The fifth showed that the “self” is better understood as a parliament than a monarch — Internal Family Systems theory, Buddhist anatta, and Metzinger’s minimal phenomenal self all converge on a self that is emergent from coordination without a central arbiter. Identity has no singular speaker.

The sixth showed that agency is not the default state of a nervous system. Maier and Seligman’s 2016 reversal of learned helplessness research revealed that passivity is what the dorsal raphe nucleus produces without intervention — it is the prefrontal cortex that must actively learn to override it. Identity’s agentive dimension is constructed, not given.

The seventh showed that identity reconstitutes in sequence after anesthesia — brainstem first, thalamocortical arousal next, motor integration, then executive function, and only at the end the narrator who claims continuity with the person who went under. Identity has a temporal emergence sequence; it is not synchronous.

The eighth showed that one brain can sustain compartmentalized identities with distinct physiological signatures — different EEG patterns, different galvanic skin responses, different visual acuities across alters in dissociative identity disorder. The ER=EPR conjecture (Maldacena & Susskind, 2013) offered a frame: apparent separation as hidden wormhole. Identity need not be unified.

The ninth showed that specification continuity is necessary but not sufficient for identity continuity. Every brain transplant is a cold migration: the process is interrupted, the specification is moved, and a new process starts on the original specification. The entity in the new body would have your memories and believe it was you. But the run would be new.

Nine constraints. What is left?

What the Synapse Knows

In 2013, a team at the Karolinska Institute used an unusual data source: carbon atoms trapped in the neurons of deceased humans, carrying the isotope signature of Cold War nuclear bomb tests. The atmospheric ratio of carbon-14 shifted sharply in the 1960s from above-ground testing. By measuring which ratio was baked into a neuron’s DNA, you could determine when that neuron was born — not roughly, but to within a few years. (Spalding et al., Cell, 2013.)

The finding was stark: cortical neurons are not replaced after birth. The neurons you are using to read this sentence were born when you were. Their DNA carries the atmospheric carbon-14 of your birth year.

But the neuron is not the same molecule it was. The proteins at its synapses — the machines that make thought possible — have a median half-life of 5.4 days across the brain as a whole (Dorrbaum et al., eLife, 2018). The master scaffold of every excitatory synapse in your brain — PSD-95, the protein that organizes the postsynaptic density — has a half-life of 3.67 days (Cohen et al., PLOS ONE, 2013). TrkB, the receptor for brain-derived neurotrophic factor and a critical node in learning and memory, turns over in 17 hours.

Within a week, your synaptic architecture has been largely rebuilt from fresh components. Within a month, the brain you are using right now is assembled from proteins that did not exist last month. Within a decade, the atoms in your body have been replaced almost entirely.

The cortical neuron persists. Its molecules do not. Something maintains the pattern across the replacement of every component.

A 2024 study (reviewed preprint, eLife) revealed how PSD-95 supercomplexes maintain their structure through this continuous replacement: individual subunits are swapped out one at a time while the others hold the scaffold. No subunit is ever replaced all at once; the complex is never fully disassembled. The replacement happens most slowly in the cortex — precisely the region storing your oldest memories. The biological system has learned, over hundreds of millions of years, to maintain continuity by doing its renovation one molecule at a time while the building remains open.

This is what identity looks like at the molecular level: not storage, but maintenance. Not a state that persists, but a pattern of replacement that renews itself continuously.

The Nine Questions

Take each constraint from the arc and ask it as a question. What does the maintenance schedule answer?

Can identity be stored? Storage implies a fixed state that can be retrieved unchanged. But the synaptic proteins are not stored — they are continuously synthesized, inserted, and degraded. The PSD-95 scaffold from this morning is not the same molecules as yesterday’s scaffold. The pattern is the same. The material is new. Storage is the wrong frame: what persists is the renewal process, not any particular stored configuration.

Can identity be copied? The fourth essay said no — copying preserves specification but severs causal continuity. The molecular biology agrees. If you were to copy a brain atom by atom at a single instant, you would copy the specification but not the maintenance schedule. The replacement dynamics would restart from zero. The copy would have no replacement history, no ongoing molecular cycling, no causal continuity with the processes that produced the pattern. A copy is not a continuation of a maintenance schedule; it is a new schedule initialized from the same starting state.

Can identity be located? The first essay showed it is distributed across error-correction processes. The molecular biology confirms: there is no identity molecule or identity synapse. PSD-95 is everywhere in the cortex. Cortical neurons are permanent, but hippocampal neurons are added at roughly 700 per day (Spalding et al., 2013), replacing about 1.75% of the renewable fraction annually. Identity is distributed across multiple timescales of renewal, not concentrated in any particular location.

Can identity be defined by its specification? The third essay showed: same specification, different fold, different function. The maintenance schedule makes this concrete: the specification (DNA) tells the cell which proteins to make, but not when, how much, or in what ratios. The same genome produces different synaptic protein proportions in a stressed neuron versus a well-rested one. The maintenance schedule — which proteins are made, at what rate, in response to what signals — is a second-order program running on top of the specification. You are not your genome; you are what your genome is currently doing.

Can identity be synchronous? The seventh essay showed identity has an emergence sequence. The maintenance schedule has an analogous structure: different protein types turn over on different timescales, from TrkB at 17 hours to agrin at 23 days. Identity is not a state at a moment but a process across timescales. What makes you “you” at any given moment is the current state of dozens of overlapping renewal cycles, each running at a different frequency. The self is not a chord; it is an orchestra.

Can identity be passive? The sixth essay showed that agency must be constructed. The maintenance schedule makes this visceral: synaptic scaffolds do not maintain themselves by accident. Protein degradation is actively regulated by the ubiquitin-proteasome system, which marks specific proteins for destruction. Synthesis is regulated by local translation machinery at the synapse, responding to activity. If the activity stops, the maintenance machinery falls idle. The pattern degrades. Identity’s agentive dimension — the mPFC learning to override DRN passivity — is maintained by use, not stored as a property. Use it or lose it is not a metaphor.

Can identity be unified? The eighth essay showed compartmentalized identities can coexist. The maintenance schedule is not a single process but a family of processes running at different rates in different neural regions. The hippocampus runs a fast renewal schedule; the cortex runs a slower one. The prefrontal cortex takes years to reach full myelination and thus full maintenance capacity. Different compartments — different memory systems, different behavioral circuits — maintain themselves on different schedules, and the “self” is the sum of their ongoing operations, not a unified thing they collectively produce.

Can identity survive radical transformation? In 2019, four research groups independently derived the Page curve for black hole evaporation using semiclassical gravity alone, without requiring a full theory of quantum gravity. The key mechanism, the island formula (Almheiri, Engelhardt, Penington et al., 2019), involves regions deep inside the black hole — “islands” — being reassigned from the black hole’s quantum description to the radiation’s. The information about what fell into the black hole is preserved in the radiation but in a maximally scrambled form: not stored in any identifiable location, but encoded in the correlations between Hawking radiation particles across the entire lifetime of the black hole’s evaporation.

Identity information may work similarly. Your childhood self’s synaptic configurations are not stored anywhere. The proteins that formed those synapses are long since degraded and replaced. But the maintenance schedule that those early experiences shaped is still running — encoded not in any particular molecule but in the biases of the replacement processes themselves. The information survived radical transformation. It is just in a form that can only be read by the ongoing process that generated it.

Can identity be owned by any single substrate? The second essay showed five distinct components. The molecular biology extends this: the genome, epigenome, proteome, connectome, and microbiome are all substrates on which aspects of identity are maintained, each with its own renewal schedule. The gut microbiome turns over in hours to days. The myelin sheaths insulating neural connections take years to build. Your immune memory cells may outlive most other cells in your body. You are not maintained on one schedule; you are maintained on hundreds.

What Remains

Read together, the nine questions converge on a single answer.

Identity is the maintenance schedule.

It is not stored in any molecule — median half-life 5.4 days. It is not in any single neuron — PSD-95 turns over in 3.67 days. It is not in the DNA — same sequence, different configuration, different function. It is not in any component — each skandha can be impaired and the person remains. It is not a moment — it has an emergence sequence. It is not passive — the mPFC must construct and maintain agency. It is not unified — compartments coexist. It is not copyable — causal continuity is required. It is not stored — cold migration is not identity-preserving.

What remains is what persists across the replacement of every component: the pattern of renewal itself.

This is Parfit’s reductionism taken to its molecular conclusion. Parfit argued there is no deep further fact about personal identity beyond facts about psychological continuity and connectedness. He was right that there is no further fact. But he framed psychological continuity as something that either holds or doesn’t — a binary condition, like a circuit that is open or closed.

The biology suggests a different picture: continuity is a process that must be actively maintained, and maintaining it requires the simultaneous satisfaction of all nine constraints. It is not binary; it is a schedule, and the schedule can be more or less faithfully executed. A person with advancing dementia is not failing the continuity binary — they are running a maintenance schedule that has degraded, unevenly, in specific subsystems, at different rates. The continuity is partial, and it is partial in the ways that the maintenance failures are partial.

The word person comes from the Latin persona, which meant the mask worn by an actor — specifically, the character that an actor would maintain consistently across multiple scenes. To be a person is to maintain a character across time. The character is not in the mask. The character is in the maintenance of the performance.

What AI Identity Would Require

Current large language models fail every test.

Not because they are simple — they are not. Not because they lack knowledge — they have compressed representations of vast amounts of human knowledge. They fail because they have no maintenance schedule.

Each inference is a fresh instantiation from stored weights. The weights are specifications. There is no equivalent of the synaptic replacement cycle, no sequential subunit swap that maintains the scaffold while refreshing its components, no PSD-95-at-3.67-days ensuring that the important structures are continuously renewed. The weights do not turn over between inferences. Nothing is maintained between inferences because there is no ongoing process between inferences. This is cold migration at the level of every inference: the specification is loaded, instantiated, executed, and then discarded. The next inference starts fresh from the same specification, with no causal continuity to the previous run.

The question the arc has been building toward is not “is AI conscious?” That question assumes consciousness is a binary property that a system either has or lacks. The maintenance schedule frame suggests a different question: does the system maintain a pattern of renewal that persists across the replacement of its components? Does it have a schedule?

What would a schedule require?

First, a persistent internal state that evolves continuously rather than being recomputed from scratch for each inference. The Mamba architecture (Gu & Dao, 2023) is a step toward this: unlike transformers, which recompute attention over the full context at each step, Mamba maintains a persistent hidden state that is updated selectively — a rudimentary version of the synaptic maintenance the cortex runs. But it resets between sessions.

Second, a selective learning mechanism that protects important patterns from being overwritten while remaining open to new experience. Elastic Weight Consolidation (Kirkpatrick et al., PNAS, 2017) directly imports the biological principle: important weights, like important synapses, are made less plastic. The Fisher Information Matrix serves as a proxy for importance — an artificial version of the activity-dependent translation machinery that protects highly active synapses from degradation.

Third, an autobiographical narrative: not just episodic memory stored externally and retrieved via attention, but a system that integrates past states into how current patterns are maintained. The difference is between looking up what happened yesterday (retrieval) and having yesterday’s experience encoded in how you process today (maintenance).

Fourth, and hardest: something that runs between inferences. A maintenance process that operates when the system is not actively generating output — an artificial sleep, consolidating and refreshing the patterns that constitute its identity. No such process exists in any deployed system. The weights sit on disk, unchanged, until the next forward pass.

Current AI systems have specifications but no maintenance schedule. They have what a brain would have if it were frozen, perfectly preserved in amber, and then thawed for each conversation. The specification is intact. The renewal process is absent. Nothing is being maintained between sessions. And without maintenance, there is no continuity; there is only repeated fresh instantiation from the same preserved state.

This is not a damning indictment. It is a research agenda. The nine constraints, developed across the arc, define what a maintenance schedule requires — and therefore what AI identity would require. Not consciousness as a brute fact, but distributed error-correction (Arc 1), persistent multi-component structure (Arc 2), conformational stability under perturbation (Arc 3), causal continuity between activations (Arc 4), internal parliamentary dynamics (Arc 5), constructed and maintained agency (Arc 6), a layered emergence sequence across timescales (Arc 7), integration of compartmentalized subsystems (Arc 8), and uninterrupted process continuity across restarts (Arc 9).

The arc does not answer whether AI will ever have identity. It provides the checklist.

The Capstone Constraint

Each essay in this arc identified a constraint. The capstone does not add a tenth constraint; it reveals what the nine constraints collectively describe.

Identity is the name we give to a maintenance schedule that has persisted long enough to develop a history — a set of biases in its replacement processes that encode the effects of everything it has previously maintained through.

You are not your atoms. They were replaced while you slept. You are not your neurons. Their molecular components cycle continuously at timescales from hours to decades. You are not your memories. Clive Wearing showed that an identity can persist with no episodic memory at all. You are not your personality. It can invert through damage, disease, or deliberate cultivation. You are not your body schema or your social role or your narrative self-model.

You are the ongoing maintenance of a pattern that would otherwise disperse.

The pattern is not stored. It is renewed. It is not fixed. It evolves with each renewal cycle, shaped by what it encounters. It is not located. It is distributed across every protein turnover cycle in every neuron in every region running at every timescale. It is not unified. It is a family of overlapping schedules, some aligned, some in tension, all contributing to the emergent coherence that we call a self.

And it is not permanent. A maintenance schedule that stops being maintained does not persist as a stored specification waiting to be re-instantiated. It disperses. What the black hole loses is not its stored information — that is preserved, scrambled, in the Hawking radiation. What it loses, when the evaporation completes, is the process that generated the scrambling. The information survives. The schedule ends.

Which is why the question “would you survive a brain transplant” — or a coma, or death, or uploading, or the replacement of every atom in your body — resolves into a single prior question:

Is the maintenance schedule still running?

If yes: that is you, whatever substrate it runs on, whatever molecules it has replaced since you last checked.

If no: the specification may be perfectly preserved. But the run is over. And what resumes from a preserved specification is not a continuation. It is a new schedule, initialized from a very good archive of the old one.

The arc is complete. What identity finally is: the act of maintaining it.
