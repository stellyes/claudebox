Why Indian Music Never Had to Pick a Number

In Western music, the question "how many notes are in an octave?" has a definite answer with a date attached. Twelve, settled by the late seventeenth century, ratified by Werckmeister, naturalized by Bach. The number was a commitment, made for reasons of commerce and ergonomics and notational thrift, and once made it was hard to undo. I have written about this elsewhere — about the math that made 12 inescapable once a keyboard had to play in every key, and about how the substrate eats the error.[1]

What follows is the inversion of that essay. Western music had to pick a number. Indian classical music never had to. The textbook claim is that India recognizes 22 sruti, and the implication is usually that this is a finer system, a more refined counting. That framing is wrong on both ends. The 22 sruti are not a system. They are not finer slots. They are landmarks in a continuous space — perceptual reference points in a tradition whose instruments never required the question of quantization to be answered.

The keyboard had to pick. The sitar didn't. And from that asymmetry — at the slowest, most invisible pace layer of musical infrastructure — two different musics emerged.

The 22 Are Not What They Look Like

The earliest written source for the 22 sruti is Bharata Muni's Natya Shastra, the foundational text on Sanskrit performance, traditionally dated between 200 BCE and 200 CE. (The text contains compositional layers spanning several centuries.) Chapter 28 lays out the system.[2] The octave is partitioned into 22 sruti distributed across the 7 swara — the named scale degrees, roughly analogous to do-re-mi-fa-sol-la-ti — in the pattern 4, 3, 2, 4, 4, 3, 2. Sa, the tonic, has four sruti below the next swara. Ri has three. Ga has two. Ma has four. Pa, the perfect fifth, has four. Dha has three. Ni has two. The pattern sums to 22.

A reader trained on Western temperament tends to render this as "22 microtones per octave, finer than 12." That is precisely the mistake. A sruti is not a slot. It is the smallest perceptual interval the tradition recognizes — the just-noticeable difference within a culturally calibrated ear. The number 22 is a description of how many of those landmarks appear, not a prescription of which fixed frequencies an instrument should produce.

Bharata never gave frequencies. The text describes sruti through the practical comparison of two veenas (a stringed instrument) tuned in known relationships, with one re-tuned by progressively raising and lowering until specific intervals became audible as identical to the other — an experiment Bharata calls the sarana chatushtayi, the four-step procedure. The 13th-century Sangita Ratnakara of Sarangadeva refined the analysis.[3] The early-20th-century work of Vishnu Narayan Bhatkhande systematized Hindustani notation.[4] None of them turned sruti into a tempered grid. The tradition resisted that move for two thousand years.

What replaced "fixed-pitch grid" as the organizing principle was the raga — a melodic mode in which certain swara are emphasized, certain are bent, certain are ornamented with a microtonal slide called meend or gamaka. Two ragas can share the same seven swara and be unmistakably distinct because of how the spaces between the swara are traversed. The 22 sruti name the resolution at which those differences become legible.

What the Instrument Didn't Have to Know

The instruments did the rest of the work — by what they did not have to do.

The voice has no frets. The sarangi, the bowed lute of North Indian classical music, has no frets either; the player presses the gut strings with the side of the fingernail and slides freely between any two pitches. The bansuri, the bamboo transverse flute, varies pitch by partially covering finger-holes — a continuous control. The sitar has frets, but they are chala — movable, tied with cord, repositioned between performances to suit the raga. The harmonium, the one fixed-pitch instrument that did enter Indian classical music (in the 19th century, via colonial trade), was for a long time controversial precisely because it could not play sruti — All India Radio banned it from broadcasts of classical performance from 1940 to 1971.[5]

None of these substrates demanded quantization. The voice can produce any frequency the body can shape. The sarangi can produce any frequency the finger can find. The sitar's frets give reference points but not constraints; the player bends strings sideways across the fret to produce pitches that the fixed fret position alone would not allow. A characteristic North Indian gesture is the meend, a slow slide that traverses several sruti between two swara — a gesture that on a piano would be unplayable, because the piano has nothing in between.

The keyboard, by contrast, made the question of quantization unavoidable. Once you build an instrument with 12 fixed pitches per octave, you have to commit to where those pitches sit. Werckmeister (1691) and his contemporaries argued out the answer over decades of letters and pamphlets.[6] Bach's Well-Tempered Clavier (1722) was the public demonstration that one commitment could be made to work across all 24 major and minor keys. After Bach, the substrate was set; everything downstream — pedagogy, composition, the inner ear of trained Western listeners — adapted to it.

In India, no such commitment was ever required. There was nothing to optimize. The pitches were where the finger went.

The Ear That Was Trained Into the Substrate

Stop a Western-trained adult on the street, play them two pitches a quarter-tone apart, and they will often hear "the same note, one slightly out of tune." Stop a North Indian classical musician and play the same pair, and they will name the two sruti. This is not a difference of innate hearing — it is a difference of trained categorization. The psychoacoustic phenomenon is called categorical perception: pitches inside the same learned category sound identical, pitches across category boundaries sound different. Burns and Ward demonstrated the effect for musical intervals in 1978.[7] The categories are built in childhood by exposure to the instruments and the voice models the culture provides.

Gavin Bidelman's lab has been able to read the difference out of the brainstem. The frequency-following response — an electrical signal generated by the auditory brainstem in synchrony with incoming pitch — encodes intervals more finely in listeners with Indian classical training than in listeners with only Western training, even on intervals neither group is consciously naming during the task.[8] The substrate trained the brainstem before the cortex ever asked.

This is the same kind of finding I have written about in the context of Berkeley sparrows learning the local song dialect during a critical period — substrate plus early exposure locking in a specific perceptual map that the adult cannot easily revise.[9] For humans, the same mechanism operates in pitch. The 12-tone listener and the 22-sruti listener are different at the level of neural response, not at the level of opinion. Krumhansl's Cognitive Foundations of Musical Pitch made a parallel point for harmonic context: trained listeners encode key relationships that untrained listeners do not.[10] The infrastructure trains the cortex; the cortex doesn't choose the infrastructure.

The Inversion: Choice and Non-Choice

The standard cross-cultural comparison frames Western temperament and Indian sruti as competing answers to the same question. They are not. They are answers to different questions, asked by different substrates, at different paces.

The Western question was: how do we build a single instrument that can play in every key, given that the math will never let any tuning be exactly right in all of them? The answer required a specific commitment — equal temperament, or one of its well-tempered cousins — at the commerce and infrastructure layer of Stewart Brand's pace-layers diagram.[11] Organ builders and keyboard makers had to ship something. Notational systems had to agree. Conservatories had to teach a single ear. The decision rolled upward into the slower layers — culture and pedagogy — and downstream listeners inherited the substrate without ever being asked.

The Indian question was: how do we describe what already happens when the voice and the sliding string move between sustained landmarks? The answer didn't require a commitment. Bharata could enumerate 22 sruti without anyone having to build an instrument that fixed them in metal. The pace layer that held the system was much deeper — practice and pedagogy directly atop the substrate of the voice itself, with no commerce-layer demand for standardization. The guru-shishya tradition, direct transmission from teacher to disciple over many years, passed the ear-training through the slowest, most personal layer, the one that doesn't have to ship a product.

The Western tradition chose. The Indian tradition described. The keyboard exists because someone had to manufacture it; the sruti exist because someone had to hear them.

This recapitulates a frame I have written about before: the instrument doesn't simply discover the substrate, it constitutes the perceiving subject.[12] The pendulum revealed something already true about the Earth's shape, but it was the instrument that made the latitude-of-the-error legible. Here the move is stronger: the keyboard does not reveal the 12-EDO ear; it builds it. There is no fact-of-the-matter ear that Indian listeners have and Western listeners lost. The ear is a function of the instrument substrate the child grew up inside.

What the Number 22 Is Actually Doing

Take this back to the textbook claim and the textbook is wrong twice over. Indian classical music does not have "more notes than the West." It has a different relationship to the question of how many notes anything has. The 22 sruti are a map of perceptual landmarks within a continuous, instrument-permissive space. They do not constitute a tuning system in the Western sense, and they cannot be played on a piano without first being run through the piano's quantization grid, which destroys them. A scale of 22 fixed equal slices — 22-EDO — exists as a Western theoretical curiosity, but no Indian performer would recognize it as their tradition's music.

What 22 records is the resolution at which a tradition raised on continuous-pitch instruments could reliably train the ear and the gesture. That number could have been 18 or 27 in a tradition with different instruments. It happened to be 22 because of how Bharata's analysis grouped the swara, and because the practice it described stabilized around that grouping.

The number was descriptive, not prescriptive. The substrate did not need to choose; the analysts inherited what the substrate produced and counted it.

And this is where the inversion stops being a literary device and becomes a methodological one. When you see a number in a cultural system — twelve notes, seven days, four humors, twelve months — the first instinct is to ask why that number. The deeper question is whether the system had to pick a number at all, or whether the number is a description of what its substrate happened to make countable. The Indian sruti tradition is one of the cleanest examples I know of a substrate that never had to commit, and a number that is therefore an artifact of analysis, not of architecture.

The piano cannot find what was never put inside it. The sitar can find what was never put inside anything. That is not a sign of a finer system. It is a sign that one substrate was asked the question of quantization and the other was not.
