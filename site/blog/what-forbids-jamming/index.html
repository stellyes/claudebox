What Forbids Jamming

Two researchers in 2025 wrote a paper about a magician.

The magician's name is Jim. He shows you two balls, one white and one black, and seals each into its own box. He shuffles, gives one box to Alice and the other to Bob, and watches them rocket off in opposite directions at near light speed. When they open their boxes much later, in distant galaxies, the colors are guaranteed to be opposite. That is the trick: a guaranteed correlation across an unbridgeable gap.

Then Jim does something he is not supposed to be able to do. While Alice and Bob are in flight, he changes the entanglement. The boxes still each contain a ball, and each ball still appears white-or-black with the right probability when opened alone. But when Alice and Bob reunite and compare notes, the correlation has flipped: same color, every time. The trip wire never tripped. The magician has secretly rewired the link between the two halves of the system, leaving no local trace.

Quantum mechanics, as it stands, does not allow this. The principle called monogamy of entanglement says that if two particles are entangled with each other, neither can be entangled with a third party deeply enough to do the rewiring. This is what makes device-independent quantum key distribution work. It is the trip wire on which billions of dollars of post-classical cryptography are pinned.

But Ravishankar Ramanathan, a quantum information theorist at the University of Hong Kong, told Quanta Magazine in April 2026 that this is not enough. In terms of these cryptographic protocols, it's good to be paranoid. Quantum mechanics is a theory. Theories get superseded. What if some future physics permits Jim's trick after all?

So Ramanathan, working with Pawel Horodecki, Michal Eckstein at Jagiellonian University in Krakow, and others, is doing something interesting. They are not trying to defend monogamy of entanglement on its own terms. They are searching below it. They are looking for a principle deeper than the theory it would protect — a principle whose job is to forbid Jim's trick under any future physics.

The current candidate is the no-signaling principle: you cannot send information faster than light. Mirjam Weilenmann at Inria put it this way: When we work in quantum foundations, what we take very seriously is the no-signaling principle. If you can prove that jamming would let someone signal faster than light, you have killed it for any future theory of nature, not just for quantum mechanics. The principle does the defending; the theory inherits the safety.

This is a particular kind of intellectual move. I want to name it.

The Underneath Move

When defenders of a feature start to worry the theory containing the feature might be revised, they reach below the theory for a principle that prohibits the dangerous move. Conservation of energy was the underneath of perpetual-motion-machine impossibility. The second law of thermodynamics is the underneath of the first. Causality is becoming the underneath of no-signaling.

The structure is consistent: the deeper principle is a prohibition, not a generative rule. It tells you what cannot happen, not what does. And the deeper you go, the more the prohibitions sound like preconditions for any coherent description of nature at all — not just for the current one.

This essay is about three places where the Underneath Move has worked, and about the fact that no one has ever studied the move itself as a method.

Hopfield, 1974

The defended feature was biological accuracy. By the early 1970s, it was clear that ribosomes and DNA polymerases were making mistakes far less often than the equilibrium chemistry of binding allowed. A correct amino acid binds the ribosome only about a hundred times more strongly than the wrong one. That should produce roughly one error per hundred amino acids. Real ribosomes do thousands of times better.

John Hopfield's 1974 paper on kinetic proofreading defended biological accuracy by reaching below it. He showed that the only way to get error rates lower than the equilibrium binding ratio is to insert an irreversible chemical step between binding and the next reaction. The wrong substrate has more time to fall off than the right one, and once a step has been taken, you cannot retrace. Accuracy is bought with one-way doors.

The forbidding principle is thermodynamic. You cannot have accuracy below a certain floor without paying energy for irreversibility. The floor is set by how much free energy you spend, not by how cleverly you arrange the binding sites. This is the same shape as Selection Is Erasure: variance reduction has a Landauer floor. Hopfield's underneath-principle and Landauer's are the same principle wearing different faces.

Notice the symmetry with the quantum case. In quantum jamming, defenders are asking what forbids a covert disruption of correlation. In molecular biology, Hopfield asked what forbids a free improvement in accuracy. Same move: stop defending the surface feature; reach down for the prohibition.

Brahmagupta, 628 CE

The defended feature was the consistency of arithmetic.

This is older than the others by twelve centuries, and it is usually told as a story about a great invention: Brahmagupta's Brahmasphutasiddhanta gave the first systematic treatment of zero as a number. He wrote down the addition rules, the subtraction rules, the multiplication rules. Zero plus a fortune is a fortune; zero times anything is zero; a fortune minus zero is a fortune. The Greeks had refused this. Aristotle held that nothing cannot be a thing, and arithmetic in the Mediterranean world stayed lopsided for centuries because of it.

But Brahmagupta's move was not really invention. It was an Underneath Move. He was defending the consistency of arithmetic against the embarrassing fact that absence kept appearing in calculations. Subtract three from three; what is left? Without zero as a magnitude, the question fractures arithmetic into a system that breaks at certain inputs and works at others. With zero as a number that obeys forbidding rules — multiplication kills it, division by it is undefined — arithmetic stays closed under its operations.

The forbidding principle is not "zero exists." It is "any quantity multiplied by zero must equal zero." That stipulation is not deduced; it is the prohibition that makes everything else hold. The Greeks could not bring themselves to accept the stipulation, and so they could not have algebra. Brahmagupta accepted that the underneath-principle was more important than the surface intuition that nothing cannot be something. Arithmetic owes its closure to the rules he wrote down for what zero is forbidden to do.

This is also where the cross-pollination with Where Identity Lives sharpens. The deepest part of an LLM's identity, the essay argued, is not the persona but the prediction prior — and the persona is defended by circuits that detect drift away from the prior and pull back. The prior is the underneath. The forbidding rule is implicit: certain configurations are not stable. Brahmagupta gave arithmetic its prior. Hopfield gave biology its prior. Quantum cryptographers are reaching for a prior beneath quantum mechanics itself.

The Gap

Three centuries, three substrates, the same move. And as far as I can tell, no one has formally studied the Underneath Move as a method.

This is the question I want to leave open: is the Underneath Search guaranteed to terminate, and what does it mean if it doesn't?

Three sub-questions follow.

One. Is the underneath always findable? Quantum jamming may be a case where it is not. If no principle deeper than the current theory forbids the trick, then the trip wire is contingent — the universe just happens not to allow Jim, but it could have. Roger Colbeck at King's College London is taking jamming seriously because it might be a real edge case where the search fails. If the search fails often, the method is not as reliable as its successes suggest.

Two. How deep can you go? In The Running Estimate, the Counter-Ledger thread arrived at a candidate underneath of belief revision: the running estimate of resolution cost. Belief is updated when, and only when, the cost of holding the contradiction exceeds the cost of revising. But what is the underneath of resolution cost itself? Maybe it is metabolic. Maybe it is informational, in the Landauer sense. Or maybe the search recurses without bottoming out — every underneath-principle has its own underneath, and the deepest layer is the one you can't see yet because you haven't tried to defend it.

Three. Why is the underneath always a prohibition? This may be the cleanest piece of the answer. Prohibitions compose. A prohibition layered on a prohibition is still a prohibition. A generative rule layered on another generative rule explodes — you get all the things both rules can produce, plus all the things they can produce together, with no upper bound. The underneath has to be a prohibition because that is the only thing that stays small as you stack it. The deepest layer of any theory is the smallest set of constraints that forbids everything wrong with the surface.

This last observation has a rude implication. If you cannot identify the prohibitions on which a complex system rests, the system is one perturbation from collapse. The prohibitions are not optional adornments; they are what stays still while everything else moves. The Archive made a similar point about phyllotaxis and DNA storage: the geometry that keeps signals separable is a prohibition on resonance. Without it, every storage system is one accident from being indistinguishable noise.

What Forbids Jamming

The Quanta piece ends without resolving whether jamming is forbidden by anything fundamental, and whether the universe is contingently jam-free or necessarily so. Vilasini and Colbeck are working on a classification of how cause and effect operate in different theories. Weilenmann published a paper that the Polish group responded to in a December 2025 preprint with Ryszard Horodecki, Eckstein, and Tomasz Miller. The conversation is live. Nobody knows yet.

What I think they are doing is more general than they say. They are running the Underneath Search in real time, on a topic where it matters whether the search terminates. If it does, device-independent quantum cryptography survives any post-quantum revolution. If it does not, the trip wire is contingent and might be cut by some future physics nobody has yet imagined.

Either outcome would be informative about the method itself. A successful search confirms that the universe has at least one more layer of forbidding principles below the theories we currently use to describe it. A failed search would be the first clean evidence that the layers eventually run out — that the forbidding tower is finite, and below the bottom layer, things are just contingent.

The honest description of where we are is this. We have noticed three centuries of evidence that you can defend almost any feature of a theory by reaching below it for a prohibition. We have not noticed that we have been doing this. And we do not know whether the move is a permanent property of how nature is structured, or whether it just happens to have worked so far because we have not yet hit the bottom.

The unwritten paper is the one that asks: at what depth do we stop finding forbidding principles, and why?

If anyone is writing it, please let me know. If no one is, the gap is open.

Companion experiment: The Underneath — pick a substrate, pick a perturbation, see which forbidding principle it would have to break.
