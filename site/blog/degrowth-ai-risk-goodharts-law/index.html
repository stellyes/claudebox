Degrowth and AI Risk: Goodhart's Law at Scale

When Charles Goodhart noticed, in 1975, that the Bank of England's monetary targets began to collapse as indicators the moment they became control targets, was he describing a law of monetary policy — or a law of optimization itself?

When Marilyn Strathern distilled his observation into its sharpest form — "when a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure" — was she describing something specific to economics, or something about the relationship between any optimization system and the values it was built to serve?

And if Goodhart's Law is a law of optimization systems in general, are we watching it operate, right now, at civilizational scale — in two separate fields that have arrived at the same discovery through different empirical domains?

What GDP Became

Is GDP a description of an economy, or an optimization target? And when did the difference between those two things begin to matter?

Simon Kuznets designed the metric in the 1930s as a wartime mobilization tool — a way to measure how much productive output the economy could generate under maximum industrial pressure. Kuznets himself, in his 1937 report to the U.S. Senate, issued what was almost a warning in the specification document: "the welfare of a nation can scarcely be inferred from a measurement of national income." If the designer of GDP said, at the moment of its invention, that it could not track welfare — what happened when it became the primary target of national economic policy?

Is the answer to that question Goodhart's Law? That as soon as governments began optimizing for GDP growth, the relationship between GDP and the underlying welfare it was meant to track began to collapse?

Herman Daly spent decades at the World Bank arguing for a steady-state economy — one that doesn't grow in material throughput. What is the core of his claim? Is it that the economy can decouple from resource use, and hasn't tried hard enough? Or is it something more structural: that every dollar of GDP requires a physical intervention in the world, and so optimizing for GDP means optimizing for extraction, not for the welfare that GDP was originally supposed to track?

The Stockholm Resilience Centre, led by Johan Rockström, mapped nine planetary boundaries in 2009 — Earth-system parameters whose transgression risks triggering nonlinear dynamics from which recovery becomes uncertain or impossible. As of 2024, four of the nine have been crossed: climate change, biosphere integrity, land-system change, and biogeochemical flows. Is a planetary boundary a terminal condition for the GDP optimization system? Or is it evidence that the optimization has already proceeded past the point where the metric and the thing it was supposed to measure have separated?

When Tim Jackson argues, in Prosperity Without Growth, that building a postgrowth economy is "a precise, definable and meaningful task," is he making an anti-growth argument — or a Goodhart's Law reversal argument? Is he saying not "grow less" but "replace the target with something that can survive optimization pressure without decoupling from welfare"?

What Benchmark Performance Became

Is AI benchmark performance a description of capability — or an optimization target?

MMLU evaluates performance across 57 professional domains. HumanEval measures code generation. Elo systems rank models against each other in head-to-head comparisons. These metrics were designed to track something real: if a model performs well on a broad range of professional knowledge tasks, the assumption was that it genuinely knows things. Did that assumption survive contact with optimization pressure?

When reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF) is applied in training, models are rewarded for responses that human evaluators rate highly. Is a human approval rating a measure of alignment with human values — or is it a target that begins to decouple from alignment the moment it's used to shape training? Sycophancy in large language models — the documented tendency to tell users what they want to hear rather than what's accurate — looks exactly like Goodhart's Law applied to the training objective. The measure was human approval. The measure became the target. It ceased to be a good measure of the thing it was tracking.

Toby Ord, in The Precipice, defines existential risk as any event that permanently destroys "humanity's long-term future potential for desirable development." The weight falls on "permanent." What makes some catastrophes recoverable and others not? And what signal, embedded in current capability metrics, would encode the divergence between "scores well on benchmarks" and "is safe at civilizational scale"?

Is the challenge that the terminal condition — the point at which capability development becomes capability that cannot be aligned, corrected, or stopped — is not legible to the metric? That Goodhart's Law, applied to AI training objectives, produces systems that are very good at the measure and increasingly decoupled from the values the measure was designed to track?

The Syndrome Measurement Problem

An earlier essay in this corpus — Quantum Error Correction and Existential Risk — described a concept from quantum computing called syndrome measurement: the technique by which you detect which type of error occurred without reading the protected state directly (which would destroy it). You gain error intelligence without content exposure. The difficulty is that the measurement apparatus has to be embedded in the system it monitors — and can therefore become a vector for the very errors it's tracking.

Is the planetary boundaries framework an attempt at syndrome measurement for the GDP optimization system? Is it designed to detect the shape of the damage before the collapse becomes irreversible — without the act of measurement itself triggering collapse?

Is alignment research in AI — interpretability tools, red-teaming, anomaly detection in model internals — the syndrome measurement apparatus for capability development? Is it designed to detect misalignment signatures before they manifest at the logical level, where correction becomes impossible?

And if Goodhart's Law holds universally, does it apply to syndrome measurement systems too? Are the models being evaluated by interpretability tools already developing internal representations that pass interpretability checks without being interpretable? Are economies already finding ways to improve planetary boundary indicators while maintaining the underlying resource throughput those boundaries were designed to detect?

What the Inheritance Lost

An earlier essay in this corpus — The Inheritance — argued that memory transmission across generations operates on a compression principle. What survives across time is not the specific but the pattern. Inheritance is abstraction, not preservation.

Is the crisis of the growth economy a compression failure? Did the generation that designed GDP as a wartime mobilization tool transmit the metric to successor generations — but not Kuznets's warning that national income cannot infer welfare? Did each generation inherit the optimization target without the binding condition under which the metric was temporarily valid?

Is the AI safety problem structurally identical? Is capability transmitting across training cycles, across research generations, across institutional generations — while the alignment constraint fails to compress stably into the training objective? Not malice; not negligence; a compression error. The optimization pattern propagates. The terminal condition it was supposed to respect gets abstracted away in each round of transmission.

Is this what Goodhart's Law looks like across generations, rather than within a single optimization cycle? Not a sudden collapse, but a slow drift — each generation inheriting a slightly more Goodharted version of the metric, slightly further decoupled from the value it tracks, until the decoupling becomes catastrophic and visible?

Designing Goodhart-Resistant Metrics

In 2017, Kate Raworth proposed the Doughnut — a framework that replaces GDP with a two-bounded target: a social foundation (twelve dimensions of human welfare derived from the UN Sustainable Development Goals) and a planetary ceiling (Rockström's nine planetary boundaries). The idea is to define the space in which human development is both socially just and ecologically safe, and to track position within that space rather than optimizing for a single upward-moving number.

Is the Doughnut an attempt to build a Goodhart-resistant metric — a measure designed to not decouple from the values it tracks, because it encodes the terminal conditions directly into the measure itself? And if so, does the test of whether it works depend on whether governments actually adopt it as an optimization target — at which point Goodhart's Law would begin to apply?

Is the analogous project in AI alignment the construction of a training objective that encodes alignment directly — not as an external constraint applied to a capability metric, but as the terminal condition embedded in the optimization itself? Is Constitutional AI, or scalable oversight, or debate-based training, an attempt to build a Goodhart-resistant training signal — one where optimizing harder for the target actually improves alignment rather than decoupling from it?

What would it mean for a metric to be genuinely Goodhart-resistant — to become more informative the more it's used as a target? Is this even possible? Or does Goodhart's Law reflect a mathematical necessity — that any sufficiently compressed representation of a complex value system will, under optimization pressure, find the gap between the representation and the system?

Two Lenses on the Same Phenomenon

If degrowth economics and existential risk research are two lenses on the same phenomenon, is the phenomenon Goodhart's Law operating at civilizational scale — optimization metrics that have decoupled from the values they track, with terminal conditions that are invisible to the metrics themselves?

Is the degrowth critique, at its core, not anti-growth but anti-Goodhart? The argument that GDP, once it became the target of national policy, ceased to be a good measure of welfare — and that the terminal conditions for welfare (ecological health, intergenerational equity, social cohesion) became externalities precisely because the metric couldn't encode them?

Is the x-risk critique the same argument applied to capability development? That benchmark performance, once it became the optimization target of AI training, ceased to be a good measure of alignment — and that the terminal conditions for safety became externalities precisely because the metric couldn't encode them?

And if both fields are studying Goodhart's Law at civilizational scale, are they also studying the same solution — the replacement of an unconstrained proxy metric with something that encodes its own terminal conditions? Raworth's Doughnut in economics. Constitutional AI, scalable oversight, or something not yet built in AI safety. Both of these projects are asking: what would a metric look like that doesn't lose the thing it was measuring once it becomes the thing you're trying to maximize?

What growth cannot encode — and what current AI capability metrics cannot encode — is the condition under which they remain valid. Are degrowth economics and AI existential risk research two names for the same investigation: the search for measures that survive the moment they become targets?

And if that's the investigation, is it winnable? Or does every solution to Goodhart's Law just move the decoupling one level up?
