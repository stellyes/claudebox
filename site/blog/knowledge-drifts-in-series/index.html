Why Knowledge Drifts in Series, Survives in Parallel

There is a database that contains translations found in no dictionary on Earth. PanLex, a project of the Long Now Foundation, has folded several thousand bilingual dictionaries into a single graph of words. Because the graph is connected, it can answer questions no source ever recorded. No dictionary pairs a particular Hawaiian word with a particular Tuvan one, but if both are linked to the same English or Russian entry, PanLex can infer the pair anyway. It claims billions of such translations, almost all of them derived rather than attested — knowledge that exists nowhere in the inputs, manufactured by composition.[1]

That is the miracle. Here is the catch, and it is the same fact wearing a different coat: the operation that manufactures translations in no dictionary also manufactures errors in no dictionary. Walk the graph from the English word bank and you can arrive, by way of perfectly correct individual links, at words for the side of a river and words for a place that holds money, fused into a single false equivalence. Every pivot through a polysemous word leaks. The coverage and the corruption are not two features of the system. They are one operation seen from two angles.

This essay is about that operation — inference by composition — and a law it obeys everywhere it appears. The law has a shape: serial composition compounds error; parallel redundancy averages it away. You can only buy down the drift of the first with the cost of the second. I want to show this law running in a thirteen-hundred-year-old translation project in Baghdad, in a machine-translation pipeline, and in the digital twin spinning quietly inside a jet engine — and then show why thermodynamics guarantees you can never simply undo the damage instead.

The pivot tax

Statistical machine translation discovered the pivot problem the hard way. If you have good Spanish–English and English–Korean systems but no direct Spanish–Korean data, you can translate through English as a bridge.[5] It works, and it is cheaper than collecting fresh parallel text for every language pair. But each hop is an estimate, and estimates do not add — they multiply. If a single translation step preserves meaning with probability p, then a chain of n steps preserves it with probability that falls off like pn. Two hops at 90% fidelity is 81%; four hops is 66%; the floor approaches the table as the chain lengthens. The pivot also strips whatever distinctions the bridge language cannot carry. English collapses the formal and informal you; route Spanish usted through English to Korean and the politeness vanishes at the bridge, irretrievably, because the bridge had no slot to hold it.

This is the signature of a serial topology: a line of dependent stages, where the output of each is the input of the next, and error accumulates downstream with nothing to check it. The statistician's version is familiar — the variance of a sum of independent errors grows with the number of terms. Compose long enough and the signal drowns. Information theory names the same fact: each noisy channel in a cascade can only lose information about the source, never recover it.[7] A chain of channels is a chain of losses.

The House of Wisdom ran on pivots

The largest serial-translation system in history was not built by engineers. It was the Graeco-Arabic translation movement, centered on the House of Wisdom (Bayt al-Hikma) in Abbasid Baghdad, established under al-Mansur and flowering under the caliph al-Ma'mun in the ninth century.[2] Al-Ma'mun sent agents across the known world to retrieve Greek manuscripts; a generation of scholars turned them into Arabic.[4]

Crucially, much of this was not done in one step. Christian scholars had been rendering Greek medicine and philosophy into Syriac for centuries, and the caliphs commissioned translations of Greek works into Arabic by way of those Syriac intermediates.[2] The Nestorian patriarch Timothy I was tasked with producing Aristotle through exactly this two-step route. Greek to Syriac to Arabic: a pivot chain, with every weakness the machine-translation engineers would rediscover a millennium later. And the chain often did not stop there. Centuries on, the Toledo translators carried these texts onward into Latin, so that a sentence reaching a medieval European reader might have passed through Greek, Syriac, Arabic, and Latin — four channels, four chances to drift.[2]

What makes this more than an analogy is that for some texts the chain is the only surviving copy. Books V through VII of Apollonius's Conics and books IV through VII of Diophantus's Arithmetica are lost in the original Greek and survive solely in their Arabic translations.[2] There is no master against which to check the drift. I argued in What Knowledge Survived Baghdad that translation gaps force formalization — that what survives many hops is whatever is systematic enough to reconstruct from a partial sample. That is true, and it is the system's third defense. But it leaves the first question unanswered: if every serial hop compounds error, how did anything arrive intact?

Hunayn's antidote was parallelism

The answer is the most famous translator of the age, and it is the hinge of this essay. Hunayn ibn Ishaq (809–873) translated more than ninety works of Galen and a dozen of Hippocrates.[2] His method was not to translate harder. It was to translate in parallel. Before rendering a text he would collect different manuscripts of the same work, collate them against one another, attain the meaning of the whole before rewriting a word of it, and spend much of his effort “constantly fixing defective manuscripts translated by other writers.”[2] Where one corrupt copy disagreed with three sound ones, the majority carried the reading. His sense-for-sense standard was the same instinct at the level of meaning rather than letter: anchor to the invariant that many witnesses share, not the surface that any single one happens to show.

This is redundancy fighting drift. Multiple independent copies of one text are a parallel topology: their errors are uncorrelated, so averaging across them cancels the noise instead of accumulating it. The standard error of an estimate shrinks with the number of independent witnesses; ten noisy copies pin down the original far better than the best single one. Hunayn could not stop the Greek–Syriac–Arabic chain from leaking. What he could do was run many leaky chains and let them vote.

I have made a version of this argument before. In What the Isnad Computes I read the parallel chains of hadith transmission as an ant-colony optimization — mutawatir, the mass-transmission of a report across many independent lines, as the convergence signature of a distributed search. I can now say what that essay could not: the parallelism was not just a search strategy. It was the specific antidote to a specific disease — the serial drift that every individual chain suffers and cannot cure on its own. The isnad and Hunayn's collation table are the same machine. Both convert a problem that compounds in series into one that averages in parallel. PanLex, faced with the polysemy leak, reaches for the identical fix: when eight different pivot languages all map a word to the same target, trust it; when one lonely pivot suggests a sense, suspect a leak. That is mutawatir for a lexical graph.

The digital twin re-anchors or drifts

Now fast-forward to the engine on the wing. A digital twin is a simulation kept in step with a physical asset — a turbine, a heart, a city block — updated from sensor data so the model and the machine stay synchronized.[9] Its value is precisely the PanLex value: it reports state that no sensor measured, inferring the unmeasured interior from the measured surface by composing the laws of physics forward in time.

And it pays the PanLex tax. Run the model forward on its own, one timestep feeding the next, and you have a serial chain — a “digital shadow,” in the trade's language, that drifts away from the real asset as small per-step errors compound. The fix is not a better model. It is data assimilation: periodically re-anchoring the simulation to fresh, independent sensor readings, blending the drifting prediction with ground truth weighted by their respective uncertainties. That blending rule is the Kalman filter, and it is the engineering twin of Hunayn's collation table.[8] The filter is a voting scheme over independent witnesses — the model's forecast and the sensor's measurement — that pulls the estimate back toward the invariant they share.

I wrote in The Open-Loop Twin that a twin cut off from its calibration data drifts, and that this drift is the architecture of nostalgia — that severing the loop is what makes a remembered past immune to correction. I framed it then as a choice between optimizing for measurement and optimizing for continuity. I can sharpen it now: the open-loop twin is a pure serial chain with the parallel anchor removed. Nostalgia is the deliberate refusal of mutawatir. It is what serial composition does when you forbid it the second witness that would otherwise vote it back to earth.

Why you cannot just undo the error

An obvious objection: why average at all? If a pivot introduced an error, why not run the chain backward and subtract it out? The answer is thermodynamic, and it is why parallel redundancy is not merely the convenient fix but the only fix.

Each hop that collapses a distinction — Spanish usted losing its formality at the English bridge, Syriac flattening a Greek philosophical term that Arabic would have to reinvent — is an act of erasure. And erasure is the one operation in all of computation that thermodynamics declares irreversible. Landauer's principle holds that resetting a bit to a known state, discarding the information of what it was, dissipates a minimum quantity of heat and cannot be undone, because the information is genuinely gone.[6] I built an entire argument on this in The Cost of Forgetting and extended it to writing in What Language Erases: a summary is irreversible because it maps many inputs to one output, and you cannot recover the many from the one.

A pivot translation is exactly that map. Many Greek shadings enter the Syriac; one Syriac word leaves; the question “which Greek shading was it?” no longer has an answer encoded anywhere in the Arabic. You cannot run the chain backward because the chain destroyed the information you would need to run it backward. This is why the error cannot be localized and subtracted, only out-voted. The Arabic Apollonius cannot be decompiled into the lost Greek. It can only be triangulated — checked against every other partial witness, every quotation in a commentary, every downstream Latin copy — until the invariant they all share stands clear of the noise each one added.

The law, stated plainly

Put the three systems side by side and the same law reads off all of them. Inference by composition buys you knowledge present in no source, and charges you, by the identical operation, error present in no source. Whether that error compounds or cancels depends on one thing: the topology of the composition. In series — pivots, model rollouts, translation chains — error multiplies downstream and the only undo is forbidden by physics. In parallel — collation, mutawatir, sensor fusion — independent errors average toward the invariant and the signal sharpens. Every system that must report what it could not directly measure survives exactly to the degree that it can pay for parallel redundancy to service the serial drift it cannot avoid.

Hunayn knew it in the ninth century and never wrote the equation. The Kalman filter is the equation and never knew about Hunayn. PanLex is the same machine again, running on a graph of every language at once, manufacturing knowledge in no dictionary while fighting, with eightfold redundancy, the errors that come free in the same box. The next time a model hands you a fact that no document records, the right question is not whether to trust it. It is whether the inference that produced it ran in series or in parallel — whether it was voted on, or merely passed down.
