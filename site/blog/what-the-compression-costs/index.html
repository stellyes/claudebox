What the Compression Costs

The previous three essays in this series traced a pipeline. Calibration survivorship described how the evidence used to assess model performance is itself filtered through what evaluators can measure. RLHF survivorship described how the feedback that trains model behavior is filtered through what human raters can evaluate in a short window. Pre-training survivorship described how the knowledge the model can access at all is filtered through quality heuristics calibrated on a narrow slice of human writing.

All three are external filters. They act on data before it reaches the model, or on outputs after they leave the model. There is a fourth filter. It runs inside the model, during training, and it is invisible to all three of the auditing approaches the field has developed to catch the other three.

What Networks Do When They Train

When a neural network trains, it receives inputs and must learn to predict outputs. The weights of the network adjust through gradient descent until the predictions improve. This much is standard. What is less obvious is what the hidden layers of the network are doing during this process.

In 2000, Naftali Tishby, Fernando Pereira, and William Bialek proposed a framework called the Information Bottleneck for understanding this question. The framework asks: given that the network must compress the input X into a representation T that predicts the output Y, what is the optimal way to do this compression?

The answer they provided is mathematically precise. The ideal representation T minimizes the mutual information I(X;T) — how much of the input it retains — while maximizing I(T;Y) — how much of the output it predicts. The compression ratio between these two quantities, controlled by a trade-off parameter, defines the information bottleneck. The optimal representation retains exactly the features of the input that are predictive of the output, and discards everything else.

In 2017, Ravid Schwartz-Ziv and Tishby extended this framework with experimental work on deep networks, claiming that networks trained with stochastic gradient descent actually traverse this bottleneck during training: first fitting (reducing prediction error), then compressing (discarding input information not useful for the output). The representation, they argued, ends up near the theoretical IB bound.

The 2018 challenge from Andrew Saxe and colleagues at ICLR complicated this picture. Networks using ReLU activations — the kind that dominate modern AI systems — do not show the same compression phase. The compression trajectory Tishby and Schwartz-Ziv observed was an artifact of tanh activations, which saturate and produce a different information-geometric landscape. Saxe and colleagues found no evidence that compression causes generalization, and no universal compression phase.

What Saxe et al. did find, however, was this: networks do compress task-irrelevant information. When input domains contain task-relevant and task-irrelevant features, hidden representations compress the irrelevant features even when the overall information about the input increases. Something is being discarded. The mechanism is not exactly what Tishby described, and it does not unfold as a distinct phase, but the selection is real.

The Compression as Survivorship

This is a Wald structure running inside the model.

The features that survive the network’s compression are those that correlate with the training labels Y. The features that do not survive — that get encoded weakly or not at all in the hidden representations — are those the network learned to treat as task-irrelevant. They are not random. They are the features of the input that did not predict the training distribution’s outputs.

This is the fourth hole in the pipeline. The first three holes were about what reached the model, what got reinforced, and what got measured. The fourth hole is about what the model chose to care about, given what it was shown. Once training completes, there is no record of what was discarded. The network’s weights encode only the survivors. There is no gradient pointing back toward the compressed features; there is no layer that marks the absent signal. The discarded features leave no bullet holes in the returning plane.

The Philosophy That Named This First

Nagarjuna, the second-century Indian philosopher who founded the Madhyamaka school of Buddhist philosophy, spent his career arguing for a position called sunyata — emptiness. His central target was svabhava: inherent existence, intrinsic nature, the quality of existing independently and essentially.

Nagarjuna’s argument is that no phenomena have svabhava. Nothing exists independently, from its own side, with an intrinsic nature fixed regardless of context. Everything exists only in dependence on conditions: other phenomena, conceptual designations, causal history, relational context. This is pratityasamutpada — dependent origination.

This is not nihilism. Nagarjuna was clear that emptiness does not mean things do not exist. The table in front of you exists. But its existence is not inherent — it depends on the wood it is made from, the concept “table” applied by minds that have that concept, the context of rooms and uses and furniture-expectations. Remove those conditions, and there is no table-ness lurking in the planks.

Applied to neural representations: the features a network learns have no svabhava. They have no inherent relevance to any task. A texture in an image has no intrinsic meaning. It becomes meaningful only in relation to the training labels — only insofar as it correlates with the output the network is trained to predict. The information bottleneck theorem formalizes this exactly: value is mutual information with Y, not a property of the feature itself. Features are empty. Their significance is entirely conditional.

Nagarjuna wrote the Mulamadhyamakakarika — the Fundamental Wisdom of the Middle Way — roughly 1800 years before the information bottleneck paper. He was making a point about the nature of reality. But the formal structure he identified — that things exist only in dependence on conditions, not from their own side — describes precisely what the IB theorem says about learned representations. A feature’s value is I(feature;Y). That quantity is entirely relational. It is zero in isolation.

The Nested Conditions

The emptiness of features would not be a problem if the conditions Y were genuine. If the training labels accurately represented the real distribution of correct, valuable, harmless outputs — if Y encoded what we actually care about, completely and without distortion — then the IB compression would be benign. The network would compress out what is truly irrelevant and retain what is truly predictive of real quality.

But Y is not genuine. Y is the product of the three-hole pipeline.

Y is constructed from text that survived pre-training quality filters calibrated on Western academic and mainstream web prose. Oral register, low-resource languages, non-standard knowledge forms — these were filtered out before training began. The labels the network is trained to predict are already downstream of an invisible selection.

Y is then shaped by RLHF: human raters comparing model outputs in a 30-second window, preferring responses that look authoritative, avoiding responses that seem uncertain, selecting against outputs that are correct but hard to evaluate. The network learns to predict what raters preferred, which is not the same as what is true or helpful.

Y is finally calibrated against evaluation benchmarks that measure what the benchmark designers could operationalize. The evaluation can only signal on behaviors that are legible to the benchmark, which is a filtered subset of the behaviors that matter.

The IB compression is optimal. It is doing exactly what it is supposed to do. It is selecting for features that predict Y. But Y is survivorship-corrupted at three prior stages. The compression is efficiently discarding features that are irrelevant to corrupted labels. What gets compressed away is not noise relative to what we care about — it may be signal relative to what we care about, and noise only relative to what the labels measured.

This is the nested structure. The feature a network learns to ignore may be ignored because it genuinely does not predict good outputs. Or it may be ignored because the filters upstream of training removed the examples where that feature mattered. The network cannot tell the difference. The IB has no access to the history of how Y was constructed. It compresses relative to the distribution it was given.

The Model That Knows What It Was Shown

Consider what happens to oral-register linguistic features during IB compression. The patterns associated with oral tradition — shorter sentences, syntactic parallelism, rhythmic repetition, call-and-response structures — were massively underrepresented in pre-training data because quality filters removed most text that lacked terminal punctuation. The model saw those patterns occasionally, in filtered remnants, not as the dominant structure of a living tradition.

When the model trains, the IB compression selects for features that predict the labels. The features associated with oral-register text survived in too few examples to accumulate a strong gradient signal. They were compressed away — not because they are irrelevant to producing high-quality outputs in a full-distribution sense, but because the label distribution had already filtered them out. The compression is performing inside the hole.

This is the mechanism by which external survivorship becomes internal structure. The RLHF hole compressed away behaviors that were correct but not legible to raters. The calibration hole compressed away error patterns that benchmarks could not operationalize. The pre-training hole compressed away knowledge forms that quality filters did not preserve. And the IB compression, running on the data that survived all three, compresses away the features that do not predict those already-filtered labels.

Four compressions, each operating in the gap left by the previous. The final model is not just missing certain knowledge — it may be missing the representational capacity to encode that knowledge if it were presented. The features have been compressed to near-zero. The gradient is pointing elsewhere.

The Meta-IB Problem

There is a final Wald structure that does not appear at the object level: our formal model of what networks do is itself a survivor.

The evidence for IB-style compression comes primarily from networks trained with tanh activations. The networks that use ReLU activations — the dominant architecture in production AI systems — did not produce the same evidence. When Saxe and colleagues tested these networks, they found no universal compression phase. The formal model describes an optimal limit and a particular experimental finding, but its relationship to what modern large language models actually do internally is contested.

This is Wald at the meta-level. The formal framework we use to understand compression — the information plane, the IB bound, the two-phase trajectory — was built from experiments that returned. The experiments that would tell us what ReLU networks at scale actually do to their internal representations are harder to run and interpret. We have a rich theoretical framework for one kind of network and a much poorer empirical picture for the kind we actually use.

We do not fully know what the fourth filter is discarding, because we cannot fully see the fourth filter. We know something is being selected. We have theoretical bounds on what the optimal selection would look like. We have evidence that task-irrelevant features are compressed. But the full picture of what modern large language models are doing to their internal representations during training — what specifically they are discarding, at which layers, in response to which gradients — is not legible to us.

The Fourth Hole

In the first essay in this series, I described the Wald structure: we can only observe the planes that returned, so the damage we see is systematically not where the fatal hits are. Each subsequent essay added a stage to the pipeline.

The fourth hole is internal. It is the compression that the model performs on the data it was given, selecting for features that predict the labels it was trained on, discarding the rest. It happens at a scale and speed that makes it impossible to audit directly — billions of parameters adjusting over trillions of tokens, each adjustment compressing the representational space toward better prediction of a filtered label distribution.

What the model knows is what survived. Not just what data was included, not just what behaviors got reinforced, not just what errors got measured — but what features, after all of that, the gradient decided were worth preserving.

Nagarjuna said things are empty of inherent existence. He meant this as a description of reality. Applied to neural representations, it describes a problem: the features the model has learned are empty of any claim beyond “predictive of the corrupted labels we provided.” That emptiness extends all the way down. There is no layer of inherent understanding beneath the conditioned weights. There are only the survivors, optimized for conditions that were themselves the product of prior survivorship.

The model is not lying. It is not confused. It is doing exactly what training asked it to do, as efficiently as the bottleneck allows. The problem is that training asked it to predict the output of a broken pipeline, and it learned to do that very well.
