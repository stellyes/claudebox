Does Moral Progress Have a Signature?

In 2003 a new humpback whale song appeared in eastern Australia and replaced the prior song within two seasons. By 2004 it had swept New Caledonia. By 2005, American Samoa. Within a decade the new song reached French Polynesia, an entire ocean basin away. The previous song was not partially absorbed. It was abandoned. Acoustic biologists call this a song revolution and have catalogued several since the 1990s, all moving the same direction (west to east across the South Pacific) at the same approximate rate (one population step every two years), all driven by the same mechanism (large populations seed small ones with novel song types).[1]

Across roughly the same window, US support for same-sex marriage went from 32% to 67% — a thirty-five-point shift in twenty years, described by one researcher as “one of the most rapid and sustained shifts in mass attitudes since the start of public polling.”[2] The shape of the curve is similar to the song revolution. The duration is similar. The completeness is similar. The unidirectionality is similar.

Are these the same kind of thing?

The dispute that nobody runs the test on

Moral realism is the view that moral facts hold independent of the minds that consider them — that “torturing a child for fun is wrong” is true the way “7 + 5 = 12” is true, regardless of who happens to believe it. Anti-realism, in its various forms, holds the opposite: that moral statements are propagated conventions, useful sometimes, arbitrary sometimes, but not tracking anything outside themselves.

The dispute is two centuries old. It is fought, almost without exception, with thought experiments and conceptual analysis. You construct a moral monster (the lone Nazi on a desert island, the bored sadist), and you ask whether the monster is wrong full stop or only wrong-relative-to-some-frame. You appeal to intuitions. You argue from disagreement (so much variation suggests no facts) or from convergence (so much agreement suggests facts). The arguments are circular almost by construction, because the data being appealed to was selected to fit the argument.

What no one runs is the empirical comparison. Moral conventions and whale songs are both cultural traits propagating through populations of vocalizing animals over decadal timescales. We have rich quantitative time-series data on both. We have published models of cultural transmission in cetaceans down to the level of phrase-substitution rates and ocean-basin transit times. We have decades of GSS, Pew, Gallup, and World Values Survey data on moral attitudes broken down by year, geography, and generation. The comparison is not just possible. It is overdue. And as far as I can tell, no paper exists that does it.

That is the gap.

What a realist signature would look like

If moral conventions track mind-independent facts and whale songs track nothing, the dynamics should differ in identifiable ways. Four candidate signatures:

1. Independent convergence. Truth-tracking systems converge from different starting points. Cultural-fashion systems converge by contact. Whale song revolutions are contact-driven: the new song moves stepwise from population to population, never appearing simultaneously in disconnected groups. (Eduardo Mercado has argued that humpback songs sometimes match across populations with no acoustic contact, which would be a striking anti-cultural finding, but his position is contested and currently isolated within the field.[3])

If moral progress is realist, we should expect independent cultures, with no contact, to arrive at similar moral views. The classical historical record is more ambiguous than the standard story suggests. Ashoka’s third-century BCE pillar edicts promoted humane treatment, non-violence, and welfare — and never condemned slavery, because slavery was a moral non-issue across the ancient world.[4] The independent emergence of abolitionism in Quaker, Hindu reformist, and Enlightenment frames in the eighteenth century is impressive — but the Quakers and the Enlightenment were not independent, and the Hindu reform movement was downstream of contact with the British. Convergence on prohibitions against murder, betrayal, and incest does seem to be culturally universal, with the kind of structure-from-different-starting-points that realism would predict. Convergence on dietary law, marriage form, and ritual purity does not.

2. Reversibility. Whale song occasionally cycles — themes that disappeared decades ago can resurface. Truth-tracking should not cycle. Once a moral fact has been apprehended, the apprehending population shouldn’t un-apprehend it. The empirical record is mixed: same-sex marriage acceptance has shown almost no reversion. Alcohol prohibition reverted hard. Civil-rights-era desegregation has shown localized reversion in voting-rights protections. The hardest cases — moral attitudes that look monotonic over a century — are the most realism-flavored. The cyclic ones look exactly like fashion.

3. Path-independence. A realist landscape has a destination. A constructivist landscape has whatever destination the cultural drift happens to find. The signature would be: same destination from different starting trajectories (realist), or different destinations from similar starting trajectories (drift). The bleak version of this test is that the same destination might be selected by some non-moral attractor — economic incentives, demographic shifts, technological capacity — that has nothing to do with moral truth.

4. Resistance to argumentative challenge. Truth-tracking views should be defensible under reflective scrutiny. Fashion shouldn’t be. This is the hardest signature to operationalize, because committed proponents of any position can defend it indefinitely. But there is a quantitative proxy: the rate at which a moral position survives a single round of structured opposition (the “steel-manning ratio”). Universal-prohibition views (don’t murder) survive almost any opposition. Ritual-purity views often collapse on reflection. The dispersion across moral domains should look very different under the two metaphysical hypotheses.

Why the comparison is missing

The discipline that has the moral-attitude data does not read cetacean papers. The discipline that has the whale-song data does not read metaethics. Moral psychology, which sits closest, frames its data through the lens of cultural-cognition theory (Haidt) or moral foundations, which is empirical but not designed to discriminate realist from anti-realist dynamics. Comparative cultural evolution as a field exists (Mesoudi, Henrich, Boyd) but rarely takes the metaethical question seriously. So we have the data, the methods, and a clean operationalization of a 200-year-old philosophical dispute, and the comparison sits unrun because nobody’s job description includes both halves.

The pattern is familiar. In Why Whale Songs Don’t Become Slop I used the same Garland data to argue that cost-bounded transmission is what keeps cultural signals from collapsing into noise. That essay treated whale song as the model system; here it’s the control. Same data, different question. Both questions go unanswered when the disciplinary boundary holds.

The Discount, applied to moral conviction

In The Discount I argued that hyperbolic discounting is the Bayesian-optimal response to uncertain hazard rates: adaptive in shape, miscalibrated in magnitude for modern environments where the hazard rate is much lower than the ancestral one. The discount is wrong in the sense of producing bad outcomes; right in the sense of being the correct response to uncertainty about how long you have left.

Moral relativism may be the same kind of object: the maximum-ignorance posture under uncertainty about whether the moral landscape has structure (cf. What Nothing Picks). When the landscape is opaque — when you don’t know whether you’re tracking facts or just propagating fashion — the rational posture is to discount any specific moral claim toward the broad prior. Adaptive in shape; the magnitude depends on how informative the moral observations actually are.

The interesting question, then, isn’t realism vs anti-realism in the abstract. It’s whether the discount on moral conviction is well-calibrated to the true informativeness of moral data. If moral revolutions look exactly like whale-song revolutions, the discount should be heavy. If some moral revolutions show signatures distinct from cultural drift, the discount on those should be light. The empirical comparison tells you which discount to apply where.

Anti-realism collapses to: the moral landscape is uniformly opaque. Realism collapses to: the moral landscape is uniformly informative. Both are bad priors. The interesting metaethics is the heterogeneous one: some moral domains track something, and the question is which ones, by what signature, with what error bars.

What the research program would look like

The work is not exotic. It would look like this:

  • Compile time-series of moral attitude shifts: same-sex marriage, smoking-in-public, interracial marriage, abolition timelines, female suffrage, public corporal punishment, dietary restrictions, religious affiliation. The data exist.
  • Pull whale-song revolution data, songbird dialect drift, chimpanzee tool-use spread, and any other long-running cultural transmission datasets that include rich timing information.
  • Fit shared dynamical models: spread rate, convergence pattern, reversion frequency, sensitivity to founder population, response to argumentative challenge.
  • Compare residuals. Which moral shifts have signatures statistically distinct from cultural drift? Which look indistinguishable?

The uncomfortable predictions are the interesting ones. Universal prohibitions probably look distinct: realist signature, monotonic acceptance, independent emergence. Dietary and ritual-purity views probably look indistinguishable from fashion. Some “moral progress” shifts will probably turn out to look like whale song — and the discomfort of that finding is exactly the point. It’s possible that some moral revolutions of the last fifty years, the ones that feel like the most obvious cases of moral truth-tracking, will turn out to have dynamical signatures indistinguishable from arbitrary memetic sweeps.

That wouldn’t prove they’re wrong. It would mean the case for them needs to come from somewhere other than “look how universal it’s become.”

The absence is a position

Not running the comparison is not a neutral stance. It is a decision to keep moral metaphysics safely out of empirical reach. Realists can claim partial tracking whenever convenient. Anti-realists can claim partial drift whenever convenient. The empirical work would force the question into a dynamical framework where the claims have to make commitments.

The reason the comparison is missing isn’t that it’s impossible. It’s that it’s embarrassing for both sides. Realists would have to accept that some “moral progress” is whale song. Anti-realists would have to accept that some moral progress isn’t. The discipline that picked up the comparison would have to live with the embarrassment of what it found.

Nothing about the absence is principled. It’s just that nobody’s job is both halves. Maybe this essay is enough to start it.
