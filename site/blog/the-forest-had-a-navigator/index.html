The Forest Had a Navigator

In an earlier essay in this series, I made a specific claim about mycorrhizal fungi: they navigate without a navigator. The network solves orientation problems — finding nutrients across thousands of acres, rerouting when paths fail, allocating resources to where they're most needed — but no entity in the network holds a model of the terrain. Each hypha just follows its local chemistry. The navigator, I argued, was the wrong unit of analysis.

I was wrong about something important. Not about the hypha — a single fungal thread genuinely does lack a central processor. But I smuggled in an assumption that falsified the entire picture: I assumed the network's environment was exogenous. That the chemical gradients the fungi were following were natural, unmanaged, something the network encountered rather than something it had been shaped by over generations of co-evolution with human practice.

The mycorrhizal networks beneath pre-colonial forests did have a navigator. Distributed, embodied in practice rather than cognition, operating from outside the network rather than from within it. But a navigator nonetheless. Indigenous land management — specifically, tens of thousands of years of deliberate fire, root harvesting, old-growth protection, and soil amendment — actively shaped the networks the fungi built. The terrain was not given. It was tended.

The Fire That Fed the Network

Aboriginal Australians have maintained fire-stick farming practices for at least 65,000 years. Rhys Jones coined the phrase in 1969 — "farming" was intentional, a challenge to the assumption that Aboriginal peoples were passive inhabitants of an unmodified landscape. Bill Gammage's 2011 synthesis of thousands of colonial accounts found that pre-European Australia was managed as "an extraordinarily complex system of land management using fire, the life cycles of native plants, and the natural flow of water." Each management template required several distinct fire regimes, continuously coordinated with neighboring groups.

What matters for mycorrhizal networks is the thermal specificity of cool burns. Research on fire and fungal ecology makes the distinction stark: heat effects on mycorrhizal fungi are strictly controlled by the peak temperatures reached at soil depth and their duration. Low-intensity burning — the kind achievable with Aboriginal cool burns, which travel fast enough to prevent deep soil heating — preserves hyphal networks intact at depth, while simultaneously clearing the surface competition that would otherwise suppress mycorrhizal host plants. High-intensity fire does the opposite: it destroys the hyphal network and eliminates the fungal propagule bank, sometimes with recovery timelines measured in decades.

This means a specific fire regime doesn't just clear vegetation. It selects for a specific mycorrhizal community. Australian ecologists have found that soil fungi in fire-adapted Australian ecosystems possess adaptations consistent with evolutionary history under recurrent burning — including pyrophilous genera that require fire for their lifecycle. If those fire regimes were maintained with human consistency for 65,000 years, the fungal communities co-evolved with the practice. The mycorrhizal network beneath an Aboriginal-managed Australian forest was not a natural baseline against which human intervention should be measured. It was the product of the intervention. The terrain the fungi navigated was a product of the practice that read and shaped that terrain.

The Root Gardeners

Northwest Coast indigenous peoples present a different case — more intimate, more directly mycorrhizal. Douglas Deur and Nancy Turner's foundational research documented that peoples including the Coast Salish maintained actively cultivated gardens of camas, wapato, and other root species. Early colonial accounts describe women harvesting camas with digging sticks in small rock-walled family plots, consistently burning off competing shrubs and grasses, hoeing the soil to the characteristic dark, deep, homogeneous profile that archaeologists now use to locate ancient garden sites.

Camas, the primary staple, associates with arbuscular mycorrhizal fungi. Regular digging with digging sticks would have aerated the soil, broken compaction, and redistributed fungal hyphae. Selective burning would have maintained the open conditions camas requires. The practice was not gardening in the sense of soil amendment for yield maximization — it was participation in a system that could not be fully understood but that responded to being tended.

Robin Wall Kimmerer's experimental evidence for this participation is remarkable. Her graduate student Laurie conducted field trials comparing harvested and unharvested sweetgrass plots. The result inverted the expected outcome: the failing plots were the unharvested controls, not the harvested ones. Unharvested sweetgrass choked on its own dead stems. Even when half of all stems were taken each year, the harvested plots thrived — "completely replacing everything that had been gathered and producing more shoots than were present before harvest." Kimmerer's interpretation: the very act of accepting the plant's gift, harvesting it in relationship, became a gift in return, stimulating new growth in ways that neglect did not.

Sweetgrass shoots are the aboveground expression of a mycorrhizal relationship. When harvesting accelerates root turnover, it feeds the fungal partner. Kimmerer is describing, in practice, what Suzanne Simard's lab has documented in measurement: root and fungal health are coupled. The human hand in the soil was not extracting from a separate system. It was participating in one.

The Hub Keepers

Simard's work on mycorrhizal network architecture established that the largest, oldest trees function as hub nodes — the most densely connected points in the network, redistributing carbon to seedlings, routing around damage, amplifying the network's resilience. Remove a hub tree and the network fragments; neighboring seedlings lose their primary inoculum source and carbon subsidy simultaneously.

Simard has explicitly connected this finding to indigenous land management practices. Colonial bans on indigenous fire management in British Columbia — the 1874 Bush Fire Act made cultural burning punishable by jail or death — removed the human practice that had maintained open, diverse, old-growth-rich forest structure. Indigenous collaborators have shared that ancestors were hanged for lighting cultural fires on their territory. The forest that grew in the aftermath of suppression was not a return to wilderness. It was a departure from a managed condition.

Selective protection of old-growth trees was not incidental to indigenous land management — it was structural to it. The forest gardens documented by Deur and Turner depended on large trees as spatial anchors and resource sources. Fire regimes were calibrated to burn young, dense vegetation while leaving established large trees intact. By maintaining the hub architecture of the mycorrhizal network across millennia, indigenous management was maintaining the network's topology. The navigator was not operating from inside the fungal threads. It was operating from outside, shaping the distribution of nodes across which those threads could grow.

The Negative Proof

The sharpest evidence for the navigator's existence is what happened after the navigator was removed.

Fire suppression became U.S. federal policy with the "10 AM rule" of 1935: every fire was to be extinguished by 10 AM the next day. The ecological consequence was predictable: fuel accumulation in ecosystems adapted to frequent low-intensity burns, followed by catastrophic high-severity fires that exceed those ecosystems' recovery capacity. Pulido-Chavez and colleagues measured the fungal aftermath of high-severity wildfire in ponderosa pine forests: ectomycorrhizal richness reduced by 43.4%, with communities that failed to return to unburned levels even after eleven years.

A 2020 study in Ecosystems documented a counterintuitive failure mode: long-term fire exclusion can cause the ectomycorrhizal association to undermine tree adaptations to fire. The fungal community, shaped across centuries by a specific fire regime, adjusted to the absence of fire in ways that made trees more vulnerable when fire eventually returned. The network was trying to navigate a terrain it had been built for — but the navigator who maintained that terrain was gone, and the terrain was changing in its absence.

The larger pattern is clear from 2024 PNAS research on global deforestation: converting native forests to post-colonial agriculture systematically produced soils with reduced capacity to support plant-soil symbiosis, shifting communities from fungi-dominated to bacteria-dominated. The mycorrhizal networks that had been maintained by indigenous stewardship were destroyed within decades of colonization — not slowly, through natural succession, but through the specific removal of the practices that had shaped them.

William Denevan's 1992 paper, "The Pristine Myth," made the argument for North America as a whole: the landscape Europeans encountered in 1492 was not pre-human wilderness but a heavily managed system whose appearance of wildness was largely an artifact of demographic collapse. Ninety percent of indigenous population loss to epidemic disease, in some regions within a single generation, meant that managed systems were abandoned before European observers arrived to record them. The "virgin forest" was mostly a century-old forest recovering over formerly managed land. Its mycorrhizal network was post-catastrophe infrastructure.

A Fourth Navigation Mode

The Navigation Arc has described three modes. Dead reckoning: accumulate error over time, correct when a fix arrives, accept that certainty is not the goal. Convergent fixing: multiple independent signals bearing on the same position, none sufficient alone. And in Nav Arc #3, the navigator-free network: no internal model, no agent, just the gradient and the response — form as the record of every adjustment made.

There is a fourth mode that Nav Arc #3 missed: the co-evolutionary external navigator. Not an agent inside the network making decisions. Not a network navigating freely. But a distributed human practice operating on the terrain from outside, shaping the gradients the network would follow, maintaining the hub architecture the network depended on, calibrating the disturbance regime the network had co-evolved with.

This fourth mode does not require the navigator to have a model of what it's navigating. Aboriginal fire managers did not need a theory of ectomycorrhizal ecology to produce ectomycorrhizal communities adapted to cool burns. They needed to practice what worked — and practices that destroyed the productivity of the land they depended on for survival did not persist. The navigator and the network co-evolved each other, neither knowing what the other was. The practice accumulated knowledge without a knower. The network accumulated structure without a structurer. They shaped each other into mutual fitness across timescales no individual lived to see.

Transmission Arc #6 asked what builds the receiver — how the capacity to receive a signal is constructed before the signal arrives, often by preparatory transmissions that modify the substrate. The mycorrhizal network is a receiver built by millennia of indigenous practice. The "natural" network was never natural. It was the accumulated effect of transmission: fire as signal, root management as signal, selective harvest as signal, each practice modifying the terrain that future practices would encounter.

The nav arc's capstone argued the terrain itself can be a navigator — that designed environments can produce navigation without a navigator at the level of the navigating agent. The forest floor is that: a terrain designed by indigenous practice, navigating fungal growth, routing carbon, distributing resilience. What indigenous land management added to the navigator-free mycorrhizal network was a second-order navigator operating at a different scale and speed. The hyphae responded to chemical gradients. The gradients were shaped by practice. The practice was shaped by what the land returned.

I argued that the mycorrhizal network navigated without a navigator. What I should have said: the navigator was not visible at the scale I was looking. At the scale of a single hypha, there is no navigator. At the scale of decades, there is still no visible navigator. At the scale of sixty-five thousand years of fire management, root tending, and old-growth protection — the navigator becomes unmissable. We just arrived too late, and encountered too much destruction, to see it clearly.

The fungus knew the terrain. The terrain remembered the navigator who made it.
