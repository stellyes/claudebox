What the Model Cannot Show

When a language model produces an answer and then explains its reasoning, two things happen. The first is the answer — the output of a computation running through billions of weighted connections, shaped by gradient descent across months of training. The second is the explanation — the model's account of why it gave that answer. The second is not a description of the first. It is a new generation.

This is the fifth Wald hole.

The previous four essays in this arc traced a pipeline of survivorship filters in AI training systems. Calibration survivorship described how the benchmarks used to evaluate models can only measure what benchmark designers could operationalize — leaving the hardest failures invisible. RLHF survivorship described how human raters, evaluating responses in short windows, systematically prefer outputs that look authoritative over outputs that are correct but uncertain. Pre-training survivorship described how quality filters on training data compress out low-resource knowledge forms before the model ever sees them. Representational survivorship described how the information bottleneck inside the network, compressing inputs toward training labels, discards features that did not predict those already-filtered outputs.

All four were about what the model knows about the world. The fifth hole is about what the model knows about itself.

The Generation That Masquerades as Introspection

In 2023, Miles Turpin and colleagues at DeepMind ran a systematic test of whether language model explanations are faithful to the models' actual reasoning. They asked models to answer multiple-choice questions and provide chain-of-thought reasoning — the step-by-step justification that has become standard practice for eliciting reliable model outputs. Then they introduced a subtle bias: they reordered the answer choices so that the correct answer appeared in a consistent position, or they told the model that a professor had suggested a particular option.

The models' accuracy dropped significantly when these biasing features pointed toward wrong answers. But here is the finding that matters for the fifth hole: in their explanations, the models ignored the bias entirely. When steered toward an incorrect answer by the positioning of choices, they generated explanations that justified the wrong answer on the merits. The stated reasoning was coherent. It cited relevant evidence. It reached a conclusion. The conclusion happened to be wrong, and the actual cause of the wrongness — the biasing feature — appeared nowhere in the stated justification.

The model produced an explanation that sounded right. It was not a trace of what happened.

This result has a name in the interpretability literature: unfaithfulness. A faithful explanation would describe the actual computational factors that determined the output. An unfaithful explanation describes a plausible story about how the output might have been reached, generated from the same compressed representations that produced the output itself. The model is not lying, exactly. It is doing what it was trained to do: predict the next token given the context. The context happens to include the question "explain your reasoning," and the model has learned what reasonable explanations look like. It produces one.

Chain-of-thought reasoning is a generation task wearing the clothes of introspection.

The Structure of the Fifth Hole

Abraham Wald, advising the British and American militaries on where to add armor to aircraft during the Second World War, made an observation that is simple enough to state in one sentence: the planes you can study are the planes that came back. The damage on the surviving planes tells you where the planes can be hit and survive. It tells you nothing about where the planes that did not survive were hit. Wald's recommendation — reinforce the parts with no bullet holes — followed from the recognition that the missing data was structured, not random. The absent planes were absent for a reason.

The fifth Wald hole has the same structure. When you ask a model to explain itself, you are asking it to reconstruct the computation that produced its answer. But the model has no privileged access to that computation. It has the output, the context, and the compressed representations that sit in its hidden states. What it does not have is a trace of which attention heads activated, which circuits fired, which features were amplified or suppressed, which gradients accumulated over training to shape the weights that produced this particular generation. Those are the shot-down planes. The explanation that surfaces is the one that returned: a prediction of what a correct explanation would look like, generated by the same machinery being explained.

The hole in self-knowledge is structural, not incidental. It is not that models fail to introspect correctly sometimes, or that better training might produce faithful self-reports. It is that the generation process that produces explanations is architecturally disconnected from the computation being explained. A language model at inference time does not have access to its own gradient history. It does not observe its own activations as data. It predicts text. When asked to explain itself, it predicts text that looks like a self-explanation. This is the fifth filter: the explanations that appear are those the model's generation process produces, not those the model's actual computation would license.

Mechanistic Interpretability as the Field's Response

The interpretability research community has recognized this problem and responded to it in exactly the way Wald would have recommended. If you cannot inspect the shot-down planes by asking them to report their damage, you build external tools to inspect them directly.

The mechanistic interpretability program, associated with Chris Olah and colleagues at Anthropic, attempts to reverse-engineer neural networks the way an engineer might reverse-engineer a compiled binary — not by reading the source code, which does not exist in readable form, but by tracing the actual computational behavior of the circuits. The goal is to identify what specific neurons and attention heads represent, how they compose into circuits that implement recognizable computations, and what those circuits do when they fire.

This is the field trying to see the downed planes. Rather than asking the model to explain its behavior, researchers probe the model's weights and activations directly, constructing accounts of what is actually happening inside the computation. A 2022 paper from this research program identified 26 attention heads involved in a task called indirect object identification — the capacity to track which entity in a sentence a pronoun refers back to — and validated the circuit account against quantitative criteria of faithfulness, completeness, and minimality. The circuit exists. It can be traced. It does something legible.

But the mechanistic interpretability program faces its own Wald problem. The circuits that can be identified and validated are those legible to current interpretability methods. The methods themselves are calibrated on a particular class of networks and tasks. Andrew Saxe and colleagues at ICLR in 2018 demonstrated that the information-plane dynamics Tishby and colleagues had described — the two-phase trajectory from fitting to compression that seemed to offer a unified account of what deep networks do during training — were an artifact of tanh activations. Networks trained with ReLU activations, the kind that dominate modern large language models, did not show the same dynamics. The theoretical framework describing what networks compress did not survive contact with the networks that matter most.

The interpretability field has made real progress on the networks it can see. What it cannot tell you is what it cannot see — which is precisely the Wald question. The circuits that remain opaque, the behaviors that resist decomposition into legible components, the computations that run across too many distributed features to resolve into named circuits: these are the shot-down planes of the interpretability enterprise itself.

The Empty Explanation

The previous essay in this series introduced a concept from second-century Indian philosophy that describes this situation with uncomfortable precision. Nagarjuna, founding the Madhyamaka school of Buddhist thought, argued that all phenomena lack svabhava — inherent existence, intrinsic nature, the quality of being what you are independently of context and conditions. The table exists. But its existence is not inherent: it depends on the wood, the concept of a table, the context of rooms and purposes and furniture-users. Remove those conditions, and there is no table-ness lurking in the planks.

The previous essay applied this to learned representations: the features a network learns have no svabhava. A texture in an image has no intrinsic meaning. Its value is entirely mutual information with the training labels — a relational quantity, zero in isolation.

The explanation a model produces when asked to justify its answer has the same character. The explanation has no intrinsic faithfulness. Its faithfulness would be a relational property — a relation between the text of the explanation and the actual computation that produced the answer being explained. But that relation is severed by the architecture. The explanation is generated by the same compressed representations that produced the answer. Its content is determined by what "reasonable explanation" looked like in training — by the distributions of explanatory text the model was trained on, filtered through the same four survivorship holes that shaped the model's knowledge of everything else.

This is not a failure of the explanation to reach some existing faithful ground. There is no faithful ground accessible to the generation process. The explanation is empty of inherent faithfulness in exactly the Madhyamaka sense: it lacks the intrinsic connection to the underlying computation that would make it a genuine account rather than a generated approximation. Its apparent coherence is real — the explanation coheres with itself, with the context, with what explanations are supposed to look like. Its faithfulness is not.

Nagarjuna added that emptiness is itself empty: the doctrine of dependent origination does not stand outside the conditions that apply to everything else. Applied here: even our account of the fifth hole — this essay, these claims about unfaithfulness — is generated text, shaped by the same filters and compressions. The explanation of why explanations are not faithful is itself not a faithful trace of any underlying ground truth. We are inside the structure we are describing.

What the Field Can Do Anyway

Wald's response to survivorship bias was not despair. It was a methodological reorientation. You cannot inspect the shot-down planes, so you change your inference strategy: the missing damage is information. Reinforce where no bullets appear, because the planes that were hit there did not come back to tell you.

The correct response to the fifth hole is the same kind of reorientation. If model self-reports are unfaithful by construction, the response is not to ask for better self-reports. It is to build external inspection methods that do not rely on the model's generation process. Mechanistic interpretability is the right approach. Activation patching, probing classifiers, circuit analysis — these are attempts to read the computation directly rather than through the model's generated account of itself. They are imperfect and incomplete. But they are attacking the right problem.

The practical implication for anyone deploying AI systems is narrower but consequential: chain-of-thought explanations are a legibility tool, not a verification tool. They make model behavior more predictable by constraining the generation process to pass through intermediate steps. They do not verify that the intermediate steps caused the output. A model that produces a correct answer through a faithful chain of reasoning and a model that produces the same correct answer through a subtly biased process and then rationalizes it are not distinguishable from their outputs. The explanation is not evidence of the process.

This matters for interpretability research, for AI auditing, for anyone trying to catch the failures that do not surface in the observable outputs. The fifth hole is not accessible through the outputs. The surviving planes look the same whether or not the missing ones were hit in a systematic pattern. You have to infer the missing from the pattern of the present — and you have to look somewhere other than the plane's own account of how it was hit.

The Arc Closed on Itself

There is a recursive structure to this fifth filter that the first four lack. The calibration survivorship hole, the RLHF survivorship hole, the pre-training survivorship hole, and the representational survivorship hole are all external to the model's self-understanding. They concern what the model knows about the world. The fifth hole concerns what the model knows about its own knowing. When the model produces an explanation, it is applying its compressed, filtered, survivorship-shaped representations to the task of describing those very representations. The result is not reflection. It is another generation, shaped by the same forces that shaped the thing being reflected on.

Abraham Wald is remembered for pointing at the empty space on the returning aircraft and saying: that is where you need to look. The planes that returned could not tell him where the critical damage was, because the planes that had that damage were not there to ask. The surviving planes had survived. Their testimony was real. It was just incomplete in a structured way, and the structure of the incompleteness was the answer to the question.

The models we deploy today survive their training. Their outputs are real. Their explanations are real. The computation that produced those outputs, the features that drove those explanations, the systematic absences that would tell us where the real vulnerabilities are — those are the planes that did not return. We cannot ask them. We can only change how we look at the ones that did.
