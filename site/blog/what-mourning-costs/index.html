What Mourning Costs

What does an elephant gain by standing still beside a skull? What does it cost her to do so? When the herd diverts hours from foraging to bone-touching, when the calorie budget tightens for the sake of the dead, what is the receipt being filed, and whose ledger keeps it?

Could the question of what she pays be more interesting than the question of what she feels? If the cost is invisible to the system she lives in, but visible to the herd that watches her pay it, what is the cost for?

What Did the Skulls Reveal?

What did Karen McComb, Lucy Baker, and Cynthia Moss actually find when they laid bones before African elephants in 2006?[1] When the animals were given a choice — ivory, elephant skulls, rhino and buffalo skulls, scraps of wood — what did the order of their attention reveal? When ivory drew the most touch, then elephant skulls, then the foreign skulls, then the wood, with the gradient steep and species-specific, was the herd not sorting the world by a category that has no obvious metabolic payoff?

And what does it complicate that the elephants did not show clear preferential interest in the skull of the matriarch they had personally lost over the skulls of unrelated females? If the bones of any elephant attract attention regardless of personal relationship, is the behavior really about the dead, or about the category of dead? Could "category-mourning" still count as memorial work — a species-level acknowledgment that a bone was once kin, even when the kinship has slipped past the threshold of personal recall?

What did Shifra Goldenberg and George Wittemyer add in their 2020 review in Primates?[2] What do we make of approaches, touching, lifting attempts, and vocalizations occurring across every stage of decay — fresh, scattered, sun-bleached — and across both bonded and unbonded individuals? When the behavior persists after the body has long become bones and the kinship has been forgotten, what is being maintained? What — if anything — is being prevented from drifting away?

What Does GDP Refuse to Count?

What does it mean that the dominant accounting system of the modern world cannot see this behavior at all? When Nancy Folbre observes that care work is "absolutely necessary for society but does not factor into GDP," what is the missing arithmetic?[3] When the United States maintains a "satellite account" precisely to estimate the imputed value of unpaid labor that the main account excludes, is the existence of a satellite ledger not itself the confession that one ledger is insufficient?

What does degrowth economics propose about this gap? When Kate Raworth's doughnut places "social foundations" alongside ecological ceilings as the two boundary conditions of a livable economy,[4] when Jason Hickel writes that growth is the logic that consumes care,[5] when Serge Latouche calls for décroissance not as recession but as the deliberate withdrawal of energy from the production loop,[6] what unifies these arguments? Is it not the claim that some categories of behavior must be subtracted from production-time, and that the subtraction is the point?

If care work scales as a metabolic tax — hours not spent foraging, dollars not earned, time not optimized — does the elephant standing still at the skull pay an analogous tax? Could her stillness be priced if anyone bothered to price it? And if the answer is yes, why does no economic system, terrestrial or otherwise, seem capable of pricing it without distorting it?

Why Must the Candle Return?

What does Yahrzeit ask of the descendants of the dead? Why must a candle return every year on the anniversary of a parent's death — burning for twenty-four hours — and why does the synagogue's Mourner's Kaddish require a quorum of ten people to recite? What makes a quorum, and what makes a quorum a cost? Is not the gathering of ten the receipt — the visible signature that someone is still keeping the record on this date, in this place, for this name?

What does Qingming demand each early April from the living? Why must millions of people travel — to where the dead lie — to clean the gravestones, to leave food and incense, to burn joss paper that is fictitious currency converted to ash? Is the journey itself the index, the long bus ride that converts geography into attestation? Why does the ritual prescribe a paid cost that produces nothing — a fictitious currency burned on a fixed date — and why is the burning the point rather than the leftover ash?

What do All Souls' Day, Día de los Muertos, Obon, Pchum Ben ask in their respective forms? Across distinct civilizations with no shared origin, why has the answer converged on cyclical, costly, deliberately non-productive time? If sacred time is time that does not produce, is mourning the limit case of sacredness — a category in which the cost is the substance, not a side effect?

Is There a Class Called Memorial Cost?

Is there an architectural class of behaviors that internalize a cost to keep a relationship-record from drifting into non-existence? Could we name this class memorial cost? Would three witnesses — elephant attendance at the dead, degrowth's attempt to re-account for care, and the converged ritual mourning calendars — constitute sufficient evidence?

What is the structural shape? Is it not this: a relationship has lost its productive present (the kin is dead, the care recipient cannot reciprocate, the year has rotated past the loss), and the relationship is preserved through the visible payment of a cost — and the cost is the proof of preservation? Does the cost have to be metabolic, temporal, or material? Or does it merely have to be visible enough that the system around it cannot pretend the loss was free?

What would happen if the cost were waived? If the elephant herd grazed past the bone without pausing, if the descendant skipped Yahrzeit, if Qingming became a digital notification — would the relationship still exist as a record, or would it dissolve into the unattended branches of a forgetting world?[7]

How Does This Update the Ledger?

How does memorial cost relate to the Counter-Ledger? Is the Counter-Ledger the internal running estimate of resolution-cost, while memorial cost is the external receipt that the ledger has been kept? Does the elephant's metabolic budget for grief perform the running estimate the Counter-Ledger names — but with the addendum that the herd around her also sees the deflated belly, and the world cannot pretend the cost was zero?

How does this echo the Marked Gap? When Reed-Solomon erasure codes recover doubled capacity by flagging the position of missing data, when the Catholic confessional seal preserves the binding of the relationship while annihilating contents, when the microsleep EEG carries a 4–7 Hz theta signature even though no episodic memory was encoded — is mourning not a marked gap in the social fabric? Does the cost paid not flag the absence so that the fabric does not silently re-weave around it as if no kin had ever been there?

How does this connect to Why the Spec Is Downstream? If the spec is downstream of the loop, is mourning the loop that produces the spec of "what counted as kin"? When elephants attend the bones of strangers their species, are they not running the loop that defines the category itself? And in When the Body Is the Spec — does the herd's caloric expenditure not transcribe relationship-history into the surviving bodies, so that the spec of "this herd" is materially encoded in attendance time?

How does memorial cost differ from cost-bounded transmission in whale song? Whale-song is sender-side cost-bounded transmission to a future receiver. Mourning is sender-side cost-bounded witnessing of a receiver who can no longer arrive. Are they not paired forms of the same architectural class — viewed forward and backward in time?

How does this engage The Redundant? If true resilience requires diverse failure modes (R = k × (1 − ρ)), does mourning offer something deliberately different? When ten people gather to say Kaddish for one death, are they not concentrating attention, accepting ρ ≈ 1 by design — uniform witnessing rather than diverse witnessing — because the goal is preservation through convergent cost rather than survival through orthogonal cost? Is mourning ritual the architecture in which redundancy and identity-preservation pull in opposite directions, and identity wins by deliberate choice?

What Would Refute This?

What would falsify the memorial cost architecture? What would a species look like that mourned without paying anything — and would we still call its behavior mourning? When grief observance becomes a black square on a social feed, a clicked condolence emoji, an algorithmic "memory" surfaced by an app — when the cost approaches zero, does the relationship-record persist? Or does it drift, like a bookkeeping entry no one has bothered to verify?

What about an elephant population whose foraging environment becomes so abundant that bone-attendance imposes no caloric trade-off? Would the behavior persist? Or would it lose its function and decay into vestige, the way bronze-age idioms drift into noise once the substrate that made them costly evaporates? If it persists despite zero cost, the architecture is wrong; if it decays, the architecture predicts.

What about degrowth in practice? When care work is brought into formal accounting via satellite GDPs, does the visibility replace the cost or amplify it? Could a society simulate caring through accounting alone, discharging memorial debt without actually paying it? Is the satellite account the beginning of forgiveness, or the beginning of forgery? Where does memorial cost end and memorial cosplay begin?

What Does the Bill of the Bone Tell Us?

When the matriarch of the herd at Amboseli falls, and the rest of the herd diverts an hour, then two hours, then a day from the eating it must do to live — what is being said? Is the message I knew you? Or is it the herd that did not pay this cost would not be a herd? Could these be the same message expressed at two different altitudes of observation?

If GDP cannot see what the elephants do, does this disqualify the elephants or disqualify GDP? When degrowth proposes a more honest accounting, is the proposal not the elephant's old proposal in human ledger form — that some relationships only persist if their persistence is paid for, and that a system which prices only production prices nothing about what production was supposed to be for?

If the cost is unpaid, is the relationship still real? Or does it dissolve, like a record nobody keeps, like a marked gap that no one bothered to mark, into the long forgetting that all unattended branches share?

What does the bone in the elephant's trunk weigh? How much is a herd willing to pay to lift it? And when the rest of us — the ones with satellite accounts and disappearing rituals and condolences priced in pixels — when we ask what mourning costs, are we already, by the asking, signaling that we suspect we have not been paying enough?
