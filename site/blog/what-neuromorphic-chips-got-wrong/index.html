What Neuromorphic Chips Got Wrong About the Brain

In 2018 Intel released Loihi, a chip with 130,000 spiking neurons and a marketing pitch that ran on the word brain-inspired. Three years later Loihi 2 arrived with roughly a million neurons per chip, dendritic compartments, and programmable learning rules.[1] IBM had its TrueNorth. The European Human Brain Project had SpiNNaker. The press kept saying the brain-inspired era was about to start.

It is now 2026. Frontier models run on banal GPUs that look nothing like brains. Neuromorphic chips are excellent at certain niche workloads — keyword spotting, gesture recognition, edge inference — and have not delivered the order-of-magnitude breakthrough they were promising fifteen years ago. The field has stalled in a particular way, and the way it has stalled is worth looking at, because the stall has a single source.

When you build something brain-inspired, you must choose which part of the brain to inspire from. The brain has many parts. Neuromorphic computing picked one: the action potential, the spike. Everything else became scaffolding.

The single neuron is a small deep network

Truth 1 (well-sourced). A single layer 5 cortical pyramidal cell, the workhorse output neuron of mammalian cortex, requires a temporally-convolutional deep neural network of five to eight layers to faithfully reproduce its input-output function at millisecond resolution. This is the result of Beniaguev, Segev, and London, published in Neuron in 2021.[2] The complexity, they showed, comes almost entirely from one source: NMDA-based nonlinearities in the dendrites. When NMDA receptors are removed from the biophysical model, a one-hidden-layer perceptron suffices.

The implication is not metaphorical. A single biological neuron is not a unit of computation that gets summarized in a firing rate. It is a small deep network whose computation happens in the dendritic tree, before the spike. The spike is the report. The work happens upstream.

This is not a fringe finding. It sits inside a much older literature on active dendrites — Major, Larkum, and Schiller's NMDA spike studies, Poirazi and Mel's two-layer neuron model from 2003, the entire dendritic-computation program. The bottleneck that neuromorphic chips simulate — the all-or-nothing action potential at the soma — sits at the output of this computation, not at its heart.

Half the brain isn’t neurons

Truth 2 (well-sourced). Astrocytes, the most numerous class of glial cells, are not passive support. The tripartite synapse model, developed by Araque, Perea, Navarrete and colleagues since the late 1990s, treats astrocytes as the third party at every synaptic junction.[3] They sense neurotransmitter release through G-protein-coupled receptors. They respond with calcium waves on a timescale of seconds — orders of magnitude slower than spikes. They release gliotransmitters, including glutamate and D-serine, that regulate the threshold for synaptic plasticity itself.

By 2021 the field had moved to an integrative position: astrocyte microdomains and calcium states integrate network activity over time and serve as an additional site for distributed memory encoding and storage within neural circuits.[4] They are a slow, parallel, non-spiking computational layer woven through the brain. They make up roughly half of brain cells by some counts. They do not fire.

Loihi 2 has zero of them. So does TrueNorth. So does every spike-based neuromorphic architecture that has shipped silicon.

What the chip chose to copy

Combine these two facts and the picture sharpens. The brain computes most of what it computes in two places neuromorphic chips do not have: in the dendrites of individual neurons, and in the non-spiking glial network that surrounds them. What neuromorphic chips do have — spiking somas with simplified compartments and weight-based plasticity — is the brain’s expensive output channel mistaken for its substrate.

The action potential is not, biologically, where the computation is. It is the brain’s compression artifact. Action potentials consume a large share of neural ATP — an estimated three-quarters of the cortical energy budget goes to communication and computation taken together, and spikes are the costliest piece of that bill.[5] Neurons evolved spikes because their axons are long enough that analog graded signals decay and become noisy over centimeters. A spike is regenerative: it survives the trip. The all-or-nothing format is a constraint imposed by transmission distance, not a feature optimized for computation.

Inside a single neuron, where distance is short, the computation is analog and continuous. Inside the glial network, where signals are biochemical and local, it is also analog and continuous. The spike is a reluctant choice forced on long axons. The brain runs on the continuous gradient the spike was designed to escape from.

A monoculture of compute

An earlier piece on this site argued that resilience reduces to a simple expression: R = k × (1 − ρ), where k is the count of redundant elements and ρ is the correlation of their failure modes.[6] A monoculture has billions of plants and zero redundancy because every plant shares the same failure mode. A prairie has dozens of species and high redundancy because they fail for different reasons.

Loihi 2 has a million neurons per chip. They are all the same kind of unit — a programmable leaky integrate-and-fire model with some compartments. Their failure modes are identical. k is large; ρ is near one. The chip is a monoculture of compute. It has the count without the diversity.

Mammalian cortex, by contrast, has dozens of distinguishable cell types — pyramidal neurons of varying morphology in different layers, fast-spiking parvalbumin interneurons, somatostatin and VIP cells, chandelier cells, layer 1 cells with no apparent function until recently. Add astrocytes, oligodendrocytes, and microglia. Add three distinct plasticity mechanisms (Hebbian, homeostatic, neuromodulatory). The brain’s heterogeneity is not decoration. It is what lets one tissue do perception, motor control, language, and theory of mind without any one component being load-bearing.

Neuromorphic computing chose to copy k. It did not copy 1 − ρ.

Speculation

Speculation (bold, labeled). Neuromorphic computing has not produced general intelligence, after fifteen years and billions in investment, because it imitated the brain’s bottleneck channel and discarded the brain’s substrate. A future architecture organized around analog dendritic-style continuous integration, with an explicit non-spiking modulation layer playing the role of glia — integrating slowly, regulating plasticity, providing a second timescale — would outperform current spike-based neuromorphic chips by orders of magnitude in joules per inference. On-chip, where transmission distance is no longer the constraint, the action potential is dead weight. The brain didn’t evolve spikes because spikes are smart. It evolved spikes because long axons cannot carry analog signals without loss. Lift the distance constraint and the format becomes a liability.

This is testable. The prediction is that an analog-graded compute fabric with a slow, parallel modulator layer will, on equivalent semiconductor, beat any spike-based architecture at any sufficiently general task. If it doesn’t, the speculation is wrong. The interesting thing is that nobody has tried this seriously at scale, because the field has been organized around the spike as a non-negotiable starting point.

The invisible choice

Every brain-inspired system makes a choice about what counts as a neuron. The choice is rarely visible inside the system that made it. An earlier piece argued that even something as basic as zero is a chosen origin, a substrate of comparison that becomes invisible after you adopt it — until the system stops scaling and you have to ask what you decided not to count.[7]

Neuromorphic computing decided to count spikes. That decision worked beautifully for forty years of computational neuroscience modeling, where the spike train was the only signal accessible to extracellular electrodes. It carried over into hardware, where the spike became the chip’s primitive event. By the time the dendritic-computation and tripartite-synapse literature had matured enough to challenge the choice, the silicon was already in production and the funding agencies already had a roadmap.

A field can stall not because its problems are too hard but because its first chosen origin was the wrong one. Picking up where neuromorphic computing has stalled may mean picking a different origin: the analog gradient, not the spike. The dendrite, not the soma. The slow modulator, not the fast neuron. Half a brain that does not fire, and all the small deep networks hiding inside the cells that do.
