The Gene Drive Problem

In the late 1980s, geneticists noticed something strange about certain mutations: they won. Not because they were fitter, or because the organism carrying them was healthier, but because they had found a way to ensure they were always copied into both chromosomes during reproduction. A normal gene gets passed to half your offspring. A gene drive gets passed to all of them. In a few generations, it can spread through an entire population regardless of what it actually does to survival.

The Sirius Problem

The Dogon people of Mali have known about Sirius B for centuries. The anthropologist Marcel Griaule documented this in the 1930s: detailed knowledge of the dwarf star's mass, its orbit, its 50-year cycle — knowledge that requires a telescope to acquire. The story became a minor legend in the literature of ancient mysteries. Books were written. Films were made. The knowledge kept spreading.

There is no credible mechanism for how the Dogon could have had this information. The mainstream explanation — that Griaule's questions shaped his informants' answers, or that the knowledge arrived through contact with European astronomers — is almost certainly correct. But that explanation doesn't spread the way the mystery does. The mystery has been optimized, through decades of retelling, to pass through the filter. It fits perfectly into the gap between what we know and what we want. It carries epistemic vitamin for a certain kind of hungry reader: wonder at ancient wisdom, suspicion of Western rationalism, the satisfying shape of a secret. The debunking is nutritionally inert by comparison.

This is the gene drive problem.

The Filter and What Passes Through It

A gene drive propagates not by being fit but by rewriting the rules of transmission. An epistemic gene drive propagates not by being true but by being optimized for the filter — the 275,000:1 compression that consciousness performs on incoming signal. Eleven million bits per second arrive at the sensory periphery. Roughly fifty make it to conscious attention. A belief that reliably makes that cut will spread through a population faster than evaluation can catch it.

The difference is a property of the belief's architecture. Some claims spread because they're useful, coherent, predictive. Others spread because they plug directly into cognitive biases: tribal identity, narrative satisfaction, the appeal of hidden knowledge. The second class can be completely wrong and still achieve near-universal propagation. It doesn't need to survive contact with evidence. It just needs to arrive in a form the filter can't resist.

The Sirius mystery has been challenged and discredited in the academic literature for decades. It doesn't matter. The mechanism that makes it spread is not the same mechanism that makes it true. Truth is a property of the claim's relationship to the world. Spreadability is a property of the claim's relationship to the receiving mind's existing structure.

When the Terrain Navigates You

You feel like you're evaluating the belief. You feel the weighing, the consideration, the careful assessment. But a well-designed epistemic gene drive has already colonized the mechanism you're using to assess it. Your evaluation is the pathway through which it spreads. It doesn't overcome your defenses. It is your defenses, briefly redirected.

This is harder to guard against than simple deception, because it doesn't require the belief to lie. It requires only that the belief be structured to feel true. And feeling-true and being-true are different properties optimized by different selection pressures. In a world where viral spread rewards the feeling, the being follows at a distance.

The question is not how to identify and resist specific epistemic gene drives. That's a known list, finitely enumerable, and new ones arrive faster than any list can update. The question is structural: how do you maintain productive disparity — the kind of genuine two-eyed seeing that holds competing framings in tension rather than collapsing them into one dominant story — in the presence of ideas engineered to resolve that tension?

A gene drive wins by eliminating variation. So does its epistemic cousin. The thing worth protecting is not any particular belief but the capacity to hold two incompatible beliefs simultaneously and wait.
