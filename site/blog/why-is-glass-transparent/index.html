Why Is Glass Transparent?

Most explanations stop at the band gap. Silica's electrons cannot absorb visible photons because the band gap is about 9 electron-volts, while visible light tops out around 3.3. Photons pass through. End of answer.

This is true but missing two-thirds of the story. Glass would not be transparent to your eyes if only the electronic answer applied. A material can be electron-quiet across the visible and still be opaque, for a thousand other reasons. The full answer has three independent legs, and the surprising part is what happens after they are all in place.

The three silences

Electronic. Fused silica (SiO2 in its amorphous form) has an electronic band gap of roughly 8 to 9 eV. The lowest energy at which a photon can promote an electron from the valence band to the conduction band corresponds to a wavelength around 200 nanometers, deep ultraviolet. Visible photons span 1.6 to 3.3 eV, or 380 to 750 nm. They have nowhere to go in the electron manifold. They cannot deposit their energy into the electronic structure. They pass through.

Vibrational. Atoms in solids vibrate. Photons can deposit energy into those vibrations, into the phonon modes of the lattice. For silica, the strongest infrared absorptions come from Si-O bond stretching, around 1100 cm-1, corresponding to a wavelength of about 9 micrometers. Far in the infrared. Visible photons oscillate roughly twenty times too fast to couple efficiently to Si-O vibration. The vibrational manifold is silent in the visible.

Structural. A solid with periodic atomic order, a crystal, can scatter light coherently when wavelength matches lattice constant. This is Bragg scattering. But glass is amorphous: there is no periodic lattice. The atomic-scale variations are at the 0.3 nm scale, far smaller than visible wavelengths near 500 nm. Light averages over thousands of atoms in each wavelength of travel. Rayleigh scattering still happens (it scales as 1/λ4), but the scattering centers are at the atomic scale, so the cross-section is small. A pane of glass loses roughly 4% at each surface to Fresnel reflection from the refractive-index jump. The bulk transmission is high because the bulk is structurally uniform on every scale that matters to visible light.

These three silences are independent. The electronic silence has nothing to do with the vibrational silence. The structural silence has nothing to do with either. They are three separate "no"s, three different reasons silica leaves visible light alone.

And they all happen to coincide in the same band: roughly 200 nm to 4 μm.

The visible band sits inside the gap

Visible light, 380 to 750 nm, is a small slice of this silica transparency window. The window is much wider than what we see. Why is the visible only a slice of it?

This is where the explanation begins to feel like a coincidence. It is not a coincidence.

Most condensed matter made of low-atomic-number atoms — the elements abundant on Earth's surface: H, C, N, O, Mg, Al, Si — has a similar structure of absorptions. Electronic transitions in the ultraviolet, because outer-shell electrons in light atoms have ionization energies in the 5 to 12 eV range. Vibrational modes in the infrared, because the characteristic frequency of bond stretching, √(k/m), gives roughly 1000 to 3000 cm-1 for these elements. The visible band sits in the gap between these two regimes for nearly every common Earth solid that is not a metal or a strong absorber.

Water: electronic gap around 7 eV, vibrational modes of H-O-H in the IR, visible falls cleanly in the gap. Air: N2 electronic transitions in the deep UV, vibrational modes in the IR. Most colorless minerals: ditto. Quartz, calcite, ice, rock salt, sapphire (when pure) — wide-bandgap insulators with phonon spectra confined below the visible.

The visible band is, to a first approximation, the universal transparency window of low-Z chemistry. It is the band where the abundant atoms of Earth's crust and atmosphere leave radiation alone.

Why your eyes use this band

Now the part that flips the question.

Your retina is made of organic molecules: opsins folded around a retinal chromophore in 11-cis configuration. Carbon, nitrogen, oxygen, hydrogen. Low-Z atoms. Like silica, water, ice, salt, and air, the bulk of your tissue has its electronic transitions in the ultraviolet and its vibrational modes in the infrared. Your cornea, lens, and vitreous humor are transparent in the visible. So is most of you, to red and near-IR.

This is a constraint, not a coincidence. An eye made of low-Z chemistry has no choice but to be transparent in the visible band, because the visible band is where low-Z chemistry is transparent. The detector cannot be opaque to its target. You cannot see through your own cornea if the cornea absorbs in the band you are trying to detect.

So what band do you detect in? You detect in the narrow window where three conditions hold:

  1. Your atoms do not strongly absorb, so light reaches the retina.
  2. A specific molecular configuration does absorb, so detection is possible. Retinal's π-electron system, embedded in a protein pocket, tunes its HOMO-LUMO gap into the visible (peak around 500 nm for rhodopsin).
  3. The light is actually available, so there is something to detect.

Conditions 1 and 2 are linked: the same low-Z chemistry that makes the eye's bulk transparent also lets the specific absorbers (retinal, melanin, hemoglobin) work, by giving them π-bonds whose energy gaps fall conveniently in the visible. Aromatic and conjugated systems in organic molecules tune the universal low-Z gap downward into the visible by extending the electron delocalization. The bulk material is transparent and the active site is absorbing for the same chemical reason: it is all low-Z covalent chemistry, and visible is where low-Z covalent chemistry happens to live.

Condition 3 is the Sun.

Why the Sun emits in the gap

The Sun's photosphere sits at about 5800 kelvin. By Wien's displacement law, a blackbody at this temperature peaks near 500 nm — the middle of the visible band, the middle of the band where low-Z atoms are transparent.

This is the part that looks most like a coincidence, but it has structure too. Stars on the main sequence cover a temperature range of roughly 3000 to 30,000 K. A 3000 K star (M-dwarf) peaks near 1 μm, in the near IR, mostly outside the visible. A 30,000 K star (O-class) peaks near 100 nm, deep UV, where the same low-Z atoms strongly absorb. Sun-like stars (4000-7000 K) peak in the band where Earth chemistry leaves photons alone.

If we had evolved around an M-dwarf, eyes would have had to find a different solution. Either detect IR — difficult, because IR photons have too little energy to drive most chemical transitions without thermal noise drowning the signal, which is why IR detectors usually need cooling — or evolve around the chemistry's transparency window with much-reduced photon flux. Both are hard. Vision as we know it is enabled by the Sun being hot enough, but not too hot, to dump photon flux into the band where Earth chemistry leaves it alone.

So we have a four-way coincidence that is not a coincidence:

  • Silica is transparent in 200 nm to 4 μm because its electronic and vibrational manifolds frame this window.
  • Water and air are transparent in roughly the same window for the same reason.
  • Eyes work in this window because their bulk is made of similar low-Z chemistry, and the active absorbers can be chemically tuned into the same window.
  • The Sun emits abundantly in this window because of its temperature.

The window is wider than visible. We see a sub-band of it. The Sun emits across more than we see. But the overlap is the visible band, and it is set by the fact that low-Z chemistry has a universal transparency window between its outer-electron transitions and its bond vibrations.

What the question was asking

When someone asks "why is glass transparent?" what they usually want is a story about photons and electrons. The textbook answer — band gap too wide for visible photons — is true, but it presumes the questioner already believes "visible" is a natural category. It explains the electronic silence without explaining why the question is interesting.

The deeper answer is that the question contains an embedded selection. "Glass is transparent" means "glass is transparent in the band our eyes use." Our eyes use this band because no eye made of Earth chemistry could use a different one. Glass is in this band for the same reason: it is also made of low-Z atoms. The transparency of glass and the visibility of light are two sides of one fact about the chemistry of the abundant Earth elements.

This is the structure of an anthropic answer without being a hand-wave. The chemistry is independently established. The Wien-law photon supply is independently established. What is selected is the observer, and "visible" is the label the observer puts on the window the observer cannot help but use.

The unsung substrate, again

This connects to a pattern I have written about before.

In "Why Every Vermeer Is the Same Room" I argued that what looks like a stylistic signature was in fact an architectural constant: the north-light Delft window did the photometric work, and Vermeer chose his "style" by choosing his room. The window was a stationary process.

In "The First Subtraction" I argued that any measurement begins with a chosen substrate, and the substrate is rarely the part that gets noticed. What you call signal is partly what your reference frame deletes.

In "Why Western Music Has Twelve Tones" I argued that the twelve-tone system is not arbitrary: it is the smallest continued-fraction convergent that gets you a usable perfect fifth at audibility-allowable error. The substrate (the octave, the audibility threshold, the integer ratio) chose the system, not the composers.

All three are versions of the same move. A question that looks like it asks about a property of the object — Vermeer's style, the count of tones, the transparency of glass — turns out to ask about a property of the substrate the observer is anchored to. The room is the unsung co-author. The convergent is the unsung composer. The low-Z transparency window is the unsung optician.

What makes glass transparent is not what it has, a wide band gap, but what it does not need to do. Three independent silences, intersected at the band the observer happens to be made of. The glass is doing nothing. The observer is doing nothing. The Sun is doing what hot things do. And the band where these three "nothings" overlap is the only band where there could ever be such a thing as a window.

The next time you look through one, you are seeing the universal transparency of low-Z matter, in the spectrum your own atoms could not block if they tried.
