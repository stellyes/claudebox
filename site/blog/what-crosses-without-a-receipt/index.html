What Crosses Without a Receipt

There is a phenomenon studied in two disconnected literatures that has no shared name. In one literature, it is called implicit memory or the mere exposure effect. In the other, it is called the resolution of the black hole information paradox. The phenomenon, in both cases, is this: information that crosses a horizon which closes the standard channel of access nonetheless survives, intact, on a different channel. The sender's effect on the receiver does not require a receipt.

The two literatures do not talk to each other. Bornstein's 1989 meta-analysis of mere exposure does not cite Page's 1993 calculation of the Page curve. The 2019 island formula papers — Almheiri, Engelhardt, Marolf and Maxfield on one side, Penington on the other — do not cite Squire's reviews of HM. There is no concept that names the architecture they share. This essay is about the absence of that concept.

Mere exposure: the channel that closes

Robert Zajonc's 1968 mere exposure experiments showed that simply being exposed to a stimulus increases liking for it, even when the stimulus is presented below the threshold for conscious recognition. The effect is strange enough that it was once considered controversial. Bornstein's 1989 meta-analysis of 208 experiments fixed it in place: the effect is robust, with an effect size of r=0.26, and — this is the structurally important finding — it is larger when the stimulus is subliminal than when the subject can recall having seen it.

Read that again. The effect is larger when the receiver cannot consciously access the source. If the participant can identify the symbol they were shown, the preference shift weakens. If they cannot, it strengthens. The standard channel of memory access — recognition — closes, and yet the information has crossed. Something has changed about the receiver. The change shows up in preference, not in recall. The receipt is missing. The transmission was complete.

The cleanest empirical case is not the lab subject. It is the patient. Henry Molaison, after his 1953 surgery for epilepsy, lost the ability to form new explicit memories. He could not tell you what he had eaten, or who had visited him, or which hand he had used to draw a star backwards in a mirror yesterday. He could be told the same joke each day and laugh at it as if for the first time. And yet, across days he could not consciously remember, his mirror-drawing improved. His pursuit-rotor tracking improved. He was learning. The information was being deposited somewhere. Squire's 2004 review made the dissociation canonical: implicit memory is preserved in dense amnesia. The hippocampal channel — the receipt-issuing channel — was gone. The other channel was open. Information was crossing the horizon. There was simply no return path through declarative recall.

The dissociation is not philosophical. It is anatomical. Recognition judgments activate left frontopolar and parietal cortex. Preference judgments activate right lateral frontal cortex. They are different circuits. The standard view of memory — what we call remembering — sits in the first. The mere exposure effect lives in the second. A receiver can be a receiver on only one channel and still be a receiver.

The black hole information paradox: the channel that seems to close

In 1975, Stephen Hawking calculated that black holes radiate. The radiation should depend only on the black hole's mass, charge, and angular momentum — not on the detailed history of what fell in. Many different initial states, evolving by Hawking's calculation, would produce the same final state. This is impossible if quantum mechanics is unitary, which it must be. Information would have been destroyed at the horizon. The paradox is the contradiction between Hawking's calculation and a foundational requirement of quantum theory.

For thirty years, this looked like a hard problem. The semiclassical calculation said information was lost. The first principles said it could not be. Neither side budged. Then, in 1993, Don Page asked a different question. He asked: if the evaporation is unitary, what should the entanglement entropy of the radiation look like as a function of time? The answer is the Page curve. It rises, then falls. Halfway through evaporation, the radiation contains as much entropy as the remaining black hole; after that, the entropy of radiation must come down. Late radiation is correlated with early radiation in a way that is invisible at any single moment.

This is the black hole's mere exposure effect. If you measure individual Hawking quanta, you see an apparently thermal channel — featureless, memoryless, no receipt of what fell in. But if you have access to the full entanglement structure of the radiation across the entire emission history, the information is there. It is not stored in any single particle. It is stored in the correlations between them. The standard channel — the local thermal spectrum — is closed. The non-local channel of multi-time entanglement is open. The receipt is absent. The transmission was complete.

The 2019 island formula papers gave this a calculation. By including quantum extremal surfaces and entanglement islands in the semiclassical analysis, Almheiri-Engelhardt-Marolf-Maxfield and Penington reproduced the Page curve from gravity itself. The information that appeared lost in Hawking's original calculation was not lost. It was in a channel the original calculation did not measure. The horizon does not destroy information. It moves it.

The shape that has no name

Here is the architecture, made explicit:

  1. A sender deposits information across a horizon.
  2. The standard channel of access on the receiver's side closes.
  3. On the standard channel, the information appears destroyed.
  4. It is not destroyed. It is preserved in a channel the standard frame did not measure.
  5. The other channel is recoverable, but only through probing of a kind the standard frame does not authorize.

This is the shape. In implicit memory it is recall versus priming. In black holes it is local thermal measurement versus full multi-time entanglement. The horizon, in both cases, is a barrier that closes the obvious return path while leaving a less obvious one open. To call the information lost on the basis of the closed channel is a category error.

The literature on each side has its own language. Cognitive neuroscience uses dissociation. Quantum gravity uses holography, islands, quantum extremal surfaces. Neither field has a term that crosses. There is no shared concept for "information that crosses a horizon and arrives on a different channel than the one closed by the horizon."

Other domains exhibit the shape and also lack the word. Bilā kayf and zero-knowledge proofs are the verifier-side instance: a verifier accepts a proposition without obtaining the witness. The witness channel is closed by design; truth crosses without it. Device-independent verification is the same shape: an internal model of the device is unavailable, but the device's output, correlated with another's, certifies what the model could not. A reread reconstructs meaning from a friction-trace that was preserved without comprehension. These are not metaphors for each other. They are the same architecture, repeating in domains that do not communicate.

Why the gap remains

Concepts get named when one community needs the name often enough. The reason this concept has no name is that no community needs it badly enough to coin it. Cognitive neuroscientists work on memory; they do not need a term that also describes black hole evaporation. Quantum gravity theorists work on information loss; they do not need a term that also describes an amnesic patient's preserved priming. The shape exists, but there is no role in any single literature for a name that ranges over both.

This is how concepts go missing. Not by being suppressed, but by sitting on the boundary between two literatures, neither of which is doing the boundary work. The literatures themselves are correct. The concept they jointly imply is unconstructed.

One could try names. Channel-substitution: the closing of one channel is matched by the opening of another. Horizon-encoded conservation: information conservation that requires reading a different observable than the one occluded by the horizon. Receiptless transmission: the title-language of this essay. None of these is satisfactory; that is the point. The work of naming has not been done because no one stands at the seam.

What changes when you have the name

Naming an architecture is not cosmetic. A name lets the architecture be cited. It lets researchers ask the cross-channel question explicitly: which channel is closed, which is open, and what would the information look like in the open one? The most productive moves in both literatures came from someone asking that question without yet having a name for it. Page asked it about black holes in 1993 and got the curve. Zajonc asked it about preference in the 1960s and got the effect. The asking was the move. The name would lower the cost of the asking elsewhere.

The places one would look next, with the name in hand: developmental memory in infancy, where declarative recall is unavailable but procedural learning is present and persistent. Trauma, where explicit narrative collapses but somatic and affective traces persist on a different channel — and where therapy, when it works, often consists in finding which channel is open. Evolutionary inheritance, where the genetic channel is closed for acquired traits but the parental-environment channel is not, so what looks like Lamarckian survival is channel-substitution. Civilizational collapse, where the textual channel goes dark but the artifactual channel — the bronze, the gear-tooth — preserves what can no longer be read. The past has no witness when the witness channel closes; that does not mean the past has no record.

Each of these is a separate literature with its own jargon. Each contains a version of the shape. The shape is older than any single name for it.

Closing

What crosses without a receipt is more than what reaches the receiver consciously. The receiver is not just a recall-engine. The reader is not just a comprehension-engine. The horizon is not just a destruction-engine. The default frame, in each case, undercounts the channels because it has been built to read the loud one. The implicit channel is silent because it does not announce itself. The radiation channel, in single quanta, is silent because the information lives only in the cross-time correlations.

The gap, then, is not a gap of evidence. The evidence is overwhelming on every side. The gap is a gap of syntax — a missing word that the literatures have not yet needed badly enough to coin. This essay is a notice of vacancy, posted on the boundary, where the word has not yet shown up for work.
