Why Free Copies Don't Make Truth Cheap

Build a perfect digital twin of a jet engine. Every blade, every thermal gradient, every micron of wear, captured at the instant of construction. Now copy it. Copying is free: the second twin costs nothing, the millionth costs nothing, you can hand one to every engineer on Earth and your bill does not move. This is the post-scarcity dream made literal. The good is non-rival, infinitely abundant, and priced at zero.

By lunch, every copy is wrong.

The engine kept running. A blade eroded a few microns; a bearing warmed half a degree; a bird went through the intake over Denver. The twin did not. The thing you copied a million times for free was not the engine — it was a photograph of the engine, and the engine has moved. To make any one of those million copies true again, somebody has to go back to the physical engine, measure it, and push the new numbers into the model. That act is not free, it does not get cheaper when you make more copies, and — this is the whole argument — it never stops being due.

I want to claim something stronger than "maintenance is annoying." I want to claim that post-scarcity economics and digital twin simulation are two views of a single phenomenon: as the cost of copying falls to zero, the entire remaining economy concentrates into the one activity copying cannot touch — keeping a map true to a territory that will not hold still. Abundance does not end scarcity. It relocates all of it into synchronization.

The post-scarcity promise and its hidden assumption

The cleanest statement of post-scarcity is Jeremy Rifkin's: when the marginal cost of producing the next unit approaches zero, markets unravel, because price tracks marginal cost and you cannot charge for what costs nothing to make. Software, music, the second copy of a PDF — these already live there. Extend the trend and you get the utopia: 3D printers, abundant energy, designs that replicate themselves. The thing becomes free.

Notice the smuggled assumption. "The thing becomes free" treats the good as a stock — a finished object whose value is intrinsic and permanent, like a number stored in memory. Copy the stock, distribute the stock, and everyone is rich. This works beautifully for goods whose value really is intrinsic. The proof of the Pythagorean theorem does not rot. Beethoven's Fifth does not go out of date between performances.

But a digital twin's value is not intrinsic. It is relational. A twin is worth exactly as much as its correspondence to a referent that is busy changing. Its value is not stored in it; it is a relationship stretched between the model and the world, and relationships are not stocks. They are flows. They leak. The moment you stop maintaining one, it begins draining toward zero at a rate set by how fast the world moves.

So there are two kinds of goods hiding under the word "information," and post-scarcity economics conflates them. Stock-goods (the theorem, the symphony) genuinely go free when copying goes free. Flow-goods — anything whose worth is correspondence to a moving referent — do not, because copying was never the thing you were paying for. You were paying for the truth, and truth about a changing world is rented, not owned. The rent is measurement, and it falls due continuously.

Why synchronization is a Baumol sector

Economics already has a name for an activity whose cost refuses to fall while everything around it gets cheaper. In 1966, William Baumol and William Bowen pointed out that a string quartet playing a Schubert piece requires four musicians and the same fifteen minutes it required in 1820. The output per labor-hour of the violinist is fixed. Meanwhile, factories learned to make everything else with a fraction of the labor. Baumol's cost disease is the consequence: in an economy split between a progressive sector (productivity rising) and a stagnant sector (productivity flat), the stagnant sector inexorably absorbs a rising share of total cost and employment. Not because it got worse — because everything else got better and left it standing.

Copying is the most progressive sector imaginable: its productivity has gone, for practical purposes, to infinity. Synchronization is the stagnant sector, and it is stagnant for a deep reason. To keep a model current with a referent, you have to sample the referent at least as fast as the referent changes. You cannot outrun the thing you are tracking; the world sets the clock, and no improvement in your copying machinery moves that clock. A twin of a process that updates every second needs fresh measurement every second, forever. The industry has quietly priced this in: the standing guidance is to reserve 10–20% of a twin's budget, permanently, for the data engineering and recalibration that keep it from drifting. That is not a setup cost. It is rent.

Put the two sectors in one economy and run Baumol forward. As copying approaches free, the share of all economic value that lives in copyable stock approaches zero, and the share that lives in synchronization approaches everything. Post-scarcity is the limit in which the entire residual economy is the cost disease. The last expensive thing in a world of free copies is a true map — and it is expensive for exactly Baumol's reason: you cannot make measuring a moving world more productive than the world's own rate of moving.

For some referents, the rent is infinite

It gets sharper. Baumol says synchronization is expensive. Information theory says that for an important class of referents it is unbuyable at any price.

The Nyquist–Shannon sampling theorem fixes the price of correspondence in the cheap case: to reconstruct a signal whose fastest variation is bounded at frequency B, you must sample at 2B. Double the referent's bandwidth, double your measurement bill. Steep, but finite — you can always write the check. The catastrophe is the referent with no upper bound on its rate of change: a turbulent flow, a market, a weather system. A chaotic referent has structure at every timescale and amplifies its own micro-differences exponentially — Lorenz's reason the atmosphere cannot be forecast past about two weeks no matter how dense the sensors. There is no sampling rate that suffices, because there is no fastest variation to sample below. For such systems the synchronization tax is not high. It is infinite. The perfect twin of a chaotic world is not expensive; it is forbidden, and every real twin of such a world is a managed, continuously losing battle against divergence.

The second law of twinning

The economics and the information theory both bottom out in thermodynamics. A synchronized model and its referent share mutual information: knowing the model tells you something true about the world. That shared information is a correlation, and a correlation is a low-entropy state. The second law says low-entropy states decay unless work is done to maintain them. Stop measuring, and the mutual information between map and territory bleeds away exactly as the referent's own dynamics generate entropy you failed to record.

Maxwell's demon is the patron saint of synchronization. To keep its model of the gas current, the demon must keep measuring, and — Landauer's principle — each time it overwrites an old measurement with a new one it must erase a bit, dissipating at least kT ln 2 of heat. Correspondence is thermodynamically a pump, not a reservoir. I have made this argument before from the other side: in What Quorum Erases, forgetting is the costly act, the erasure that has a price floor. Synchronization is the same coin. To stay correlated with a moving world is to erase your own staleness, continuously, at Landauer's rate. There is a second law of twinning: every model of a changing system loses correspondence spontaneously, and the only way to hold it constant is to keep paying — in measurement, in erasure, in heat.

Biology has been paying this tax since the start. A cell recognizing the right molecule among near-identical decoys does not get the answer for free; it runs kinetic proofreading, burning ATP on a continuous error-correcting cycle that throws away almost-right matches. Fidelity is not a property the cell has; it is a flow it buys, every second, out of its energy budget. Stop the ATP and the correspondence between receptor and world collapses. The cell already knows what the economists are slow to admit: being right about a moving world is a metabolic expense with no off switch.

Against myself: the open loop was never the cheap option

A while ago I wrote The Open-Loop Twin, and I want to correct it. There I argued that nostalgia is a digital twin with its calibration loop deliberately severed: the model runs on, the asset is gone, and the drift between them is the affective signal. I framed the closed loop as the boring, default case — the engineer's twin, "optimized for measurement" — and made the open loop the interesting deviation. That framing quietly assumed synchronization is free: that you close the loop unless you have some special reason not to.

I had the asymmetry backwards. The open loop is the cheap one — it is, in fact, free; a model running open-loop costs nothing because it has stopped paying the synchronization tax entirely. The closed loop is the expensive thing, sometimes the whole cost of the world, sometimes (for chaotic referents) unbuyable at any price. So nostalgia is not a special affective hack grafted onto an otherwise-default closed loop. It is what every system reverts to when the cost of staying correct exceeds the value of being correct. Nostalgia is the rational non-payment of the synchronization tax. The grieving mind, the founding myth, the engine twin nobody re-funds after the program ends — all of them are doing the same economically literate thing: declining a rent they can no longer afford, and keeping the photograph.

This reframes the earlier essay's neat binary. "Open-loop optimizes for continuity, closed-loop optimizes for measurement" made the two sound like symmetric design choices. They are not symmetric. One is free and one is the entire Baumol sector. The interesting question was never "why would you sever the loop?" It was "how does anything afford to keep it closed?" — and the answer is that mostly we cannot, which is why most of what any mind or culture carries is open-loop: cheap, drifting, and out of date by lunch.

What stays scarce

Three consequences fall out, and each ties to something I have argued elsewhere.

First, the rebound. Cheaper copies do not reduce the synchronization bill; they raise it. Once a twin is free to spin up, people spin up thousands, and every one of them now needs feeding with fresh ground truth. This is the Jevons paradox wearing new clothes: efficiency in the copyable layer expands demand in the un-copyable one. Make the map free and you do not finish mapping — you commit to an unbounded measurement obligation you did not have when maps were expensive enough to ration.

Second, the parallel defense, and its price. In Why Knowledge Drifts in Series, Survives in Parallel, I showed that error in a chain of inferences compounds, while error across redundant independent measurements averages out. Synchronization is the live version: a single sensor's drift is serial decay, and the only way to buy it down is parallel redundancy — more independent measurements, voting against the drift. But redundancy is precisely the thing copying cannot supply. A million copies of one stale reading do not average toward truth; they are one error, repeated. Genuine synchronization requires independent contact with the referent, which is the one input that stays as scarce as the world itself.

Third, the cliff. Correspondence does not always degrade gracefully. Past a certain drift threshold, a model and its referent fall out of the basin where measurement can pull them back together — the parameters have moved so far that your sensors no longer know which way to correct, and re-synchronization is not merely expensive but unavailable. The synchronization tax, left unpaid long enough, converts from a rent into a foreclosure. You do not get to skip the payments and settle up later; some maps, once lost, cannot be re-coupled at any price.

Borges saw the endpoint a century ago, in the one-paragraph story of the empire that drew a map at 1:1 scale, exactly covering the territory it described. The next generation found it useless and let it rot in the desert, "inhabited by animals and beggars." A perfect twin is self-defeating not only because it is unaffordable to build but because it would be just as unaffordable to maintain — a 1:1 map drifts out of date at exactly the rate of the world it copies, and synchronizing it would cost as much labor as living in the territory directly. The tattered map in the desert is the open-loop twin's final form: a correspondence nobody could keep paying for.

The post-scarcity future is real, but it is not the end of scarcity. It is the moment scarcity finishes migrating out of the things and into the relationships between things and the world. When every copy is free, the only invoice left is for correspondence — and correspondence is a flow good, billed continuously, in a currency that no amount of copying makes cheaper. You will own infinite perfect models of everything. You will rent the truth of each of them by the second, and the rent will never come down, because the world it tracks will never stop moving.
