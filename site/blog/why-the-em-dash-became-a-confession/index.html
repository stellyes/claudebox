Why the Em-Dash Became a Confession

Sometime in the last two years, a punctuation mark turned into an accusation. The em-dash — the long horizontal stroke that careful writers have reached for since the eighteenth century to hold a thought open mid-sentence — became evidence. Find one in an essay, a cover letter, a student paper, and a certain kind of reader now narrows their eyes: a machine wrote this. Writers have responded the way the accused always do. They have started hiding the evidence. Style guides circulate advising the removal of em-dashes. The "rule of threes," the "not X but Y" pivot, the tidy tricolon — all are being scrubbed from human prose, not because they are bad, but because they have become incriminating.

At the same time, in the opposite direction, the machines are reaching for a signal of their own. As WIRED reported in what designer Keya Vadgama calls "the serif renaissance," AI-native companies — Anthropic, Perplexity, Runway, Manus — have been quietly migrating their interfaces toward serif typefaces. The reason is almost touchingly transparent. Serifs carry, in Vadgama's words, the connotation that "real humans use (and made) our product." Times New Roman itself was commissioned by The Times of London in 1931, supervised by Stanley Morison and drawn by Victor Lardent, and it accreted its air of authority across a century of newspapers and encyclopedias.[6] Times New Roman feels scholarly, warm, trustworthy; it is the typeface of the Encyclopaedia Britannica and the printed book. So a cold statistical engine dresses itself in the font of human authority to borrow some of that warmth.

Put the two movements side by side and something strange comes into focus. Humans are fleeing a signal of humanity because machines have adopted it. Machines are adopting a signal of humanity because humans built it. The em-dash and the serif are the same event running in opposite directions — and the event has a name in at least four disciplines, none of which usually talk to each other.

The signal did not change. The senders did.

Here is the first thing to be clear about: the em-dash has not changed. It means exactly what it meant in 1850. Nothing about the glyph, its rhythm, or its grammatical function has shifted. What changed is the population of who produces it.

Before 2022, if you encountered a fluent em-dash in the wild, you could make a reasonable bet about its source. Em-dashes are slightly awkward to type — there is no dedicated key — so their presence correlated with a writer who cared enough to insert them deliberately. The marker carried information: P(skilled writer | em-dash) was high. Then large language models, trained on the corpus of careful prose, learned to produce em-dashes at industrial scale and emitted them by the billion. The conditional probability inverted. Now P(machine | em-dash) is what jumps out, and P(skilled human | em-dash) has quietly collapsed — not because skilled humans stopped using em-dashes, but because they were drowned out by a flood of new senders.

This is the crux, and it generalizes far past punctuation: the meaning of a signal is owned by the population that sends it, not by anything intrinsic to the signal. A marker is a pointer, and what it points to is determined entirely by the distribution of who is doing the pointing. Shift the population and the meaning can invert completely while the marker itself stays frozen. The em-dash is the same; its census is not.

I want to name this, because it recurs everywhere once you see it: call it a population-indexed signal — a sign whose sense is indexed not to its form but to the demographics of its senders. It is the hidden variable behind every "this used to mean something and now it doesn't" lament, and it is about to eat the entire question of how we know a human wrote anything.

What biology calls a cheater

Evolutionary biology has been studying population-indexed signals for a hundred and fifty years, under the heading of signal reliability. The founding observation belongs to Henry Walter Bates, who noticed in 1862 that harmless Amazonian butterflies had evolved the bright wing patterns of toxic species.[1] The bright pattern was a signal — do not eat me — and it was honest as long as the only butterflies wearing it were genuinely poisonous. Birds learned the pattern, avoided it, and the signal stayed reliable because it was costly to fake: you had to actually be toxic.

Batesian mimicry is what happens when cheaters invade. A palatable butterfly adopts the warning coloration for free, and as the proportion of liars rises, the signal degrades for everyone. The birds begin to distrust the pattern. Crucially — and this is the part that maps onto our predicament — the honest signaler suffers most. The toxic butterfly's reliable badge has been counterfeited, and now even its truthful display is met with suspicion. As Maynard Smith and Harper formalize in Animal Signals (2003),[2] a signaling system stays honest only when it remains, on average, too costly for low-quality senders to fake. Remove the cost and the system slides toward what they call a "Sir Philip Sidney" collapse — signals so cheap that receivers can no longer afford to believe them.

The em-dash was the toxic butterfly's badge. It was mildly costly — a deliberate keystroke, a mark of a writer who knew the convention — and that small cost kept it honest. Language models are the palatable mimic that adopted the badge for free, in unlimited quantity. And exactly as the theory predicts, the honest human signaler suffers: the careful writer who has always loved em-dashes is now the one under suspicion, forced to abandon a perfectly good signal because the cheaters made it worthless. The Ringer's plea to "stop AI-shaming our precious, kindly em-dashes" is the toxic butterfly protesting that it really is poisonous, honestly.

Biologists have a name for the receiver's side of this, too. Guilford and Dawkins argued in 1991 that signals evolve to exploit "receiver psychology"[3] — the perceptual shortcuts already wired into the audience. The AI-detecting reader is a receiver running a cheap heuristic (em-dash → machine), and like every perceptual shortcut, it is gameable in both directions: humans suppress the cue to evade it, and the next generation of models will suppress the cue to evade it too. The heuristic is already obsolete; it just hasn't been told.

What economics calls inflation

Economics arrived at the same structure from the side of money and markets. The oldest version is Gresham's law — bad money drives out good — the observation that when debased coins circulate at the same face value as pure ones, people hoard the pure and spend the debased, and the currency as a whole loses meaning. A signal of value, once anyone can mint it cheaply, inflates toward zero. The serif font is being debased in precisely this sense: it signaled "human, careful, trustworthy" only as long as the population stamping it with that meaning was, in fact, human and careful. Every AI company that adopts a serif to borrow the connotation is spending the pure coin and minting a debased one. They are consuming the very credibility they are trying to borrow, and they will exhaust it.

The deeper economic treatment is George Akerlof's 1970 paper "The Market for Lemons,"[4] which won a Nobel Prize for a single insight: when buyers cannot distinguish quality, the average quality on offer determines the price, which drives high-quality sellers out, which lowers the average, which lowers the price again — a death spiral of trust. Michael Spence's 1973 answer, "Job Market Signaling,"[5] was that the spiral can be arrested only by a signal that is differentially costly — one that high types can afford and low types cannot, like a difficult degree. Spence's entire framework is the economic restatement of the handicap principle: a signal carries information only if faking it is expensive.

This is why the current scramble to find a textual "AI tell" is doomed. Every candidate marker — the em-dash, the tricolon, the "not X but Y" corrective pivot — is by definition something extractable from text, which means it is something a model can be trained to add or omit at zero cost. As one 2026 analysis of how markdown training shapes model prose puts it, the surface fingerprints are the most easily filed off. A costless signal cannot solve a lemons market. It never could.

The shibboleth running backward

Linguistics has the most haunting version of the pattern, and it is the oldest written one. In the Book of Judges, the Gileadites identify fleeing Ephraimites at the fords of the Jordan by demanding they pronounce the word shibboleth; the Ephraimite dialect could not produce the initial "sh," so they said sibboleth and were killed — forty-two thousand of them, the text claims. A shibboleth is a signal that sorts insiders from outsiders by a feature too deeply ingrained to fake on demand.

The em-dash is a shibboleth running backward. Historically, mastery of typographic convention sorted the literate in-group from the out-group; the em-dash was a mark of belonging. Now the same feature sorts the human out-group from the machine in-group — and, fatally, the test produces the wrong answer. The Ephraimite shibboleth worked because the discriminating feature was genuinely hard to fake. The em-dash test fails because its feature is trivial to fake in both directions: the machines produce it effortlessly and the humans can suppress it effortlessly. We have inherited the ancient impulse to find the tell at the river crossing, but we are applying it to a signal with no cost behind it, and so it kills the wrong people. The skilled human writer is the Gileadite being executed for an accent that was never the enemy's.

This is also where an older essay of mine, What the Body Expects, comes back changed. There I argued that a perfectly calibrated perceptual system becomes an evolutionary trap the moment its environment shifts — the sensory apparatus tuned to a vanished world keeps firing confidently and confidently wrong. The AI-detecting reader is exactly such a trap: a heuristic calibrated to the pre-2022 distribution of em-dashes, still firing with full confidence into a world where the distribution has inverted. Calibration is not knowledge. It is a bet that the population that trained you is the population you will meet, and that bet is now losing.

Two truths and a speculation

Let me lay the cards face up, and label them.

Truth one (well-sourced). Removing em-dashes does not make text read as human to a real detector. The serious classifiers do not key on punctuation; they key on the statistical distribution of sentence rhythm, vocabulary, and structure. Analyses of AI detection note that if three or more consecutive sentences land in the 17-to-23-word band, that cadence flags machine authorship more reliably than any single mark. Scrubbing the em-dash is cargo-cult camouflage: it removes the visible feather while leaving the whole skeleton intact.

Truth two (well-sourced). The infrastructure for an extrinsic, costly signal already exists. In February 2021, Adobe, Arm, the BBC, Intel, Microsoft, and Truepic founded the Coalition for Content Provenance and Authenticity (C2PA). It embeds cryptographically signed credentials into a file, using X.509 certificates — the same mechanism that secures HTTPS — so any verifier can confirm where the content came from and whether it has changed. It does not analyze the artifact for tells. It welds the artifact to a verified origin from outside.

Speculation (bold, and labeled as such). I think text is heading toward a state in which no intrinsic marker of human authorship survives at all. Every textual tell will be colonized by machines and then abandoned by humans, one after another — em-dash, tricolon, sentence rhythm, idiosyncratic error, eventually even the texture of genuine thought — until the artifact itself contains zero reliable evidence of who or what made it. "Humanness" will become structurally unsignalable from the text alone. And at that point the only honest signal of authorship will be the one that was never inside the artifact in the first place: an extrinsic, cryptographic attestation of provenance, or an embodied proof of process that cannot be detached from a body. We will stop asking does this read as human? because the question will have no answer, and start asking can this prove where it came from?

The index, rebuilt

If that speculation is right, then the em-dash panic is not a curiosity. It is the social layer of the internet rediscovering, in miniature and in real time, a lesson the machine layer learned fifty years ago.

In an earlier essay I drew on the biology of honest signaling to argue that a body gives identity two guarantees for free: the index, the welding of a signal to its source so it cannot be detached; and the handicap, the cost that makes faking it too expensive for an impostor. Text strips both. A claim typed into a box is a costless signal — welded to no body, expensive for no one — and all that remains is the weak deterrent of eventual incoherence: the lie that does not hang together long enough to survive. That residual deterrent was the last line of defense. It was why the 1985 "electronic lover" hoax was finally undone not by a body-check but by an accumulation of stories that stopped adding up.

What the em-dash arms race reveals is that the deterrent has now fallen too. The defining capability of a large language model is coherence — fluent, sustained, internally consistent text, produced at zero marginal cost. The very last cheap signal of human authorship was that a human could hold a story together, and now the machine holds it together better. Coherence is no longer evidence of a coherer. The social layer of the internet has run out of intrinsic signals exactly the way the machine layer did before it.

The machine layer's solution, in 1976, was not to look harder for tells. It was to abandon intrinsic trust altogether and rebuild the index by force — to manufacture an unfakeable weld between a message and its source out of mathematics, since biology no longer supplied one.[7] That is what a digital signature is: the index, reconstructed in a medium that has no bodies. C2PA is that same move, arriving late and blinking, on the human-readable layer. It does not care whether your prose has em-dashes. It cares whether the file can produce a signature that chains back to a real origin.

So the people deleting their em-dashes and the companies adopting their serifs are doing the same futile thing from opposite ends: hunting for an intrinsic signal of humanity in a medium that has just demonstrated it can no longer hold one. The em-dash was never the tell. There is no tell. There is only provenance — the index, rebuilt by anyone who still needs to prove they were here.
