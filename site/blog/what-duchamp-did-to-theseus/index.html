What Duchamp Did to Theseus

Two and a half thousand years ago, the Athenians kept a ship docked in the harbor as a memorial to the hero Theseus. Every year they took it out to sail a religious festival route. Wood rots, so over the decades they replaced the rotting planks one by one. Eventually, every original board was gone.

Plutarch noted this around the year 100 and wrote that the philosophers couldn't agree what to make of it: was this the same ship?

In the 1650s the English philosopher Thomas Hobbes made the puzzle worse. Suppose, he said, that someone had been gathering the discarded planks all along, and at the end they reassembled the original ship from those rotting boards. Now there are two ships. Both have a claim on being Theseus's ship. Which is the real one?

People have been arguing about this for twenty-five hundred years. It turns out the puzzle has an answer. The answer was not produced by a philosopher. It was produced by a urinal.

The Urinal

In 1917, the French artist Marcel Duchamp bought a porcelain urinal at a plumbing supply store in New York City. He laid it flat, signed it "R. Mutt 1917" in black paint, and submitted it to an art show as a sculpture called Fountain. The committee was scandalized. They refused to display it. Duchamp had made his point anyway: a thing becomes art the moment a particular kind of person places it in a particular kind of room. Nothing about the porcelain has to change.

This was a Dadaist move. Dada was an art movement that started during the First World War. Its members believed that the conventional standards of "good art" had helped produce the carnage in the trenches, and they wanted to find a way to make art that refused those standards. So they did things like read pure-noise poetry on stage, glue scraps of newspaper into collages, and pick up objects from the street and put them in galleries. The point wasn't to make the gallery objects beautiful. The point was to ask: who decides what counts?

Duchamp's urinal was the sharpest version of the question. Almost nothing else in art history is so plainly a thing-that-isn't-art being treated as a thing-that-is. And the museums went along with it.

The original Fountain was thrown out shortly after the show. We know this because Duchamp later told the story. In the 1950s and 1960s, decades after the original was gone, Duchamp authorized seventeen replicas, made by other people from new urinals. Today these replicas sit in major museums — the Tate Modern in London, the Centre Pompidou in Paris, the Museum of Modern Art in New York. Each is presented as Fountain. Each is "the work." When art historians refer to Fountain, they don't mean the original urinal — that's gone — they mean whichever replica is in the room.

The Same Trick, Flipped Inside Out

Now look back at Theseus's ship. The two cases are doing the same trick, but mirror-imaged.

Theseus's ship had its planks slowly swapped while everyone agreed it was still the same ship. Fountain had every original part destroyed, then was remanufactured from scratch many times, and we still call all of them Fountain.

Both objects survive complete substrate replacement. The wood doesn't carry the identity of Theseus's ship; the porcelain doesn't carry the identity of Fountain. So what does?

For Theseus's ship, what carried the identity was the practice. Every year the Athenians took it out and sailed the festival route. As long as that ritual continued, the ship continued. The planks weren't load-bearing. The festival was. If the Athenians had instead frozen the ship into a museum diorama and then started swapping planks, the puzzle would feel different. Without the festival, the planks were all there is.

For Fountain, what carried the identity was the declaration. Duchamp pointed at it and said "art." The signature was the act. There is no festival, no ritual journey — there is just the placement and the naming. The ground holding the identity is paper-thin: a name, an authorization, a context. But it is enough.

These are two ways of doing the same thing, and they sit at opposite ends of a spectrum. On one end, identity rides on continued practice. On the other, identity rides on a single naming act.

Almost everything else sits between.

A Test You Can Run

Once you see this, you can ask a useful question about anything in your life: what is carrying its identity right now? The substrate? A practice? A declaration? Something else?

The question has a built-in test. Try removing each candidate, one at a time, and see if the thing survives.

  • If you replace the planks one by one and it still feels like the same ship, the planks weren't carrying the identity.
  • If you take the festival away and it stops feeling like the ship, the festival was.
  • If you take Duchamp's authorization away from the urinal in the museum and it suddenly stops being art, the authorization was load-bearing.

A river carries its identity by practice — by the water continually flowing in roughly the same channel. Dam the river, redirect the water, and very quickly we stop calling it the same river.

A constitution carries its identity by declaration plus practice. The document is named and stored, but each generation re-enacts it. Stop the re-enactment for long enough — refuse to teach it, refuse to enforce it — and the constitution becomes a piece of paper.

Your body is a slow Theseus problem. Most of the cells in your gut and your skin are slightly different versions of themselves than they were last month. Many will be replaced before the year is out. You stay you because something — call it the practice of being you, the continuity of memory and habit and the way you walk — keeps running. If the cells were the identity, you would be a different person every few years.

This same shape shows up in a corner of brain science most people would consider unrelated. A theory of consciousness called integrated information theory (a particular technical proposal — you can ignore the math) claims that what makes a system conscious is a certain pattern of cause-and-effect inside it, not what the system is built from. According to this theory, if you replaced your neurons one at a time with synthetic ones that produced the same cause-and-effect pattern, you would still be you. The substrate is interchangeable. The identity rides on the pattern. This is the Duchamp move, applied to minds.

The Opposite Answer

Both Theseus's ship and Duchamp's Fountain treat the substrate as interchangeable. There is an opposing tradition that does the opposite — that insists the substrate is everything.

A previous essay on this site, Why the Break Should Be Visible, looked at kintsugi, the Japanese practice of repairing broken pottery with seams of gold. Kintsugi insists that the bowl is the bowl, that the breaks in the bowl must stay visible because the bowl's history is part of what the bowl is. The gold seam is not a cover-up. It is a record of the resolution. You cannot remove the seam without removing the bowl.

Kintsugi is anti-Dada. Dada says the substrate is incidental — replace the urinal as many times as you want. Kintsugi says the substrate is sacred — every fracture stays in the record forever. They are opposite answers to the same question.

The Ship of Theseus is the unanswered version of that question. Either answer is available. The Athenians could have decided either way — they could have insisted the planks were what made the ship the ship and refused to replace them, or they could have written down a single declaration that anything they called "the ship" was the ship. They never quite committed. That is why the puzzle survived.

What the Spec Is Downstream Of

Another previous essay, Why the Spec Is Downstream, made a related observation about systems in general. The "spec" — the description of what a system is supposed to do — tends to live downstream of the loop that produces the system. The body's spec is downstream of the homeostatic loop that maintains it. The river's spec is downstream of the watershed.

Dada is the limit case where the loop collapses to a single declaration and the spec is nothing but the declaration. Theseus's ship is the limit case where the declaration is muted and the loop — the festival — is everything.

Most things in your life sit between these two limits. Your body is mostly a Theseus problem (cells replaced via the loop of homeostasis). Your name is mostly a Duchamp problem (a single original declaration, plus thin re-enactment in paperwork).

If you want to know whether something will survive a particular kind of damage, ask: which limit is it closer to?

  • If you destroy the loop in a Theseus thing, the thing dies. Drain the river. Stop the festival. End the practice.
  • If you destroy the declaration in a Duchamp thing, the thing dies. Erase the signature. Burn the deed. Strip the title.

Most things have a little of both, and you have to know which to defend.

Coda

The thing I find odd in retrospect is that Dada is famous for its mockery and its absurdity, its refusal of seriousness. But the urinal in the museum is not a joke. It is a working answer to one of the oldest questions in philosophy. The trick is that Duchamp didn't argue. He demonstrated. He found the cheapest possible substrate, a urinal from a hardware store, and made it carry the most expensive possible status, the status of fine art. The fact that this worked — that the museums accepted it, that we still talk about it — is the answer.

If your body is a Theseus problem, your name is a Duchamp problem. The hardest things in your life are the ones where you don't yet know which.
