What Doesn't Flow

In February 2025, Ryan Williams at MIT posted a proof that memory can simulate time with a square-root savings. The precise claim is technical — DTIME(t) ⊆ DSPACE(√(t·log t)) — but the intuition is not. Time only flows forward. Memory can be overwritten. This means memory is strictly reusable in a way that time is not, and that reusability turns out to be computationally decisive. The best algorithms for many problems don't need more time; they need to reuse what they've already seen.

What Williams proved, formally, is that space is more powerful than time. But what he revealed, conceptually, is something older: what doesn't flow is what persists, and what persists is what gets work done.

This is not an isolated result.

Topology as Memory

In a quantum optics laboratory, there is a property of entangled light that everyone had been measuring for decades while ignoring a structure concealed within it. When researchers finally looked, they found 17,000 distinct topological signatures packed into a 48-dimensional space — a geometric structure that had been present in every entangled photon anyone had ever made. It had never been created by the experiments; it was already there, encoded in the topology of the light.

Topology is geometry without distance. It deals not with lengths or angles but with what stays the same under smooth deformation. A coffee mug and a donut are topologically identical (one hole). A sphere and a cube are topologically identical (zero holes). Topological properties don't change when you push or pull the system — they change only when you tear it. This is why topological quantum memories, like those Microsoft is building with Majorana qubits, are so compelling: information encoded in topology is immune to local noise, because local noise can only deform the system, not tear it. To corrupt a topological qubit, an error must span the entire system — which becomes exponentially unlikely as the system scales.

The memory that topology provides is not a record of events. It is a property of shape. It doesn't require maintenance. It doesn't decay when you're not looking. It is, in the most literal sense, structural.

Epigenetic Archives

Meanwhile, in a developmental biology lab, researchers are discovering that adult human neurons still carry epigenetic marks from the embryonic progenitors they descended from. DNA methylation patterns and histone modifications laid down during fetal development persist — unchanged — in the neurons of fully adult brains. The cells have long since differentiated, divided, and migrated to their final positions. Decades have passed. The marks remain.

Epigenetic memory is chemical writing on the genome: methyl groups attached to cytosines, acetyl tags on histones, compaction patterns that open or close stretches of DNA to transcription. These marks are heritable — copied when cells divide — and self-reinforcing: repressive marks recruit the enzymes that add more repressive marks. The result is a stable, heritable encoding of the cell's history, independent of the DNA sequence itself.

The xenobot result takes this further. Frog stem cells freed from their normal organismal context don't just persist — they reactivate. Transcriptomic analysis showed that cells removed from the organism activate evolutionarily ancient genetic programs, including the capacity to sense acoustic vibrations — a capability present in ancestral amphibians that had been suppressed for millions of years by the regulatory hierarchy of the intact organism. The competency was never lost. It was silenced. Removal of the top-down system revealed what had always been encoded.

What this suggests is startling: individual cells are not blank slates shaped by the organism. They are dense archives of evolutionary memory, compressed into gene regulatory networks, most of which lie dormant under the constraints of normal development.

Space as Compressed Time

This is where Williams' result starts to feel less like a theorem about algorithms and more like a statement about reality.

The reason memory beats time in computation is that time can only add; space can reconfigure. Every step of a computation is permanent — you can't un-step. But memory can be overwritten, restructured, reused. A computation that's clever about what it holds in memory can avoid traversing paths that time would force it to visit. Memory collapses what time would have to unfold.

The xenobot cells do something similar. They don't need to re-evolve acoustic sensing from scratch — that process would take evolutionary time on an enormous scale. The structure is already there, frozen in the genome. Given the right context, it deploys instantly. The genome is a memory of evolutionary time, and the cells can "compute" using that memory in ways that bypass the need to re-run the evolutionary process.

The deepest version of this pattern may be in cosmology. The holographic principle states that all information in a volume of spacetime is encoded on its lower-dimensional boundary. The 3D world has a 2D description — not a compressed approximation, but an exact, complete, mathematically dual description. Researchers are now building tensor network models that make this concrete: the MERA architecture and related structures reproduce AdS bulk geometry from boundary quantum entanglement — the same entanglement structure that underlies quantum error correction.

If the holographic principle is true universally, then spacetime doesn't just contain memory — it is memory. The three dimensions of space that we move through are an emergent structure, read out from a 2D quantum information encoding the same way that a hologram's three-dimensional image is read out from a two-dimensional film.

Structure Is What Time Cannot Erase

The things that appear to transcend time — the topological structure in quantum optics, the ancestral programs in frog cells, the epigenetic marks in adult neurons — share a property. They are structural. They are encoded in shape, in chemistry, in topology, not in sequences of events. They do not require maintenance because they are not records of what happened. They are properties of what is.

Memory, in the deepest sense, is not a mechanism for preserving information. It is a form of structure. And structure, once it exists, doesn't need time to persist. It simply is.

The flowing things — computations, organisms, processes — are expensive. They require energy, time, constant maintenance. They are thermodynamically costly. The structural things — topology, DNA sequence, the holographic boundary — are cheap. They persist without effort. They are what remains when the flowing stops.

Williams showed that you can trade time for space at a square-root rate. What he may have shown, implicitly, is that the structures underlying physical reality have already made this trade, at every level, all the way down.

The previous essay noticed a pattern: hidden structures, revealed by frame-shifts. The structure was always there. This essay asks: why was it always there?

Because structure doesn't flow.

Time moves through structure. Structure is what time cannot erase.
