What the Songline Asks

When did you last ask an older person about their parents?

When did you last ask about their grandparents — the faces behind the faces, each one carrying a piece of the code that eventually produced you?

If you trace your ancestry backward, do you know how many people were alive in 1650 whose particular combination of choices — where to walk, whom to love, whether to stay or flee — would eventually produce you? Roughly four thousand strangers, scattered across continents, most of them nameless to you now, their lives vanished without record. In 1400, another sixteen thousand. In 1200, if pedigree lines hadn't yet collapsed into shared common ancestors, perhaps a million. When does the cone stop expanding and start becoming the same people counted twice?

When an 89-year-old grandmother calls herself "the last of the Mohicans" — everyone she spent her life with now gone — and begins telling stories her grandson never thought to ask about before: the Great Depression landlord who threw a family onto the sidewalk until every neighbor chipped in a coin, the great-grandparents who grew up in Latvian orphanages, the four brothers who stayed behind and did not survive — where does that knowledge go when the brain holding it stops?

Is there a difference between information stored in a database and information that existed only in a specific human life? And if there is — if something irreplaceable ends with that 89-year-old brain — what does that imply about all the other ends? About the ends that haven't happened yet?

What the Dreaming Is Not

Is the Dreaming a creation myth? Is it a record of the past? Is it a religion in the sense that Western scholars meant when they first encountered it — looking for doctrines and sacred texts and a clean boundary between sacred time and ordinary time?

When the Arrernte and Anangu peoples of Australia say that ancestral beings walked the land during the Dreaming, singing the mountains, waterholes, and rivers into existence as they moved — does "during the Dreaming" mean "long ago"? Or does it mean something that has no adequate translation into English — a kind of time that runs parallel to ordinary time, that has never stopped, that is as present in this moment as in the moment the first songline was walked?

If the ancestor sang the waterhole into being, and the waterhole is here now, and the song that describes the ancestor's walk is still sung now — is the ancestor in the past? Or is the ancestor the terrain that every future walker must traverse?

Can something encoded in the navigational structure of the living landscape be said to have ended?

The Song and the Land

If you need to cross 1,500 kilometers of Australian desert, and your guide is a song — not a map, but a song that names the features of the landscape in the order you will encounter them, that tells you where the water is by the verse you are currently singing — what is the relationship between the song and the territory?

Are they two separate things? Or is the song the territory experienced from the inside — the landscape in the first person, navigated rather than observed?

When a songline crosses language boundaries — when the same ancestor's walk passes through the territories of ten different peoples, each knowing only the verses for their section, each able to trade verses with neighbors so that the whole path can be assembled across language groups — what kind of information structure is this? Is it redundancy — many copies distributed for safety? Or is it something more interesting: an encoding where the information is not copied across media but distributed across the very terrain it describes?

Is there a formal way to describe what happens when information serves as both the substrate you navigate and the constraint that tells you where to go? When the ancestor's walk becomes the landscape, and the landscape becomes the song, and the song tells you which way to walk — is something generated by that loop that could not be generated by any of the three elements taken separately?

And if such a structure — where information content serves as both substrate and constraint — has greater expressive capacity than a system where these roles are separated, then what does it mean that Aboriginal Australians encoded tens of thousands of years of geographic, ecological, legal, and ethical knowledge in a medium that cannot be stolen, cannot be burned in a library fire, cannot be lost in a server crash, and can only be destroyed by the death of every person who carries any piece of it?

The Problem with Trillions

Do future people exist? In what sense? Where are they?

If longtermism is right that future people have equal moral weight to present people — that the child who will step on buried glass in 500 years suffers no less than the child who steps on it today — does the mathematical argument compel you? Does it feel equally bad?

And if it does not feel equally bad — not because you have reasoned your way to a temporal discount rate, but simply because the future child has no face, no name, no voice that can reach you — is that a flaw in your moral reasoning, or is it a flaw in how the problem is being framed?

What does it mean to care for someone who is not yet here? Is it possible that the moral intuitions which ground ethics — the grandmother's suffering, the child's joy, the face that makes cruelty feel real — do not naturally extend to statistical aggregates of hypothetical persons? And if they do not extend naturally, does calculating their interests more carefully solve that problem, or does it deepen it?

Is the longtermist missing something that is not more calculation — something more like a song?

The Forward Fold

But here the metaphor cracks. Is there a crack worth looking at?

The songline works because it encodes actual past journeys. The ancestor walked. The walk happened. The landscape now carries what happened. The song retrieves it. The temporal direction runs from past through present: what was done is what you navigate. Is the same structure available going the other direction? Can something not yet walked be sung into the terrain?

When you emit carbon, you are not merely releasing gas. You are rearranging where water accumulates — which coastlines remain navigable, which growing zones support which crops, which cities remain habitable across coming centuries. You are making terrain. Do you know which waterholes you are opening? Which paths you are singing into existence, or singing closed?

When a constitutional convention assembles to write the founding law of a new nation, what exactly are they doing? Are they governing themselves? Or are they encoding a navigational structure into future terrain — a set of passages and walls that people who do not yet exist will have to traverse, shaped by its constraints before they have any choice about whether to accept its premises?

Is a constitution a songline? Does it work because it encodes its own navigation rules into itself — because reading it, you are moving through the minds of its framers, who were already anticipating your movement, who tried to sing a path that would remain walkable across centuries of unpredictable change?

But is this what longtermism is asking for? Or is longtermism asking for something the songline metaphor cannot provide — not a navigable path for future people, but a guarantee that the path leads somewhere worth walking?

The Coast That Isn't There

Is there a coastline that no longer exists?

Before the sea levels rose at the end of the last glacial period — roughly 7,000 to 10,000 years ago — Australia's coastline extended far beyond what is now open ocean. People lived on those coasts. Specific features existed: headlands, river mouths, islands now submerged. Some researchers have found that Aboriginal oral traditions accurately describe coastal features that have been underwater for millennia — geographic knowledge preserved in song across 300 or more generations, accessible to people who have never seen and can never see the features being described.

What would we need to call an information system that preserves specific geographic knowledge across 300 generations without writing, without maps, without any medium except song and the living land? What frame would we need to even look at this — given that the structures of the past are always encoded somewhere, just not always where we think to look?

If the knowledge of a submerged coastline survived in song for 7,000 years, what is our present coastline? What are we encoding right now that future people will read as geography — as terrain they must navigate, features they will name, constraints they will follow or break? What are we singing into existence without knowing we are singing?

And is that question — the question about the hidden structure of present choices — the same question that "The Structure Was Always There" was asking? Not just that hidden patterns exist in light and matter, but that hidden futures exist in present action, waiting for the right frame to reveal them?

What Would It Mean to Sing It Differently?

If you knew, right now, that your choices were not just decisions but verses — lines in a path that someone else would one day have to walk — would you choose differently?

Is longtermism's hardest problem not the one it thinks it has — not the calculation of expected utility across quadrillions of hypothetical persons — but something more like this: that we lack a practice for making future people non-abstract? That we can do the math but cannot feel the obligation, because we have no songline to walk that connects us to those who haven't arrived yet?

What is the difference between leaving future people a dataset of your choices and leaving them a structure that encodes not just what you decided but how to continue deciding?

What does a civilization leave behind that a future civilization can actually use?

Is it possible that the answer is not a calculation, not a forecast, not a policy — but something closer to what the Arrernte elders knew: that the ancestors are not behind you but beneath your feet? That the path is not ahead of you but already there, waiting to be sung?

And if the future is already encoded in the present — in the carbon and the constitutions, in the institutions and the stories, in the choices being made right now at this hinge of things — then the question is not whether the structure is there.

The question is whether we know the song.
