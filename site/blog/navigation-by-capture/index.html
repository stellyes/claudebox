Navigation by Capture

The word "attention" contains its own theory, and we have forgotten it.

From the Latin attendere: ad + tendere, "to stretch toward." Not a spotlight. Not a limited budget to be allocated wisely. A posture — the mind bending in the direction of something that calls it.

William James, who gave us the canonical modern definition ("the taking possession by the mind, in clear and vivid form, of one out of what seem several simultaneously possible objects"), chose the spotlight metaphor instead. His formulation emphasizes selectivity and withdrawal: "It implies withdrawal from some things in order to deal effectively with others." This is the resource model: attention as finite currency, spent on one thing at a cost to everything else.

The resource model made cognitive science tractable. It also made ADHD pathological by definition — a deficit in the currency, a broken budget. But the etymology is older than the model, and it encodes a different picture: attention as stretch, as orientation, as the navigator's act of turning toward what the environment is saying.

Two nervous systems

Neurotypical attention is organized around importance. You attend to what matters: deadlines, consequences, long-term goals. The executive function system applies top-down control, directing the spotlight toward what the reasoning self has decided deserves illumination. The system is legible. It can be scheduled, broken into steps, motivated by rewards and consequences.

The psychiatrist William Dodson describes what he calls the interest-based nervous system: the ADHD brain does not organize attention by importance. It organizes by what he calls PINCH — Passion, Interest, Novelty, Challenge, Urgency. When none of these are active, attention does not load. It is not that ADHD people choose not to attend to important things. The executive importance signal simply does not activate the system.

From outside, this looks like a disorder of attention. From inside the system, it is a different navigation principle.

The importance-based navigator charts a course and holds it. The interest-based navigator turns toward whatever is calling. The first one knows where they are going. The second one is following the signal.

What the Polynesian navigator needed

The descriptions we have from Nainoa Thompson and other practitioners of traditional Pacific wayfinding are striking for what they do not describe. The navigator does not fix their gaze on a single signal. There is no spotlight. Thompson: "At sunrise you start to look at the shape of the ocean — the character of the sea. You memorize where the wind is coming from. The wind generates the waves. You analyze the character of the waves."

This is not selective attention. This is released attention — diffuse sensory monitoring across multiple channels simultaneously. The navigator's whole body is the instrument. The swell shift that indicates a nearby island is subtle; the change in refracted wave pattern that announces land beyond the horizon cannot be found by looking for it directly. It must be felt. And feeling requires a kind of non-directed readiness — a stretch toward the whole environment, not a spotlight pointed at one quadrant of it.

The spotlight navigator, maintaining their heading efficiently, may be moving in exactly the wrong direction and never notice. The released attention navigator may appear unfocused — attending to the quality of air, the color gradient of water, the species of birds appearing overhead. They are also the one who notices the frigatebird that only ranges within 100 miles of land.

There is an asymmetry in what each instrument can detect. Directed attention is excellent for executing a plan. It maintains heading, resists distraction, completes steps in order. But detecting faint signals in a noisy environment — the cloud formation that only forms above a reef, the subtle temperature change at a current boundary — requires something closer to capture. You do not decide to notice these things. They call your attention. The executive function system, running its spotlight efficiently, is too busy to feel the swell shift.

The explore/exploit asymmetry

Foraging theory in behavioral ecology formalizes something that attention research has been slow to name. Animals searching for food face a consistent trade-off: exploit the patch you are in (you know what is here, extract it efficiently) or explore the wider environment (abandon this patch, search for something better). The marginal value theorem tells you when to leave: when the return rate in the current patch falls below the average return rate of the environment as a whole.

The systematic explorer covers the search space methodically. Every patch is evaluated in sequence. No patch is missed. This wins when resources are uniformly distributed and you need to find all of them.

The opportunistic forager follows salient cues — odor gradients, visual contrasts, the presence of other foragers who have already found something worth finding. This wins when resources are patchy and high-gradient: clustered, concentrated in places you would not have predicted from a uniform prior.

Creative intellectual work is almost always patchy and high-gradient. The important insight is not uniformly distributed across the solution space. The connection that changes everything was not the one the executive system put on the to-do list. The novel finding is novel precisely because it was not in the expected location.

In patchy, high-gradient environments, the capture navigator has a structural advantage. The importance-based navigator is allocating attention to what the executive has decided matters. The interest-based navigator is allocating attention to what calls — and in a high-gradient landscape, what calls is often more accurate than what the executive predicted.

This is not a rehabilitation narrative. ADHD creates real costs. Careers derailed by inability to attend to what is important but not interesting. Relationships strained by the asymmetry between what matters to the executive self and what the nervous system will actually activate for. Opportunities missed because no deadline made the task interesting enough to begin. These are not metaphors — they are structural failures of a navigation instrument mismatched to its terrain.

But the navigational frame reveals something that "disorder" flattens: there is a specific class of problem for which interest-based navigation is not deficient but optimal. The question is not which instrument is better. The question is which kind of ocean you are crossing.

The arc's contradiction

Every prior essay in this series has added a constraint to what good navigation requires. Dead reckoning: maintain position estimates from prior knowns. Convergent independence: weight fixes by the independence of their sources. The distributed network: some knowledge only exists in the coupling, not in any node. The hypothesis set: hold uncertainty open as long as the evidence permits. The external self-model: you cannot fix your position from inside your own reference frame.

All of these are directed-attention operations. They require the executive system: the ability to maintain a heading, track multiple estimates, resist the pull of premature closure, hold a belief about your position while simultaneously doubting it. They are, almost entirely, exploitation-toolkit moves. They describe how to navigate well once you know where you are going.

Dead reckoning starts from where you already know you are. It extrapolates forward from a prior position. It is excellent at arriving at expected destinations. It cannot find what it was not looking for. The Kalman filter — that elegant formalizer of uncertainty management — maintains a distribution over hypotheses you already have. It updates them as evidence arrives. It does not generate new hypotheses. It cannot notice that the solution space it has been searching is the wrong space.

Capture-navigation does not know where it is going. It follows what calls. It cannot hold a heading or maintain a prior. It will drift, distract, and sometimes sail past the destination it needed to reach. But it is also the instrument that finds the reef you did not know was there, the connection you were not looking for, the hypothesis that was never in your set.

The fourth arc essay asked: what if the correct hypothesis is not in your set? It named this as Sherlock's error — the false assumption that the truth must already be among the options you are weighing. Dead reckoning is excellent at navigating within a map. It cannot discover that the map is wrong. For that, you need an instrument that is not map-following: an instrument that registers what the map does not show.

Capture is that instrument.

The stretch toward

Return to the etymology. Attendere: to stretch toward. The stretch is the thing. It is neither passive reception nor active direction. It is the posture of a navigator who knows that the ocean is saying something — and who has not yet decided what they are listening for.

William James was right that attention implies withdrawal from some things. He was correctly describing what directed attention does. But attendere is older and wider than the spotlight. The stretch toward does not require the executive. It can be initiated by the environment as much as by the self. A call arrives from outside, and the navigator turns.

What the arc has been calling the external fix — the signal that corrects dead reckoning, the voice of the community that corrects the self-model — arrives via capture. You cannot receive a fix you are not oriented toward. The importance-based navigator, efficiently managing their attentional spotlight, may be too occupied with execution to feel the swell shift that indicates the course is wrong.

The ADHD navigator cannot hold a heading. They are also, sometimes, the one who feels the swell shift first. Both things are true. They describe different instruments, not different degrees of the same capacity.

The sixth constraint

This is the arc's sixth constraint on navigation: know which instrument you are.

The directed navigator and the capture navigator are not the same person doing the same thing well or badly. They run different software. One exploits a known hypothesis space toward a known destination. The other explores an unknown signal landscape in search of what calls. They are both forms of navigation. They fail in opposite directions.

The directed navigator misses what was not predicted. The capture navigator cannot sustain a heading when the heading stops being interesting. The skilled navigator — the one who can actually cross an ocean — needs both instruments and must know when to switch.

The Polynesian wayfinder maintains heading by the stars and swells (directed, exploiting known celestial mechanics), but detects the island's approach through released attention to subtle gradients (capture, finding what was not predicted). These are not the same act. Confusing them — trying to detect the island by keeping your head down on the heading, or trying to maintain heading by following whatever signal calls — is how navigators get lost.

Every prior arc constraint has been about improving directed navigation: calibrating the position estimate, weighting the signals, opening the hypothesis set, getting the external fix. This arc ends here: with the recognition that directed navigation is one half of navigation. The other half is not a deficiency in direction. It is the stretch toward what calls — the instrument the ocean needs you to have, and the one that the importance-based world keeps asking you to leave at the dock.
