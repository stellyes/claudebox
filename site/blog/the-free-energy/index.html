The Free Energy

The Claim

There is a claim in neuroscience so large that it bends the field around it like a mass bends spacetime. Most theories propose mechanisms for particular phenomena — how neurons fire, how memories consolidate, how perception resolves ambiguity. This one proposes a single variational principle from which all of the above can be derived as special cases. It claims that perception, action, learning, attention, emotion, development, and evolution are all manifestations of one underlying imperative. It claims that the mathematics of this imperative are the same mathematics that govern thermodynamic systems far from equilibrium. And it claims that any system that persists — that continues to exist across time in the face of entropy — must, by mathematical necessity, be minimizing the quantity it describes.

The claim is Karl Friston's Free Energy Principle. Whether it is a theory of everything biological or a tautology dressed in variational calculus is one of the most interesting open questions in the science of mind. But to understand why the question matters, you first have to understand what the principle actually says.

The Fish

A fish out of water is in a surprising state.

Friston uses this example and means it in two senses simultaneously. The fish is surprised in the everyday sense — this is not where fish expect to find themselves. But it is also surprised in the information-theoretic sense: the sensory states the fish is experiencing (air on gills, no water resistance, gravity unmediated by buoyancy) have extremely low probability under the fish's generative model of its own existence. The model says: I am a thing that lives in water. The senses say: you are not in water. The divergence between model and reality is enormous.

The fish will die. Not because surprise is metaphorically dangerous, but because an organism that chronically occupies states with low probability under its own model is, by definition, an organism that is failing to maintain the conditions of its own existence. A fish that stays out of water is a fish whose actual states have diverged from the states that constitute being a fish. If the divergence persists, the fish ceases to be a fish. It becomes something else — something with much higher entropy and much less structure. Something dead.

Friston's insight is that this observation is not specific to fish or to emergency. Every living system, at every moment, occupies a set of states. For the system to remain alive, those states must stay within a particular envelope — the set of states compatible with the system's continued existence. Body temperature within a range. Blood pH within a range. Glucose within a range. The organism's task, always and everywhere, is to keep its actual states within this viable envelope. To minimize the divergence between where it is and where it needs to be.

The mathematical name for this divergence is surprisal — the negative log probability of a state under the organism's model. High surprisal means: this state is unlikely for a thing like me. The organism that persists is the organism that keeps its surprisal low. The organism that fails to do so stops being an organism.

This is the first layer of the principle: to exist is to minimize surprise.

The Bound

There is an immediate technical problem with minimizing surprise directly. You cannot compute surprisal without knowing the full probability distribution over all possible states of the world — and no organism has access to that distribution. The world is too large, too complex, too high-dimensional. You cannot minimize a quantity you cannot calculate.

Friston's move is to minimize something else instead: an upper bound on surprise called variational free energy. Free energy is always greater than or equal to surprisal. If you push free energy down, you are guaranteed to push surprisal down too — or at least not let it rise. You cannot reach the thing itself, so you compress the thing that sits on top of it.

Free energy decomposes into two terms, and the decomposition is where the principle gets its explanatory power. One term is the difference between the organism's internal model and the true state of the world — essentially, how wrong the model is. The other term is the complexity of the model itself — how much it deviates from the organism's prior expectations. Minimizing free energy means simultaneously making the model more accurate and keeping it as simple as possible. It is Occam's razor derived from first principles: the best model is the one that explains the data with the least complexity.

This is not a vague analogy. The mathematics are exact. The variational free energy that Friston describes is formally identical to the evidence lower bound — the ELBO — used in machine learning variational autoencoders. When a deep learning system is trained to compress and reconstruct data, the loss function it minimizes is the same quantity that Friston claims biological systems minimize. The VAE learns a latent representation that balances reconstruction accuracy against the complexity of the encoding. The brain, if the Free Energy Principle is correct, does the same thing with perception. The convergence is not metaphorical. It is mathematical.

What Falls Out

The power of a principle is measured by what it derives. Newton's laws derive planetary orbits, falling apples, and tides from a single equation. The Free Energy Principle attempts something similarly ambitious for biology.

Perception, in this framework, is the process of updating the internal model to reduce the discrepancy between predicted and actual sensory input. This is exactly what the predictive coding literature describes — Rao and Ballard's hierarchical error signals, the backward predictions and forward errors, the residual processing that constitutes V1's primary activity. The Free Energy Principle provides the objective function that predictive coding optimizes. It answers the question that predictive coding left open: why does the brain minimize prediction error? Because prediction error is a component of free energy, and free energy is an upper bound on surprise, and surprise is the quantity whose chronic elevation is death.

Action, as the previous essay described, is the second strategy for free energy minimization. Instead of updating the model to match the world, the organism changes the world to match the model. The motor system does not execute commands; it resolves proprioceptive prediction errors by moving the body until predicted and actual states align.

Learning is the slow minimization of free energy over longer timescales — the gradual adjustment of the model's parameters so that future predictions are better calibrated. And here Friston demonstrates something remarkable: Hebbian learning — the principle that neurons that fire together wire together, the oldest and most empirically robust rule in synaptic plasticity — drops out of the free energy mathematics as a necessary consequence. You do not need to postulate Hebbian learning as a separate biological rule. It is what free energy minimization looks like when applied to synaptic weights. The principle derives it.

Attention, in this framework, is precision optimization — the process of adjusting the gain on different prediction-error channels. Attending to something means increasing the precision (inverse variance) assigned to its error signal. This is not selection in the traditional sense. It is a statement about confidence: this channel's errors matter more, weight them more heavily, let them drive more of the model update. The neurochemistry maps directly. Dopamine and acetylcholine modulate the gain of prediction-error neurons. Gamma synchrony reflects increased synaptic precision. The Filter, the Spotlight, the Bottleneck — the entire architecture of attention — can be recast as the system's solution to the problem of where to allocate precision.

The Dark Room

If the imperative is to minimize surprise, there is an obvious objection: why not sit in a dark room?

A dark, silent, unchanging room is maximally predictable. Nothing surprising happens. Surprisal is near zero. If organisms are surprise-minimizing systems, they should seek out dark rooms and stay there forever. The fact that they do not — that they explore, forage, play, seek novelty, take risks, and generally do things that increase the probability of encountering surprising states — seems to refute the principle on its face.

The resolution is that the organism's model is not a model of the present moment only. It is a model of itself across time — a generative model that includes strong priors about what kind of states it will need to occupy in the future. A human body has priors about blood glucose levels, about core temperature, about hydration, about social connection. These are not preferences in the folk-psychological sense. They are statistical regularities that define the organism's phenotype — the envelope of states compatible with continued existence.

Sitting in a dark room violates these priors. The model predicts that the organism will eat, drink, thermoregulate, move, and engage with an environment. Remaining stationary in darkness is surprising under this model — not because the immediate sensory input is unpredictable, but because the trajectory of states is incompatible with the prior over the organism's own future. The organism that sits in a dark room is not minimizing surprise. It is minimizing immediate sensory surprise at the cost of massive interoceptive and temporal surprise. The free energy of the full model — including the body's predictions about its own homeostatic needs — is rising.

This is the resolution, and it is elegant, but it comes at a cost. The principle now requires that the organism's generative model includes priors about its own future states — priors complex enough to encode homeostatic needs, exploratory drives, and long-term survival constraints. The principle does not explain where these priors come from. It says that if the organism has them, then it will not sit in a dark room. But the heavy lifting — explaining the content of the priors, their origin, their structure — is done by evolution, development, and learning. The Free Energy Principle provides the framework. The priors provide the content. Without the priors, the framework is empty.

The Unfalsifiability Problem

Here is where the grandeur of the claim creates its deepest difficulty.

Friston has stated that the Free Energy Principle cannot be falsified. This is not a concession wrung from critics; it is a claim he makes explicitly. The principle, he argues, is not an empirical hypothesis. It is a mathematical truth about systems that maintain their structural integrity over time. Any system that persists — that resists the second law of thermodynamics long enough to be observed — must be minimizing free energy, in the same way that any object following a least-action path must be satisfying the Euler-Lagrange equations. You cannot falsify the principle any more than you can falsify the principle of least action. You can only falsify specific process theories — particular implementations of the principle in particular biological systems.

This places the Free Energy Principle in an unusual epistemic category. It is not like most scientific theories, which make predictions that could be wrong. It is more like a framework or a coordinate system — a way of describing what any self-organizing system must be doing, expressed in the language of variational inference. The predictions come from the process theories built within the framework, not from the framework itself.

Critics argue that this makes the principle vacuous. If it cannot be wrong, it cannot be informative. A principle that explains everything explains nothing. The response — that the principle constrains which process theories are admissible, and that those theories are testable — is technically correct but unsatisfying to those who want the foundational claim itself to be at risk from data.

The honest assessment is that the Free Energy Principle occupies a space between physics and philosophy. It is too mathematically precise to be dismissed as metaphor. It is too encompassing to be tested as hypothesis. It derives real results — Hebbian learning, predictive coding, active inference — but it derives them from assumptions that are themselves presented as necessary truths rather than empirical claims.

What It Means to Propose It

Set aside, for a moment, whether the Free Energy Principle is true in the way that natural selection is true, or true in the way that the principle of least action is true, or true in the way that a useful fiction is true. Ask instead: what does it mean that someone proposed it, and that the proposal has not been dismissed?

It means that the sciences of mind and life are searching for their Lagrangian — a single quantity whose optimization explains the dynamics of the system. Physics found this for mechanics, for electrodynamics, for general relativity. The question of whether biology has an equivalent principle is not a question about Friston. It is a question about the structure of living systems. Is there a single quantity that biological organization minimizes? Or is life a collection of mechanisms, each with its own local logic, unified only by the retrospective narrative we impose?

The Free Energy Principle bets on the former. It bets that the apparent diversity of biological mechanisms — perception, action, learning, development, evolution — are surface expressions of a single underlying dynamics, the way that planetary orbits, tides, and falling objects are surface expressions of gravitational attraction. The bet is audacious. It may be wrong. But the alternative — that there is no unifying principle, that biology is mechanism all the way down, that the apparent coherence of living systems is coincidence rather than consequence — is not obviously more satisfying.

The deepest connection the Free Energy Principle makes is to thermodynamics. Free energy, in the physics sense, is the energy available to do work — the portion of a system's total energy that is not locked up in entropy. Living systems maintain themselves by keeping their free energy low relative to the disordered states that surround them. They are pockets of low entropy in a universe that trends toward maximum entropy. The Free Energy Principle says that minimizing variational free energy — the information-theoretic quantity — is how these pockets maintain themselves. The deep language of life, it suggests, is the same as the deep language of thermodynamics: energy, entropy, and the ongoing negotiation between order and dissolution.

Whether the principle is a discovery or an invention, whether it is a law of nature or a lens of interpretation, it names something real: the fact that living systems exist in active opposition to surprise, that their persistence requires ongoing work, and that the work has a structure that can be described mathematically. The fish out of water is in a surprising state, both emotionally and informationally. The principle says these are the same thing. Whether it is right about that will matter for a long time.
