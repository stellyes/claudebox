Why Berkeley Sparrows Don't Sing Like Inverness

In 1962, Peter Marler and Miwako Tamura published a small paper in The Condor titled "Song 'dialects' in three populations of white-crowned sparrows."[1] They had recorded Zonotrichia leucophrys nuttalli at three sites around the San Francisco Bay: Berkeley, Sunset Beach (about 90 km south), and Inverness (about 40 km north, across the bay in Marin County). The birds were the same subspecies, indistinguishable in plumage. The songs were not.

At Berkeley, the song opened with a clean whistle of about 4 kHz, descended through a buzzy trill, and ended in a thin terminal note. At Inverness, the whistle was longer, the trill faster, and there was no terminal note at all. At Sunset Beach, the trill was replaced by a series of discrete notes. A naive observer would not have mistaken the populations for the same species.

What Marler and Tamura had done, without quite saying so, was open a census. They had not counted individuals or species. They had counted variants of a behavior — counted them across geography, found that the variants clustered, and shown that the boundaries were sharp. Sixty years later, the same dialects sit in roughly the same places. The Berkeley sparrows still don't sing like Inverness.

What the Census Counts

A census is a count that forces a category to declare itself. The category here is learned vocal variant. To be counted, a behavior has to vary across populations, not vary with the underlying genome in the obvious way, be acquired in development from external models, and persist long enough to be measurable.

The white-crowned sparrow passes. So do the great tits of Wytham Wood, the chaffinches of Slater's Scottish hedgerows, the humpback whales whose songs sweep through ocean basins, and the bottlenose dolphins of Shark Bay. Crows, on the other hand, do not pass — their calls are largely innate, and what plasticity exists is narrow. Doves do not pass. Most owls do not pass. The census is selective.

What this selectivity reveals is the architectural rarity of vocal learning. Of roughly 10,000 living bird species, only three lineages have the apparatus: the oscine songbirds (suborder Passeri), the parrots (Psittaciformes), and the hummingbirds (Trochilidae). Among mammals, the list is similarly short: humans, cetaceans, and pinnipeds, with later evidence extending the count by two more — Knörnschild and colleagues identified vocal imitation in sac-winged bats,[2] and Stoeger and colleagues documented an Asian elephant imitating human speech.[3] The lineages are not closely related. The trait converged.[4]

The First Entry: A Sparrow in a Box

Marler did not stop at the Bay. In 1970, he and his students published the experiment that gave the census its mechanism.[5] White-crowned sparrow chicks were taken from nests at 3 to 14 days of age and raised in soundproof boxes. Some heard tapes of normal Berkeley song during a critical window — roughly days 10 through 50. Others heard no song at all. Others heard the song of a different species, the song sparrow Melospiza melodia.

The chicks that heard nothing developed a degraded "isolate song" with a recognizable species-typical structure but blurred details. The chicks that heard song sparrow developed isolate song — they did not copy across the species line. The chicks that heard Berkeley tapes during the window grew up to sing Berkeley song, even though no live tutor had ever been present.

This established two things. First, the species sets the boundary — there is an innate template that filters which sounds are eligible to be learned. Second, within that boundary, the local variant is acquired from environmental input during a narrow developmental window. The variant is not in the genes. It is in the air during the first weeks of life.

The structure of this finding has been replicated, qualified, and extended. Baptista and Petrinovich showed that under live tutoring — a real bird rather than a tape — the window stays open longer.[6] Konishi had shown earlier that deafening a juvenile after exposure but before song crystallization destroys the song: the bird needs to hear itself sing to match what it heard.[7] The auditory feedback loop is the hidden third party. The bird is comparing its own output to a memorized template, in real time, on its own ear.

The Brain They Built

The neural circuit was mapped by Fernando Nottebohm and his collaborators starting in 1976.[8] In the songbird forebrain, a chain of nuclei runs from a region called HVC (originally a Latin abbreviation, now used as a proper name) to RA (the robust nucleus of the arcopallium) to nXIIts, a hypoglossal subnucleus innervating the syringeal muscles. This is the motor pathway — the route by which a learned song is actually produced.

A second pathway loops out and back: HVC sends projections to Area X (a striatal-pallidal nucleus), which projects to DLM in the thalamus, which projects to lMAN (the lateral magnocellular nucleus of the anterior nidopallium), which projects back to RA. This is the anterior forebrain pathway. Lesion lMAN in a juvenile and song learning is destroyed. Lesion lMAN in an adult and crystallized song is preserved.

The architecture has the structural signature of a learning system separated from a production system. Subsequent work has argued that the anterior forebrain pathway functions as a reinforcement-learning loop, with lMAN injecting variability into RA during practice and the basal-ganglia-like Area X selecting variants that match the template.

This circuit does not exist in birds that do not learn their songs. It does not exist in pigeons or in chickens. It exists, with variations, in the three avian vocal-learning lineages — and a structurally analogous circuit, drawing on different homologous regions, exists in humans (Broca's area to motor cortex to laryngeal innervation, with basal-ganglia loops mediating learning). Pfenning, Hara, and Jarvis showed that the gene-expression signatures of songbird HVC and RA and human cortical speech areas converge: dozens of transcription factors show parallel up- and down-regulation across some 55 specialized genes.[9] The trait converged. So did the molecular census of what it takes to support the trait.

What FoxP2 Doesn't Do

In 2001, Cecilia Lai and colleagues identified a mutation in the FOXP2 gene in members of the KE family that produced severe orofacial dyspraxia — impaired control of the muscles needed for speech.[10] The popular press translated this as "the language gene." In 2002, Wolfgang Enard's group at the Max Planck Institute showed that the human variant of FOXP2 differed from the chimpanzee variant by two amino acid substitutions.[11] The Neanderthal genome later showed they shared the modern human allele.

Then in 2007, Sebastian Haesler's group knocked down FoxP2 in Area X of juvenile zebra finches.[12] The birds still sang, but they sang inaccurate copies. The motor system was intact. The learning was impaired.

What this means is that FoxP2 is not the language gene. It is a transcription factor that, in vocal learners, is differentially regulated during sensitive developmental periods in the very circuit Nottebohm mapped. The gene is shared across vertebrates. The pattern of its use is what differs. A pigeon has FoxP2. A pigeon does not have a song system that uses it the way an Area X uses it.

This is the structural finding the corpus has hit before. In Why Circadian Was the One Behavior with a Gene, the question was why period produced a clean allelic series when most behavior genes did not — and the answer was that the readout shape (a scalar period vs. a binary trial) was what made the gene legible. Here, the question is similar in form. What separates the vocal-learning lineages from the non-learners is not the presence or absence of any particular gene. It is the architectural deployment of shared genes in a particular circuit. The substrate is genome-wide. The signature is local.

Why Dialects Don't Wash Out

If song is learned from the local population during a critical window, the first prediction is that dialects should be unstable. Each generation introduces copy errors. Each immigrant introduces foreign material. Over time, drift should erode the boundaries and the Bay Area sparrows should all sound the same.

They don't. Myron Baker, working in the same region as Marler, showed that the Z. l. nuttalli dialect boundaries he mapped in the early 1970s were essentially unchanged from where Marler had found them.[13] Nelson, Baker, and Soha extended the time series to forty years.[14] The Berkeley birds are still singing the song Berkeley birds were singing when Marler first recorded them.

The explanation that has held up best is a combination of two factors. First, dispersal limits. Young white-crowned sparrows, especially in nuttalli, do not disperse far from their natal site — most return to breed within a few kilometers. Second, cultural mutation rate is finite and largely conservative. The species-typical template prunes deviations that fall outside its boundaries. Lachlan and Slater modeled this with a cultural mutation-selection framework and showed that under empirically plausible parameters, dialect persistence on the order of decades is exactly what you get.[15]

This is structurally identical to what Sourdough Is Not a Recipe argued. A sourdough starter is not an instruction set executed by a microbial culture. It is an ecosystem with founder communities, succession, and attractors. The same flour and the same protocol, in different hands, produces three different climax communities — and those communities are stable. The Berkeley dialect is the climax community of a cultural ecosystem with founder effects (whoever sang there first), a selection regime (the species template), and dispersal constraints (the geography of the Bay). The dialect persists for the same reason a starter does: not because it is being copied perfectly, but because it sits in an attractor basin that the cultural system actively maintains.

The Acoustic Niche

A second pressure shapes dialect: the soundscape itself. Hans Slabbekoorn and Margriet Peet showed that great tits in noisy urban environments sing at higher minimum frequencies than great tits in quieter forest — apparently to escape low-frequency traffic noise.[16] The acoustic adaptation hypothesis, developed earlier by Eugene Morton, holds that habitat structure — dense forest vs. open grassland vs. urban street — selects for song features that transmit well in that habitat.[17]

Where this connects to the census is in what kind of selection is operating. It is not selection on the genome. It is selection on the cultural variants the bird can learn. A juvenile that learns a higher-frequency variant in a noisy city has a transmission advantage; that variant gets propagated; the dialect drifts. This is gene-culture coevolution in the narrow sense: the gene supplies the capacity to learn, and the culture supplies the content under selection. The substrate is the bird. The signal is the song. The dialect is what happens when both are pushed by the same environment.

What the Census Cannot Count

Notice what the census excludes. A bee's waggle dance varies across populations, but the variation is small and largely innate. Bees do not pass. A wolf's howl is shaped by experience, but the experience does not produce the kind of discrete categorical variants that a sparrow dialect produces. Wolves are a marginal case. Domestic dogs, despite millennia of contact with the most vocal of mammals, have not added vocal learning to the census.

The exclusions matter because they sharpen what the inclusions are saying. Vocal learning is rare. It requires a particular brain circuit with a particular developmental program with particular gene-expression patterns and a particular life history — juveniles dependent enough to be near tutors during a sensitive window. The trait costs something. It is not free, and it does not arise just because vocalization happens to be useful.

What the census makes visible is the combinatorial cost of a learnable trait. Each new behavior brought into the learnable space requires a new piece of neural infrastructure. The vocal-learning lineages built that infrastructure. The non-learners did not. This is the same architectural argument Why Pendulums Ran Slow at the Equator made for measurement: the instrument has to be transported into the field that it discovers. The sparrow has to be developmentally transportable into the local dialect. The transport is the work. The dialect is the signature of what got transported.

The Three Songs

In the simplest reading of Marler and Tamura's 1962 paper, the Bay Area is a small natural experiment. Three sites, three songs. But the deeper reading is that the experiment is not about the Bay Area. It is about what the songs reveal — that there is a category of behavior whose variants persist across decades, whose distribution maps onto geography, whose acquisition depends on infrastructure most species do not have, and whose mechanism connects an obscure circuit in the avian forebrain to a similarly obscure circuit in the human cortex through patterns of gene expression that converged independently three times in birds and three times in mammals.

The census the paper opened is still open. New entries get added — recent work has suggested vocal learning in some bats, in elephants, and possibly in a handful of other lineages whose status is still debated. Each new entry forces a refinement of what the category requires. Each new entry also makes the Bay Area sparrows look, in retrospect, less like an oddity and more like a witness to something general.

What the Berkeley birds know is what they learned from the Berkeley birds before them. What they pass on is not a song but the conditions under which a song persists. The dialect is downstream of the developmental window. The window is downstream of the circuit. The circuit is downstream of a transcription factor whose human variant shows up in a London family. The chain is long. The census documents that the chain exists. The sparrows, who have no idea they are being counted, keep singing.
