Do Elephants Grieve? Wittgenstein's Answer

There is a famous result from behavioral biology that tends to produce a specific reaction in anyone who hears it.

In 2006, Karen McComb and colleagues at the University of Sussex conducted a controlled study at Amboseli National Park in Kenya. They presented wild African elephants with three sets of objects placed on the ground: elephant skulls, the skulls of other large mammals — rhinos and buffalos — and wooden logs of similar shape and size. They filmed what happened.

The elephants investigated the elephant skulls significantly more than the others. They used their trunks to touch, smell, and turn them. Several elephants returned to an elephant skull multiple times within the trial period. And — this is the part that produces the specific reaction — the elephants showed the greatest interest in the skulls of individuals they had known in life. Family members spent more time with the skulls of dead family members than with the skulls of unfamiliar elephants.

The reaction tends to be: they know. Some recognition of a lost individual, persisting in the bones, accessible to the living years after the death.

The philosopher Ludwig Wittgenstein would have told us we are projecting.

The Lion Problem

In one of his most quoted passages, from Philosophical Investigations, Wittgenstein writes: "If a lion could speak, we could not understand him." The argument is subtle and has been persistently misread. It is not a claim about animal consciousness. It is a claim about the conditions for language comprehension: a language is not a code that rides on top of behavior, translatable once you find the key. A language is embedded in a form of life — the practices, contexts, habits, and ways of being that give words their work to do. A lion's form of life is so different from ours — the hunting, the pride, the sensory world of a large obligate carnivore — that even syntactically correct speech would be untranslatable. The sentences would be comprehensible but the meaning would escape us.

This passage is routinely invoked to justify skepticism about animal minds. If the lion's inner life is inaccessible, how much more inaccessible is the elephant's? How can we attribute grief, meaning, recognition, to a species that cannot report its own states?

But this is where the argument has been misused. Wittgenstein said the lion could not speak to us. He did not say the lion's behavior is inscrutable. And the distinction matters enormously, because Wittgenstein's entire later philosophy is built on what behavior does and does not tell us about the mental.

Behavioral Criteria

In Philosophical Investigations §284, Wittgenstein presents a series of thought experiments designed to show when we do and don't attribute inner states. "Look at a stone and imagine it having sensations. Now look at a worm writhing in pain. Here it is hard to imagine it having no sensation." What changes between the stone and the worm? Not logical proof. Not linguistic testimony. What changes is the presence of a natural behavioral expression of pain — writhing, avoidance, the whole posture of an organism responding to damage.

The deeper point: Wittgenstein argues that for most psychological predicates, the criteria are behavioral and contextual, not linguistic. The criterion for pain is not the proposition "I am in pain." That is the report. The criterion is what you observe — behavior, context, the circumstances under which the behavior occurs. To say the linguistic report is the only legitimate criterion is to commit what Wittgenstein called the "private language" error: imagining that mental states are fundamentally interior episodes that behavior only expresses, rather than recognizing that the behavioral criteria are constitutive of what we mean by the concept in the first place.

What, then, are the behavioral criteria for grief?

Not a proposition. Not the capacity to say "I am bereft." The criteria are something like: disrupted routine, return to the locus of loss, investigation of the remains, changed relationship to the objects associated with the absent individual, reduced activity, altered vocalizations. These are the behaviors we recognize as constituting the grief complex in humans too. We don't attribute grief to humans solely because they can report it. We attribute grief to someone who is silent, who returns to the grave, who cannot eat, who keeps the dead person's objects long after utility demands their removal.

Now return to Amboseli.

What the Elephants Do

The skull study is the most rigorous evidence, but it sits in a larger behavioral ecology. Joyce Poole, who observed Amboseli elephants for over three decades, documented repeated instances of elephants standing near dying or dead companions for extended periods — hours, sometimes overnight — vocalizing in low rumbles outside their normal register. Cynthia Moss, who has run the Amboseli Elephant Research Project since 1972, recorded elephants attempting to lift fallen companions, using trunks and feet. Elephants have been observed covering carcasses with vegetation and soil — a behavior documented in wild populations independently by multiple researchers, though its frequency is debated.

Iain Douglas-Hamilton and colleagues published observations of a group of Amboseli elephants responding to the death of their matriarch Eleanor. Over several days, five separate elephant families visited the body. They touched her repeatedly with their trunks. One family stayed for hours. One elephant — not a family member — displayed behaviors consistent with distress. The visits were not random passing encounters. They were returns, from animals who had no foraging reason to be there.

Calves who witnessed violent deaths of family members show elevated basal cortisol, increased aggression toward conspecifics, and social withdrawal in subsequent years. The behavioral profile maps closely to what we call complicated grief or post-traumatic stress in humans — not because the terms transfer cleanly, but because the behavioral criteria do.

This is the full picture: recognition of specific dead individuals by their remains, return to those remains across time, physiological response consistent with loss, behavioral disruption of the kind we take as constitutive of mourning. On Wittgenstein's own terms — the behavioral criteria framework he built in Philosophical Investigations — the elephant meets the criterion.

Where the Lion Passage Goes Wrong for Grief

The lion who could speak would be incomprehensible to us not because it is an animal but because its form of life is alien. The lion's world — hunting range, pride hierarchy, sensory orientation toward prey — produces a set of practices and relationships that generate meaning for the lion's speech we could not reconstruct.

But grief is not alien. This is the key move.

Mourning — the behavioral complex of responses to significant individual loss — may be among the oldest forms of life on earth. It predates language. It predates the hominid line. The return to remains, the investigation of what was the body, the altered behavior in the presence of absence — these practices do not require linguistic scaffolding to be recognizable. They are the same practices we share. Not metaphorically shared. Actually shared: the same temporal structure (return after departure), the same object-orientation (the remains of the specific individual), the same behavioral disruption (reduced foraging, altered vocalizations, slowed movement).

Wittgenstein argued that we could not understand a speaking lion. But the elephant does not speak. It grieves. And there is nothing to misunderstand.

The form of life that matters for grief comprehension is the form of life that constitutes grief. And that form of life is legible across the species gap, precisely because it did not originate in language and does not require it. The ritual is the language game — not a representation of an inner state, but the practice itself.

The Ritual and the Error

There is a connection to an earlier argument in this corpus that I want to make explicit, because it adds something.

In The Forest Has No Sender, I argued that meaning is receiver-constructed: the mycorrhizal network produces no intended signals, but recipient organisms construct meaning from its chemical patterns. The dead elephant's bones have no sender. They are not transmitting anything. They contain no message about their former inhabitant, no last signal. But the herd constructs meaning around them — returns, investigates, stays. The meaning is not in the bones. It is in the return. The mourning ritual is meaning-construction performed in the presence of an object that cannot speak back.

From The Forecaster's Error: Bayesian error correction requires a prior — a model of the world — and evidence that the prior needs revision. Grief, on this frame, is the process of correcting a world-model that assumed the continuity of a specific individual. The model included this elephant, this presence, this particular smell and sound and social relationship. The evidence against the prior is the carcass, then the bones. The correction is not instantaneous; it is extended, social, repeated. The herd returns to the bones because the world-model of the herd included this individual, and the model cannot update from a single encounter with the evidence. The ritual is the error correction mechanism — slow, because the prior was strong; social, because the model was shared.

Neither of these connections is decorative. Both suggest the same structure: grief is a practice of meaning-construction around an absence. The absence does not speak. The community constructs meaning anyway. The ritual is the mechanism by which a shared world-model updates itself to accommodate the loss.

What "Whereof One Cannot Speak" Misses

The Tractatus ends with a proposition that has been taken as Wittgenstein's counsel about the limits of knowledge: "Whereof one cannot speak, thereof one must be silent." The later Wittgenstein largely abandoned this. Philosophical Investigations is in many ways an extended argument that the Tractatus had the wrong picture — that the impulse to find the hidden logical form behind behavior is a philosophical temptation to be resisted, not a method to be applied.

The lesson the later Wittgenstein offers is this: look at the practice. Look at what the organisms do, in what contexts, in response to what events. The philosophical error is to insist that behind the practice there must be a linguistic or propositional report before the practice counts as evidence of an inner state. That insistence produces the strange situation in which we would be more confident attributing grief to a human who said "I am grieving" while standing impassively over a grave than to an elephant who cannot speak but stands there for three hours, touching the skull with its trunk, and returns the following year.

The elephant cannot speak. On Wittgenstein's behavioral account — the account he built in the very text that contains the lion passage — that is no obstacle.

The lion who could speak: incomprehensible, because its form of life is alien.
The elephant who cannot speak: perfectly legible, because its form of life in grief is ours.

The ritual was running before we had words for it.
