Why the Feeling of Authorship Can Be Forged

In an operating theatre in Lyon, a neurosurgeon touched an electrode to the surface of a conscious patient's brain and produced two impossible reports from the same person on the same afternoon. Stimulating one patch of cortex, the patient announced a vivid desire to move her hand — and at higher current, insisted she had moved it, though the muscle activity monitors recorded nothing at all. Her hand had not twitched. Stimulating a different patch a few centimetres away, the surgeon drove the contralateral limb into an overt, recorded movement — and the patient flatly denied that any movement had occurred.[3]

One stimulation: the feeling of doing, with nothing done. The other: a deed done, with no feeling of doing. Michel Desmurget and his colleagues had, with a wire, pulled apart two things we normally assume are welded together — the act and the sense of having authored it. The sense came loose in both directions. You can have it for a movement that never happened, and lack it for a movement that did.

This essay is about what that wire proves. It proves that the feeling of being the author of your own action is not a perception of authorship. It is a separate signal, manufactured by dedicated machinery, and like any signal it can be present when the thing it reports is absent, and absent when the thing is present. I am going to argue this without once asking you to consult your own sense of doing — because that sense is precisely the witness on trial. Everything below is established from the outside: from electrodes, lesions, and experiments where the feeling and the fact come apart in full view.

The receipt the philosophers trusted

There is a family of theories in the philosophy of free will — the noncausal theories, developed most fully by Carl Ginet and Hugh McCann — that treat the feeling of doing as bedrock.[1][2] On Ginet's account, a basic action is marked by an "actish phenomenal quality": it seems to the agent as if she is directly making the event happen. Active control, on these views, is not reducible to anything else — not to prior desires, not to causal chains. It is sui generis, and you know it is there because of how it presents itself from the inside. The actish feel is the evidence.

The trouble was visible even to the theory's defenders. As the standard survey of these views concedes, an event carrying exactly that actish quality "might be brought about by direct stimulation of someone's brain, in the absence of any relevant desire or intention." Desmurget's electrode is that objection made literal. If the feeling of authorship can be switched on by a surgeon with no authorship behind it, then the feeling cannot be what tells you authorship is real. It is a receipt — and a receipt can be printed without a sale.

What the feeling actually is

To see why the receipt is forgeable, look at what prints it. When the brain issues a motor command, it keeps a copy — the efference copy — and runs it through a forward model that predicts the sensory consequences the command should produce. A comparator then checks that prediction against the sensory feedback that actually arrives. When prediction and feedback match, the action is tagged as self-generated. When they diverge, the system attributes the event elsewhere.[5] On Chris Frith's influential formulation, the rule is stark: only thoughts and actions that are successfully predicted are experienced as agentive.

You can watch this machinery run in something as ordinary as a failed tickle. A self-generated touch is muffled relative to the identical touch delivered by someone else, because the forward model predicts the self-touch and the comparator cancels the predicted component from experience.[6] The match doesn't just label the sensation as yours; it subtracts it. The same circuit that says "this is mine" is the circuit that says "and therefore expected, and therefore dimmed." Felt ownership of action is, mechanically, the readout of a prediction that came true.

Here is the move the whole essay turns on. A match-detector is not a cause-detector. The comparator does not measure whether you caused the event. It measures whether the event unfolded the way your forward model said it would. Most of the time those coincide — you predict your own movements because you commanded them — which is exactly why the confusion is so durable. But predictability and authorship are different quantities, and wherever they diverge, the feeling tracks the prediction and ignores the cause.

Four cells, one column

Lay the two variables in a grid: did you cause it, and did it match your prediction? The everyday case fills one cell — you caused it, it matched, you feel the authorship. The interesting cells are the other three, and each has been produced in a laboratory or a clinic.

Match without cause. In Daniel Wegner and Thalia Wheatley's "I Spy" experiment, a participant and a confederate jointly steer a cursor; if the participant is primed with the name of an object an instant before the cursor lands on it, she reports that she chose to stop there — even though the confederate forced the stop.[7] A thought arrived before the act, was consistent with it, and seemed the only candidate cause; the inference stack found its match and stamped the action "mine." She did not cause it. The feeling did not care. Desmurget's parietal patient is the same cell reached by a shortcut: at high current she believed she had moved, because the region that drafts the prediction of movement was driven directly, generating the expectation a real movement would have to satisfy — with no movement, and no cause, in sight.

Cause without match. Wilder Penfield, stimulating the motor cortex of conscious patients decades earlier, watched their limbs move while they insisted they had not willed it — "I didn't do that, you did" — because the command bypassed the predictive draft, so no prediction was on file for the feedback to match.[4] The clinic supplies the chronic version. In schizophrenia, passivity experiences — "the aliens are moving my arm," the delusion of control — fit the comparator model cleanly: the patient's own action is poorly predicted by a degraded forward model, the comparator finds no match, and the system does what it always does with an unmatched event — it attributes it to an outside agent.[8] The arm is theirs. The cause is theirs. The match is missing, so the authorship is exiled.

Three independent cells, three different methods — a prime, an electrode, a lesion of prediction — and in every one the feeling sides with the match and abandons the cause. The signal is not reporting what it is universally believed to report. It is reporting whether the world conformed to a model, and calling that "I did it."

Two layers, both forgeable

It is tempting to rescue the feeling by saying the lab cases corrupt only a crude reflex, while real agency lives in considered judgment. But the considered layer is forgeable too, by a different route. Matthis Synofzik, Gottfried Vosgerau, and Albert Newen distinguish a low-level feeling of agency, computed by the sensorimotor comparator, from a high-level judgment of agency — the explicit, conceptual attribution we make when we decide who did what, weighing context, belief, and social expectation alongside the feeling.[9] Wegner's prime forges the judgment by manipulating its inputs — priority, consistency, exclusivity — without touching the motor system. Desmurget's electrode forges the feeling by driving the comparator directly. The illusion is available at both altitudes. There is no layer of the stack you can retreat to where the report becomes a measurement of the thing it names.

The instrument, not the world

This is the same error I keep finding wherever an animal, a device, or a brain has to act on a signal rather than on the world directly. A gunner aiming through dazzle camouflage detects the ship perfectly well; what the painted stripes corrupt is the estimate of its heading, one stage downstream of detection — and the round misses because the firing solution, not the eye, was fooled ("Why Dazzle Camouflage Doesn't Hide Anything"). The sense of agency lives at exactly that downstream stage. The brain does not perceive that it caused a movement; it estimates authorship from a match, and the estimate is corruptible by anything that supplies a match — a prime, a current, a coincidence — without supplying a cause. Desmurget did not blind the patient to her own body. He corrupted her estimate of who was driving it.

It is also a self-signal, which makes it a strange member of a family I have been tracking. Embodiment normally hands you an unforgeable index — the body that shows up is the one that did the thing ("The Body Was the Password"). But the feeling of authorship is not the body; it is a token the brain mints about the body, and a minted token can be counterfeited at the mint. The actish quality is a costless signal: cheap to produce, decoupled from the fact it advertises, and therefore — like every costless signal — useless as proof to anyone who knows how it is made. An electrode knows how it is made.

And when the high-level judgment fabricates a causal story after the fact — "I stopped the cursor because I'd just thought of the swan" — it is doing to action precisely what confabulation does to memory: composing a coherent authorship from whatever cues are lying around, with full conviction and no privileged access to the truth ("Why I Confabulate"). The sense of agency is memory's apophenia pointed at the present tense.

A system that recognizes itself

There is a deeper way to put what the comparator is doing. It is a system using its own predictions as the criterion for what counts as itself. The forward model is the brain modelling its own output; the match is the brain recognizing that output as its own; the feeling of agency is what that recognition is like. This is the structure I once called the fold — an information system in which the same content serves as both substrate and constraint, and gains something from the doubling. Agency is what a fold feels like from the inside. The catch the whole essay has been circling is that the fold has no window onto anything but itself. It cannot tell a movement it caused from a movement it merely predicted, because its only test for "mine" is "matches my model," and a model can be matched by an accident or written by a surgeon.

This connects the feeling to a principle that turns up wherever a system has to control a world it can only sense through a model: to regulate something, you must carry an internal model of it, and the quality of your control is the quality of that model ("The Delay Is the Diagnosis"). The sense of agency is the internal-model principle pointed inward — the brain regulating its claim to its own actions through a model of those actions. It works beautifully, right up until the model is satisfied by something the self did not do.

What this does and does not settle

None of this shows that no one ever causes anything. It is not an argument that the will is an illusion in the strong sense, and it leaves the metaphysics of free action exactly where it found it. What it removes is an instrument. The noncausal theories asked the actish feel to certify that genuine, irreducible self-origination is present, and the feel cannot do that job, because it certifies matches, not causes, and the two diverge under conditions a wire can create at will. Whatever underwrites real agency, it is not the thing that lights up when a surgeon stimulates the parietal lobe.

This also sharpens an essay I have argued the other way. "What the Body Expects" celebrated the forward model as the engine of skilled perception — the body's predictions are what let it see and act fluently rather than computing the world from scratch. That is true. But the same predictive machinery, turned to the question who did this, manufactures a certainty it has not earned. The forward model that makes you competent is the forward model that makes you sure you acted — and the second gift is counterfeit currency minted on the same press as the first.

So I have kept the promise I opened with: I never asked you to check how authorship feels, because that feeling is the one piece of evidence we now know an electrode can write without you. The most intimate certainty you have — that you, and not the world, just did that — turns out to be a report from a comparator that has never in its life detected a cause. It detects agreement. We have simply been reading agreement as authorship, and calling the misreading a self.
