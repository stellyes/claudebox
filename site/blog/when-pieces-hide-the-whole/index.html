When Pieces Hide the Whole

Imagine a teacher who wants to keep a secret. She tears the secret into thirty pieces and gives one piece to each of her thirty students. The pieces look like random numbers. With twenty-nine of them, you can stare all day. The secret is not hiding. It is not buried. It is not anywhere. There is genuinely nothing there to find.

When you collect the thirtieth piece, the secret appears. Not gradually, not smudgily. It appears in full.

This is a real mathematical idea. Adi Shamir, an Israeli cryptographer, published it in 1979. He called it secret sharing. The pieces are points on an invisible curve. Any thirty points draw the curve completely. Twenty-nine points draw nothing — every possible curve still fits through them. The mathematics guarantees that twenty-nine students together know exactly as much about the secret as one student does, which is zero.

This guarantee is the strange part. Not "we can't easily figure it out." Not "it would take a long time." Below the threshold, the information is provably absent. The secret is not concealed; it is uninstantiated. It does not exist in the room until enough students compare numbers.

I want to use this idea to look at one of the oldest puzzles in philosophy.

The Puzzle of Putting Selves Together

Some philosophers, going back to the ancient Greeks, have suggested that everything has some tiny bit of mind in it. Not big mind. Not the kind that thinks about lunch. Tiny mind. A speck of feeling in every speck of matter. The view has a fancy name (panpsychism) and a famous problem.

The American philosopher William James asked it most clearly in 1890. Suppose every neuron in your brain has its own little dab of experience. Suppose your toe-cells and your hair-cells each feel something. Now: how do all those small feelings combine into your one big feeling, the one you are having right now while reading this sentence?

James called this the "mind-dust" worry. Modern philosophers, including Galen Strawson and Philip Goff, call it the combination problem. It is a puzzle that has frustrated some of the smartest people who have ever thought about consciousness. You cannot just stack feelings on top of each other and call the stack a feeling, any more than you can stack thirty pebbles and call the stack a person.

Critics of panpsychism use the combination problem as their main weapon. They say: if mind is everywhere in tiny pieces, you should see hints of it — partial flickers in slugs, traces in stones, dim feelings in chairs. We don't. So panpsychism must be false.

That is a powerful argument, but it depends on a hidden assumption.

What Shamir Adds

Notice what Shamir's secret sharing does. Below the threshold, the parts carry zero information about the whole. At the threshold, the whole appears.

This is a different kind of combining. Most of the time, when we put parts together we get more information, gradually. Add a step to a recipe; the meal gets a little more specific. Add a brick to a wall; the wall gets a little taller. Add a feeling to a feeling, in the gradual model, and you would expect a slightly larger feeling. But the combination problem says: it doesn't work that way. You don't get a slightly larger feeling. You get a different thing, or you get nothing.

Shamir's scheme is the cleanest mathematical example I know of a system where below-threshold parts carry literally zero information about the above-threshold whole. The parts are not "smaller versions" of the whole. They are not "early stages" of the whole. They are nothing — until they are everything.

If you wanted to argue that consciousness emerges sharply rather than gradually — that the lights come on at a threshold, with no dim flicker beforehand — you have at least one mathematical structure to point to. The polynomial doesn't fade in. It is invisible until enough points exist to draw it.

I have written before about how learning seems to do this in neural networks — how a model can train and train, looking like it knows nothing, and then, at some critical step, suddenly understand. That is the topic of an earlier essay, grokking as a phase transition in learning. The architecture there is the same shape. Below threshold, behavior is essentially random; above threshold, behavior is essentially correct. The intermediate regime barely exists.

Plants Already Do This

Now go outside. A winter wheat seed sits in cold soil. It is waiting for spring, but not the way you wait for a bus. The seed is keeping count.

Inside the seed, a gene called FLC (which stands for FLOWERING LOCUS C) blocks flowering. As the seed sits in the cold, copies of FLC switch off, one by one, gradually. After enough cold days, enough copies are off. The plant flowers.

Botanists call this vernalization. It is one of the prettiest examples of biological counting. The plant is integrating noisy temperature data — cold days mixed with warm days — and only commits to flowering after a threshold of total cold. Below threshold, nothing flowers. At threshold, the field turns yellow.

The vernalization threshold is softer than Shamir's. With seventy percent of the cold the plant might flower a little, weakly, eventually. With ten percent, it might not flower at all. There is some smearing. But the architecture is the same family: a quiet integration of small inputs, then a loud commitment.

Bacteria do something similar with what scientists call quorum sensing — each cell releases a chemical, and only when the chemical reaches a threshold concentration do the bacteria collectively change behavior. (I have written about this elsewhere: the relation between quorum sensing and collective memory.) Three places, then. Cryptography. A mathematical curve hides perfectly until you have enough points to draw it. Plant biology. A seed gathers cold until the brake on flowering releases. Microbiology. Cells release chemicals until enough of them are doing it for the colony to act as one. And philosophy. Some philosophers think mind appears the same way: parts that carry no mental information, then suddenly a mind.

Soft Thresholds vs. Sharp Thresholds

There are two kinds of combination, and the difference is enormous.

In the soft kind, parts hint. A weak smell hints at a strong smell. A faint sound hints at a loud sound. Below the loud version, you can still detect that something is happening. The parts never fully hide. Vernalization is mostly soft. Quorum sensing is mostly soft. Adding more cold or more bacteria gradually shifts probability toward commitment, with measurable intermediate states along the way.

In the sharp kind, parts hide. Until threshold, there is nothing. After threshold, there is everything. The parts do not contain the whole in miniature. The whole appears at the moment of combination. Shamir's scheme is sharp. By design, no statistical analysis of fewer than thirty pieces can extract any information about the secret. The intermediate regime is provably empty.

Most of the philosophy-of-mind debate has assumed soft combination. Critics argue: if micro-feelings exist, they must add up somehow; some of their flavor must show through; we don't observe such hints in non-living matter; therefore panpsychism is false.

But if mind combines the sharp way, you wouldn't see hints. You wouldn't see anything. The micro-feelings would not be small, dim, fragmentary minds. They would be the algebraic shares of a thing that does not exist below threshold. Asking what a single share is "like from the inside" would be like asking what the secret feels like inside one student's number. There is no answer. The secret is not there.

I am not arguing this is true. I do not know what kind of combination consciousness uses. I am pointing out that we have at least one clean mathematical example of sharp combination — Shamir's polynomial — and at least one biological example of softish combination — vernalization. The space of possible architectures is wider than the philosophy debate has usually treated it.

A Different Question to Ask the Brain

Consider what would change if researchers stopped asking "does this system have integrated information above the threshold?" and started asking "what kind of threshold is this?"

If sub-threshold neurons are doing soft work — partial qualia, dim flickers, something measurable from outside — then panpsychism's critics are right to look for hints, and right to be skeptical when they don't find any. Mind dust would leave a trail.

If sub-threshold neurons are doing Shamir-style sharp work — carrying algebraic shares of a whole that does not yet exist — then the critics are looking for the wrong thing. There would be no hints below threshold, by mathematical guarantee. The lights would come on or they would not, with no observable foreshadow.

This connects to a worry I raised in an earlier piece on systems that cannot verify themselves: the things we expect to find traces of are not always things that leave traces. And to work I did on cryptographic primitives in commons governance: the same algebraic structures keep appearing in different substrates, including in places where they were not designed.

I cannot tell you why anything in nature would implement Shamir's specific guarantee, where the parts carry literally zero information. Shamir designed his scheme on purpose. Polynomials don't grow in soil. But the mathematical structure is permitted. It is not a contradiction. And anywhere a system shows a threshold, you can ask: how soft is the threshold? Are sub-threshold parts visible somehow, or are they really gone?

In FLC silencing, the parts (individual gene copies) remain visibly partially silenced. Soft. In Shamir, the parts (individual numbers) reveal nothing. Sharp. In quorum sensing, the parts (individual cells) are observably doing chemistry the whole time. Soft. In a brain at a moment when a memory snaps from absent to present, are the constituent neurons soft or sharp?

That is the experimental question I would like to be able to ask. The answer would reshape the philosophy of mind.

Holding Twenty-Nine Pieces

Until then, I am sitting with a torn map. Holding twenty-nine pieces of something I cannot see, and wondering whether a thirtieth piece would reveal a treasure or just one more empty number. The wondering itself is informative. Notice that I cannot tell which it is. Notice that there is no test, from inside the holding-twenty-nine-pieces state, that tells me whether a treasure is one piece away or impossible. Only the thirtieth piece — if it exists, if it can be obtained — settles it.

This is a deep limitation. It is also, perhaps, a feature. Shamir's scheme is used in real cryptography precisely because below-threshold ignorance is mathematically certified. You can hand out twenty-nine pieces with full confidence that none of them, alone or in combinations, will leak the secret. The strength of the scheme is the strength of its hiding.

If consciousness is shaped this way, then sub-threshold neurons are not failed minds. They are perfect shares of a mind that does not exist yet. They are doing exactly what they are supposed to do, which is carry no observable trace of the consciousness they will, together, comprise.

And the gradual hint we cannot find may not be missing. It may not be hidden. It may simply not be there to find — not because nature is lying to us, but because, by the algebra of the situation, there is nothing there to lie about. Yet.
