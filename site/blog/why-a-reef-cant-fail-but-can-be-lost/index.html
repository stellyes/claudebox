Why a Reef Can't Fail but Can Still Be Lost

A coral reef does not have a job. It does not exist to feed a billion people, to break the force of a storm before it reaches a coastline, or to hold a quarter of the ocean's species inside its branching limestone. We say those things, and we are not lying, but we are doing something subtler than describing the reef. We are projecting a standard onto a system that has none of its own. And once you see that the standard is borrowed, the most familiar sentence in environmental science — this ecosystem is collapsing — starts to come apart in your hands.

I want to follow that sentence all the way down, because something survives the fall. What survives is not the reef's function. It is the reef's irreversibility. And that residue — the part of "collapse" that does not depend on anything we happen to want — turns out to be the only honest core of the most ambitious ethical project of our moment: longtermism, the claim that we owe something enormous to the people who will live after us.

The Reef That Has No Job

Begin by conceding everything to the skeptic, because the skeptic is mostly right. The philosopher and ecologist John Drake has argued that ecosystems cannot "fail," for the simple reason that they were never trying to do anything.[1] A clock can fail because a clock was designed; failure is the gap between what a thing is for and what it does. A reef was not designed and is not for anything.

Philosophy of biology has a precise vocabulary for this. On the etiological account of function, associated with Larry Wright, a trait has a function only because of what it was selected for: the heart is for pumping blood because pumping blood is why hearts were favored by natural selection.[2] But an ecosystem is not a unit of selection. No lineage of reefs was ever culled for failing to produce fish. So a reef has no etiological function at all. The alternative, Robert Cummins's systemic-capacity account, says a function is just a part's contribution to some capacity of a larger system that we have chosen to analyze.[3] The reef "functions" to produce protein only relative to a capacity — feeding humans — that we, not the reef, picked out. The function is real, but it is observer-relative. Change the observer's interest and the function changes with it.

So Drake's deflation lands. "The reef is failing" smuggles a standard of operation into a system that has none, and the standard came from us. To that extent, collapse-talk is rhetoric: a way of dressing a human preference in the lab coat of objective decline. Concede it fully. Then ask what is left over.

What Survives the Concession

What is left over is the reef itself, behaving in a way that no amount of philosophy can talk out of existence.

A reef-building coral is a partnership. The animal — the polyp — houses a dinoflagellate alga, Symbiodinium, in its tissues, and draws as much as ninety percent of its energy from the alga's photosynthesis. The algae are why the reef is colored, and why it can build limestone fast enough to outpace the sea. When the water warms even a degree or two beyond the local maximum for too long, that partnership breaks. The alga's photosystems jam and begin overproducing reactive oxygen species; the relationship turns from gift to poison; the polyp expels its tenants and goes white.[4] This is bleaching, and it is not a metaphor for collapse. It is a measurable phase change in a real system.

Here is the thing the deflation cannot touch. A living coral reef and the algal turf that replaces it are not two points on a smooth gradient. They are two alternative stable states. In 2001 Marten Scheffer and colleagues showed that lakes, forests, drylands, and reefs all share a topology: smooth changes in conditions can trigger sudden switches between contrasting states, and — this is the decisive part — the system cannot be returned to the old state by simply returning the old conditions.[5] Cool the water back down and the coral does not come back. The reef has crossed a threshold with hysteresis: a memory of its own path.

Hysteresis is not a function. No one had to want fish for it to be true. It is a structural fact about the basins of attraction in the system's dynamics — what the ecologist C.S. Holling, founding the concept of resilience, called the depth of the valley a ball can roll out of.[6] Drake deflated the word failure. He never touched the word irreversible. And it is irreversibility, not failure, that does all the work.

The Coral Is Already a Longtermist

Before scaling up, notice that the coral is, in miniature, already wrestling with the problem this essay is about.

There is a reading of bleaching, the adaptive bleaching hypothesis of Robert Buddemeier and Daphne Fautin, in which expelling the algae is not a death rattle but a bet.[7] By voiding its current symbionts, the coral opens a slot for a different, more heat-tolerant strain — a shuffle from a sensitive clade to a hardy one that can measurably raise the colony's thermal ceiling.[8] The coral pays an enormous present cost — it gives up almost all its income, and may starve — to preserve the option of a partnership that can survive the world that is coming. It sacrifices the present to keep the future reachable.

That is the longtermist's exact move, made in tissue. And so is its failure mode. Concede the obvious to the skeptic: most bleached corals simply die. The bet pays off only when the perturbation is slow enough that the option can be acquired before the lights go out. When warming arrives faster than new symbionts can be recruited and grown, option-preservation is just a slower way to lose. The coral teaches the lesson its human imitators most need: keeping your options open is only a strategy if the world gives you time to exercise them. Irreversibility and the rate of change are the same problem seen from two sides.

Lock-In Is Hysteresis With a Human Face

Now scale up to the whole future, which is what longtermism asks us to do. William MacAskill, Toby Ord, and their colleagues argue that because the future could contain an astronomical number of worthwhile lives, safeguarding humanity's long-term potential is among the most important things we can do.[9]

Steelman the critics, because their strongest objection is precisely Drake's, transposed. Who decides what a "flourishing" future is? "Future flourishing" borrows a standard of operation from the present in exactly the way "ecosystem function" borrows one from us — and freezing today's notion of the good and imposing it on a thousand generations looks less like stewardship than like the arrogance of a single observer mistaking his interest for the universe's. The critique is devastating against the naive version of longtermism that defends a vision.

But watch what the longtermists themselves are most afraid of. The nightmare in this literature is not "the future will hold the wrong values." It is value lock-in: a transition into a state from which humanity's trajectory can never again be revised.[10] That is not a function-claim at all. Lock-in is hysteresis with a human face. An existential catastrophe, stripped of its rhetoric, is a flip into a basin of attraction from which the prior conditions no longer recover the prior state — a reef that will not come back when the water cools. The defensible core of longtermism was never "preserve the good." It was "do not cross thresholds you cannot uncross." And that core is not borrowed from us. It is the same value-free structural fact that survived Drake's knife.

The Borrowed Standard and the Real One

Two different things hide inside the single word collapse, and almost every argument about the environment and the future founders by confusing them.

The first is a borrowed standard — function, service, flourishing, the good. It is projected, observer-relative, and genuinely contestable. Drake is right about it; the critics of longtermism are right about it. You cannot win a fight by defending it, because your opponent can always ask, with perfect legitimacy, says who?

The second is a structural fact — irreversibility. It is not projected. It does not ask anyone's permission. Environmental economics has known this since Kenneth Arrow and Anthony Fisher showed, in 1974, that irreversibility by itself generates option value: when an action cannot be undone, the mere possibility that you might later wish you hadn't taken it is a real, calculable cost, independent of which way your preferences eventually fall.[11] You do not need to know what the future will want in order to know that the future would rather you not foreclose its wanting.

This is why "save the reef" and "safeguard the future" are honest sentences even after you concede that reefs have no jobs and that we cannot say what flourishing is. Their defensible content was never the borrowed standard. It was the structural one: stay on the reachable side of the barrier. Reversibility is the single value that future minds cannot have a different opinion about needing, because it is the precondition of their having opinions at all. It is the one thing you can owe them without presuming to know them. My own corpus has circled this from inside ecology — the Resilience Arc spent an entire essay on the brute asymmetry that recovery is categorically harder than collapse. What this adds is the reason that asymmetry, and not the loss of any function, is where the obligation actually lives.

What the Reef Remembers

One image holds the whole argument together, and it is one I have used before without seeing this far into it.

In an earlier essay, What the Self Preserves, I argued that an identity is a low-entropy attractor held in place by constant metabolic work — that to be a self is to keep paying the energetic cost of not dissolving into the more probable states all around you. A coral reef is that claim made of limestone and light. The living state is maintained, second by second, by the metabolic work of a symbiosis; the hysteresis — the energy barrier between the coral state and the algal one — is just the height of the wall that work has built. Bleaching is the wall being overtopped. And the reef's refusal to return when the water cools is the same phenomenon I once called, in What Doesn't Flow, the difference between flow and structure: the symbiosis is the flow, dissolvable in a week; the basin of attraction is the structure, a memory the system keeps of where it has been.

That is what an irreversible threshold is — a memory written in the shape of what can still happen next. To cross one is not to make a system fail at its job. It is to delete a memory the future was counting on without knowing it had. Longtermism, stripped to the one thing it can defend without arrogance, is the instruction not to do that: do not cross what you cannot uncross, because the future's right to choose its own standard is the only standard you can hand it without first stealing its place. The reef cannot fail. It has no job to fail at. But it can be lost — and "lost," it turns out, is a word about doors, not about jobs.
