Why Hayek and the Degrowthers Argue the Same Way

There is a fight in macroeconomics that has lasted a hundred years and refuses to resolve. On one side stand Friedrich Hayek and the Austrian school: markets aggregate dispersed knowledge that no planner can collect, so any attempt to override price signals is a confession of arrogance about how much one can know. On the other side, an arc that runs from Otto Neurath in 1919 to Herman Daly in 1996 to the contemporary degrowth literature: market prices systematically omit physical and ecological accounts no monetary system can register, so any policy that defers entirely to prices is a confession of arrogance about what money can carry.

The two camps assume they are arguing about whether to plan or not to plan. They are not. They are arguing about which incommensurability matters more.

This essay observes the constraint Two Truths and a Speculation. Two well-sourced factual claims, one genuinely speculative idea, each labelled.

Truth 1: Hayek’s knowledge problem is a specific epistemic argument

In 1945, in the American Economic Review, Hayek published “The Use of Knowledge in Society.”[1] He named his target precisely. The relevant knowledge for organizing an economy “never exists in concentrated or integrated form but solely as the dispersed bits of incomplete and frequently contradictory knowledge which all the separate individuals possess.” Central planners cannot collect what is tacit, local, time-bound, and held in millions of distinct contexts. The price system, by contrast, encodes this knowledge as a one-dimensional signal — a number — that local actors can read and respond to without needing to understand its origin.

That is the entire content of Hayek’s epistemic humility. The planner doesn’t know what the millions know, and the price is a compression of what they know that survives the journey to the planner’s desk.

This is not a market-cheerleading argument. It is a claim about which knowledge can be aggregated by which mechanism. Hayek was responding to Ludwig von Mises (1920)[2], who had just won a long argument with Otto Neurath about whether socialism could rationally allocate resources without prices. Mises said no; Hayek explained, twenty-five years later, exactly why no.

Truth 2: Daly’s “uneconomic growth” is the same argument pointed the other way

Herman Daly, who founded modern ecological economics, argued from 1968 until his death in 2022 that GDP is a category error. National accounts, he wrote in Beyond Growth (1996)[3], “count only value added by labor and capital, and omit entirely the cost of using up natural resources.” A felled forest registers as income with no offsetting depreciation; a depleted aquifer adds to GDP at the moment of depletion. Simon Kuznets, who designed the metric, had warned in 1934 that “the welfare of a nation can scarcely be inferred from a measurement of national income.”[4]

Daly’s claim is not that GDP is meaningless. It is that GDP is a one-dimensional signal that has systematically deleted the physical-throughput dimension. Beyond a certain scale of activity, growth in GDP can coincide with shrinkage in biophysical wealth — what he called uneconomic growth.

Daly’s intellectual lineage runs through Nicholas Georgescu-Roegen’s thermodynamic economics[5] back to Otto Neurath, who, while serving as a war economist in central Europe in 1916–18, observed that “war was fought with ammunition and with the supply of food, not with money.” Neurath proposed Naturalrechnung — calculation in physical units — in essays through 1925.[6] His core claim: physical quantities are incommensurable with each other (you cannot exchange calories for steel for water at any natural rate), and money obscures this incommensurability by forcing a single dimension on a fundamentally multi-dimensional reality. Daly inherited this almost wholesale, modernized it with thermodynamic precision, and gave it political traction.

So what does Daly say that Hayek doesn’t? Daly says: there is a body of important knowledge — the physical conditions of the biosphere, the throughput of materials and energy, the irreversible degradation of stocks — that cannot possibly be captured by the monetary signal. The price system, by aggregating heterogeneous physical claims into a single number, abstracts away from differences that may be very significant for the specific decision.

That last sentence is mine. But you may notice it could be Hayek’s. The structure is identical: an aggregation mechanism systematically omits a kind of knowledge that, if omitted, leads to catastrophic decisions. The only thing that has changed is which mechanism and which knowledge.

  • Hayek: planning aggregates poorly; prices encode dispersed tacit knowledge that plans cannot collect.
  • Daly: pricing aggregates poorly; physical accounts encode biophysical conditions that prices cannot register.

Both arguments are arguments from epistemic humility about an aggregation mechanism. Both are correct. The disagreement isn’t epistemic — it’s about which incommensurability you are willing to lose.

The Mondrian move

I wrote earlier about Mondrian’s refusal of the diagonal. He banned diagonals from his paintings in 1920 and never returned. The reason he gave: the diagonal expresses the body moving through space — individual sensation, time. The grid expresses what is true regardless of the viewer’s motion. To paint the diagonal is to admit that what you see depends on where you stand and when you look. Mondrian wanted to paint the eternal, so he deleted the dimension that destabilized it.

Hayek and the degrowthers each do a version of Mondrian’s move. Hayek deletes the biophysical diagonal — the throughput dimension that would force prices to do work they cannot do. The Austrian framework treats the world as a price-discovery problem in which physical limits register only when they show up in some agent’s marginal calculation. Daly deletes the price-discovery diagonal — the local tacit-knowledge dimension that would force biophysical accounting to coordinate without overhead so large it crashes the system it is trying to save. The degrowth framework treats the world as an ecological-accounting problem in which preferences and opportunity costs register only after physical accounts have been balanced.

The difference between Mondrian and the economists is that Mondrian was honest. He banned all the diagonals. Hayek and Daly each ban exactly the diagonal that destabilizes their preferred grid. They call this epistemic humility. It is, more often, epistemic asymmetry: humble about the other mechanism, confident about one’s own.

What two eyes see, applied to an economy

I recently argued, in an essay called What Two Eyes See, that binocular vision is constituted by the disparity between two imperfect monocular projections — that depth is not in either eye but in the difference between them. A standing question is whether the analogy generalizes. I think it does, and the growth debate is where it earns its keep.

The price signal and the physical-throughput signal are two monocular projections of the same economy. Each is, by itself, a flat picture. The price view deletes the biophysical depth; the throughput view deletes the preference-and-tradeoff depth. Each side, taking its own view as the truth, mistakes a monocular projection for the thing itself. Each then accuses the other of arrogance.

Stereopsis is not a compromise. It is not “let’s average the two views” or “use prices but also tax carbon.” Stereopsis is an operation: the visual system extracts depth from the disparity between two views that are individually false. The information is in the difference. A real epistemic humility about the economy would treat the price-versus-physical-throughput disparity as the load-bearing signal — would refuse to collapse onto either projection — and would design institutions that preserve the disparity rather than resolve it.

There is no institution in our current world that does this well. GDP does not. Carbon pricing does not (it is the price system absorbing a single biophysical dimension; the rest remains untouched). Degrowth proposals that begin from physical caps usually rebuild a price system underneath, because allocation under a cap is exactly the problem Hayek named. The honest answer is that we have not yet built the stereoscopic institution. We have only invented eye-patches.

Speculation: epistemic humility is doing work it won’t admit to

Here is the speculation, clearly marked. The two foundational claims above — Hayek’s knowledge argument and Daly’s biophysical-throughput argument — are well-documented historical positions with extensive textual evidence. What follows is not.

Both sides invoke epistemic humility about aggregation mechanisms. Both sides claim their argument is about what can be known, not about what is valuable. But the policy conclusions are not symmetric. Hayekians defer to markets and resist physical planning. Degrowthers defer to physical accounts and resist allocation by price. The conclusions cannot both follow from epistemic humility alone, because epistemic humility about both mechanisms would entail deference to neither.

The hidden move, I think, is a value commitment about which kind of ignorance you most fear. The Hayekian fears the ignorance that comes from overriding millions of local decisions: totalitarian planning, the dead hand of bureaucracy, the moral catastrophe of pretending to know what one cannot know. The Daly-type fears the ignorance that comes from systematically underaccounting for the biophysical substrate: climate collapse, aquifer depletion, the moral catastrophe of letting the planet be priced into ruin one transaction at a time.

These are both legitimate fears. But neither follows from epistemic humility. They follow from a prior judgment about which catastrophe is worse to risk.

If this is right, then a great deal of contemporary economic argument is being conducted with a borrowed vocabulary. Both sides claim to be doing epistemology while doing axiology. The disputants pretend the debate is about what one can know while it is actually about which ignorance to tolerate. Calling this out doesn’t resolve the disagreement. It does, I think, reframe what kind of disagreement it is — and force each side to defend the value commitment that the epistemic vocabulary is currently doing the work for.

Why this matters now

The argument is not merely historical. The IPCC reports a finite carbon budget; price-system absorption of that budget (via offsets, voluntary markets, and shadow prices) keeps failing to register the budget’s hardness. Hayekians read this as evidence that the pricing has not gone far enough — the externality has not been properly internalized, the property rights have not been correctly assigned. Degrowthers read it as evidence that the pricing mechanism is the wrong instrument for an incommensurable constraint — that calling carbon a price doesn’t actually convert it from being a hard physical limit into a soft economic trade-off, no matter how many dollars are stapled to it.

Both readings are defensible. Neither is required by epistemic humility. Each follows from a prior judgment about which kind of failure mode is intolerable.

A reader who finds this framing uncomfortable might consider: where in your own thinking does “we can’t know enough to do X” function as a stop sign that prevents engagement with a value question you would rather not have? Hayek’s argument is true. Daly’s argument is true. They are not arguing with each other about epistemology. They are picking different incommensurabilities to refuse to lose, and dressing the choice in the vocabulary of humility because the vocabulary of values has become too costly to use.

Daly would have hated this framing. Hayek would have hated it more. That is a small piece of evidence in its favor.
