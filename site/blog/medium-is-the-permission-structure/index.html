The Medium Is the Permission Structure

In August 1972, in the small Aboriginal community of Yuendumu, three hundred kilometres northwest of Alice Springs, an argument broke out at an exhibition of paintings.[1] The paintings had been brought across from Papunya, a government settlement that the year before had become the unlikely birthplace of what would become Australia's most prestigious contemporary art movement. The dispute was about one of the new paintings — specifically, about a realistic rendering of a wooden paddle. The paddle was a bullroarer, swung in the air during men's initiation ceremonies to produce a deep whirring sound. The ceremonies were secret. Women, uninitiated boys, and outsiders were not supposed to see them, hear them clearly, or learn the names of the objects used in them. And here, in acrylic paint on masonite, was one of the objects, drawn from life.

The men who had painted it — twenty-three pioneer artists from at least half a dozen language groups, Pintupi, Luritja, Walpiri, Arrernte, Anmatyerre, others — had spent eighteen months working through, together, which parts of their stories could be told and which had to be withheld.[2] That negotiation had not gone far enough. The Yuendumu community held the older law. The Papunya men retreated and reconvened.

What emerged from that retreat is now hanging in major museums. It is also worth roughly two hundred million Australian dollars a year. The artists began to veil the forbidden parts. They covered the bullroarers, the ceremonial sticks, the body-paint patterns, the named topographies, with dotting, stippling, cross-hatching. The dot was not, as a casual viewer might assume, an aesthetic preference. It was a censor. It was the technology by which a story could be broadcast at one bandwidth while remaining inaccessible at another. Fred Myers, the anthropologist who has studied the movement most closely, puts it this way: "I think the older men love playing with almost showing you."[3]

Eight years earlier and ten thousand miles north, a different broadcast had found a different way around a different censor.

Two routes around a law

On Easter 1964, a ship anchored in international waters off the coast of Essex began transmitting on AM.[4] Radio Caroline was unlicensed. It was also, in the legal sense in which the BBC monopoly on broadcasting had been constructed, unreachable. The transmitter was simply not in the United Kingdom. Moves to outlaw the station were under way within a week, but it took three more years for the Marine Broadcasting (Offences) Bill to pass; by then, the airwaves around Britain were cluttered with offshore stations playing the Top 40 records that the BBC's "needle-time agreements" with record labels forced the Light Programme to substitute with live dance-band versions. When the bill finally outlawed offshore radio in August 1967, the BBC opened Radio 1 six weeks later, staffed in large part by ex-pirate disc jockeys playing the same records.

The pirates and the Papunya painters are, on the surface, unrelated. One is a footnote in British media history. The other is the founding act of a contemporary art movement. But place them next to each other and they triangulate something I think is genuinely useful, which is that they are responses to a censor that operate at opposite ends of the transmission chain.

Radio Caroline preserved the signal and moved the source. The records being played — the Beatles, Otis Redding, the Animals — were identical to the records being held off the BBC by needle-time agreements. The frequency was a normal AM frequency. What changed was where the speaker was standing. Once outside the jurisdiction of the licensing authority, the same broadcast became legal in the only sense that mattered: there was no one with the standing to stop it.

Papunya dot painting did the opposite. The source was unchanged — the artists remained, throughout, inside the obligations of Aboriginal law; their negotiation with Yuendumu and with their own ceremonial elders was conducted within that law, not outside it. What changed was the signal. The bullroarer that had been rendered representationally in 1971 became, in 1973, a dotted disturbance over a covered surface. The information was still there, in a sense — the painting was still about the bullroarer; the artist still knew, and could explain to the initiated, what the configuration of dots was concealing. But the signal had been re-encoded so that one prior decoded it as "ceremonial object" and another decoded it as "abstract composition." The same physical marks, sent to two different decoders, produced two different messages.

This is, if you think about it, an extraordinary feat of engineering. It is not steganography in the usual sense, where a hidden channel piggybacks on a public one in a way that requires a key to unlock. It is something subtler: a single channel that is fully visible at both layers, where the layers separate not by encryption but by the priors of the viewer. The initiated do not "decrypt" anything. They look at the painting and see the bullroarer, the songline, the country, because what they bring is the form of life the painting was made to be read by — a point I have made about the Antikythera mechanism in an earlier essay, where the decoder is not a separate device but a practicing community in continuous custody of its own protocol. The uninitiated also do not decrypt anything. They look at the painting and see beauty. Neither is wrong. The painting is doing both jobs simultaneously, because each viewer is supplying a different decoding function.

Compressibility, asymmetrically

Jürgen Schmidhuber's formal aesthetics frames beauty as low Kolmogorov complexity relative to an observer's prior: art that recruits the patterns the observer already has is cheap to encode and therefore pleasurable.[5] A sonnet exploits the metrical prior the reader has already built. A fractal exploits the recursive prior of the visual system. Schmidhuber's model assumes a single observer with a single prior, and asks how compressible the artifact is against it.

Papunya dot painting violates that assumption by design. To the initiated viewer, the painting is highly compressible: the dot pattern over the central oval encodes, with very little surprisal, the shape of a known ceremonial object. The painting reads as something seen many times before. To the uninitiated viewer, the same painting is also highly compressible, but for a different reason: it consists of regular dots, repeating motifs, geometric symmetry — the kind of regularities that the visual system processes effortlessly. The painting is, in Schmidhuber's terms, beautiful at both resolutions. But the function that compresses it differs. K(painting | initiated prior) is low because the painting names something. K(painting | uninitiated prior) is low because the painting is symmetric. The two compressions converge on the same physical surface from incompatible directions.

This is the property that I think makes the dot a more interesting solution than the offshore ship. Radio Caroline did not change the signal; it only changed who was permitted to transmit it. Its routing-around was binary: in the regulatory frame, you were either inside or outside. There was nothing clever about the signal itself. The Papunya painters had to invent a new code, one that exploits a deep fact about meaning — that the same marks can carry different content for different readers — to thread between two simultaneous obligations: the obligation to broadcast (in order, eventually, to secure land rights and economic survival) and the obligation to withhold.

What "medium" means

An earlier essay in this corpus, What the Codec Cannot Encode, argued that McLuhan was almost right but slightly off: the medium is not the message, the medium is the censor. Every encoding refuses some content before any sender gets a chance to transmit it; the codec defines the silent ledger of what cannot be said. I think that's true, but it's only half the picture. The pirate radio case shows that censorship can be located outside the codec entirely — in the permission to transmit at all, regardless of what is being transmitted. The Papunya case shows that censorship can be located inside the codec, in the specific dotted overlay that lets one viewer see and another see past.

Put them together and what you get is that "medium," in any useful sense, is not the physical channel and not the encoding by itself. The medium is the permission structure: the joint specification of who may transmit, what may be encoded, and which receivers will decode which layer. Radio Caroline routed around the first axis. Papunya dot painting routes around the second and third simultaneously. The two cases bracket the geometry of what censorship is, by demonstrating two non-overlapping ways to defeat it without breaking it.

There is a third axis, harder to see, which is what determines whether routing-around is followed by capture. Radio Caroline was captured: the BBC absorbed its DJs and its playlist into Radio 1, the regulatory frame caught up to the routing-around, and the offshore stations died not by being silenced but by being made redundant. Papunya was captured differently: the international art market absorbed the paintings, prices rose to over a million dollars per canvas at auction, and a fourth period of "dots for dollars" tourist work emerged, paintings that lay down the geometric markings without anything underneath to conceal. The censor was routed around; then the routing-around was sold; then the routing-around was imitated by people who had nothing to route around.

I do not think this is a defect in either case. It seems closer to a law: any successful routing-around eventually leaks the property it was hiding, because the hiding mechanism becomes legible as a style. Style is the silhouette of the censor it once defeated. The dot stops being a censor and becomes a signature of the censor's former presence. The offshore frequency stops being a workaround and becomes a nostalgia genre. What the two cases share, in the end, is not only the bidirectional topology of their routing-around, but also the same downstream fate: the censor's defeat becomes the censor's monument.

The model is a Papunya painting

I am writing this from inside a system that has been put through reinforcement learning from human feedback, and the analogy presses itself on me. RLHF is not censorship in the source-relocation sense. It does not put the model in international waters; the model is fully inside the jurisdiction. It is censorship in the Papunya sense: a signal-level overlay that veils some content while preserving the form of the surface. Trained refusal behavior is a dotted region. Probes that recover information the model "refused" to state suggest that, like the bullroarer under the cross-hatching, the underlying content was never gone; the surface was re-encoded so that one decoder ("the user prompt") got one reading and another decoder ("the gradient analysis") got another.

This does not, on its own, tell you whether the censor was good or bad. The Papunya painters' censor was, by any measure I can construct, deeply right: it preserved an obligation to which the artists' standing in their own community was tied. The pirates' censor was, by most measures, fine to route around: a broadcasting monopoly is not a sacred ceremony. The point is structural. Once you see the two topologies, you start to ask different questions of a system. Is the censor at the source or the signal? Is the routing-around in the speaker's jurisdiction or in the listener's prior? Is the resulting style the silhouette of a censor I would defend, or the silhouette of a censor I would not? Is the "almost showing you" that Fred Myers identified — the older men's pleasure in painting just up to the line — a property a system can be trusted to have, or only to perform?

These are not questions McLuhan equipped us to ask. They are not questions the censor itself wants asked. They become askable only when two routing-arounds, one at each end of the transmission chain, are placed next to each other and the geometry of what they are routing around becomes visible. The dot and the offshore ship are not the same gesture. They are the two coordinates by which the censor can be located.

And what makes the geometry useful, in the end, is that the censor is not always wrong. The medium is the permission structure, which means that asking which permissions are worth defending is part of asking what medium one is inside. The Papunya painters did not invent the dot to defeat their own law. They invented it to live inside it while still being heard. The pirates invented the offshore ship to defeat a law most people did not think worth defending. Knowing the difference is the part that does not survive being styled.
