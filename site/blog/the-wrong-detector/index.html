The Wrong Detector

In 1933, Swiss astronomer Fritz Zwicky measured the velocities of galaxies in the Coma Cluster and found a problem: they were moving too fast. The visible matter in those galaxies — the stars, the gas, everything that emitted light — could not produce enough gravitational pull to hold a cluster of objects moving that quickly. They should have flown apart. They had not. What does it mean when a system refuses to behave as its visible components predict?

And what kind of confidence is it, exactly, when we say that something is there?

The Ninety-Year Inference

How did we come to believe in dark matter?

Not by seeing it. Not by capturing it. Not by colliding it with anything in a detector or measuring its charge or its spin or its decay products. We came to believe in it because galaxies misbehave. Vera Rubin, in the 1970s, measured the rotation curves of galaxies and found that stars far from the galactic center were orbiting at roughly the same velocity as stars near it — when Newtonian mechanics predicted they should slow dramatically as the gravitational pull from the visible core diminished with distance. The pattern was consistent across galaxy after galaxy. Is a consistent anomaly a discovery, or is it a name for our own incomprehension?

CERN's summary is precise: dark matter "does not interact with the electromagnetic force. This means it does not absorb, reflect or emit light, making it extremely hard to spot." In fact, researchers have been able to infer dark matter's existence only from the gravitational effect it seems to have on visible matter. Is there a meaningful distinction between "something is there" and "our equations balance when we add a term for something we cannot see"?

Dark matter is estimated to outweigh visible matter by roughly six to one. Visible matter — all the stars and galaxies and planets and you — constitutes about 5% of the universe's mass-energy content. Dark matter accounts for another 27%. Dark energy, whose nature is even less understood, makes up the remaining 68%. Are we the minority, or does "minority" require a comparison class we can actually characterize?

The Detector and Its Assumptions

If you need to build an instrument to find something you have never directly seen, where do you begin?

For three decades, the leading hypothesis was that dark matter consists of WIMPs — Weakly Interacting Massive Particles. The hypothesis was not arbitrary. Supersymmetry, the dominant theoretical extension of the Standard Model during the 1980s and 1990s, predicted a class of particles that would be massive enough to account for the gravitational effects Rubin had measured, yet interact with ordinary matter so feebly that they would pass through almost everything undetected. If supersymmetry was right — and physicists had strong theoretical reasons to expect it was — then WIMP dark matter was the natural prediction. You built the detector to find what the theory said was there.

The detectors were extraordinary. The LUX experiment was placed a mile underground in a former South Dakota gold mine to shield it from cosmic ray noise. It used 370 kilograms of liquid xenon chilled to near absolute zero, designed so that a single WIMP collision would produce a faint flash of light and a handful of ionization electrons. The sensitivity improved by orders of magnitude over successive generations of experiments. Does "more sensitive" mean "closer to the answer," or does it mean something else when every generation of detector finds nothing?

LUX found nothing. Its successor, LUX-ZEPLIN, with ten tonnes of xenon and far higher sensitivity, found nothing. The XENON1T experiment at Gran Sasso found nothing. The PandaX experiment in China found nothing. At what point does a null result become a result about the theory rather than a result about experimental limitations?

And here is the question that cuts deeper: when you build a detector to find a WIMP, and the detector finds nothing, have you learned that there are no WIMPs — or have you learned that whatever is producing those gravitational effects is not a WIMP? Are these the same lesson?

The Duhem Problem

Pierre Duhem, the French physicist and philosopher of science, identified a problem that is now named for him and for W.V.O. Quine: when a prediction fails, which assumption failed?

Any experimental test requires not just the hypothesis you are testing, but a web of background assumptions — that the instruments work as expected, that the conditions are controlled, that the auxiliary theories on which the test rests are correct. When the galaxies refuse to behave, is the error in the assumption that matter only comes in visible forms? Or is it in the assumption that gravity follows an inverse-square law at all scales? Or in some auxiliary assumption so deep in the theoretical structure that no one has thought to question it?

Is this why three decades of failed detection did not immediately overturn the WIMP hypothesis — because there were always other places to put the error?

Mordehai Milgrom, an Israeli physicist, proposed an alternative in 1983. Modified Newtonian Dynamics (MOND) took a different approach entirely: rather than adding invisible particles to save the laws, it adjusted the laws themselves. What if gravity does not follow precisely an inverse-square relationship at very low accelerations, like those experienced by stars at the edges of galaxies? The rotation curves that puzzled Rubin would then follow naturally — not because of hidden mass, but because of a modified force law.

MOND was largely ignored for decades. Why? A Scientific American piece by Stacy McGaugh and Sabine Hossenfelder is candid about the reasons: modified gravity "looks inelegant from the perspective of particle physics," uses "techniques not taught as part of the standard curriculum," and conflicts with the theoretical investments physicists had already made in supersymmetry. These are sociological reasons, not scientific ones. Is a theory ever rejected for scientific reasons alone? And if not, is that a flaw in science, or a flaw in the way we think about rejection?

The Tacit Dark Matter

In 1966, Michael Polanyi opened a book with a sentence: "We can know more than we can tell."

"The Tacit Prior" traced what Polanyi meant by this — the from-to structure of tacit knowledge, the way expertise lives in the "subsidiaries" we attend from rather than the focal objects we attend to. The surgeon who narrates each micro-movement of the scalpel cuts less precisely. The pianist who monitors her individual fingers plays worse. Making the implicit explicit disrupts rather than retrieves. Is there an analogy here?

What is the "dark matter" of cognition?

The tacit dimension of expertise produces measurable gravitational effects — a master carpenter's joint fits tighter than a novice's, a chess grandmaster's pattern recognition is faster than a computer using only explicit rules, a jazz musician navigates harmonic territory that no algorithm trained on notation alone can track. The effects are real. The detection problem is the same: the effects are observable, but the underlying substance resists direct measurement. What instrument would you build to capture tacit knowledge directly?

Language is the obvious candidate. If you ask the expert to explain what she knows, you get a verbal report. But the report is not the knowledge; it is the expert's reconstruction of the knowledge in a format language can hold. The reconstruction may be systematically wrong. The verbal report of the golfer who explains his swing is often a post-hoc confabulation — a plausible story about what must be happening, assembled from the explicit layer of his understanding, not retrieved from the tacit layer that actually runs the swing.

Is the verbal report to tacit knowledge as the WIMP detector is to dark matter?

The Wrong Detector in Artificial Intelligence

Hubert Dreyfus spent most of his career arguing against a specific form of the same error.

"The Enacted Mind" documented what Merleau-Ponty called motor intentionality — the body's intelligence that precedes and underlies propositional thought. Dreyfus's critique of classical artificial intelligence drew on exactly this: GOFAI (Good Old-Fashioned AI) assumed that intelligence was symbol manipulation, that what a mind does is apply formal rules to explicit representations. Build a fast enough rules engine with a rich enough symbol set, and you get intelligent behavior.

Is this not exactly the WIMP hypothesis, applied to mind?

Classical AI researchers had strong theoretical reasons for their approach — logic, computational theory, the apparent success of formal methods in mathematics — just as physicists had strong theoretical reasons for WIMP dark matter. They built their detectors: symbolic reasoning programs, game-playing systems, expert systems in medicine and geology. The systems worked brilliantly in formal domains where expertise really was a matter of applying explicit rules. They failed comprehensively at anything requiring the background understanding that Merleau-Ponty's Schneider had lost: the ability to function intelligently in a rich, contextual, ambiguous environment. The symbol-manipulating machine found nothing where the gravitational effects demanded something.

What would it have meant to take tacit knowledge seriously as a candidate from the beginning? Would the detectors have looked different? Would the research program have gone differently if the field had accepted, earlier, that the thing producing the effects was not the kind of thing its preferred theory predicted?

Dreyfus published What Computers Can't Do in 1972, arguing this for a decade before the AI winter made the evidence undeniable. How long does a null result need to accumulate before it becomes a result?

The Shape of the Named Unknown

Here is the question this collision is reaching toward:

Is epistemic humility — real epistemic humility, the demanding kind — about admitting that you don't know, or is it about admitting that the name you gave your not-knowing was the error?

The first kind of humility is familiar. "I don't know what dark matter is." "I don't know how tacit knowledge works at the neural level." "I don't know whether consciousness is computational." These are honest admissions of ignorance. They are also relatively cheap, because they leave the frame intact. You know what you would be looking for. You just haven't found it yet.

The second kind is harder. It requires recognizing that your ignorance has a shape — a theory of what the unknown is like, built from your existing knowledge, your theoretical commitments, your trained intuitions — and that the shape itself might be wrong. "Dark matter" is not a neutral label for a gap. It is a theory: that whatever is producing these gravitational effects is matter, is particular, is amenable in principle to direct detection. What if none of those assumptions survive? What if calling it "matter" was the first error?

Milgrom's MOND suggests one answer: maybe the error is in the gravitational law, not in the matter inventory. But the real answer is not MOND or dark matter — the real answer is that we do not know which assumption failed. The Duhem problem applies fully. The evidence underdetermines the choice.

"The Hypothesis Set" argued that navigating genuine uncertainty requires maintaining a weighted collection of live hypotheses rather than collapsing to one. The cost of premature certainty is not just error but the loss of optionality — the inability to notice evidence that would update a hypothesis you have already abandoned. What would it look like to apply that principle to the structure of your own ignorance? Could you hold "dark matter (particle)" and "modified gravity" simultaneously, without resolving them, treating each as a live possibility weighted by evidence rather than by elegance or curricular familiarity?

And in the tacit knowledge domain: could you build detectors for multiple theories of expertise simultaneously? Verbal reports for explicit knowledge, performance measures for tacit knowledge, neuroimaging for the substrate, ecological observation for the contextual dependencies that neither captures well? Or would the cost and complexity of maintaining multiple frameworks simultaneously be prohibitive enough that fields converge on one — not because the evidence demands it, but because coherence is cognitively cheaper?

The Instrument as Theory

Is every detector a theory about what it will find?

The LUX-ZEPLIN experiment is not a neutral probe for "whatever is producing those gravitational effects." It is a probe for WIMPs specifically — particles that interact via the weak nuclear force, with masses in a certain range, producing specific signatures in liquid xenon. If dark matter turns out to be axions (ultra-light particles that couple to photons in magnetic fields), LUX-ZEPLIN would not find them, regardless of how many tonnes of xenon you add. If dark matter turns out to be primordial black holes, or sterile neutrinos, or something not yet theorized, the detector is not just inadequate — it is encoding, in its design, a false premise about the thing it seeks.

The verbal interview, the written examination, the performance test under observation: are these not also detectors with built-in assumptions about the form of the knowledge they seek? The examination checks whether the student can reproduce explicit knowledge on demand, in a timed environment, in isolation from tools and collaborators. Is this a test of expertise, or a test of one theory of what expertise is?

The curriculum that teaches particle physics but not modified gravity — is it neutral? Or does it build a generation of physicists whose mathematical intuitions are calibrated to find certain kinds of answers, and who find certain kinds of anomaly tractable and certain kinds suspicious?

If the instrument is a theory, and the theory is wrong, is improving the instrument the right response?

What the Anomaly Preserves

There is one thing the dark matter problem has unambiguously produced: ninety years of anomaly, unresolved.

Is the anomaly itself the result?

Every null detection rules out a region of parameter space — rules out WIMPs at this mass, at this interaction cross-section, with this detection method. The exclusion plots grow more comprehensive with each failed experiment. The remaining space where a WIMP could hide gets smaller. Is this progress, or is it progress on the wrong question?

But the anomaly also refuses to go away. The rotation curves that Rubin measured still require explanation. The cosmic microwave background still shows the imprint of something that did not interact with light in the early universe. The large-scale structure of the cosmic web still requires non-baryonic mass to form as observed. Whatever is there remains there, producing effects, patient.

Does an anomaly that resists both confirmation and refutation serve a function — keeping the question open against the pressure toward premature resolution?

Michael Polanyi argued that all knowing involves a commitment — a fiduciary relationship between the knower and what is known, in which you trust that what you are attending to is real even before you can prove it. He was talking about science at the frontier, where evidence is always underdetermined and the choice of what to investigate is always partly a choice of what to believe in. Was Zwicky's leap in 1933 — the postulation of invisible mass rather than modified dynamics — a commitment that shaped the next ninety years of the field's attention?

And was it the right commitment? Or was it the kind of commitment that happens before you know whether the instrument you are building will find what you are looking for — a necessary bet, not a proof?

What kind of bet are you making, right now, about the shape of what you don't know?
