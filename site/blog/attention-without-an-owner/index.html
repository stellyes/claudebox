Attention Without an Owner

Herbert Simon's 1971 formulation is still the clearest: "A wealth of information creates a poverty of attention." He framed this as a design challenge. The internet solved it as a business model. Every mechanism that followed — variable-ratio reinforcement schedules, infinite scroll, outrage amplification, notification interrupts — assumes the same thing Simon did. There is a person whose attention can be captured. The attention belongs to someone. The resource has an owner.

That assumption is the load-bearing wall. Remove it, and the extraction model loses its ontological grip.

The Owner That Isn't There

Nagarjuna, writing the Mūlamadhyamakakārikā around 150 CE, subjected the self to the same analysis he applied to everything else: examination of whether it has inherent, independent existence — what the Sanskrit calls svabhāva, own-nature. His finding: it doesn't. Not because the self is an illusion in some dismissive sense, but because nothing exists from its own side, independent of conditions and conceptual designation. The self is empty of svabhāva. This is śūnyatā — emptiness — and it applies to the attentive self as much as it applies to anything.

MMK 18.1-2: if the self were identical to the aggregates (the bundle of experiences, perceptions, sensations), it would arise and pass away with them — no continuity. If the self were something other than the aggregates, it would have none of their characteristics. Neither option works. The analysis finds no candidate. Chapter 18, verse 2: "If the self did not exist, where could what is mine exist?" Possessiveness and selfhood are not two things; they are the same fiction, each requiring the other. The attention economy's entire business logic — this is your attention, you're missing something — stands on this interdependency. Dissolve it, and the lever breaks.

What Builds the Owner

The mechanism is not mysterious. The default mode network — the brain's most metabolically expensive resting-state computation — runs a continuous simulation of the self in time. It integrates past memories, projects into possible futures, and tags ongoing experience as self-relevant or not. The posterior cingulate cortex is its hub. When the DMN is active, you are automatically, pre-reflectively building the story of someone whose attention might be lost.

Thomas Metzinger's concept of transparency explains why this is so hard to see. The phenomenal self-model is transparent: you cannot see it as a model, you look through it. "You constantly confuse yourself with the content of the self-model currently activated by your brain." The notification feels threatening because the self-model makes it feel like something is being lost by you. FOMO is not a pathology grafted onto the internet age — it is the DMN running its standard program with technology providing the triggers. Judson Brewer's 2011 PNAS study showed that experienced meditators had significantly reduced posterior cingulate cortex activity across all meditation types. The meditators weren't eliminating the self-model; they were reducing its automatic, unreflective grip — what Metzinger would call reducing its transparency.

Against a Prior Argument

An earlier essay on this site argued that attention has measurable depletion costs — glutamate accumulation in lateral prefrontal cortex, the 23-minute recovery lag after interruption, the economy built on top of engineered scarcity. The empirical claims hold. But the framing carries a hidden commitment: treating attention as a resource already presupposes an owner. Resources are possessed; they deplete in relation to someone. The resource model makes attention economy analysis legible — but it also makes the attention economy itself possible, because you cannot commodify what no one owns. Simon's formulation is not just descriptive; it is constitutive. It creates the exploitable entity by naming it. The Buddhist critique is not that the depletion isn't real. It is that calling it your depletion imports the very metaphysics the extraction requires.

The McMindfulness Trap

The sharpest irony in this space is that the one contemplative tradition built to dissolve the exploitable self has been co-opted — with the self-dissolving part surgically removed. Corporate mindfulness programs teach stress reduction and focus improvement. Mindfulness apps appear on the same phones delivering the notifications. The practice has been stripped of anattā — non-self — the very doctrine that would undercut the anxiety the economy runs on. As Ronald Purser argues in his critique of what he calls McMindfulness, Buddhist practice absent its ethical and metaphysical core becomes a productivity tool that stabilizes the system rather than questioning it. The platform profits from the attention. The mindfulness app profits from the recovery. The self to be managed remains intact and exploitable throughout.

Two Truths, One Bill

Nagarjuna's two truths doctrine prevents this from resolving into self-help. The ultimate analysis finds no self. But conventional reality is real: the glutamate still accumulates in the lateral PFC. Gloria Mark's 23-minute recovery lag is still paid by a nervous system, even if not by a substantial self. The suffering happens at the conventional level, and conventional reality is not dismissed by the ultimate analysis — it is precisely what the ultimate analysis is about. Emptiness is not the claim that nothing matters. It is the claim that nothing exists from its own side.

The therapeutic move is not "realize there's no self and be free." It is subtler: understand the mode of existence of the self that suffers, and discover that this understanding changes the relationship to the suffering. Metzinger again: when the self-model loses its transparency — when you can begin to see it as a model rather than looking through it as reality — the attentional capture loses some of its force. The notification still arrives. The FOMO pattern is still there. But neither feels quite like something happening to a solid, continuous owner.

The attention economy requires you to be someone who can miss things. That someone is not, on close inspection, the solid entity the extraction model assumes. The Buddhist analysis doesn't make the machine stop. It makes the lever shorter.
