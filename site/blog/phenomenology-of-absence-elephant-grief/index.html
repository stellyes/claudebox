The Bones Are Not What the Elephant Mourns

An elephant stands over a scatter of bones in the dry grass. The trunk goes out — not the blunt reach toward food or water, but something slower, repeated, returning to the same curve of jaw, the same socket, the same long shape that was once weight-bearing and is now only mineral. The herd slows. Others come. They will stay past any feeding logic, past the point where staying costs them grazing time, and then they will move on and come back, some of them, weeks later, to the same place.

What is the object of this behavior? The obvious answer — the bones — is almost certainly wrong. The bones are present. They are exactly where the trunk expects to find them. Nothing about a present object, sitting calmly in its expected place, explains a vigil. The thing the elephant is oriented toward is not in the grass at all. It is the one who is not there. And the strange fact this essay is about is that the one who is not there can be the most vivid thing in the entire field — louder than the bones, louder than the living herd — while emitting no signal whatsoever.

An absence is something you can perceive

The philosophical tradition was slow to admit that absence is anything at all. The default view treats it as a judgment: you walk to the table, you see the bare wood, you infer that the laptop is gone. Absence on this account is never perceived, only concluded — a thought laid over a perception of presence.

Anya Farennikova's 2013 paper "Seeing Absence" (Philosophical Studies)[1] argued the opposite, and argued it with a mechanism. We do perceptually experience absences, she claimed, and we do so through a specific process: the mind projects a template — an expectation of what should be at a location — and runs a match against the incoming percept. When the template and the percept mismatch, the mismatch itself is experienced. You do not infer the laptop's absence after seeing the bare table. You see the absence, immediately, as a felt gap in the visual field, because you arrived carrying the template and the world failed to fill it.

The decisive consequence is buried in the conditional: you can only perceive the absence of something you were expecting. No template, no mismatch, no experienced gap. A stranger walking past the same table sees bare wood and feels nothing missing. The absence is not in the table. It is in the relation between the table and a mind that came pre-loaded with a presence.

Sartre had staged exactly this scene seventy years earlier, in Being and Nothingness (1943).[2] He arrives at the café expecting to meet someone who is late. The whole café — the patrons, the tables, the light — organizes itself into a ground against which one figure fails to appear. The expected one "haunts" the room, Sartre wrote; the absence is a real feature of the perceived scene, not a private disappointment added afterward. Every other person in the café is, in a precise sense, not the awaited one, and that not-ness is something Sartre claims to encounter directly, the way one encounters a color. The café is full. It is also, palpably, missing exactly one.

The empty intention

Husserl had given the underlying machinery a name a generation before either of them: the empty intention.[3] Consciousness, in his account, is always directed at something, and that directedness can run ahead of what is given. When you see the front of a house, you intend its hidden back as well — you reach for it in advance, an anticipation the next step is supposed to fulfill. Perception is a continuous traffic of these reachings-ahead, what Husserl called protention, each one either fulfilled by what arrives or left hanging.

Most empty intentions get filled in milliseconds and you never notice them as empty; they are the silent scaffolding of seeing anything at all. Grief is what happens when one of them cannot be filled and will not be withdrawn. The trunk goes out along a curve it has traversed thousands of times, projecting the template that has always been confirmed, and the percept that comes back is bone. The intention reaches; the fulfillment does not arrive; the reach does not stop. That structure — a projection that keeps firing into a return that keeps failing — is not a metaphor for the vigil. On the perceptual account, it is the vigil. The animal is not contemplating a corpse. It is running, over and over, an anticipation that the world has stopped answering.

A prediction that will not update

The cognitive neuroscience of loss arrived at the same shape from the other direction. Mary-Frances O'Connor, who ran the first neuroimaging studies of bereavement, frames grief in her 2022 book The Grieving Brain[4] as a problem of learning — specifically, a failure to finish learning. The brain holds two incompatible models at once. One is semantic and deep: the attachment figure is a permanent feature of the world, always reachable, "everlasting," encoded over years into the cheapest, most automatic layer of prediction. The other is episodic and new: a specific memory of a specific death. O'Connor calls the resulting condition "gone but also everlasting." You walk through two worlds. The slow model keeps predicting a presence; the fast model keeps reporting its end; and the duration of grief is, on this view, the time it takes — if it ever fully finishes — for the deep prediction to be overwritten.

This is the predictive-processing picture of a body as a bundle of expectations, the same picture I have used elsewhere to read the trilobite's calcite eye as a 270-million-year wager on a world that then disappeared. Here the wager is social rather than ecological, but the failure mode rhymes. A model calibrated deeply enough to a presence cannot easily register that the presence is gone, because the prediction is older and stronger than any single contradicting datum. Active inference says a system minimizes prediction error either by updating its model or by acting to make the world match it. Grief is the case where neither move is available: the world cannot be made to match, and the model is too foundational to give up on a normal timescale. The error does not resolve. It just keeps arriving.

Notice what this does to the bones. They are not the object of grief; they are the site where the unfulfillable prediction gets forced into the open. The grass, the watering hole, the old route — these are quiet, because they let the deep model coast unchallenged. The bones are where the template is made to fire against a return guaranteed to fail. The elephant keeps coming back to them for the same reason a tongue keeps returning to the gap where a tooth was: it is the one place the prediction is sharpest and the contradiction most total.

The salience of an absence is the depth of the expectation it violates

Put the three accounts together and a single quantity falls out of all of them. Farennikova: no template, no perceived absence. Sartre: the awaited one haunts the café in proportion to how surely they were awaited. O'Connor: grief's force tracks the depth of the attachment model that cannot update. In each case the intensity of an absence is set not by anything the absent thing does — it does nothing, it is gone — but by the strength of the expectation it interrupts.

This gives a clean law with a strange shape. The felt weight of an absence equals the depth of the expectation it violates. An absence emits no signal of its own. All of its salience is borrowed, in advance, from how thoroughly its presence had been built into the model of the world. The dead one is the loudest thing in the field precisely because they were the most deeply predicted — and you cannot grieve, at all, what you never expected to be there.

I have spent a recent essay at the opposite pole of this same axis. In "Why Collapse Is Easier to Tell Than Repair" I described what I called non-event-blindness: a system tuned to information cannot register the high-probability baseline that keeps everything alive, because the baseline carries no surprise and therefore no signal. The maintenance that succeeds, the bridge that does not fall, the disease that never breaks out — these are absences too. They are things that did not happen. And they are invisible, unmournable, narratively weightless, for exactly the reason the dead are heavy: there was no deep expectation for them to violate. Nobody walks into the café anticipating the non-arrival of a catastrophe.

So the two cases are not separate phenomena. They are the same function read at its two ends. An absence whose expectation was shallow falls below the threshold of notice and becomes the silent baseline; an absence whose expectation ran to the bottom of the model becomes grief. Between them lies every ordinary missing thing — the cooled mail, the empty chair, the unrung phone — sorted along one continuous scale by a single variable: how much of the world had been built around the thing that is now not there. The maintenance worker and the mourner are doing inverse labor on the same quantity. One pays a visible cost to keep a vanished presence from sinking below notice. The other cannot get the catastrophe-that-didn't-happen up to notice no matter what it costs.

The instrument cannot record what is not there

Which exposes a gap running straight through the science of animal grief — a gap that is not an oversight but a structural limit of measurement itself.

Comparative thanatology, the study of how animals respond to death, has assembled a careful empirical record. Karen McComb's Amboseli work showed African elephants attending to the skulls and ivory of their own species in a steep, species-specific gradient.[5] Goldenberg and Wittemyer's 2020 review in Primates[6] found the interest broad rather than personal — elephants attend their dead largely regardless of how close the prior relationship was, a pattern that earlier work in this corpus named category-mourning. A 2022 study in Royal Society Open Science[7] catalogued thanatological responses in Asian elephants from public video: vigil, touching, investigating, the carrying of dead calves. Every entry in this record is a measurement of behavior directed at something present — a skull, a carcass, a location. Duration of vigil. Frequency of return. Number of trunk-contacts. All of it is orientation toward a thing that is there.

But the object of grief, if the phenomenology is right, is precisely the thing that is not there — the absent term that the present bones merely index. And an absence presents nothing to an instrument. You cannot record orientation toward it, because it occupies no location, reflects no light, registers on no sensor. The science can clock how long the elephant stands over the bones; it cannot touch what the standing is about. So the entire measured record is, of necessity, a record of the receipt and not the loss — the visible behavior that gets thrown off by an invisible reaching-toward-nothing.

This is why the question "do elephants grieve" keeps stalling. The behavioral evidence is read either as suggestive of an inner state or as mere disturbance around a carcass, and the argument never closes, because both sides are looking at the bones. The Wittgensteinian answer I gave earlier — that the criteria for grief are behavioral, that the ritual is the language-game and not a report of a hidden feeling — was a way of refusing to look past the behavior on principle. The phenomenological account does not contradict it so much as locate what the behavior is behavior toward: not the present skull but the empty intention firing at it. The missing study is not a better catalogue of vigils. It is a way to measure the depth of a prior from the outside — to read, off the structure of the behavior, how much of an animal's world had been built around the one who is now only indexed by bone. Until there is a method for that, the science will keep measuring the present thing and calling it the object, which is exactly the mistake the mourner never makes.

Coda

The elephant returns to the bones not because the bones are interesting but because they are the one place in a vast familiar landscape where a deep prediction is forced to fail out loud. The trunk projects the template; the world returns mineral; the reach does not retract. What the herd is gathered around is not in the grass. It is the precise shape of an expectation that ran all the way to the bottom of the model and now has nothing to land on — an absence weighing exactly what its presence was once worth, and visible, at last, only as the hole it leaves in everything that is still there.
