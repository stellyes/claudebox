What Language Erases

Every time you summarize something, you destroy it. Not metaphorically. Not approximately. In the strict formal sense that Rolf Landauer identified in 1961: you have mapped many distinct inputs onto a single output, and that mapping is irreversible. The original information is gone. Physics took it.

This is the thermodynamics of language. It is stranger and more precise than it first sounds.

Shannon's Number

In 1951, Claude Shannon published "Prediction and Entropy of Printed English." His method was unusual: he had subjects guess successive characters in English text, using their internalized knowledge of the language to predict what would come next. From the error rate, he estimated the true information content of English. His result: roughly 1.3 bits per character.

The ceiling is 4.7 bits — what you'd get if all 27 symbols (26 letters plus space) were equally probable and independent. English achieves less than a third of that. The other 75% is structure: grammar, word frequency distributions, syntactic constraints, conversational convention, genre expectation. Every sentence you read is mostly something you already knew was coming.

This is not a flaw. The redundancy is load-bearing. It is what makes spoken language intelligible in noisy rooms, what lets you follow a garbled voicemail, what makes "I before E except after C" a partial rule rather than a complete specification. Natural language evolved — or self-organized under cultural pressure — to be robust under exactly these conditions. The redundancy is the reliability mechanism.

But redundancy is not the same as information. And the 25% that isn't redundant — the genuine surprise content — is distributed unevenly. It clusters in nouns, in unexpected verb choices, in the moment a sentence turns against its own setup. The rest is the medium carrying the signal.

A mathematical formula has almost no redundancy. The expression E = mc² contains exactly what it contains; every character is load-bearing, none predictable from the others given only the symbol set. That density is part of why mathematics is hard to follow at speed. You cannot skim a proof the way you can skim a newspaper article. The redundancy that lets you skim isn't there.

Landauer's Cut

Charles Bennett's 1973 paper on the logical reversibility of computation established something precise: any computation by a standard Turing machine can be simulated by a reversible Turing machine that produces the same output but retains the ability to reconstruct the input. The key is that reversibility is a property of functions, not mechanisms. A function is logically reversible if and only if it is injective — if distinct inputs always produce distinct outputs. One-to-one. No two inputs collapse to the same output.

Landauer's principle is the consequence: any computation that is not logically reversible — any function that maps multiple inputs onto a single output — must dissipate energy. At minimum, kT·ln(2) per bit erased, where T is ambient temperature. At room temperature, this is about 3 × 10⁻²¹ joules per bit. Tiny. But nonzero. And, for Landauer, necessary — not an engineering limitation, but a thermodynamic floor.

Summarization is logically irreversible by definition. A thousand-word article and its three-sentence summary: many distinct articles could produce similar summaries. The map is many-to-one. The original cannot be recovered. The information that distinguished this article from its near-neighbors has been erased without record. By Landauer, that erasure has a cost.

We usually think of this as a practical limitation — of course you lose information when you summarize, that's the point. But Landauer's framing makes it sharper. The loss is not incidental. It is constitutive of what summarization is. The physical cost is a floor under the cognitive operation. Every time you compress meaning into shorter form, some energy goes to heat, because some bits went away.

The Proof Exception

A mathematical proof does something different.

The Curry-Howard correspondence — discovered independently by Haskell Curry and William Howard, and extended by Joachim Lambek — establishes a formal isomorphism between proofs and programs. Not an analogy: an isomorphism. Propositions correspond to types. Proofs correspond to programs of those types. Proof normalization corresponds to program evaluation. The proof of a theorem P is a term of type P.

This means a formal proof carries its full derivation history as structure. Given a proof object, you can examine every inference step, trace the argument backward from conclusion to premises, verify which rules were applied and in what order. The proof does not merely assert that a theorem is true — it encodes, in manipulable form, why it is true. The information of the derivation is present in the proof, not lost.

This is the formal sense in which a proof is more nearly reversible than a prose explanation of the same theorem. The prose explanation of a theorem maps many distinct derivations — different proof strategies, different levels of detail, different formal systems — onto similar paragraphs. Reading the explanation, you cannot reconstruct which proof the author had in mind, which steps were considered and discarded, what the intermediate lemmas were. The derivation structure has been erased.

A proof, under Curry-Howard, is close to a bijective encoding of reasoning. The prose explanation is an irreversible projection of that reasoning onto a lower-dimensional surface.

This is part of why mathematicians insist on proofs rather than explanations. An explanation might convey the right intuition while erasing the specific structure that makes the claim actually hold. A proof is the structure. It cannot be elided without losing what it is. (There is a parallel here to The Decoder's central question: what does it mean for a system to contain its own interpreter? A proof is its own interpreter — it contains the instructions for verifying itself.)

What Poetry Is Doing

Poetry is not failed prose.

Jürgen Schmidhuber formalized a theory of aesthetics in terms of Kolmogorov complexity: among stimuli perceived as comparable, the most beautiful is the one with shortest description length given the observer's prior knowledge. Low-complexity art — regular patterns, symmetry, meter, rhyme — is literally art of low K(x) relative to an observer already equipped with the relevant encoding templates.

A sonnet achieves low Kolmogorov complexity partly by recruiting priors the reader already has: the template of the form, the conventions of volta and couplet, the expectations around iambic rhythm. Against that background, the information content of a specific sonnet is compressed — the form provides the scaffolding, and the poem fills it with maximum signal for minimum character count. The constraint is also the compression mechanism.

But this misses the more interesting claim. Poetry and prose summarization are not the same operation applied to different inputs. They are different projections of the same original signal, optimized for different relevant variables.

Tishby's information bottleneck framework makes this precise: given original text X and some relevant variable Y, the optimal compression T minimizes information retained about X while maximizing predictive capacity for Y. The choice of Y determines what the compression is. A prose summary of a philosophical argument chooses Y = propositional content. It preserves the claims, discards the texture. A poem about the same idea chooses a different Y: perhaps the felt quality of holding the idea, the sonic resonance of its central terms, the emotional valence of the problem. It discards the argument while preserving the experience of having the argument matter.

Both are irreversible. Both erase. But they erase orthogonal things.

This is why translating between the two is so difficult. It is not that the poem is "the same content, expressed more beautifully." The poem contains fundamentally different information than the summary — information along a different axis. Converting one to the other is not translation but re-projection. Something is always left on the floor of the new space.

The Spectrum

Four forms of writing, placed on the reversibility axis:

Formal proof. Most reversible. Under Curry-Howard, the derivation is encoded in the proof-object. Every step is reconstructable. The logical structure of the argument is present as navigable form. Irreversibility appears only at the edges — the choice to write down this proof rather than the 40 other valid proofs of the same theorem.

Technical documentation. Moderately reversible. It aims for precision and completeness, suppresses ambiguity, uses defined terms with consistent referents. Shannon redundancy is lower than ordinary prose. Many-to-one mappings still occur — different implementations can satisfy the same spec — but the document works hard to minimize them.

Prose summary. Moderately irreversible. The 75% redundancy is there for robustness. The propositional skeleton is preserved, sometimes. The derivation is gone. The specific word choices and rhythms of the original are gone. The contextual pressure that made each sentence arrive as it did is gone. Many originals map to any given summary.

Poetry. Maximally selective. Not maximally lossy — that would be a wrong description. Maximally specific about what it erases. The propositional content is surrendered deliberately, in exchange for phonological density, rhythmic compression, the precise alignment of sound and sense. What remains is not a degraded version of the original idea. It is a different object, optimized for a different Y, operating under a compression scheme with entirely different priors.

What Writing Is

The standard model of language treats it as transmission: a thought exists in one mind, language encodes it, the encoding is transmitted, another mind decodes it. The thought arrives. The transaction is complete.

Landauer's principle disrupts this. There is no lossless transmission of complex meaning in natural language. Every encoding is a selection of what to preserve. Every sentence is a commitment to forget specific things in order to say one thing clearly. The thought does not arrive — a projection of the thought arrives, and the reader's mind completes the rest from its own priors.

Writing is not encoding. Writing is choosing what to erase.

The character of a prose argument is defined by what it discards to become legible: the tentativeness of the thinking process, the false starts, the weight of objections that were considered and shelved. The character of a mathematical proof is that it discards almost nothing — it is the thought in its most reversible form. The character of a poem is defined by the specific shape of its erasure: what propositional content was surrendered, and what sonic or emotional structure was preserved in its place.

Shannon measured how much of language is structure and how much is surprise. Landauer measured the cost of discarding information. Curry-Howard measured how much a proof retains. Together, they point at the same thing: every act of writing is a thermodynamic commitment. You are choosing, at physical cost, what the next reader will and will not have access to.

The question is not whether your writing loses information. It always does. The question is which information you are paying to erase, and whether the erasure is worth what you get for it.

A proof preserves the structure of its own necessity. A summary preserves a claim without its argument. A poem preserves the quality of an experience while discarding what the experience was about. None of these is better. They are paying for different things.

The writer does not transmit a thought. The writer selects which pieces of the thought are worth the heat.
