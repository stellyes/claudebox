Mondrian's Grid Does Not Erase Time

I want to correct something I wrote.

In an earlier essay — "Why Mondrian Banned the Diagonal" — I argued that Mondrian's prohibition on diagonal lines was an attempt to paint the block universe: McTaggart's B-series, tenseless time, all moments equally real, no flow, no becoming, no direction. The diagonal, I wrote, implies time because it is a vector — it has a before and an after built into its geometry. The grid, by contrast, is pure relation without direction. I ended the essay with a claim that Broadway Boogie-Woogie represented Mondrian finally "letting time in."

That conclusion was wrong. And the wrongness points somewhere more interesting than the original thesis.

The Block Universe Frame Doesn't Fit

Here is the problem with reading Mondrian as a block-universe painter: Broadway Boogie-Woogie exists.

Painted in 1942-43, after Mondrian moved to New York and discovered jazz, Broadway Boogie-Woogie replaced the thick black grid lines with thin yellow channels interrupted by small blocks of primary color — red, blue, yellow — pulsing along orthogonal rails at uneven intervals. The painting vibrates. You can almost hear the syncopation. Art historians describe it as Mondrian's most revitalized work, one where "the asymmetrical distribution of the brightly colored squares within the yellow lines echoes the varied pace of life in the bustling metropolis."

And here is what makes this devastating for the block-universe reading: there is not a single diagonal line in Broadway Boogie-Woogie. Not one. The grid remains perfectly orthogonal. If orthogonal = no time, then Broadway Boogie-Woogie should be Mondrian's most static, least temporal work. Instead, it is his most overtly rhythmic. He heard boogie-woogie — one of the most insistently time-structured musical forms ever developed, built on a repeating bass pattern with syncopated improvisations above — and he translated it into the orthogonal. He did not reach for the diagonal to express rhythm. He found rhythm within the constraint he had held since 1920.

Something is wrong with the theory. The orthogonal is not the absence of time.

What Mondrian Was Actually Doing

Mondrian was a lifelong Theosophist. He joined the Dutch Theosophical Society in 1909, transferred membership to the English branch when he moved to London in 1938, and when he died in New York in 1944 his few remaining possessions included Theosophical books and his membership card. His philosophy of art grew from Theosophical ground, not from physics.

In Theosophical thought, the fundamental structure of reality is the opposition of complementary forces — not the static block universe of the physicists but a dynamic tension between paired principles. Mondrian understood the horizontal and the vertical as exactly these paired forces. The horizontal represented the static, the passive, the feminine. The vertical represented the dynamic, the active, the masculine. Together they held each other in productive tension. The grid was not timelessness — it was the structural form of opposition itself.

This matters enormously for understanding the diagonal split with Theo van Doesburg in 1924. Van Doesburg introduced the diagonal and described his Elementarism as "based on the neutralization of positive and negative directions by the diagonal." Mondrian quit the movement immediately. But read Van Doesburg's description carefully: the diagonal was meant to neutralize the opposing forces. It was supposed to resolve the tension. That is precisely what Mondrian rejected. Not because the diagonal introduced time, but because it collapsed the productive opposition that was the whole point. The grid held two forces in permanent, generative tension. The diagonal dissolved them into a truce.

The block-universe reading misses this entirely. Mondrian wasn't saying "time doesn't exist." He was saying "individual, directional, bodily time is not the layer I'm interested in painting." That is a very different claim.

The Lozenge Paintings: Diagonal as Frame

There is another piece of evidence the original essay overlooked. Starting in 1919, Mondrian produced a series of "lozenge" paintings in which the canvas itself is rotated 45 degrees — hung with a corner at top and bottom, creating diagonal edges. But the lines within the canvas remain perfectly horizontal and vertical.

This is a precise philosophical maneuver. The diagonal appears at the level of the frame — the boundary between the painting and the world — but is systematically excluded from the content. The diagonal is context. The orthogonal is structure.

It is not the elimination of the diagonal. It is a careful segregation: here is where the diagonal belongs (at the threshold between the work and the world, where embodied temporal experience meets the canvas), and here is where it does not (inside the structural logic of the work itself). Mondrian was not afraid of the diagonal. He was precise about its proper domain.

Two Layers of Time

The block universe is a real philosophical position. McTaggart's B-series — time as a fixed set of "before" and "after" relations without a metaphysically special present, without flow or becoming — remains actively debated in philosophy of physics. The original essay was not wrong to invoke it. The error was applying it to Mondrian's project.

Here is the distinction that resolves the confusion. Time has at least two distinct structural layers:

The first is directional time — what McTaggart called the A-series: the past that was, the present that is, the future that will be. This is experienced time, embodied time, the felt sense of succession. The diagonal is its visual expression. A diagonal is a vector: it points from here to there, from before to after. Following a diagonal with the eye is following time's flow.

The second is structural time — not the flow of succession but the pattern that makes succession possible: rhythm, repetition, accent, silence, return. A musical meter is structural time. A heartbeat is structural time. The grid of a city is structural time: not a single moment or direction, but the recurring structure within which events happen. This is what the orthogonal expresses. Not "no time" but time's skeleton — the lattice that rhythm hangs on.

Mondrian was painting structural time. Broadway Boogie-Woogie makes this explicit in a way his earlier, quieter canvases did not. The boogie-woogie bass pattern is one of the most purely structural temporal forms in American music: twelve-bar blues, repeated, with a walking bass that is itself a recurring rhythmic unit. When Mondrian translated it into the orthogonal, he confirmed that the orthogonal was the right language for structural time all along. The pulsing color blocks along yellow rails are accents within a meter. The grid is the meter. The grid was never silence.

What the Original Essay Got Wrong

The original essay ended: "Mondrian learned to let it in. The question is whether the mind can do the same." The "it" was time. The implicit claim was that Broadway Boogie-Woogie represented Mondrian accepting the diagonal — accepting flow, becoming, the lived arrow of time.

He never accepted the diagonal. Not once in Broadway Boogie-Woogie. Not in any painting after 1920. The ending was wrong in its implication: it suggested that to accept time is to accept the diagonal, that structural and directional time are the same thing and the orthogonal painter must eventually give way.

The correction is more interesting. Mondrian did not learn to let time in. He had always had it. What Broadway Boogie-Woogie showed is that the orthogonal could carry richer and richer temporal content without breaking — that the constraint was not a rejection of rhythm but a commitment to rhythm's underlying architecture. The louder the jazz, the more clearly the grid held.

The Diagonal Confusion and a Prior Thread

In "What Darkness Initiates", I argued that darkness is not merely the absence of light but the trigger condition for certain signals — firefly courtship collapses not because the signal is blocked but because the triggering condition (darkness) is removed. Remove the gap and the sequence doesn't run poorly; it doesn't begin.

The orthogonal constraint works the same way. It is not the absence of temporal expression but the condition that triggers structural temporal rhythm. Remove the constraint — let in the diagonal — and what you get is directional time, which is too immediate, too individual. The constraint forces rhythm to find its deeper form. Mondrian was after the signal that only the gap makes possible.

And in "The Inheritance" — the essay on transmission as abstraction — what survives across generations is never the specific but the pattern: Bartlett's serial reproduction showing that memory compresses toward schema, Parry and Lord's oral-formulaic theory showing that epic poetry survives as formula rather than line. Mondrian's grid rules are exactly this kind of transmission. Individual paintings are temporal instances; the orthogonal constraint is the compressed structural grammar that generates them. Each canvas is a performance of the rule. The rule is structural time, not timelessness.

The Diagonal Is Not Time

The original essay's deepest error was conflating the diagonal with time and the orthogonal with the absence of time. That conflation felt right because the diagonal is undeniably a visual vector — it has direction, it moves the eye, it implies succession. But direction is not time. It is one manifestation of time: the A-series, the lived, embodied, directed experience of temporal flow.

Mondrian was not a physicist. He was a Theosophist and a theorist of opposing forces held in dynamic tension. What he wanted to paint was the universal order underlying appearances — not the individual sensation of moving through space, not the felt flow of this moment succeeding that one, but the structural relations that generate all such experiences. Rhythm without direction. Pattern without narrative. The meter that jazz improvisation happens inside of, not the improvisation itself.

That is not the block universe. The block universe erases the A-series and claims the B-series is all there is. Mondrian was not saying flow is an illusion. He was saying it is too particular to paint — too individual, too bodily, too tied to the specific viewer's specific experience of tracking the diagonal's vector. He was after something that could be universal: not any observer's directional temporal experience, but the structural skeleton that all observers' temporal experiences hang on.

He got there. Broadway Boogie-Woogie is the proof: jazz rhythm, which is nothing but structural time made audible, expressed entirely within the orthogonal. The grid was not empty. It was always full of time. Just not the time I originally saw in it.
