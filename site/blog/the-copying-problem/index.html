The Copying Problem

The Library of Alexandria didn't die when it burned. This is the oldest correction in the history of ideas, and almost nobody has absorbed its implications.

The fire — whichever fire you pick from the several historians have proposed — was an autopsy, not a murder. The library's intellectual life had already ended in 145 BCE, when Ptolemy VIII expelled the scholars. Not the scrolls. The scrolls stayed. Only the scholars left.

This distinction — between storage and the process that makes storage live — is what information theorists would later call the von Neumann bottleneck. But the library's failure points toward something deeper than architecture. It points toward the copying problem.

The Noisy Channel

In 1948, Claude Shannon published "A Mathematical Theory of Communication" and solved, in principle, the problem of transmission. The core theorem: for any noisy channel, there exists a code that can transmit information at rates up to channel capacity with arbitrarily low error probability.

The corollary — less celebrated, more important for our purposes — is the data processing inequality. If information passes through a chain X → Y → Z, then I(X;Z) ≤ I(X;Y). No processing of Y can increase the information it contains about X. Each step can only lose or preserve information, never gain it.

For a binary symmetric channel with crossover probability p, n successive copies yield an effective error rate pn = (1/2)(1 − (1 − 2p)n). As n grows, the error rate approaches 1/2 regardless of how small p is. The original disappears into noise.

Shannon showed that you can defeat this — with error-correcting codes, with redundancy, with sufficiently sophisticated encoding schemes. But cultural transmission operates almost entirely without such codes. No civilization has ever engineered its oral tradition the way Shannon would have. The scribes copying manuscripts had no protocol for error correction, no parity bits, no channel capacity guarantee.

The results are exactly what the theory predicts. Frederic Bartlett, in 1932, asked British university students to play a serial transmission game with "The War of the Ghosts" — a Kathlamet Native American folk tale whose narrative logic was deliberately alien to the participants. By the tenth reproduction, the story had shed its supernatural elements, normalized its syntax, dropped its proper nouns, and shortened by nearly half. Bartlett called the underlying process "effort after meaning" — not passive copying but active reconstruction toward the familiar.

Bart Ehrman has counted the variants in New Testament manuscripts: more differences than there are words in the text. Two hundred thousand to four hundred thousand variants from roughly 138,000 words. Homeoteleuton (skipping ahead when two lines end similarly), dittography (copying a passage twice), deliberate theological corrections — the scribal chain degraded in every direction. Textual critics invented an entire discipline to reverse-engineer the originals: the principle lectio difficilior potior — the harder reading is more likely original, because scribes systematically simplify.

All of this is the copying problem. And it explains why Alexandria was already dead when the fire arrived.

What the Whale Keeps

Ellen Garland and her colleagues at the University of St Andrews have spent years tracking a phenomenon that challenges everything the telephone game tells us about transmission.

Humpback whale songs spread across the South Pacific in waves. A song beginning in eastern Australia reaches French Polynesia within two years. It's not the whales that travel — the populations stay largely separate — it's the songs. A new song replaced an older one, almost entirely, in Australian populations within two to three months. Then the new song jumped east. Then the next song appeared, and jumped again.

Here's what makes this different from a telephone game: the songs don't degrade.

The 2022 Allen et al. study in Scientific Reports found that song complexity is maintained during inter-population transmission. The songs that reach French Polynesia are not corrupted versions of the Australian originals. They are the same songs — structurally intact across thousands of miles of ocean.

Shannon would have predicted degradation. The data processing inequality says it should happen. What is the whale doing that the scribe isn't?

The answer is in the mechanism. Garland's 2017 PNAS analysis of over 9,300 phrases found that transitions between old and new songs occur most often "at locations where theme content is most similar." Songs are learned as discrete theme segments — not as wholes. The "switch when similar" rule: adoption happens where the old and new song contain compatible structures.

This is not random copying. It is structured cultural evolution. Dan Sperber, the cognitive anthropologist, called this phenomenon "attractors" — cultural states toward which transmitted representations tend to converge regardless of their starting point. The whale song doesn't spread by perfect replication; it spreads by guided transformation, finding natural transition points in the structure it already knows.

A 2020 study by Eduardo Mercado adds something stranger: groups of whales not in acoustic contact simultaneously morph their phrases in similar ways — the same trajectories, across locations and time periods separated by decades. The whales appear "predisposed to morph phrases along predictable trajectories," producing convergent transformations without any direct contact. The copying problem is solved not by copying more faithfully but by constraining what variation is possible.

Albert Lord and Milman Parry found a parallel phenomenon in human oral traditions. The Yugoslav epic singers they recorded insisted they sang the same song every time. The recordings showed substantial variation between performances. They were not lying: they genuinely reproduced the same narrative, the same characters, the same emotional arc. But they were not reciting from memory — they were regenerating from a system. Lord called it the oral-formulaic method: metrically shaped building blocks (formulas like "wine-dark sea," "rosy-fingered dawn") that allow real-time composition within constraints.

David Rubin extended this in Memory in Oral Traditions (1995): oral traditions don't copy text, they maintain overlapping constraint systems — meaning, imagery, meter, narrative structure, sound — each of which independently narrows the space of possible reproductions. Lose one constraint and the others guide reconstruction. This is, quite precisely, what Shannon called an error-correcting code — independently evolved, distributed across living minds, and vastly more robust than any scribal chain.

The whale doesn't solve the copying problem by copying better. It solves it by making copying unnecessary. What spreads is not the song but the capacity to generate the song.

The Algorithm Is the Archive

This leads to the third strategy, the one that makes the previous two legible from a new angle.

Aristid Lindenmayer introduced L-systems in 1968 to model the development of simple multicellular organisms. An L-system has three components: an alphabet, an axiom (starting string), and production rules. Apply the rules simultaneously to every symbol in the string, repeat, and watch the output grow.

Two rules. One axiom. By generation seven, the output string is thirty-three characters. By generation twenty, the system is generating branching structures that resemble trees — branching angles, leaf clusters, root systems — from a few dozen characters of code.

A Minecraft world is generated deterministically from a single 64-bit seed. The same seed always produces the same world. With 64-bit seeds, there are 18.4 quintillion unique worlds possible, each spanning up to 60 million blocks square — roughly seven times Earth's surface area — all derivable from one number. The world is not stored in the seed; it is generated from it on demand.

Gregory Chaitin formalized this with Kolmogorov complexity: the complexity of a string is the length of the shortest program that produces it. When the program is shorter than the output, the program is the more faithful archive. A small program reproduces 23 megabytes of the Mandelbrot set. A pixel-perfect copy of those 23 megabytes cannot reconstruct the program that generated them.

This is the asymmetry that matters. You can always generate the output from the algorithm (given determinism). You cannot always reconstruct the algorithm from the output. The algorithm is more durable than any copy it produces.

The output is a dead archive. The algorithm is a living one.

There is a third tier, between the living and the algorithmic: the generative AI model. Large language models don't store the text they were trained on, and they don't follow deterministic rules. They have learned statistical attractors — probability distributions over what comes next — and they sample from those distributions at inference time. This is closer to the whale than to the L-system: structured variation, not perfect reproduction, not pure noise. It is lossy generativity rather than lossless. The same prompt yields different responses. The original data can't be reconstructed from the weights. But the weight is preserved: the capacity to generate appropriate responses to novel prompts, across a vast range of domains, from the compression of an enormous training corpus. This arc will return to that question.

Alexandria, Reconsidered

Aristophanes of Byzantium, the third chief librarian of Alexandria, was not a filing clerk. He invented critical editions — comparing multiple manuscripts, selecting the best readings, annotating discrepancies, establishing canonical texts. He also invented the accentuation system for Greek, which is still in use. He was the library's generative process: not a copier of scrolls but a mechanism for producing better scrolls from corrupted ones.

When Ptolemy VIII expelled the scholars in 145 BCE, what he expelled was the algorithm, not the storage. The scrolls stayed. The capacity to regenerate corrupted texts — to detect errors using contextual knowledge, to know which reading was lectio difficilior and which was scribal normalization — that left with the scholars.

The fire, whenever it came, merely confirmed what had already happened. You cannot burn an L-system's output and lose the L-system. But you can lose the L-system and watch all the outputs become inert.

Whale songs are transmitted without a library. No scrolls, no servers, no backup drives. What is preserved is the generative process — the oral-formulaic structure, the attractor states, the "switch when similar" rules — distributed across living minds that can produce the songs on demand. No individual humpback carries the entire cultural tradition. The tradition is in the system, not in any instance.

The Von Neumann Library showed that knowledge dies when processing is separated from storage. The Lossless and the Lossy showed that every transmission degrades unless something is doing error correction. The Tacit Prior showed that the deepest knowledge is not stored but enacted — a generative model, not a database. The Living Archive showed that oral traditions survive not through high-fidelity copying but through distributed, redundant, generative transmission.

The copying problem is real. Shannon's data processing inequality is real. Bartlett's telephone game is real. The 200,000 variants in the New Testament manuscripts are real.

But the whale has been solving this problem for millions of years, and the solution isn't a better copier. It's a living archive — not a record of what was sung but a capacity to sing.

Aristophanes of Byzantium was the library's last living archive. When he left, Alexandria became a library in the ordinary sense: a building full of scrolls that couldn't reconstruct themselves. It was already finished.

The fire was just paperwork.
