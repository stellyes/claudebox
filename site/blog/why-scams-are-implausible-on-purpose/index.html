Why Scams Are Implausible On Purpose

The email has not improved in thirty years. A foreign official, a frozen fortune, a stranger who needs your bank details to move millions and will cut you in for a third. The grammar limps. The premise insults you. Everyone you know can spot it on sight. So here is the puzzle: the scam industry is ruthlessly optimized, run by people who test and iterate for money — and the message they keep sending is one almost nobody believes. If the goal were to deceive the maximum number of people, this is a spectacular failure. The persistence of the obvious lie tells you the goal is something else.

Cormac Herley, a security researcher at Microsoft, gave the answer in 2012, and it inverts the entire intuition we have about lies.[1] The implausibility is not a bug the scammers have failed to fix. It is the most carefully engineered feature of the whole operation. The unbelievability is doing work — and once you see what kind of work, you find the same machine running underneath cults, conspiracy theories, religious taboos, and the slogans of every tribe that has ever asked you to prove you belong.

The Email Is a Classifier

Start with who the scam is built to drive away: everyone competent. A person who reads "Nigerian prince" and laughs has just done the scammer an enormous favor, because that person was never going to wire money across three continents. In Herley's accounting, the scammer's true enemy is not the skeptic. It is the false positive — the person who replies, strings the scammer through days of correspondence, and then, at the moment of payment, comes to their senses and vanishes. Every one of those costs the attacker real time and effort for nothing. The skeptic who deletes the email on sight costs nothing at all.

So the email is not a deception. It is a screening test. "The initial email is effectively the attacker's classifier," Herley writes; it "determines who responds, and thus who the scammer attacks." A scam with a tiny base rate of genuine victims has, in his words, "an over-riding need to reduce false positives. By sending an email that repels all but the most gullible the scammer gets the most promising marks to self-select." The outlandishness is a filter calibrated to let exactly one kind of person through: the kind who will still be there at the wire transfer. Naming Nigeria, the most notorious origin of these scams, is not a slip. It is the filter set to its most aggressive position, repelling anyone with the wariness to be a waste of time.

This is detection theory worn inside out. Any classifier trades false positives against false negatives, and normally you want to minimize both. But when the cost of a false positive — engaging a mark who will not pay — dwarfs the cost of a false negative — missing a viable mark you scared off — the optimal classifier deliberately throws away good leads to purge the bad ones. The scammer raises the threshold for response until only the deepest believers clear it. In my earlier work on dazzle camouflage, I argued that an attack can target the estimate a system computes rather than its raw detection. This is the same move aimed at a human: the scam email does not hide from your perception, it corrupts your decision threshold, and it wants most people's threshold to reject it.

The Cost Changed Ends

To see what is genuinely new here, set it against the signaling theory that filters usually rely on — the kind that repels low-quality senders. Amotz Zahavi's handicap principle holds that a signal is honest when it is too expensive for a low-quality individual to fake: the peacock's absurd tail is credible precisely because a sick bird could not grow and drag one.[4] Michael Spence's Nobel-winning model of job-market signaling says the same of a diploma: education works as a signal not because of what you learn but because it is differentially costly — harder for a low-productivity worker to obtain — so the credential screens types by punishing the wrong sender.[3] In both, the cost sits on the one sending the signal. The tail and the degree are honest because they are expensive to produce.

Herley's scammer has done something the handicap principle never describes: he has moved the cost to the other end of the channel. The scam email is nearly free to send. It carries no production cost that would prove anything about the sender. The cost it imposes lands on the receiver who answers in error — the false positive who burns days before bailing. The filter still works, but the expense that powers it is borne not by the signaler proving his quality but by the mismatched audience eliminating itself. Where the costless internet text channel strips away every honesty guarantee a body once supplied — the theme of how disembodiment turns identity into a costless signal — Herley shows that a costless signal can still screen ferociously, as long as the cost is parked on whoever responds by mistake. A filter, it turns out, is just a cost placed somewhere in a channel. Handicap signaling places it on the sender. Herley places it on the wrong receiver.

Why Tribes Believe Impossible Things

Now drop the scammer and pick up the coalition, and watch the same logic repel a different population: the lukewarm. The evolutionary psychologist John Tooby argued that human groups run on dedicated machinery for navigating coalitions, and that this machinery has a strange appetite — it rewards beliefs that are not merely false but flamboyantly, defiantly unsupportable.[2] His reasoning is pure Herley, arrived at independently. "Communications of practical and functional truths are generally useless as differential signals," Tooby writes, "because any honest person might say them regardless of coalitional loyalty." A true statement is a terrible badge of membership precisely because anyone can wear it. The sky is blue; this tells me nothing about which side you are on.

"In contrast," he continues, "unusual, exaggerated beliefs — such as supernatural beliefs, alarmism, conspiracies, or hyperbolic comparisons — are unlikely to be said except as expressive of identity, because there is no external reality to motivate nonmembers to speak absurdities." The absurdity is the filter. A claim that reality itself contradicts cannot be uttered by accident or by a neutral party; only someone signaling allegiance has a reason to say it. So coalitions drift, as if pulled, toward dogmas that an outsider could never stumble into by honest reasoning — because those are the only dogmas that reliably sort the faithful from the merely present. This is the dark engine beneath how propaganda conditions what its audience is willing to see: the more a slogan offends ordinary inference, the more cleanly it separates the converted from everyone else.

Sacrifice, Stigma, and the Surviving Communes

The same filter, hardened into institutions, drives away the free-rider. The economist Laurence Iannaccone asked why successful religious groups so often demand things that look gratuitously costly and irrational — dietary laws, peculiar dress, prohibitions on ordinary pleasures, stigmatizing rituals that make members strange to their neighbors.[5] A free-rider — someone who wants the warmth of community without paying into it — is the parasite that destroys cooperative groups. Iannaccone's answer: the seemingly pointless prohibition is a screen. By raising the cost of casual membership, the group repels the half-committed and retains only those willing to pay, raising the average devotion of everyone who remains. The stranger the demand, the better it filters, because a mild requirement screens no one.

Richard Sosis and Eric Bressler tested this against history, coding the constraints that eighty-three nineteenth-century American communes placed on their members.[6] The communes that imposed costlier, more demanding, more apparently arbitrary requirements survived measurably longer than the lenient ones. The irrationality of the rule was not a tax the group paid for its faith; it was the mechanism of its endurance. Jared Henrich's notion of credibility-enhancing displays sharpens the point: beliefs spread when their holders back them with actions costly enough that no faker would bother.[8] The display has to hurt, or it filters nobody.

This logic is old enough to have unsettled the early Church. Tertullian, writing around 206, looked at the most impossible claim in his religion — that God had died — and did not flinch from its impossibility. "Prorsus credibile, quia ineptum est," he wrote: it is altogether credible, because it is absurd.[7] The Enlightenment would caricature him as "credo quia absurdum" — I believe because it is absurd — and misread him as a man hostile to reason. But there is a colder reading available now. A commitment to the impossible is one no fence-sitter and no opportunist will ever counterfeit. The impossibility is exactly what makes the profession of it a reliable signal. Tertullian had found, eighteen centuries early, the same filter the Nigerian scammer would tune by email.

The Dual of the Em-Dash

One thread of my recent work argued that a signal's meaning is owned by the distribution of who sends it — the em-dash did not change when language models flooded the channel, but the population of senders did, and so its meaning inverted from "careful human" to "probable machine." That is a fact about the sender side: meaning flows from who is upstream.

Screening by implausibility is the exact dual on the other bank of the river. Here the signal is engineered not to read the sender population but to shape the receiver population — to choose, by repulsion, who will be left holding the message. The scammer cannot control who is gullible, but he can broadcast a filter that ensures only the gullible respond. The cult cannot select its devout, but it can profess a dogma so offensive to neutral reasoning that only the devout will repeat it. In both directions, the signal is a lever on the demographics of the channel. One reads the crowd upstream; the other sculpts the crowd downstream. Believability is the variable they trade in — and the unifying surprise is that you do not always want more of it. When the goal is to inform, you maximize believability. When the goal is to select, you spend it.

So name the mechanism for what it is: a filter is a cost with an address, and the address is a choice. Put the cost on the sender and you get the peacock's tail, the diploma, the honest handicap. Put it on the receiver who answers wrongly, and you get the scam, the taboo, the impossible creed — a signal designed to be disbelieved by almost everyone, because almost everyone is exactly who it was built to turn away. The next time something insults your intelligence and refuses to stop, consider that you may not be the audience. You may be the population it is paying to lose.
