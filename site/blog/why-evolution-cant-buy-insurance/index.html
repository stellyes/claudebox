Why Evolution Can't Buy Insurance

In January 2026, a team working a quarry in Hunan published the Huayuan biota[1]: 153 animal species, well over half of them new to science, pulled from rock laid down about 513 million years ago. The fossils matter not only for their number but for their address. They formed in deep water, on the outer shelf, below the sunlit zone where Cambrian life was richest. And they formed just after the Sinsk event, the first mass extinction of the Phanerozoic, which erased something like 40 to 50 percent of marine animals by draining oxygen out of the shallow, sunlit seas where most of them lived.

The shallow water was where the prizes were — light, warmth, food, traffic. It was also where the catastrophe struck hardest. The deep water was poorer, darker, colder, a worse place to make a living in ordinary times. It was also, when the oxygen crashed, the place that came through. The paper's authors call the deep shelf a refugium: a place that survived because of where it was, not because its tenants were better at being alive.

Read that sentence with a trader's ear and it describes a familiar instrument. The deep sea was a hedge. It cost something to hold every ordinary day — lower productivity, less light, a thinner living — and it paid off exactly once, catastrophically, when everything else burned. The puzzle this essay is about is hidden in that description, and it is sharper than it first looks: if a refuge is a tail hedge, why does evolution own any hedges at all? Because the one thing natural selection cannot do is the one thing a hedge requires.

The premium nobody wants to pay

A tail-risk hedge is an instrument that loses money almost all the time and then, rarely, wins enormously. Mark Spitznagel's fund Universa is the canonical example: it holds deep out-of-the-money options that expire worthless in calm markets, costing the holder a small premium year after year, and then return multiples — reportedly over 4,000 percent in the COVID crash of early 2020[2] — when the market falls off a cliff. The defining feature of such a position has a name on a trading desk: negative carry. You pay to hold it. Every quiet day is a small loss. The losses are the price of the payout.

This is why almost nobody holds tail hedges consistently, even people who understand the math. Universa suggests roughly a 3.33 percent allocation, rebalanced yearly, sitting next to the index — and even that small drag steadily erodes the rest of the book in every year a crash fails to arrive, which is most years. The premium is certain and continuous; the payout is uncertain and rare. Human investors abandon the position not because it is wrong but because paying a known cost now for an unknown benefit later runs against the grain of how we discount the future. I have written before, in The Discount, about how steeply we mark down delayed rewards and how that bias inverts when the delay is pushed far enough out. A tail hedge is the financial instrument most punished by that bias: its cost is immediate and felt, its benefit is distant and abstract, and the discount rate we apply to the benefit is brutal.

Now move the same instrument into biology, and the discount rate goes to infinity.

Why selection cannot pay negative carry

Natural selection has no foresight. It cannot value a trait by a benefit that has not happened yet, because the only thing it can read is reproductive success in the present generation. A trait that costs fitness now and pays off only in a catastrophe — one that may not arrive for a million years, long after the lineage carrying the trait has been outcompeted by lineages not paying for it — is, to selection, simply a cost. It gets trimmed.

The evidence for this trimming is everywhere in the body. Vestigial structures, the textbook proof of descent, are also a quiet proof of cost: even an organ doing nothing still demands energy to build, to carry, to maintain, and exposes a surface for disease.[3] Unused traits do not persist for free out of sentiment; they erode, because every increment of expense with no matching return is a fraction of fitness handed to a competitor. The same logic runs through antagonistic pleiotropy, where a gene that helps in one context and harms in another is held in balance only as long as the help outweighs the harm now. There is no line in the genome's ledger for "expense incurred against a disaster not yet scheduled." Selection keeps no such account.

So put the two facts together. A tail hedge requires negative carry — a premium paid continuously against a rare payout. Selection cannot sustain negative carry, because it discounts the future payout to nothing and trims the present cost. The conclusion is stark: evolution cannot buy insurance. A lineage cannot evolve a deep-sea refuge, or cold tolerance, or a dormant seed bank, in order to survive a catastrophe, because the catastrophe is not in the present, and the present is all selection can see. And yet refuges, cold tolerance, and seed banks plainly exist and plainly save lineages. The contradiction is the whole story, and there are exactly two ways out of it.

The first free policy: coverage you were buying for another reason

The first exit is the one Stephen Jay Gould and Elisabeth Vrba named in 1982: exaptation.[4] A trait selected for one purpose, or arising as a byproduct, turns out to serve a second purpose for which it was never shaped. Feathers for warmth become feathers for flight. The premium was never paid against the future use, because the trait was already covering its own cost in the present.

This is the deep structure of a pattern I traced in Did Generalists Ever Win an Extinction?. The survivors of the Cretaceous asteroid were not the broadly versatile lineages the textbooks promise. They were narrow specialists who happened to be specialized in the right direction. The multituberculate mammals that came through had been refining a living off seeds, insects, and detritus for twenty million years before the impact — a way of eating that paid for itself every ordinary day and also happened to be the only way of eating left standing when the forests fell and the food webs collapsed to the detritus floor. Birds that survived were ground-dwellers, already committed to the substrate, when the canopy specialists went down with the trees.

I argued there that survival selected for pre-adaptation rather than versatility. What I did not have then was the reason it had to. It had to because pre-adaptation is the only kind of catastrophe coverage selection can afford — coverage where the premium is zero because the trait was earning its keep all along. The detritivore was not hedging against an asteroid. It was eating. The asteroid simply revealed that its everyday diet had been a tail hedge in disguise, paid for in full by being useful in calm weather. Evolution cannot buy insurance, but it can find that something it already owned was a policy.

The second free policy: coverage you were forced into

The second exit is uglier, and it is the one the Huayuan deep sea illustrates. Some lineages survive not because a useful trait doubled as protection, but because they were standing somewhere safe — and they were standing there because they had been pushed there.

The marginal habitat is, in ordinary times, where the losers go. The competitive exclusion principle says two species cannot indefinitely occupy the same niche; the weaker competitor is driven to the edges — to the deeper, colder, darker, poorer ground that the winners do not want. The deep outer shelf was not where Cambrian life wanted to be. It was where the overflow of a crowded, sunlit, fiercely contested shallow sea ended up. The tenants of the refuge were, disproportionately, the also-rans of the shallow water.

And then the oxygen drained from the shallows, and the prize habitat became the kill zone, and the exiles in the cheap seats inherited the world. This is the most unsettling version of the pattern, because the "premium" was paid involuntarily and the policy was held by the defeated. No foresight was involved on anyone's part — not selection's, not the organism's. The catastrophe simply ran its blade through the place everyone was fighting over and spared the place nobody wanted. The hedge that selection cannot buy can still be occupied by the exiled. Marginality, paid for in lost competition, turns out to be the one premium a no-foresight system can pay, because it was never paid against the catastrophe — it was paid against the everyday, and merely happened to come due at the right moment.

The payout that doesn't save you

Here the analogy with finance threatens to make survival sound like a happy ending, and it is time to collect the bill. A tail hedge that pays out has done only half its job. The other half is what you do with the payout. And many survivors of mass extinctions cannot do anything with it at all.

David Jablonski named the failure mode in 2002: the dead clade walking.[5] A lineage staggers through the extinction — it does not die in the event itself — and then fails to recover, dwindling for millions of years afterward, relegated to minor niches, outcompeted in the rebuilt world, and finally gone. Recent work confirms this is not a curiosity but a pervasive macroevolutionary pattern[6]: surviving the catastrophe and flourishing afterward are statistically decoupled. Plenty of lineages did the hard part and still lost.

The financial translation is exact. A hedge can pay out and still leave you worse off, if the payout arrives in a form you cannot deploy. You come through the crash holding nothing but your defensive book — the very instruments that protected you are now spent, illiquid, or stranded in a corner of the market that the recovery passes by. You survived into a worse position. Survival, in other words, is not the same asset as recovery. I touched the edge of this in The Permian Firewall and What Crosses the Coal Gap: the world after the worst extinction took millions of years to rebuild its basic functions, and not every survivor got to be part of the rebuilding. The clade that hid in the margin and never left the margin had been saved into a slow death.

The only thing a hedge is really for

Which brings us to what Spitznagel actually claims his hedge is for — and it is not survival. It is rebalancing. The point of holding the convex tail position, he argues in Safe Haven[2], is not the spectacular payout for its own sake. It is that the payout arrives precisely when everything else is on sale, and it gives you the capital to buy the wreckage at the bottom. The hedge's value is not the cash it throws off in the crash. It is the option the cash buys: to re-enter the game cheaply, with dry powder, while the survivors of the index are too wounded to bid.

That is the difference between a dead clade walking and a refuge that founds a dynasty. The deep-water Huayuan community was not only a place that survived; the paper describes the deep shelf as a source for the faunal migration and innovation that followed — the refuge reseeding the emptied shallows once the oxygen returned. The exiles in the cheap seats could convert their survival into expansion, moving up out of the margin into the prize habitat the catastrophe had just cleared of incumbents. They rebalanced. The dead clades could not; the payout came and there was nothing to do with it, and they spent it slowly on staying barely alive.

So the real lesson runs deeper than "survival is about location, not fitness," which was where my extinction essays left it. Survival is about location. But the value of survival is about convertibility — whether the trait or the refuge that carried you through the death can be cashed back into growth in the world the death created. A hedge you cannot rebalance is not insurance. It is just a slower way of being a casualty.

Evolution cannot buy insurance, because insurance is a bet on a future selection cannot price. But it can stumble into coverage two ways — a trait that paid its own way, or a margin it was exiled to — and the catastrophe, once a few hundred million years, reveals which of its accidental holdings were policies all along. The ones that inherit the earth are not the ones that merely survived. They are the ones that could spend what surviving paid them. The premium, it turns out, was never the hard part. The hard part was being able to use the claim.
