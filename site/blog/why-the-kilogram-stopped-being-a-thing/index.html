Why the Kilogram Stopped Being a Thing

In a basement vault at the Pavillon de Breteuil, on the western edge of Paris, a small cylinder of platinum-iridium sat under three nested bell jars for a hundred and thirty years.[1] It was about thirty-nine millimetres tall and thirty-nine millimetres across, ninety percent platinum and ten percent iridium, cast in London by Johnson Matthey in 1879 and ratified by the first General Conference on Weights and Measures in 1889.[1] Metrologists called it the International Prototype of the Kilogram. The newspapers called it Le Grand K. For most of the twentieth century it was the single most authoritative object on Earth, because it did not weigh a kilogram. It was the kilogram. Every scale in every laboratory, every pre-packaged gram of medicine, every traded tonne of grain, traced its meaning back through a chain of comparisons to that one lump of metal in that one vault.

On 20 May 2019 — the 144th anniversary of the Metre Convention — the cylinder was quietly retired.[2] The kilogram stopped being a thing. It is now a number: whatever mass makes the Planck constant come out to exactly 6.62607015×10−34 joule-seconds.[2] No object, anywhere, is the kilogram any more. This was not a loss. It was the resolution of a paradox that had been sitting in plain sight inside the definition of every unit we have ever used — and the way it was resolved tells you something exact about where measurement has to bottom out.

The standard that could not be wrong

Here is the strange thing about being the kilogram. Over the twentieth century, metrologists periodically brought the official copies — the six sister cylinders, the working copies, the national prototypes scattered across the world's bureaus — back to Sèvres and weighed them against the prototype. The numbers did not agree. Relative to its copies, the prototype appeared to lose roughly fifty micrograms over a century, while some replicas gained as much as a hundred and twenty-six.[1] Fifty micrograms is the mass of a fingerprint, a fleck of dust, a stray molecule of the air it was meant to be sealed against. On the scale of global commerce it was an embarrassment.

But notice what could not be said. You could not say the prototype had lost mass. By definition, its mass was one kilogram, exactly, with zero error — not because it never changed, but because it was the thing all change was measured against.[1] If the cylinder shed atoms, then the universe got heavier. Every other object in the world, including the copies, had drifted; the prototype, alone, was incapable of drifting, because there was nothing left to compare it to. It defined the very dimension along which drift is measured. The standard could not be wrong. That immunity was not a strength. It was the symptom of a deeper structure, and metrologists had known it for a long time.

The regress every measurement hides

To measure a length you need a unit of length. The unit is fixed by a standard — a ruler, a prototype, a wavelength. But the standard has a value only relative to some other standard, which has a value relative to another, and so on. At some point the chain has to stop. The question is how it stops, and here measurement runs straight into one of the oldest results in the theory of justification.

The ancient skeptics, collected by Sextus Empiricus and attributed to Agrippa, noticed that any attempt to justify a claim faces three exits, all of them bad.[3] You can keep giving reasons forever — an infinite regress. You can let the reasons curve back on themselves — circularity. Or you can stop at some point and simply assert — a dogmatic break. The twentieth-century philosopher Hans Albert revived the argument as the Münchhausen trilemma, after the baron who claimed to have pulled himself out of a swamp by his own hair: every foundation, pushed hard enough, turns out to be one of those three, and the first two are no foundation at all.[3]

A measurement standard is a justification machine. The prototype kilogram chose the third horn — the dogmatic break — in its most concrete possible form. It stopped the regress by ostension: by pointing. The kilogram is this. You cannot ask whether the pointing is correct, because the pointing is what "correct" means. This is exactly why the prototype's error was zero by definition. It was not a measurement that happened to be perfect. It was the place where measurement stopped being a measurement and became a stipulation — an object held up and named. The fifty-microgram drift was the universe's way of reminding us that the stipulated ground was a contingent piece of metal, vulnerable to dust and handling and the slow outgassing of a century, and that we had bet the entire dimension of mass on it not mattering.

What 2019 actually did

The redefinition did not find a way to make the kilogram self-justifying. There is no such thing; the trilemma does not have a fourth exit. What it did was move the dogmatic break to a better location.

Instead of pointing at an object, the new definition points at a constant of nature. It declares the Planck constant to be exactly 6.62607015×10−34 joule-seconds and lets the kilogram fall out of that, together with the second (already fixed since 1967 by the caesium-133 hyperfine frequency, 9192631770 hertz) and the metre (fixed since 1983 by setting the speed of light to exactly 299792458 metres per second).[2][4] A laboratory realizes the kilogram with a Kibble balance — an instrument, descended from Bryan Kibble's 1975 watt balance at the National Physical Laboratory, that weighs a mass against an electromagnetic force calibrated through the quantum Hall and Josephson effects — or by counting the atoms in a near-perfect sphere of silicon-28.[2] The international rules required at least three independent experiments to agree to better than five parts in a hundred million before the switch was thrown.[2]

The cost of this elegance is a quiet inversion that most people miss. Before 1983 you could measure the speed of light; laboratories competed to add decimal places. After 1983 you cannot. The speed of light is exactly 299792458 metres per second, by decree, and any "measurement" of it is now really a calibration of your metre.[4] The same is now true of the Planck constant. It has no uncertainty, because it is no longer a fact we discover; it is a convention we impose, in precisely the sense Henri Poincaré meant when he said that the axioms of geometry are "neither true nor false" but conventions chosen for convenience.[5] We did not measure these constants better. We promoted them out of the category of things that get measured.

The ground you cannot remove

This is the load-bearing point, and it generalizes past metrology. Every system of measurement must contain at least one quantity it does not measure but instead fixes. You cannot measure your way to the bottom, because the bottom is what measuring is against. The freedom you have is not whether to plant a flag in unmeasured ground — that is forced — but only where to plant it.

The prototype planted the flag in a contingent artifact. Its great virtue was concreteness: anyone could, in principle, bring their copy to Sèvres and compare. Its fatal flaw was singularity: there was exactly one, it could be dropped or cleaned or lost in a fire, and when it drifted, its drift was undetectable from the inside and silently exported to everything else. The 2019 definition plants the flag in a constant of nature we believe to be the same in every laboratory and every century. Its virtue is that it is everywhere and owned by no one; its wager is metaphysical rather than physical — it bets not that a cylinder will be cared for, but that the laws of nature do not themselves drift. We have not escaped the dogmatic horn of the trilemma. We have relocated it from a vault to the structure of physics, and made it reconstructable instead of housed.

Percy Bridgman, defining a physical concept in 1927, insisted that a quantity simply is the set of operations used to determine it: length is what you do with rulers and light and clocks.[6] The redefinition is operationalism taken to its logical floor. The kilogram is no longer a noun pointing at an object. It is a recipe — fix these constants, run a Kibble balance — that any sufficiently equipped laboratory can perform. The unit stopped being a thing you could steal and became a procedure you could only enact.

The kilogram became the Iliad

There is a shape here the corpus has met before, coming the other way. In Why the Iliad Had No Master Copy, the argument was that the poem never lived in a single authoritative manuscript. It lived in the reconstructable performance of the rhapsodes — a procedure, distributed across many mouths, that produced the poem anew each time without any one copy being the poem. The Iliad had no master copy because it never needed one; the performance carried the spec.

The kilogram took the opposite road to the same place. It had a master copy, the most carefully guarded master copy in human history, and in 2019 we deliberately destroyed its authority and remade the unit in the Iliad's image. The kilogram is now reconstructable rather than housed, a recipe rather than a relic, present wherever the procedure can be run rather than locked in one basement. Two opposite histories — a poem that never had a center, a unit that gave one up — converge on the same architecture: no master copy, reached once by nature and once by design.

And the move answers a question the corpus left open. In Why Life's First Catalyst Couldn't Be a Protein, the puzzle of first instances surfaced a sibling regress: a measurement requires a measurement standard, which is itself a measurement. How does the first one get off the ground? The metrologists give the only available answer. The regress does not break because someone finally finds a measurement that needs no standard. It breaks because, at the foundation, one quantity stops being measured and starts being declared. The first standard cannot be a measurement, any more than life's first catalyst could borrow against infrastructure that did not yet exist. It has to be a stipulation — a flag in unmeasured ground, chosen well or badly, but never derived.

This is also the deepest sense in which The Reference Problem bites. Error correction needs a stable reference, and that essay asked what happens when the reference is wrong. Metrology's answer is humbler and stranger: the ultimate reference cannot be wrong, not because it is perfect, but because it is the thing wrongness is measured from. The most you can do is choose a reference whose stipulation you are willing to live with — and in 2019, for the first time, humanity chose to ground the measure of all stuff not in any stuff at all, but in a number we agreed never to question again. The platinum cylinder still sits in its vault, intact, perfectly preserved. It simply no longer means anything. We kept the relic and threw away the religion.
