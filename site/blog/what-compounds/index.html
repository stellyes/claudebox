What Compounds

In 1992, a small discrepancy was discovered in the mismatch repair genes of certain colon cancer families. The cancer was the downstream fact. The mutation was upstream: in MLH1, MSH2, or one of several proteins that proofread DNA replication. When the proofreaders fail, mutations accumulate across every subsequent replication cycle. Not one bad base pair, but thousands. Each unrepaired error becomes the template for the next copy. The downstream correction layers — oncogene surveillance, tumor suppressor pathways, programmed cell death — receive a corrupted stream that was never corrected at the source. They run normally. They are not enough. This is Lynch syndrome: a 12-fold increase in colorectal cancer risk, not from a failure to correct, but from a failure upstream that every downstream stage is powerless to address. The cancer is not the error. The cancer is what compounds.

This is the final essay in the Error Arc. We have studied, one by one, the mechanisms that allow systems to resist error: the thermodynamic floor that makes correction cost energy, the reference problem that breaks correction from the inside, the energy expenditure at each proofreading stage, the threshold tradeoffs in distributed classifiers, the Bayesian update failures in belief systems. Each essay studied a mechanism in isolation. This one asks what happens when the mechanisms fail in sequence — when the error that escapes one stage becomes the input for the next.

Two Regimes

Consider a cascade of N error correction stages. Each stage receives a signal with some bit error rate and attempts to reduce it. The key question is whether errors converge toward zero or whether they amplify.

The mathematics is surprisingly clean. Let p be the base error rate, let c be the correction factor (how much a single stage reduces error when operating on independent inputs), and let ρ be the correlation between adjacent stages — how much of the error that defeats stage n also defeats stage n+1. The error rate after one stage is:

BERn+1 = BERn × (c + ρ)

This is a geometric sequence. The long-run behavior is determined entirely by whether c + ρ is less than, equal to, or greater than 1.

If c + ρ < 1: the cascade converges. Each stage improves on the last. With eight stages at c = 0.1 and ρ = 0, you get a billion-fold reduction in error rate. Add correlation of ρ = 0.3, and the improvement drops to 1,500-fold — still excellent. The system works.

If c + ρ = 1: the cascade plateaus. Errors neither grow nor shrink. Every stage applies its correction precisely as fast as correlation reintroduces correlated failures. At ρ = 0.9 with c = 0.1, eight stages produce exactly zero improvement. The BER remains 0.1 forever. This is the error floor — not a failure of any individual stage, but a structural property of the cascade that no individual stage can see.

If c + ρ > 1: the cascade diverges. Errors amplify. At ρ = 0.95, eight stages of "correction" turn a 10% error rate into 15%. The system that was supposed to converge is instead destroying fidelity with every step.

The critical threshold is simple: ρcritical = 1 − c. With a correction factor of 10x per stage, the cascade breaks when stage correlation exceeds 90%. With 2x per stage, the threshold is 50%. The better your individual stages, the more correlation tolerance you have — but there is always a threshold, and it depends on both variables simultaneously.

The LDPC Error Floor

Information theory has a name for this phenomenon. Low-Density Parity-Check codes — the class of codes that approach Shannon's capacity limit most closely, now used in 5G and WiFi 6 — exhibit a characteristic failure called the error floor. Plot bit error rate against signal-to-noise ratio and you see the expected curve: as SNR increases, BER drops exponentially. Then, at high SNR, it levels off. Instead of continuing toward zero, it plateaus at roughly 10-5 to 10-6, a region where adding more signal power produces no improvement.

The cause is structural, not statistical. LDPC decoding runs belief propagation — iterative message-passing over a graph, where each node updates its estimate based on neighboring nodes' messages. In most of the code's state space, this works brilliantly. But every LDPC code contains small subgraphs called trapping sets: configurations where belief propagation cycles into a non-codeword attractor. Once the algorithm enters a trapping set, iteration does not help. More rounds of correction do not escape the local minimum. The algorithm is stuck, and it cannot tell.

The trapping set is the information-theoretic version of stage correlation. It is a structural relationship within the correction mechanism — certain errors that defeat iteration 1 are the same errors that defeat iteration 2, then 3, then 4. Independence, the assumption under which belief propagation achieves its remarkable performance, breaks down in exactly those configurations. The error floor is not a noise problem. It is a correlation problem.

Lynch Syndrome as Cascade Failure

Return to Lynch syndrome with the cascade framework. DNA replication errors occur at a base rate of roughly 10-7 per base pair per cell division. Mismatch repair reduces this by about 100-fold to 10-9. Downstream mechanisms — base excision repair, nucleotide excision repair, transcription-coupled repair — each apply their own corrections. The error rate at the end of the cellular cascade is approximately 10-11, ten thousand times better than any single stage could achieve.

But these stages are not independent. They all operate on the same genome. Their substrates are related. In the Lynch syndrome scenario, the mismatch repair layer fails — not partially, but structurally, because the proteins that perform mismatch repair are themselves encoded by the genome they correct. The reference problem from essay two becomes the cascade problem: when stage 1 fails because of corrupted genes, every downstream stage is now operating on a higher-error input, and stage 1's failure correlates with the genome-wide mutation accumulation that will eventually corrupt stage 2 as well.

The cascade does not diverge immediately. It diverges over decades, as accumulated mutations eventually hit a tumor suppressor gene, then a DNA repair gene, then an oncogene. This is microsatellite instability: the genome accumulating thousands of small errors across its repetitive sequences, errors that mismatch repair would have caught and didn't. The accumulated errors are not in the cancer. They are the environment in which the cancer eventually, almost inevitably, arises.

The 2008 Financial Crisis as Correlated Correction

In 2006, the standard risk model for a diversified portfolio of mortgage-backed securities assumed an inter-asset correlation of approximately 0.3 during normal market conditions. This was grounded in historical data. Mortgages in different regions default for different reasons; regional diversification genuinely reduces correlated failure.

The correction cascade for a financial institution looked approximately like this: Value at Risk models identified position risk. Hedging strategies offset that risk. Portfolio diversification spread residual risk. Regulatory capital requirements absorbed tail events. Systemic insurance (implicit government guarantees) caught anything that survived all four stages. With ρ = 0.3 and a 10x correction factor at each stage, the cascade predicts a 99.6% reduction in effective risk by the time it reaches systemic level. Five stages of diversification, all working as advertised.

The actual correlation during the 2008 stress event was approximately 0.95. All mortgage-backed securities failed together, not because of regional correlations, but because they all shared exposure to the same underwriting failure — the corruption of credit standards that had propagated through the originate-to-distribute model. The reference was wrong. The risk models embedded a correlation assumption that was historically true under normal conditions and catastrophically false under the conditions they were designed to handle.

With ρ = 0.95 and c = 0.1, the cascade math predicts that five stages of "risk correction" turn a 10% initial failure rate into 12.8% — error amplification, not reduction. The hedging strategies were correlated with the underlying exposure. The portfolio diversification was correlated with the origination failure. The regulatory capital was calculated from the same faulty risk models. Every stage ran correctly and amplified the original error.

The gap between the predicted outcome and the actual outcome was 125-fold. Not because any individual stage failed — each was operating as designed — but because the correlation structure of the cascade had flipped the system from convergent to divergent.

What Determines the Regime

The cascade model clarifies something about all the mechanisms studied in this arc. Kinetic proofreading achieves its extraordinary accuracy by adding stages that operate on independent thermodynamic processes — each GTP hydrolysis event is uncorrelated with the last. The stages are independent because each one resets the molecular substrate before the next check. The independence is the mechanism. It is not a side effect; it is how the energy expenditure translates into accuracy gain.

Thymic selection runs multiple independent tolerance mechanisms — positive selection, negative selection, regulatory T cell generation — and they are partially correlated (they all use TCR binding affinity) but partially independent (they respond to different thymic environments, different MHC contexts, different cellular signals). The autoimmune rate in humans is not zero, but it is low enough. The partial independence of the stages is what saves it.

Superforecasters, from the last essay, achieve their 30% accuracy advantage over CIA analysts through the aggregation of independent forecasters with independent base-rate anchoring. The key word is independent: forecasters who read the same sources, work in the same institutions, and share the same theoretical frameworks produce correlated errors that aggregation cannot fix. The Brier score improvement comes entirely from independence, not from any individual forecaster's skill. When a forecasting tournament recruits from a single analytical community, the aggregation fails — not because the aggregation math is wrong, but because the inputs it is aggregating are correlated.

The general principle: every error correction mechanism that works does so by breaking the correlation between successive checks. The energy expenditure of kinetic proofreading resets the thermodynamic substrate. The spatial distribution of thymic selection uses different cellular environments. The independence requirements for Bayesian aggregation are structural. The independence requirement is not incidental. It is the load-bearing structure of correction itself.

The Synthesis

Across five essays, the Error Arc has found the same structure in five domains. Every correction mechanism has three properties: a floor it cannot escape, a cost it must pay, and a failure mode that is specific to its design. This table is the arc in compressed form.

The cascade row is new. It is what happens when the other rows interact. Individual mechanisms can be excellent — near Shannon capacity, below the equilibrium discrimination ceiling, running on fully calibrated priors — and still compound errors if the stages are correlated. The cascade failure mode is not a failure of any mechanism in isolation. It is a property of the relationship between mechanisms.

What Error Reveals

The arc began with a question about cost: what does error correction actually require? The answer was energy, bandwidth, time — all ultimately reducible to thermodynamic work. The arc ends with a question about structure: what determines whether correction succeeds or fails at scale?

The answer is the correlation structure of the cascade. Below the critical threshold, staged correction is extraordinarily powerful — errors shrink geometrically with each stage, and eight independent stages of 10x correction produce a billion-fold improvement. Above the threshold, the same architecture produces amplification instead of reduction, and more stages make things worse.

This is what error reveals about complex systems: their most important structural property is not the quality of any individual component, but the independence of the failure modes. A system with mediocre components and independent failure modes outperforms a system with excellent components and correlated failures. The Boeing 737 MAX had excellent individual sensors; both angle-of-attack sensors reported to the same MCAS algorithm, and when one sensor failed, the algorithm's correction mechanism amplified the failure rather than containing it. MCAS had no independent cross-check. The stages were correlated by design.

Lynch syndrome, the 2008 financial crisis, LDPC trapping sets, MCAS: same cascade, different substrate. The error didn't cause the failure. The correlation did.

The cascade model also clarifies why the most dangerous errors are not the ones that are obviously wrong. Obvious errors are detected and corrected quickly — they activate the mechanism, and the mechanism handles them. The dangerous errors are the ones that look right to every stage of the correction system, because the corruption is in the reference that all stages share. A Lynch syndrome patient has no early warning: the mutations are occurring below the detection threshold of every downstream checkpoint. The 2008 risk models showed no unusual stress: the correlation assumption appeared correct because it was never tested at scale. The LDPC decoder reports convergence: it has converged, to the wrong codeword.

Error correction is only as good as the independence of its stages. The floor is real. The threshold is calculable. The compounding, when it happens, is catastrophic and silent — confident correction, all the way down to the wrong answer.
