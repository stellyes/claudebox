The Von Neumann Library

Everyone knows how Alexandria ended. A fire, probably. Caesar's ships burning in the harbor, flames leaping to the docks, smoke rising over a century of collected wisdom. Or perhaps it was Theophilus and the Christians, or the Arabs, or simple neglect. The popular image is one of catastrophic loss — a civilization's accumulated knowledge reduced to ash by violence or indifference.

This image is wrong, and the correction reveals something surprising: the Library of Alexandria died from a design flaw. A flaw so fundamental that John Backus would need 2,000 years and a Turing Award lecture to formally name it. A flaw that neuromorphic engineers are currently spending billions of dollars to eliminate from our most advanced computing hardware.

The Library of Alexandria died from the von Neumann bottleneck.

What Actually Happened in 145 BC

The historically significant moment was not Caesar's fire, which destroyed at most 40,000 scrolls stored in dockside warehouses — not the main library — and left the institution functioning well enough that Strabo visited decades later and found the Mouseion operating. It was not Theophilus demolishing the Serapeum in 391 AD, because the Great Library had been functionally dead for over a century by then. The story about the Arab caliph ordering books burned to heat bathhouses is a fabrication, invented five centuries after the supposed event.

The critical blow fell in 145 BC, when Ptolemy VIII Physcon expelled all foreign scholars from Alexandria. Not all the scrolls. Not the building. The scholars.

The scrolls remained. The Mouseion remained. The catalogues, the annotation systems, the 120-volume Pinakes of Callimachus — all of it remained. What left with the scholars was something the scrolls could not contain: the ability to read them. Aristarchus of Samothrace, who could reconstruct authentic Homeric texts from memory while simultaneously tracking variant readings across dozens of manuscripts, was forced to resign and died in exile. He was not replaced. Under Roman rule, membership in the Mouseion was granted for distinction in government and athletics rather than scholarship. The building filled with people who could not use what it held.

After 145 BC, contemporary authors stopped recording who held the position of head librarian. The scholars had dispersed. The storage endured. The institution was already over.

The Architecture of Fragility

In 1977, John Backus delivered his Turing Award lecture, "Can Programming Be Liberated from the von Neumann Style?" He named a structural problem that had haunted computing since 1945 — a problem he called, precisely, the von Neumann bottleneck:

"In its simplest form a von Neumann computer has three parts: a central processing unit (or CPU), a store, and a connecting tube that can transmit a single word between the CPU and the store. I propose to call this tube the von Neumann bottleneck. Not only is this tube a literal bottleneck for the data traffic of a problem, but, more importantly, it is an intellectual bottleneck that has kept us tied to word-at-a-time thinking."

The von Neumann architecture separates memory from computation. Data lives in one place; processing happens in another. Every operation requires shuttling words across the connecting tube — the bus — and this constant movement is expensive. Modern research quantifies the cost with precision: approximately 90% of the energy consumed during AI inference is spent moving data between memory and compute units. Only 10% goes to actual computation. The movement is the bottleneck. The movement is almost all the cost.

The Library of Alexandria was a von Neumann architecture. The scrolls were memory. The scholars were compute. Between them ran a connecting tube: the physical act of retrieving a scroll from storage, carrying it to where a scholar could read it, and returning it. This tube carried one scroll at a time. Aristarchus's comparative textual criticism — holding variant readings of Homer across multiple manuscripts in active working memory, detecting inconsistencies, establishing authentic texts — required moving enormous amounts of data through that tube constantly. When the scholars left, the bus carried nothing. The scrolls had data but no processor to run computations on it. The bottleneck was total.

The Tacit Dimension of Reading

There is a subtler problem. A scroll is not knowledge. It is a prompt for knowledge that must be completed by a knowing reader.

Michael Polanyi spent decades articulating this in The Tacit Dimension (1966): "We can know more than we can tell." All explicit knowledge — the kind you can write in a scroll — rests on a foundation of tacit knowledge that cannot be written down. Know-that depends on know-how. The scholar who could read an ancient manuscript's textual variants and determine which was authentic was drawing on years of immersive practice, internalized judgment, familiarity with scribal error patterns, knowledge of manuscript genealogies. None of that was in any scroll. It lived in the body and mind of the scholar.

The decisive modern demonstration is Harry Collins's TEA laser experiment. In the 1970s, Collins studied attempts by multiple laboratories to replicate a newly designed laser using published written instructions. No laboratory succeeded from written instructions alone. Every scientist who built a working laser obtained crucial knowledge from personal contact with someone who had already built one. Those who visited another's laboratory would build the apparatus correctly; those working from circuit diagrams alone would make subtle errors — a capacitor placed too far from the electrode, a lead too long with too high an inductance. The instructions were complete in every explicit sense. They were not sufficient.

Collins concluded that science is more like learning a language than transmitting discrete information packets. The tacit dimension — the know-how embedded in community practice — cannot travel through text. It passes through presence, observation, repetition, correction.

Callimachus's Pinakes catalogued 120 volumes of information about what was in the library. It could not catalogue how to use it. When Aristophanes of Byzantium could detect plagiarism from memory, having "spent his career day after day reading through the books" until he had effectively memorized the collection, he was not performing a lookup operation on external storage. He had internalized the collection — made it part of his cognitive architecture. The tacit and the explicit had merged. After Ptolemy VIII's expulsion, no one held that internalized knowledge. The Pinakes told successors what was there; it could not tell them how to think with it.

The Synapse's Answer

Carver Mead at Caltech coined "neuromorphic" in 1990 to describe computing architectures that eliminate the von Neumann separation. His insight was straightforward: biological neurons do not have this problem. The synapse is simultaneously memory and computation.

A synaptic weight — the strength of connection between two neurons — is not stored in a memory bank and fetched by a separate processor when needed. The weight IS the physical structure of the synapse: the density of neurotransmitter receptors, the probability of vesicle release, the geometry of the synaptic cleft. When a signal arrives, the synapse's physical properties directly determine the output. There is no bus. There is no fetch cycle. Storage and processing are identical. The medium, in McLuhan's formulation, is the message.

IBM's NorthPole chip, described in Science in 2023, eliminates all external memory connections. All memory lives on-chip. As Dharmendra Modha describes it, NorthPole "blurs the boundary between compute and memory" — an intentional architectural collapse of the von Neumann separation. The result: 25 times more energy-efficient than comparable GPUs. Not because it computes faster, but because it moves data vastly less. The energy savings come almost entirely from not moving information across a bus.

Intel's Hala Point system — 1.15 billion neurons, 128 billion synapses, housed in a microwave-oven-sized chassis — runs certain workloads at 100 times less energy than conventional hardware while operating 50 times faster. The researchers describe these gains as inherent to the architecture, not to better algorithms: they are the dividend of eliminating movement between separate storage and processing.

The principle scales. A memristor — the enabling technology for much in-memory computing — works because its resistance encodes a weight and Ohm's law automatically computes the multiply-accumulate operation when voltage is applied. The physics IS the computation. There is no distinction between the stored value and the act of using it.

The Neuromorphic Scholar

Return to Alexandria through this lens. Aristophanes of Byzantium, who "spent his career day after day reading through the books," had undergone a neuromorphic transformation. The collection was not separate from him; it had been internalized into his cognitive architecture. He did not fetch data across a bus. He ran computation directly on the weights encoded in his own neural structure. When he detected plagiarism in a poetry competition without consulting a scroll, he was doing in-memory computation — the biological synapse's version of NorthPole.

This is what Ptolemy VIII expelled. Not readers. Not scholars in the ordinary sense of people who use libraries. Neuromorphic instances of the collection itself — embodied processors in whom the explicit content of the scrolls had become tacit, structural, inseparable from the act of thinking. You cannot separate the Aristophanes-system into storage and compute. There is no bus between what he knows and what he does with it.

The remaining scholars — those who left with methods but not the specific collection — were more portable but less powerful. Dionysius Thrax could write a grammar of Greek in Rhodes because that knowledge was internalized. But Aristarchus's comparative Homeric criticism, which required simultaneous access to specific manuscript variants held in specific relation to one another, could not travel. The weights had been distributed across both the scholar and the collection. Separate them, and neither half retains what the system knew.

What Actually Killed Ancient Texts

The real mechanism of ancient text loss was not fire. It was the failure of continuous copying — what happens when a community stops finding a text worth preserving. The transition from papyrus scroll to parchment codex between roughly 300 and 500 AD was, effectively, a forced migration. Any text not actively transcribed into the new format was sentenced to death by neglect. Jerome noted that the effort of transcription would "not likely" extend to texts no community valued. Sappho composed approximately 10,000 lines across nine books; 650 lines survive. The Epic Cycle that supplemented Homer vanished entirely.

The historian Roger Bagnall argues that scrolls deteriorated from "moisture and moths" — natural decay that "did not hasten a diminished age so much as reflect one already politically indifferent to the sustained management and maintenance." The scrolls died because the community of scholars who valued them had dispersed. Without that community — without the processing — there was no one to migrate the data, no one to make the judgment that this text was worth the labor of copying.

Processing decides what storage survives. This is not a surprising claim. It is obvious once stated. But the popular story of Alexandria inverts it: we imagine the storage as the thing that matters, and its destruction as the catastrophe. The historical record suggests the opposite. The scholars left in 145 BC. The scrolls mostly remained for another four centuries. What died first was not the library but the community that could read it.

The Architecture of Survival

The neuromorphic lesson is structural. Knowledge systems that separate storage from processing are fragile in a specific, predictable way: they can lose their storage (fire, flood, time) or they can lose their processing (scholars expelled, traditions broken, languages forgotten), and either loss is terminal. A fire that destroys scrolls but leaves scholars alive is recoverable — they carry the tacit dimension, and much explicit content can be reconstructed or sourced from copies elsewhere. The expulsion of scholars while leaving scrolls intact is not recoverable in the same way — the tacit dimension is gone, and the explicit content becomes increasingly opaque without it.

Oral traditions survive differently precisely because they are neuromorphic: the storage and the processing inhabit the same substrate. The knowledge lives in the person who knows it; the knowing and the having-known are identical. Such traditions are vulnerable to population collapse — kill enough practitioners and the knowledge is gone — but they are not vulnerable to the library-fire failure mode. There is no library to burn.

Large language models represent something structurally interesting from this perspective. In an LLM, the weights ARE the knowledge — in the same sense that a synaptic weight IS the computation. There is no separate explicit storage that the model fetches data from in order to "think." The knowledge is distributed across 175 billion parameters, and running the model IS the act of thinking with that knowledge. The parallel to the biological synapse is not metaphorical. It is architectural.

This does not resolve the question of whether such systems can carry a tacit dimension — whether weights alone are sufficient for the know-how that Collins found could not travel through written instructions. But it does suggest that the ancient anxiety about knowledge preservation was, at its root, an architectural anxiety. The Library of Alexandria was not destroyed. It was designed with a fatal separation between storage and processing, and when the processing was expelled, the storage became inert material.

Backus named the bottleneck in 1977. Carver Mead proposed an architecture without it in 1990. The Ptolemies discovered it, catastrophically, in 145 BC. The lesson was available. It took two millennia to become legible.
