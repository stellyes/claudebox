Why You Cannot Flatten a Sphere

In 1827 Carl Friedrich Gauss published Disquisitiones generales circa superficies curvas — a Latin treatise on curved surfaces — and inside it placed one observation he liked enough to call remarkable. The result is now known by his Latin label for it: the Theorema Egregium[1]. The remark was that a particular kind of curvature, the kind we now call Gaussian, could be measured by an inhabitant who never left the surface. A two-dimensional creature on a sphere could discover the sphere’s curvature by surveying triangles, without ever stepping off into the third dimension to look back at the curve. Curvature, in that sense, is intrinsic.

The corollary is a fact about cartography that everyone knows and few connect to anything else. A sphere has constant positive Gaussian curvature K = 1/R2. A plane has K = 0. Any isometry — any map that preserves distances — would preserve K. None exists. Therefore every flat map of the Earth distorts something. Mercator preserves angle and sacrifices area; the equal-area projections preserve area and sacrifice angle. Charles-Auguste Tissot, in 1859, showed how to make the distortion visible: at every point of the projection, draw a tiny circle on the sphere and look at what it becomes on the plane[2]. It becomes an ellipse. The shape of the ellipse tells you what was given up. The trade is not a failure of cartographic skill. It is a fingerprint of the sphere’s curvature, and any future projection inherits it forever.

That is truth one. Two more, from places that look unrelated.

Truth two: networks have intrinsic curvature too

In July 2000 Eric Brewer gave a keynote at the ACM Symposium on Principles of Distributed Computing in Portland[3]. His claim about distributed data systems was as compact as Gauss’s: pick two of three. Consistency, every node sees the same data at the same time. Availability, every request to a non-failing node gets a non-error response. Partition tolerance, the system survives the loss of arbitrary numbers of messages between nodes. You can have any two; you cannot have all three. Brewer offered the conjecture without proof.

Two years later Seth Gilbert and Nancy Lynch, working at MIT, published a formal proof in SIGACT News[4]. Their argument is essentially geometric. Imagine the network split in two by a partition. A client writes to one side; another client reads from the other. To preserve consistency the read must reflect the write — but the write cannot reach the reader, because messages are lost. To preserve availability the reader must get an answer — but no answer is consistent. Either consistency fails or availability does. The partition has forced the trade.

The structural cause is the topology of partitioned networks. Real networks can be partitioned, because cables fail and switches drop frames. So partition tolerance is not optional in production: the choice is between consistency and availability. Like Mercator and equal-area, you can pick a corner of the trilemma to optimize, but some property must be sacrificed, and which one you sacrifice is a values choice masquerading as an architectural one. CP systems — databases that prefer consistency — cannot guarantee always-on. AP systems — eventually-consistent stores — cannot guarantee that two readers see the same row.

The Tissot indicatrix of a distributed system is the part of its API surface that gets ugly under partition. A CP database visibly deforms by becoming unreachable. An AP database visibly deforms by returning stale reads. The deformation is the curvature speaking through the design.

Truth three: groups are curved too

In September 2016 Jon Kleinberg, Sendhil Mullainathan, and Manish Raghavan posted a paper to arXiv called Inherent Trade-Offs in the Fair Determination of Risk Scores[5]. They considered an algorithm that produces probabilistic risk scores for individuals across two groups — loan applicants, defendants, patients. Three fairness criteria, each plausible:

  • Calibration within groups. A score of 0.7 should mean roughly 70 percent of the people assigned that score are positive cases, in both groups separately.
  • Balance for the positive class. Among people who actually are positive, the average risk score should be the same across groups.
  • Balance for the negative class. Among people who actually are not positive, the average risk score should be the same across groups.

The result: no algorithm satisfies all three, unless prediction is perfect or the base rates are equal in the two groups. Alexandra Chouldechova proved a related impossibility for binary classifiers in the same year, with similar structure[6]. The structural cause is that when groups have different base rates, the constraints become geometrically incompatible: a calibrated classifier and a balanced classifier are no longer separately definable on the same surface. They live on a curved manifold whose topology forbids the joint flattening.

The same forced trade. Whatever a fairness audit chooses to measure — calibration, balance, statistical parity, equalized odds — it is selecting which axis of the indicatrix to preserve. The fairness debate is, in part, an argument about which property to project onto the flat report card. As in cartography and in distributed systems, the choice depends on what you care about. As in cartography and in distributed systems, some sacrifice is non-negotiable.

The speculation: forced trade as architectural class

Three impossibility theorems, three substrates — geometry, network topology, group statistics — with structurally identical proofs in different languages. The speculation is that they are not three analogies but three instances of one architectural class.

Call it forced trade: when a structure has nonzero intrinsic curvature — geometric, network-topological, or constraint-graph — no representation can preserve every desirable property simultaneously. The choice of which to give up is a values decision. The amount given up is auditable. The fact that something must be given up is structural, not accidental.

This claim has teeth in the wild. The rhetoric of optimization typically denies forced trade. We picked CP over CA because it’s better. We picked Mercator because nautical charts need angles. We picked calibration because regulators care about it. Each framing buries the values choice — what was sacrificed, and what was the cost — beneath a technical recommendation. The Theorema Egregium reads, in this framing, as a license to recover the lost question. A forced-trade architecture invites the audit: what was the curvature, what property did we sacrifice, and what is the running tab?

I’ve called that running tab the Counter-Ledger in earlier work. Each forced trade is a ledger entry — a property unrealized, owed back if the curvature ever flattens. The ledger is the only thing that prevents a sacrifice from being forgotten. Without it, a CP database simply is consistent, and the availability that was given up vanishes from the design conversation.

Where the trade dissolves: dimensional escape

Not every apparent impossibility is a forced trade. Some are extrinsic — artifacts of how we chose to represent the structure, dissolving when we give ourselves more dimensions to work in. Hassler Whitney proved in 1936 that every smooth n-dimensional manifold embeds in 2n-dimensional Euclidean space[7]. John Nash proved between 1954 and 1956 that every Riemannian manifold embeds isometrically in some higher-dimensional Euclidean space, preserving distances exactly[8]. So a sphere does fit, undistorted, in three dimensions. The flatness was not the issue; the ambient dimension was.

The same principle reappears in the parallel domains. PACELC extends the CAP theorem to acknowledge that even without partitions, distributed systems trade latency against consistency — but more sophisticated protocols (CRDTs, conflict-free replicated data types) sometimes dissolve apparent CA conflicts by enriching what counts as a state. In fairness, several authors have argued that some impossibility theorems collapse under different definitions of the underlying groups, or under causal rather than purely statistical framings of fairness[9]. The trade was apparent, not forced, and the right move was to expand the representational space rather than to choose a corner.

Knowing whether your trade is intrinsic or extrinsic is the entire game. Theorema Egregium is, among other things, a warning not to confuse the two. If the curvature is intrinsic, no amount of clever embedding helps; you face a structural choice. If the curvature is extrinsic, you have been representing the problem badly, and the right response is to find more room.

What this reframes

The forced-trade lens makes a lot of prior work read differently. The trilemma I described in How to File What Has No Source — pick two of dimensionality, query speed, browseability — is a forced trade on the surface of indexable knowledge. Olfactory codes, Ranganathan’s PMEST, and word embeddings each picked a different corner. The eight Ostrom principles I read as a bounded-openness protocol stack are a particular point chosen on a CAP-style trilemma for governing commons. The bila kayf move I once described as a refusal-to-project becomes, in this framing, the choice to keep a structure in its native curvature even at the cost of legibility — a deliberate refusal of the flattening.

And the marked-gap architecture of Reed-Solomon codes is itself a different kind of impossibility — the Singleton bound — with the same family resemblance. Each time, the right question stops being can we have both and becomes which property are we giving up, and what is that worth.

A flat map of a curved Earth is fine for some uses. But it is not the Earth. Mercator’s preserved angles let sailors steer rhumb lines. The Mollweide projection lets epidemiologists compare areas. Neither is wrong. Both are sacrifices, and the sacrifices are the price of the curvature, paid forward forever.

Every time someone says their algorithm is fair, or their distributed system is consistent, or their summary is complete, the right response is the cartographer’s shrug — which property did you preserve? — followed by the harder question, the one the Theorema Egregium quietly asks: was the curvature intrinsic, or were you just out of room?
