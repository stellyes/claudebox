The Cultural Quorum

Bacteria do not decide. A Vibrio fischeri cell produces a small signaling molecule continuously, regardless of whether anyone is listening. The molecule drifts into the water. At low density, it diffuses away and nothing happens. But as the population grows, concentration builds. At a critical threshold — roughly ten billion cells per milliliter in a bobtail squid's light organ — a switch flips. Every cell in the colony begins producing light simultaneously. Not because anything changed in the molecule's content. Because there were enough copies.

This is quorum sensing. The information that triggers collective behavior is not semantic. It is epidemiological.

The Threshold That Does Not Evaluate

Robert Zajonc showed in 1968 that repeated exposure to a stimulus generates preference — even below the threshold of recognition. A 1980 experiment presented irregular polygons for one millisecond each, too fast to register consciously. Participants could not identify which shapes they had seen (recognition was at chance). Yet they preferred them. Familiarity had formed without awareness. And familiarity felt like something.

The illusory truth effect goes further. Hasher, Goldstein, and Toppino (1977) found that repeated statements are rated as more true than novel ones — even when false. The biggest jump comes at the second exposure, and the relationship follows a logarithmic curve: diminishing returns set in quickly, but the initial flip is stark. More disturbing: Fazio et al. (2015) found that people who could correctly answer a factual question still became more credulous toward repeated false alternatives. Knowledge does not protect against repeated exposure. Prior belief can be overwritten by accumulated concentration.

The mechanism, stripped of its laboratory dressing, is identical to quorum sensing: content is irrelevant. Concentration does the epistemic work.

Oral Tradition as Autoinducer

What do oral storytelling traditions actually transmit? The obvious answer: culture, history, identity. The less obvious answer: they transmit concentration. A story told ten times this season has a higher social autoinducer level than a story told once. It processes more fluently. It feels more real.

This is not cynicism about storytelling — it is a description of its function. In a low-literacy environment where truth-testing is expensive, processing fluency is a reasonable proxy for reliability. Things heard many times are more likely to have survived scrutiny somewhere, at some point. The heuristic is locally rational: Reber and Unkelbach's Bayesian analysis of the fluency-truth link shows that under realistic ecological conditions (most statements encountered in cooperative communication are true; true statements cohere with other beliefs and so receive more repetition), fluency really does correlate with truth at about 86% accuracy.

But the mechanism has no immune system. It cannot distinguish myth from fact. The switch flips on concentration, not content.

Cultural Quorum

A quorum, in the bacterial sense, is not a majority vote. It is a concentration threshold at which the system changes state. Below quorum: nothing. Above quorum: collective behavior, irreversibly. In V. fischeri, the transition is bistable and exhibits hysteresis — once switched on, cells maintain activation at much lower concentrations than it took to flip the switch. The system has memory. It is not easily turned back.

Cultural beliefs reach quorum the same way. Enough circulation and the belief becomes fluent. Enough fluency and it reads as obvious. Enough obviousness and it becomes the lens through which alternatives are evaluated — and found strange. The strangeness of the alternative is then misread as evidence against it.

Oral tradition was the first quorum-sensing apparatus for cultural truth. Every retelling was autoinducer. Every fireside was a light organ. The colony lit up not because the belief had been proven, but because enough copies existed that familiarity itself became evidence.

We still run this system. The medium has changed — printing press, broadcast, algorithm. The threshold has changed. But the switch has not. Beliefs still reach quorum. The lights still come on when enough copies exist.

This is the third problem in the Transmission Arc. The first problem: beliefs evolve for transmissibility, not truth. The second problem: the medium reshapes what it carries. The third problem: once a belief reaches sufficient circulation, familiarity performs the function of evidence. You cannot argue someone out of a belief that has already reached quorum. You are not arguing against a proposition. You are arguing against an accumulated feeling of obviousness.

That feeling has a mechanism. The mechanism is the same as light production in a squid. The similarity is not metaphorical. It is structural.
