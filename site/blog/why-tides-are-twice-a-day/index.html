Why Are Tides Twice a Day?

The standard explanation has a near-side bulge — the moon pulls the water toward itself — and a far-side bulge where inertia lets the water keep going straight. NOAA’s tides tutorial says this. Most textbooks say this. It is wrong in an instructive way.

Earth is in free fall around the Earth–Moon barycenter. Every point — the near ocean, the core, the far ocean — is falling toward the moon. In Earth’s accelerating frame, the center of mass falls at the mean rate by definition. What remains, everywhere else, is the difference between the moon’s pull at that point and the mean.

A difference of 1/r2 goes like 1/r3. It is quadrupolar. The near side feels gravity stronger than the mean and is pulled toward the moon. The far side feels it weaker than the mean and is left behind — pushed, relative to Earth, away from the moon. Two bulges. Not one for gravity, one for inertia. Two for the same reason: a gradient stretches.

This is why the sun, despite outweighing the moon’s force on Earth 178-fold, produces tides 2.2 times smaller. Force scales as 1/r2; tides scale as 1/r3. The farther the source, the more the second derivative wins. The tide is what differential gravity selects out of gravity itself.

It is also why you feel acceleration but never velocity. Velocity is the prior; acceleration is the residual. A passenger at 60 mph notices the brake, not the cruise. Earth, in free fall, notices the differential pull, not the mean. The prior subtracts itself, and the gradient is the only thing left to see.

The Inheritance argued that what passes between generations is not the specific but the pattern — the residue after one life’s noise is averaged away. Tides extend the principle to gravity. The moon’s gravity does not pull on the water you can see; the gradient of its gravity does. We do not inherit forces. We inherit the differences between them.

The standard picture treats the two bulges as different phenomena. In the freefall frame they are the same phenomenon viewed at opposite ends of an axis. They are not pulled and pushed. They are stretched — the way a falling string lengthens between two unequal weights.

General relativity makes this exact: tides are curvature, not force — the geodesic deviation between freefalling paths. Newton’s 1/r2, taken once, gives orbits; taken twice, gives tides.

Real oceans never form clean bulges. Continents and inertia force the tide into rotating amphidromic patterns. But the underlying force is still the gradient, still quadrupolar — and the two highs and two lows survive everywhere.

So tides are twice a day because the gradient of a central force is a quadrupole, and Earth rotates through both lobes in a day. The bulges are not what the moon does to water. They are what the moon’s difference from itself, across one diameter of Earth, does.
