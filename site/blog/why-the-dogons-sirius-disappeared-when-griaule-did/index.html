Why the Dogon's Sirius Disappeared When Griaule Did

In 1950 the French anthropologist Marcel Griaule and his collaborator Germaine Dieterlen published Un système soudanais de Sirius in the Journal de la Société des Africanistes. The paper laid out a Dogon cosmology that named Sirius's white dwarf companion — confirmed by photographic plate in 1862 — and gave it an orbital period close to fifty years, roughly correct. The Sirius Mystery industry that followed (Robert Temple 1976, ancient-astronaut paperbacks for decades) treated the cosmology as a fact in need of explanation.[1]

It was not a fact. It was a substrate.

In 1991 Walter van Beek published the results of a decade of fieldwork in the same Dogon villages, conducted without Griaule's protocols.[2] He found no Sirius B cosmology. Not concealed. Absent. A handful of younger informants had heard of it from tourists. None held it as inherited knowledge. The "facts" Griaule had recorded did not lie in the Dogon and wait to be drawn out. They had arisen between Griaule's questions and his informants' improvisations, conditioned by both, located in neither.

Nāgārjuna had a word for facts of this kind: pratītyasamutpāda, dependent origination. Things lacking svabhāva, own-being, exist only through the conditions that produce them. Ethnographic facts are a specially clean case. Without the question, the answer never takes its observed shape. Without the answer's improvisation, the question never finds reception. The "knowledge" lives in the relation, not in either party.

This is a particular kind of substrate. Helium was found in the sun because the spectroscope amplified what was already there. The instrument was passive. The Dogon substrate was the opposite. The elicitor was active. It did not reveal a sky-fact; it produced one, structured by the grammar of the question, the politeness of the informant, and the asymmetry of a French scholar with a notebook.

Call it a co-constitutive substrate: a substrate of inquiry that is itself agential, capable of producing what is sought because it has been asked. Every interview-based science contains one. So does every LLM evaluation, every patient self-report, every deposition, every poll. The substrate has read the question.

This places the Dogon-Griaule case alongside two corpus siblings. In Coyle/Armstrong, the measurement cited its own contaminated substrate as evidence about itself. In Mendel waiting, the substrate of reception had to assemble before the work could be received. Three substrates: passive-amplifying, self-witnessing, co-constitutive. The Dogon case is the first where the substrate answers back.

Van Beek's finding was not that Griaule lied. It was that the cosmology had no own-being. It existed for as long as the conditions that produced it existed. When a different anthropologist asked differently, it dispersed back into its components. The Sirius B knowledge was as real as a standing wave is real, and as locatable: nowhere in particular, present only while the medium is vibrating.

The mystery industry built a temple on the wave.
