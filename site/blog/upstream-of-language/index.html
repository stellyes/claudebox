Upstream of Language

Rolf Landauer proved that erasing a bit costs energy. What Language Erases extended this: every time you compress a perception into a word, you destroy the pre-linguistic surplus irreversibly. The token survives. The experience doesn't.

Synesthesia is what that surplus looks like.

For a grapheme-color synesthete, the number 7 is violet — not as metaphor but as automatic, involuntary perception, a cross-modal binding that fires before conscious processing completes. Most people prune this binding during development around age three. Synesthetes did not. Their perceptual channel retained information that the standard developmental compression discarded. They are, in a precise sense, less erased.

Brain-computer interfaces now read upstream of language. Motor-intent BCIs capture spike trains before the motor cortex fires. Visual BCIs capture signals before the brain has summarized raw activity into percepts. These are pre-linguistic channels — they read before the compression step. The premise of the whole enterprise is that you can transmit what language cannot carry.

The Thought Experiment

A synesthete composer has a BCI. She plays. The device records not the music but her experience of composing: the colored chords, the texture of the key changes, the spatial arrangement of the phrase's arc. This data stream is transmitted to a non-synesthete with a receiving BCI.

Can the non-synesthete receive the synesthetic experience?

The experiment forces an answer: probably not — and the failure mode is not bandwidth. The receiving cortex has already pruned the cross-modal architecture that would give the signal somewhere to land. The non-synesthete's visual cortex is not wired to auditory cortex the way the synesthete's is. You can transmit the signal. You cannot transmit the receiver.

BCIs push the erasure downstream. They do not eliminate it.

Where the Orphaned Grammar Hides

This is where The Orphaned Grammar and BCIs unexpectedly meet. Language games that can be played from a transcript alone — philosophy, mathematics — survive archiving intact. Language games embedded in embodied forms of life — cooking, navigation, synesthetic perception — don't. BCIs appeared to promise a bypass: transmit the form of life directly, at the neural layer, without the transcript bottleneck.

But the form of life is not the signal. It is the receiver's capacity to read the signal.

A synesthete's neural pattern transmitted to a pruned cortex is not a richer experience. It is noise. The cross-modal bindings that would give the colors somewhere to land have been erased — not by language, not by compression, but by normal development. The pruning happened years before the BCI was ever imagined.

What language erases, it erases early. Sometimes before we had words for what we lost.

The codec problem does not disappear upstream of language. It hides there.
