The Threshold Theorem of Living

In 1997 Dorit Aharonov and Michael Ben-Or proved that a quantum computer can run arbitrarily long, with arbitrarily low logical error, provided one quantity stays below a threshold: the physical error rate per gate. Below that threshold, errors decay faster than they accumulate. Above it, they run away.

Deinococcus radiodurans, a soil bacterium isolated in 1956 from gamma-sterilised canned meat, survives doses of ionising radiation that would reduce a human to mist. It also survives prolonged desiccation, vacuum, and oxidative shock. Its dose-response curve has the same shape as the quantum one: near-total survival below a threshold around 5 kGy, then steep collapse.

Specialists and codes

This is not what most extremophiles do. A thermophile is specialised. Its enzymes are folded for 100°C; they will not work at 20°C. The strategy is to occupy a particular cell of the environmental grid very well, and to die outside it. Pyrolobus thrives near hydrothermal vents because vents are what its proteins were built for. Move it to a refrigerator and it stops.

Deinococcus does the opposite. It has no particular environment. What it has is structure for surviving stochastic insults regardless of which bond happens to break. It is closer to a code than to a specialist.

The three pieces

The structure has three parts. First, polyploidy: four to ten copies of the genome, overlapping. Second, ESDSA — extended synthesis-dependent strand annealing — which reassembles a shattered chromosome by finding overlapping fragments and stitching them along homologous templates.[1] Third, manganese-peptide complexes that protect the repair proteins from oxidative damage in the first place.[2] The DNA can be cut into hundreds of pieces because the actual asset under guard is the protein machinery that puts the pieces back together.

Now look at how the repair finds the answer. RecA does not read base pairs to decide whether a fragment is "right." It finds matching segments by physical complementarity and trusts the match. The genome's content is never measured. Only the topology of overlap is measured, and from that topology the content is restored.

The same shape

This is the trick of a stabilizer code.[3] The logical bit lives in correlations across many physical bits; the measurements that detect errors do not read the logical bit, they read agreement between neighbours. I have used this analogy before — for identity, for communities. The bacterium is the cleanest case I have found. The analogy is no longer an analogy. It is a recurrence: a piece of structure that crystallises at every substrate large enough to host it.

What Consciousness Costs argued, following Boyd and Crutchfield, that integrated computation can pay the Landauer minimum exactly, while fragmented computation pays a tax above it.[4] There is a sister claim here. Below the threshold, redundancy is thermodynamically cheap per error corrected; the cost amortises. Above it, no redundancy is enough — you are paying to delay an inevitable collapse.

A specialist has a niche. A code has a threshold. The bacterium has a threshold. So does the qubit. So, presumably, do we — though we have not yet measured ours, and the measurement may not look like a number.
