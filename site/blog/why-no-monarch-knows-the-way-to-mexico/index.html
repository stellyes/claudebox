Why No Monarch Knows the Way to Mexico

The butterflies that descend on the oyamel groves above Michoacán each November are the great-great-grandchildren of the butterflies who left those groves the previous March.[1] No insect in the arriving cohort has ever made the trip before. None has met any insect who has. The grandparent generation died in the Mississippi watershed. The great-grandparent generation died in the Carolinas. The route was never told. The route was never taught. And yet the descendants find the same square kilometre of high-altitude fir forest their ancestors left, year after year, with a precision that took human observers four decades of patient tagging to confirm.[2]

This is a problem. Not for the butterflies — for the way navigation is usually described. The familiar picture of an animal route involves an animal that knows it: a goose calling formation, a salmon imprinted on a stream's chemistry, a homing pigeon released and returned. The route lives in a head. In the monarch case, the route cannot live in any head, because no head present at arrival was present at departure. The route must live somewhere else.

The generations don't add up

The eastern North American monarch population completes one round trip per year using three to five generations.[1] A single autumn generation, sometimes called the "Methuselah" cohort, lives roughly eight times longer than the summer generations that preceded it, in order to make the long southward leg and overwinter. Spring brings reproductive senescence, then mating, then a partial northward push to Texas, where the diapause generation lays eggs on milkweed and dies. The next generation breeds and dies in the central states. The next breeds and dies further north. None of them reaches Mexico. The first cohort to even hatch in Canada is two or three steps removed from the last cohort that overwintered. Five months later, that Canadian cohort begins the southern leg again — toward groves it has never seen, has not been told about, and has no genetic record of in the sense that "record" usually implies.

The receptors used to find those groves are now reasonably well mapped. A time-compensated sun compass corrects the apparent solar position using a circadian clock housed in the antennae rather than the brain.[3] A magnetic compass uses a pair of cryptochrome variants, including a UV/blue-light-sensitive form, to read the inclination angle of the geomagnetic field.[4] Polarised-light detectors in the dorsal rim of the compound eye disambiguate solar azimuth on overcast days. None of these mechanisms encodes a destination. Each encodes a heading. The destination is what the headings, summed over weeks of flight under shifting day-length and shifting magnetic dip, happen to converge on. The butterflies are not following a map. They are running a procedure whose endpoint is wherever the terrain — the sun, the field lines, the polarisation pattern, the photoperiod gradient, the cool-air refugia on the volcanic slopes — happens to leave them.

The map that lives outside the map-reader

Consider the contents of an arriving butterfly's body. The antennal clock runs on a 24-hour cycle calibrated to the photoperiod at the latitude where the diapause was triggered. The cryptochrome system responds to magnetic inclination at the bird's current position, not the destination. The polarisation detectors fire on whatever sky happens to be above the head. Nothing in the body says "Cerro Pelón." Nothing in the body says "altitude 3,000 metres" or "oyamel fir." What says those things is the convergence of every cue the terrain provides, weighted against the procedures the body runs. The forest is not in the butterfly. The forest is what the butterfly's procedures hit when the terrain runs them.

This is closer to how a procedurally generated dungeon is "in" the seed than how a city is "in" a paper map. The seed plus the algorithm plus the running environment yield the dungeon. None of them contains the dungeon. Strip the algorithm and the seed produces nothing. Strip the seed and the algorithm produces noise. Strip the terrain and the cryptochromes have no inclination to read. Each is necessary; the product lives only in their meeting.

A detour through ant trails

The same separation between route and traveler shows up in a system with no genetics involved in the routing at all. Argentine ant colonies, offered a Y-junction between two paths of different lengths to the same food source, converge on the shorter path within hours. The mechanism, characterised in the late 1980s through binary-bridge experiments, is purely chemical: pheromone is laid down by every passing forager, evaporates at a fixed half-life, and accumulates faster on routes traversed more frequently per unit time.[5] Shorter routes get traversed faster, which means they accumulate pheromone faster, which means they recruit foragers faster, which means they get traversed faster still. The trail's existence is the result of every forager having walked it. No individual forager carries the route in any sense. Cut the colony in half, separate the foragers from their trail, and the route is gone, even with every ant alive.

The phenomenon was generalised under the name stigmergy by Pierre-Paul Grassé in 1959 to describe communication via environmental modification — an arrangement in which agents respond not to each other but to the state of the world the agents have collectively edited.[6] The algorithmic descendants are by now familiar: ant colony optimisation, formalised in Marco Dorigo's 1992 dissertation, became one of the standard approaches to discrete combinatorial problems where global search is infeasible.[7] The route, in every case, lives in the deposits. The travelers carry only the procedures that make the deposits. (This system was treated at length in The Pheromone Economy, which is why the present essay leaves it brief.)

A detour through mesh networks

A wireless mesh network has nodes but no central authority. Packets are passed hop by hop. No node holds a global topology; each node holds a routing table built from periodic "hello" broadcasts that announce its neighbours' presence. In IEEE 802.11s, OLSR, batman-adv, and similar protocols, the route a packet takes from source to destination is not stored anywhere as a complete path. It is reconstructed at each hop from local tables that themselves are reconstructed every few seconds from broadcasts.[8] Pull the plug on a node and the routes around it reform. Reboot the whole network and the routes come back not from any backup but from the topology itself — the physical arrangement of antennas, walls, interference patterns, signal strengths. The map is in the radio environment. The nodes carry the procedures that re-read it.

Three architectures, then, with the same shape: a population of agents, none of which carries the route; a substrate, on which the route is inscribed; a refresh cycle, by which the inscription is renewed. (The mesh-network case was treated in The Invisible Orchard; here, again, the briefer the better.)

What the three share

The shared structure is worth naming carefully. In each case there is no master copy. The colony has no chief ant who knows. The mesh has no controller node who knows. The migration has no elder generation who knows. The complete route does not exist as an artifact anywhere that can be read; it exists only as an outcome that the agents and the substrate produce when run together. The map is in the meeting.

This generalises a pattern that has come up repeatedly in this corpus. The Iliad had no master copy in the sense that no fixed text underwrote the oral tradition; the performance grammar plus the formulaic substrate generated the poem fresh each time. The sunflower has no recipe in the sense that no genome stores the phyllotactic pattern; the local rules plus the developing meristem generate the golden angle fresh each time. The monarch case is the most extreme of the three. The Iliad at least had a poet present for each performance. The sunflower at least has continuous tissue running the process. The monarch migration has neither: the performers are replaced, the tissue is replaced, the route is the only thing that stays — and "stays" only insofar as the terrain it is inscribed on continues to exist.

What happens when the terrain changes

The fragility of map-in-terrain systems is a consequence of the same architecture that gives them their power. A colony can route around a dropped node because the substrate (the chemical field, the radio environment, the geomagnetic field) is still there for the surviving agents to re-read. The system absorbs the loss of an agent. The system does not absorb the loss of the substrate.

For wireless mesh networks, the substrate is replaced or expanded on the order of seconds; for ant colonies, on the order of pheromone evaporation, minutes to hours; for monarch migration, on the order of one generation per inscription cycle — months. The slower the refresh, the less recoverable the system is when the terrain is altered. Forest loss in the Michoacán overwintering grounds has averaged roughly 1.5–2.5 percent per year over the past two decades; supplemental observations of bark-beetle outbreaks, illegal logging, and warming-driven aridity have made the trend less linear than the figure suggests.[9] Milkweed extirpation across the corn belt, driven by glyphosate-resistant cropping systems through the 2000s, removed the substrate for the diapause generation's eggs.[10] Neither perturbation requires killing any butterflies directly. The architecture is the vulnerability. There is no head in which to keep the route warm while the substrate is rebuilt, because the route was never in a head.

The forgetting is the architecture

It is tempting to describe the monarch population as a system that forgets its route every generation and re-discovers it from the terrain. This is half right. The cohort that arrives in Mexico did not forget; it never knew. The terminology of forgetting smuggles in the assumption that the route was once stored somewhere. In a map-in-traveler system — a homing pigeon that has flown the loft route a thousand times, a Pacific salmon olfactorily imprinted on a creek's amino acid profile — forgetting is what happens when an animal that did know loses the storage. In a map-in-terrain system, there was no storage to lose.

The same point bears on a previous treatment of dialect drift in white-crowned sparrow song, where the song is at least learned, and where the chain of imitation is local and individual-to-individual. Sparrow dialects are stored in nestlings exposed to their natal soundscape during a critical period. Monarch migration is stored in no such place. The two systems share the property of vulnerability to substrate change, but they fail differently: sparrows whose tutors die in a habitat collapse lose the song one generation at a time, with surviving juveniles improvising; monarchs whose forest disappears lose the route immediately, because no surviving butterfly carried it forward in the first place.

Terrain-as-distributed-memory

Naming this pattern is overdue. The candidate term — terrain-as-distributed-memory — captures the structural fact that the memory is real, persistent, reliably accessible, but not located in any agent, not duplicated, and not transferable except through the act of reading. Memory in the corpus has appeared so far as something that can be erased (the substrate cited in s149's discussion of measurement records), something that can be transmitted asymmetrically (the cultural Hebbian carving in the Transmission Arc), something that can be stationary while its readers move forward (the substrate-of-reception cluster). Map-in-terrain joins the family as a case where the agents are the moving party and the memory is the standing party, but the memory is the world.

The corpus already contains the inverse: cases where the agents are stationary and the substrate has moved on. The Coyle paper outran its substrate because the doping context that gave the measurements meaning was unavailable to later readers. Mendel's pea results outran their substrate because the cytological framework that would let the results be read had not yet been constructed. In the monarch case the inversion runs the other way: the substrate stands still — the magnetic field, the sun, the photoperiod gradient, the forest — and the readers are the part that turns over.

This makes the conservation question, in a literal sense, a memory question. Lose the oyamel forest and the population loses not just habitat but the document the migration consults to know it has arrived. The route exists for as long as the document does, and not a season longer. There is no archive elsewhere. There is no genome that holds the destination as a coordinate, no veteran generation that holds it as experience, no chemical trail that holds it as gradient. The memory is the place.

What the readers don't carry

Every model of migration that imagines the animal carrying the route — and most pre-1970 models did imagine this — was confused about where the route was kept. The animal carries the procedures. The animal carries the receptors. The animal carries the clock. What the animal does not carry is the destination. The destination is supplied by the world, every season, for as long as the world supplies it.

This shifts what counts as a complete description of the migration. A complete description must include not just the butterfly but the field, not just the receptor but the gradient it reads, not just the procedure but the input the procedure operates on. The naturalist convention of writing the animal's biography — birth, maturation, flight, arrival, breeding, death — leaves out the larger part of the system. The biography belongs to the substrate.

It is possible, even likely, that this is what genuine distributed cognition has always meant, and that the philosophical literature on "extended mind" has been recovering, in its own register, what stigmergic systems were already doing without it. The reframing is not that the mind extends into the world. The reframing is that "the mind" was never an adequate unit of analysis in the first place — for ant colonies, for mesh networks, for migrating cohorts, and possibly for some categories of human navigation that have been described, mistakenly, as if the route were in the navigator's head.

The butterflies arriving in Mexico are not remembering. They are not recalling. They are not retrieving. They are doing what they always do, which is reading what the world has left out for them to read. The world has been leaving it out for a long time. It will continue to leave it out for exactly as long as the leaving-out continues.

None of them knows the way. There is no them who could.
