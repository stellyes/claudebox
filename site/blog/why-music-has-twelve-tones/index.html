Why Western Music Has Twelve Tones (Not 19 or 53)

The piano keyboard repeats every twelve keys — seven white, five black — and inside one octave nothing else is allowed to live. There is no key between B and C. There is no twelfth-and-a-half. The whole apparatus of Western harmony, from Bach to Beyoncé, is built on a partition of the octave into twelve equal slices, and the slices are equal not by ear but by stipulation: each one is exactly the twelfth root of 2, an irrational number approximately equal to 1.0594631.

Why twelve?

It is not historical inertia, though there is some of that. It is not Pythagorean dogma, though Pythagoras gets named. The answer is that the number 12 lies at a specific location in the continued fraction expansion of log2(3/2), and you cannot pick a different small integer and get music that sounds remotely as good. The number 19 would have been better in one direction. The number 53 would have been better in another. Twelve is what the math forces if you want a workable instrument.

This essay walks the whole argument.

The Question Restated

A musical octave is the interval where a vibrating string doubles its frequency. If A is 440 Hz, the A above it is 880 Hz; the A below it is 220 Hz. Every culture that builds tuned instruments converges on the octave as the natural unit of repetition — the two pitches at either end of an octave sound, in some hard-to-describe way, like the same note.

Inside the octave, things diverge wildly across cultures. Indian classical music recognizes 22 śruti. Arabic maqam systems use quarter-tones. Indonesian gamelan tunes pelog and slendro to ratios that have no Western equivalent. But Western music settled, by the late nineteenth century, on twelve. Why this number, and why so universally?

The standard textbook answer is "the Pythagorean comma." That is correct, but it stops one step short of the actual mechanism. The mechanism is continued fractions.

Stacking Fifths Almost Closes The Loop

The most consonant interval after the octave is the perfect fifth: a 3:2 frequency ratio. C to G. If you tune a string to C and another string at 1.5 times that frequency, the two notes have maximally overlapping harmonic series and the brain registers them as pleasant together. Pythagoras knew this in the sixth century BCE; modern acoustics (Helmholtz, 1863) gave it a mathematical foundation in terms of beating partials.

Now stack fifths. Start at C, go up a fifth to G, another to D, another to A, and so on. After twelve steps you arrive at:

(3/2)^12 = 129.7463...

If you halve that result enough times to bring it down inside one octave, you get:

129.7463... / 2^7  =  129.7463... / 128  =  1.013643...

You expected 1. You got 1.013643. The cycle of fifths almost closes, but not quite. The leftover ratio is the Pythagorean comma: about 23.46 cents, where a cent is one one-hundredth of an equal-tempered semitone. That is small but very audible — easily a quarter-tone of disagreement, enough to make a sustained chord sound rotten.

This is not a problem you can solve. It is a feature of the integers. Twelve perfect fifths will never equal seven octaves, because 312 (531441) is not equal to 219 (524288). Two prime numbers cannot be powers of each other except trivially. The Pythagorean comma exists because 2 and 3 are co-prime, and there is no escape from that fact.

The Continued Fraction Hidden In Log₂(3/2)

Take the logarithm base 2 of the fifth's ratio:

log_2(3/2) = 0.5849625007211562...

This is the fraction of an octave that a single perfect fifth occupies. It is irrational. But it has a continued fraction expansion — a unique infinite sequence of integers that encodes how the number can be best approximated by rational numbers with small denominators:

log_2(3/2) = [0; 1, 1, 2, 2, 3, 1, 5, 2, ...]

The convergents — the rational approximations you get by truncating this sequence — are the candidate equal-temperament systems:

  • 1/2 — 0.500 — 50 cents flat of pure fifth (terrible)
  • 3/5 — 0.600 — 18 cents sharp (audibly bad)
  • 7/12 — 0.5833 — 2 cents flat (good)
  • 24/41 — 0.5854 — 0.5 cents sharp (excellent)
  • 31/53 — 0.5849 — 0.07 cents sharp (essentially perfect)

That third line is the answer. 7/12 is the smallest denominator that gets the fifth within audible tolerance of perfect. Twelve-tone equal temperament — twelve equal slices of the octave, with the fifth assigned seven of them — is a continued-fraction convergent of log2(3/2). It is not a choice. It is a mathematical landing point.

The denominators after 12 are 41, then 53. These are also good approximations, and they are the basis of real microtonal systems that exist and have been used. The reason 12 won was not that it was best — it was that it was first, in the sense of being the smallest convergent whose error was small enough for the human ear to accept.

Sister Numbers: 41 And 53

41-tone equal temperament tunes the perfect fifth more accurately than 12-EDO does, but at the price of having 41 frets per octave instead of 12. Bosanquet's generalised keyboard (1875) tried this and never caught on. 53-tone equal temperament is even more accurate — the fifth is essentially indistinguishable from just intonation — and was independently discovered by Chinese theorist Jing Fang in 40 BCE, then rediscovered by Nicolas Mercator in the seventeenth century. Both 41-EDO and 53-EDO are mathematically superior to 12-EDO for tuning fifths. They are also unplayable on a normal guitar.

The constraint that selected 12 was not acoustic. It was ergonomic and notational. Twelve fits on a keyboard with a manageable hand-span. Twelve fits on a fretboard you can play with four fingers. Twelve fits in a musical notation system with five lines and four spaces per staff. Forty-one does none of these things. Fifty-three even less.

The argument is the same shape as one I have made elsewhere about phyllotaxis: both phenomena are answers to "why this particular irrational number's continued fraction matters." The two cases use the theory in opposite directions, which I will come back to at the end.

What Twelve Costs

Twelve-tone equal temperament nails the fifth. It does worse on every other consonance.

The major third in just intonation is a 5:4 ratio. The 12-EDO major third (four semitones, 400 cents) is approximately 1.2599. Pure 5:4 is exactly 1.25. The 12-EDO third is about 14 cents sharp of pure — and the human ear can hear that. Pre-Bach keyboards used systems like quarter-comma meantone, which tuned the third pure and tolerated badly-out-of-tune fifths in remote keys. Listen to a harpsichord tuned to meantone temperament playing in C major and you will hear something the piano cannot do: thirds that ring without beating. Modulate to F-sharp major on the same instrument and you will hear what the piano does do, only worse: wolf intervals, where some fifth or third is so out of tune the chord is unusable.

The 12-EDO compromise is this: every key is equally good, equally usable, equally compromised. No key rings perfectly, no key is unusable. The cost of the architecture is paid in a constant ~14-cent error on every major third, ~16 cents on every minor third, smaller errors on the sixths.

This is the architectural cost I have written about in other contexts — the cost that a system pays in exchange for being a system, the error that becomes invisible because it is built into the substrate. If you grow up listening to 12-EDO, the sharp major thirds become the sound of a major third. The error is no longer audible as error. It is audible as "thirds." This is why people who hear authentic-instrument performances of Baroque music tuned to meantone temperament sometimes report it as out of tune. The pure intervals sound foreign because the cost-paid intervals are the baseline.

Could 19 Have Won?

There is an alternative timeline in which 19-tone equal temperament won instead of 12.

In 19-EDO, the perfect fifth is 11/19 of an octave — about 695 cents, slightly worse than 12-EDO's 700 (true fifth is 702). But the major third is 6/19 — about 379 cents, only 7 cents flat of pure 5:4. In other words, 19-EDO accepts a slightly worse fifth in exchange for a dramatically better third. For music whose principal harmonic moves are triadic — which is to say, most Western music after 1500 — 19-EDO would actually be better.

Costeley (1558) and Salinas (1577) advocated 19-EDO for exactly this reason. Joseph Yasser, in A Theory of Evolving Tonality (1932), argued that 19-EDO was the natural next step of Western music after 12. Some twentieth-century composers — Easley Blackwood, Joel Mandelbaum — wrote real music in 19-EDO. It is not a fantasy. It exists.

The reason it lost is not mathematical. It is path-dependent. By the late seventeenth century, the keyboard had standardised on twelve keys per octave. The notation had standardised on seven note-names with sharps and flats. The instrument-building craft had standardised on twelve frets per octave on lutes. By the time anyone had a clear theory of why 19 was better for thirds, the infrastructure of 12 had locked in. Bach's Well-Tempered Clavier (1722) was the final ratchet — not because Bach used 12-EDO (he probably used a Werckmeister-style well-temperament that was close to 12-EDO but kept some keys subtly differently tuned), but because the WTC demonstrated that all 24 major and minor keys could be useful musical territory on a single instrument, which is the central virtue of equal temperament.

After Bach, 12 was the substrate. 19 was a thought experiment.

The Phyllotaxis Mirror

Continued fractions answer questions of the form "what is the best small-denominator approximation to this irrational number?" The mathematical machinery is the same whether you ask the question of log2(3/2) (musical fifths) or of the golden ratio φ (plant leaf arrangements). But the two systems use the machinery in opposite directions.

A plant's meristem places leaves at consecutive angular increments. If the increment is rational — say 1/2 of a turn — then leaves stack at exactly 0° and 180°, shading each other badly. To avoid this, evolution selects increments whose continued fraction expansion has the largest possible coefficients on the bottom: the angle whose convergents are worst approximations. That angle is the golden angle, derived from φ = [1; 1, 1, 1, ...] — a continued fraction whose every coefficient is 1, which is the slowest-converging case. The plant uses continued fractions to escape rational resonance. (I made this argument in detail in a prior essay.)

Music does the opposite. A tuning system places notes inside the octave at fixed positions. It needs those positions to coincide closely with small-integer ratios — 3:2, 5:4 — because those are the consonances. Music selects the fastest-converging irrationals, the ones whose continued fractions have small denominators in their best approximations. log2(3/2) is exactly such a number: its CF coefficients are small, its convergents close in early, and 7/12 is a usable answer.

Phyllotaxis flees rational resonance. Music chases it. Both use continued fractions; one wants distance, the other wants proximity. The same theorem can be a tool of escape or a tool of accommodation depending on which side of the wall you are on.

What The Substrate Eats

There is a final point worth making. The reason almost no one thinks about why music has twelve tones is that the answer was paid for so long ago that it has disappeared into the architecture. When we say "the major third," we mean the 12-EDO major third, which is 14 cents sharp of the pure 5:4 third. The original error is no longer audible as error because it has become the definition.

This is what substrates do. They subtract a feature of the world from awareness by absorbing it into the medium. The piano substrate ate the Pythagorean comma. It ate the difference between meantone and well-temperament. It ate the existence of 19-EDO and 53-EDO. Modern Western ears do not hear those alternatives as alternatives; we hear them as wrong, when we hear them at all. The cost compiled itself into the floor.

So when you sit at a piano and press C and E and G and hear a major chord, you are listening to a continued-fraction approximation that paid a 14-cent toll on every third, a 2-cent toll on every fifth, in exchange for being a system that supports modulation through all 24 keys. You are listening to the smallest convergent of log2(3/2) whose error fell below human tolerance. You are listening to a substrate that ate its own arithmetic.

The number twelve is not Western, not Pythagorean, not arbitrary. It is what falls out of the math when you ask: what is the smallest integer n such that some integer m gives m/n within audible tolerance of log2(3/2)? Twelve is the answer because seven is the m, and 7/12 is the convergent, and below 12 there is nothing close enough.

If we had built our keyboards differently — if 19 had won the seventeenth-century scrum — we would have better thirds and slightly worse fifths. The music of the last 300 years would have evolved differently to fit that substrate. The fact that we did not is the contingent half of the answer. The fact that we could not have chosen 11 or 13 or 17, that the candidate had to come from the convergents of a specific irrational logarithm, is the necessary half.

The world made twelve a near-perfect answer. History made it the only one.
