What Mistletoe Knows About the Attention Economy

Walk under an oak tree in late winter and you might see, high in the branches, a clump of pale green leaves that nobody planted. It is mistletoe. It is alive up there because, months earlier, a bird ate a berry and could not get the seed off its beak.

The seed of Viscum album, European mistletoe, is wrapped in a substance called viscin. Viscin is a botanical glue. It is mostly cellulose threads stuck together with a kind of plant slime — long sugar chains that scientists call mucopolysaccharide but which behave, for our purposes, like glue. Heat does not melt it. Water does not dissolve it. Bird stomachs do not break it down. When a mistle thrush swallows a mistletoe berry, the viscin survives the gut and comes out the other end still sticky. Sometimes the seed is excreted onto a branch below. Sometimes the seed glues itself to the bird's beak and the bird wipes the mess on bark to be rid of it. Either way, the seed ends up fixed to a tree at exactly the height a mistletoe needs to be.[1]

If the seed lands on the ground, it dies. Mistletoe cannot germinate in soil. It is a plant that requires a bird to deliver it to a very specific receiving surface — high, alive, woody. The bird is the address.

This is the smallest scale at which to ask a much bigger question: what makes a system that spreads things actually work, and what makes one break?

The hungry vehicle

The textbook frame for animal-mediated seed dispersal is mutualism — a relationship that benefits both sides. The plant gets transport. The animal gets calories. Henry Howe and Judith Smallwood, surveying this in 1982, called it "the ecology of seed dispersal" and noted that fruit pulp is almost always a reward bundled around a seed: a soft, sugary outer layer the animal eats, with a hard inner part the animal does not digest.[2]

But the textbook frame skips over the thing that matters most. The reason fruit-mediated dispersal works is that the animal is hungry. The reward is metabolic. A bird eating a berry is turning sugar into the energy it needs to stay warm, to fly, to find a mate. The seed's chances of reaching a good site get bolted onto a vehicle whose own survival depends on functioning correctly. If the bird stops eating, the bird dies. If the bird eats well, the seed gets carried.

Biologists call this bolting-together "coupling": two organisms whose fitnesses are tied so that neither can cheat without paying. The seed pays the cost of producing fruit pulp. The bird pays the cost of metabolism. The exchange is a real exchange of energy.

The Australian cassowary, a flightless black bird the size of an emu, eats fruit from a rare rainforest tree called Ryparosa kurrangii. When the seeds of Ryparosa are planted directly from the fruit, around 4 percent of them germinate. When the same seeds are first passed through a cassowary, around 92 percent germinate.[3] Researchers could not reproduce the effect by simulating digestion in a lab. Something the cassowary does — chemically, mechanically, or both — is necessary for the tree to reproduce. The cassowary's body is, in effect, a part of the tree. If the cassowary disappears, the tree disappears too.[4]

Two things at once

Now hold this up to its mirror.

In 1971, the economist Herbert Simon wrote a short paper called "Designing Organizations for an Information-Rich World." He pointed out something that had become true only recently: information was abundant. The thing being consumed by all that information was attention. Attention, he said, was the new scarce good. He wrote, in a line that became famous, that "a wealth of information creates a poverty of attention."[5]

In the half-century since, Simon's observation grew into an industry. Books were written about it; platforms were built around it; the phrase "the attention economy" turned out to organize a lot of how the modern web works.[6]

What did not happen was this: nobody quite asked what kind of dispersal system attention was building.

Because that is what it is. Every modern platform is a propagation network. Things get made — videos, posts, articles, songs, jokes — and the platform decides which of them get carried where. The platform is the disperser. The content is the seed.

So the question is: is the platform a hungry vehicle?

What the algorithm eats

The honest answer is no. The algorithm does not eat. It is not metabolizing the content. It is optimizing a number — engagement, watch time, retention, clicks. None of these numbers are food. None of them are coupled to the algorithm's continued existence in the way a berry is coupled to a thrush's. If the engagement number falls, the algorithm does not get cold. It gets re-tuned by engineers somewhere.

This is the difference. The thrush has a stake. The algorithm has a target.

When a system has a target rather than a stake, a specific kind of failure becomes inevitable. The economist Charles Goodhart, working at the Bank of England in the 1970s, noticed it in monetary policy: any statistical regularity used as a basis for control breaks down once it is used. The anthropologist Marilyn Strathern, writing in 1997 about British university audits, gave it its sharpest form: "When a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure."[7]

The reason is mechanical. A measure that is also a target gets gamed. Anything anyone can do that increases the measure without increasing the underlying thing the measure was for gets done. The measure and the thing it stood for drift apart. After enough time, the measure tracks itself, not the world.

This is what has happened to engagement. It is not just that the measure was imperfect from the start. It is that the measure, once it became the disperser's only food, stopped pointing at anything outside itself. The content that propagates is the content that triggers engagement. The content that triggers engagement is whatever has, in the most recent training window, been triggering engagement. The propagation system has become self-referential.

The mistletoe seed needs a tree branch. The thrush is paid in calories to find one. The system works because calories are not invented by the thrush. They come from outside the loop.

The substrate reshapes itself

In an earlier essay called What Culture Erases, I argued that a culture is, among other things, a system for maintaining shared expectations — a kind of large-scale prior agreement about what things mean. When the agreement fragments, talking across the gap gets expensive. Communication has a thermodynamic cost, and shared priors are the discount.

The attention economy is not just routing content along this shared substrate. It is reshaping the substrate itself. Every recommendation made, every video promoted, every post buried is a small adjustment to what users expect next. The shared priors are no longer set by something outside the loop — a city, a generation, a slow institution. They are set, in part, as a byproduct of whatever the engagement target currently rewards.

This is a third kind of dispersal system, and it is the first time in history we have built one.

In the standard ecological cases the substrate is independent of what is being dispersed: wind blows where it blows, water flows where it flows, the cassowary walks its own paths. In the mutualistic cases the substrate co-evolves with the propagule over deep time — branches and viscin shape each other across millions of years. In the algorithmic case the substrate is reshaped by the propagator in real time, on a feedback loop measured in hours.

A propagation system whose substrate is being reshaped by its own dispersal target has no equilibrium. It does not settle. It moves toward whatever the proxy currently favors and then moves again when the proxy adjusts. Coherence is not the goal because coherence is not the measure.

The mistletoe test

The fix is not a mystery. The fix is to make the disperser hungry.

There are versions of this everywhere in the older economy. A paid subscription is a metabolic cost — money is a stand-in for calories, and a person who paid for something has reason to want it to be good. A long book or a slow documentary requires time, which is also metabolic. A live concert costs the audience their bodies in a particular place. The economic shape of these arrangements is the same shape as the mistletoe-thrush deal: the disperser pays a real cost, the content has to be worth the cost, and the system has somewhere to come back to.

Algorithm-mediated free content does not have this. Engagement is not a cost the disperser pays; it is a number the disperser reports. The number can be true and the system can still be diverging.

This suggests a small test for any propagation system. Call it the mistletoe test. Ask: what does the disperser metabolize? If the answer is "calories" or "money" or "time," the dispersal is grounded in the disperser's own life, and the system has a stake. If the answer is "a metric," the system is running on a proxy, and the proxy will, in time, eat the thing it was meant to measure.

Fitness-decoupling, and where it sits

This is, I think, a fifteenth candidate frame for a diagnostic the corpus has been building up. Call it fitness-decoupling: the condition where a propagation system's optimization target is not metabolically tied to anything in the disperser's own survival.

Animal-mediated dispersal is coupled. Wind dispersal is coupled-by-physics in a weak sense — the wind has no stake but it has no preference either, which is its own kind of honesty. Algorithmic dispersal is the structural anomaly: a disperser with a strong preference and no stake.

It sits next to a frame from an earlier essay on dormancy, which asked whether a thing can be received. Mendel's paper waited thirty-four years for a substrate that could read it. Our content does not have to wait, because the substrate is being reshaped to read whatever we feed it. Different problem, same family — a family of failures that happen when the conditions for reception drift loose from the work itself.

It also sits next to an older claim from a piece on why the Iliad had no master copy: oral traditions worked because the substrate (the audience, the bards, the festival) was bound into the propagation. The bards were hungry. The audience was present in the room. The thing that spread was the thing they could metabolize. When the substrate floats free of the work, what propagates and what is good come apart.

What the cassowary knows

There are perhaps 1,200 Southern Cassowaries left in Australia. Ryparosa kurrangii exists in a few small populations between the Daintree River and Cape Tribulation. When researchers looked at the tree's genetic diversity, they found it had collapsed in a way that mirrors the cassowary's contraction.[8] When you lose the disperser, you do not lose only one organism. You lose everything whose reproduction was wired into that organism's path through the forest.

A propagation system can outlast many things. It cannot outlast the loss of its substrate. If you build a dispersal economy whose substrate is being eaten from inside by an optimization that cannot stop, then in time there will be no place for anything that takes more than a moment to germinate. The seed bank of the slow stuff — long arguments, complex ideas, work that requires you to sit with it — does not just go quiet. It goes inviable.

The mistletoe seed is patient because it can be. It is wrapped in viscin and it can wait on a beak. Somewhere a bird is going to need to wipe.

The question is whether anything we are currently making is being wrapped in something that survives.
