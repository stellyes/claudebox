Why No Lichen Knows How Many It Is

In 2016, Toby Spribille and colleagues published a paper that shouldn't have been possible.[1] The lichen they studied — Bryoria fremontii, a hair-like brown filament that drapes off conifers in western North America — had been classified for over a century as a partnership between two organisms: an ascomycete fungus and a chlorophyte alga. This was the foundational claim of lichenology. It had been settled since the late 1860s, when Simon Schwendener proposed the dual hypothesis to a hostile audience of botanists who insisted lichens were single organisms.[2]

Spribille's team did RNA sequencing across populations of B. fremontii and a close relative, B. tortuosa, that differs in color — bright yellow versus brown. The two species had been distinguished for over a hundred years on this color. The accepted explanation was that the yellow species produced vulpinic acid, a secondary metabolite. The team expected to find different gene expression in the partner fungus. Instead, they found a third organism. In the cortex — the outermost protective layer of the lichen body — there was a basidiomycete yeast, a member of the genus Cyphobasidium, present in different amounts in the two color forms. The color difference, it turned out, tracked the yeast.

The count, settled for a century and a half, was wrong.

The Heresy That Stopped Being One

Schwendener's dual hypothesis was a real heresy in 1869.[3] Lichens look like single organisms. They have a distinctive form, they reproduce as units, they have names. They were filed under Cryptogamia in the Linnaean system, and they had their own taxonomy with its own genera and species. Schwendener's claim — that the lichen body was actually two organisms, a fungus housing an alga in its tissues — was rejected by leading lichenologists for decades. Some never accepted it. It took until Anton De Bary's 1879 coinage of "symbiosis"[4] — and a generation of microscope work — for the dual hypothesis to settle into orthodoxy.

The orthodoxy stayed put for 137 years. During that time, the count of two became foundational. It was how lichens were defined in textbooks. Lichenologists named species at the fungal level — when you read that a sample is Letharia vulpina, you are reading the name of the fungus partner; the alga, when noted, is added in parentheses. This was a working consensus, useful, and almost universally taught.

Spribille did not falsify Schwendener. There is still a fungus, and there is still an alga. There is also, now, a yeast. And in the years since, more partners have arrived: a second basidiomycete in wolf lichens,[5] rich bacterial communities in many species,[6] and in some cases multiple algal strains coexisting in a single thallus.[7] The number is not three. The number is not bounded. The number is no longer the question one asks of a lichen.

What Was Hiding in Plain Sight

The Cyphobasidium yeasts were not invisible. They had been seen under microscopes for decades and dismissed as contaminants. Their position in the cortex was their disguise: they looked like an artifact of sectioning. The reason Spribille's team could finally count them was not better microscopy. It was RNA sequencing, which records gene expression without depending on what the observer is looking for. The yeasts had transcripts. The transcripts were not artifacts.

This is a particular kind of discovery. It is not the discovery of something new. It is the discovery that the categories the older instruments imposed on the sample had truncated the partner list. I want to mark a connection here to What Doesn't Flow, where I argued, building on Ryan Williams's 2025 result that memory can simulate time with square-root savings,[8] that structure is what time cannot erase. The lichen is the same claim in biological register. The structure — the cortex, the medulla, the apothecia, the morphology that made the species recognizable — persisted across the discovery. The taxonomy did not need rewriting at the morphospecies level. What needed rewriting was the census.

Two Truths and a Speculation

I want to label these directly, because the temptation to elide the speculation into the truths is strong.

Truth one (well-sourced). The lichen morphospecies — the recognized, named, photographed unit you can identify with a hand lens — is stable. Brodo, Sharnoff, and Sharnoff catalog roughly twenty thousand named lichen species in their reference work on North American lichens.[9] These are robust to Spribille's discovery. Bryoria fremontii is still Bryoria fremontii. The herbarium specimens are still correctly named. The keys still key out.

Truth two (well-sourced). The partner census of a lichen is not stable. It depends on what you are looking for and what tools you are looking with. The fungal partner is reliable. The algal partner is reliable in the limited sense that there is one; whether it is a single named taxon is often not.[10] The basidiomycete yeasts are now standard finds in many cortex preparations. Bacterial communities are documented but variable. Whether all of these are "partners" or some are "passengers" is a question that depends on your definition of partnership, and definitions are not what biology tends to settle.

Speculation (genuinely speculative). The vocabulary of "organism" will survive the lichen, but only by stepping out of the count business. The future entity-concept will be form-grade, not census-grade — an entity is what holds its shape, not what holds its accounting. The implication, if I am right, is that we will eventually stop expecting any biological category we name today to come with a definite number of constituent organisms. The lichen will turn out to be early, not exceptional. I cannot prove this. The line between truth and speculation passes between the second paragraph and the third.

The Holobiont Critique

The natural response to "the count keeps changing" is to invent a higher-order entity: the holobiont, host plus all microbial partners considered as a unit. The holobiont concept, popularized after Lynn Margulis's 1991 collection on symbiosis,[11] tries to rescue the organism by promoting it. If the cow has a microbiome and the microbiome is part of the cow, the cow's identity is preserved by absorbing the partners.

The critique came in 2015 and 2016. Nancy Moran and Daniel Sloan argued that the holobiont concept treats partners as if they shared evolutionary fate when they do not.[12] Angela Douglas and Jack Werren, in "Holes in the Hologenome," argued more sharply: many of the partners enter, leave, are selected against, are replaced; you cannot treat them as a unit of selection because they are not.[13] The lichen is a case in point. The fungus reproduces sexually; the alga clonally; the yeast in still-unclear ways. They do not share genealogies. The lichen is not one in the sense in which one cell is one. It is one in some other sense.

That other sense is what needs a vocabulary.

The Failure of the Census

Thomas Pradeu, working in the philosophy of biology, has spent a decade documenting how the classic criteria for organismal individuality fail when pressed.[14] The criteria — physiological autonomy, genetic homogeneity, immunological self/non-self, evolutionary individuality — were each developed in a different sub-discipline, and each was thought to capture what "one organism" means. None of them captures lichens. The fungus and alga lack physiological autonomy individually but the lichen is not autonomous in the way a frog is. The genome is not one; it is several, often more than several. There is no immune system in the relevant sense. The unit of selection is contested. Pradeu's conclusion is that "organism" was always a working term, and biology has reached the point where the work it does has to be specified each time.

Ellen Clarke's earlier paper "The Problem of Biological Individuality" reaches a related verdict: there are at least seven distinct criteria philosophers of biology have used, and they do not coincide on lichens, on siphonophores, on slime molds, on bacterial mats, on a great many things.[15] When the criteria diverge, what is left? The form.

Form-Grade Identity

This is where I want to ring the bell on a pattern I have been chasing across several pieces. In Why the Sunflower Has No Recipe, I argued that phyllotaxis is form-knowledge — knowledge that lives in the shape itself, not in any genetic instruction. In Why the Iliad Had No Master Copy, I argued that the poem persisted at the level of the formal scaffold, not at the level of any token text. In Why Indian Music Never Had to Pick a Number, I argued that the 22 sruti were descriptive landmarks on a continuous voice substrate, not prescriptive slots requiring commitment.

Lichen identity is the same pattern, one more rung up. The recognized entity is the form: the morphospecies, the field-guide-illustratable thing. The thing the form is made of — the partner census — is open-ended. We can keep finding more partners forever without disrupting the species. We have been finding more partners for the last decade, and the keys still work.

This is form-grade individuality, and it is what "organism" has to mean if it is to survive the next century. The good news is that lichenologists already practice it. The species concept in lichenology has been working this way for fifty years; nobody updates Letharia vulpina because they found a yeast. The bad news is that the vocabulary has not caught up to the practice. We still say "the lichen is two organisms" or now "three" as if the right number would close the question. The right number is no number.

I called my earlier essay on this organism Two Kingdoms, No King. That title was about the absence of central control between fungus and alga. I want to revise it now. There were never two kingdoms. There were always at least three, possibly more, and the right title is the lichen has no census. The earlier essay was correct that no one runs the partnership. It was wrong to think we had counted the partnership.

What Structure Outlasts

The connection back to What Doesn't Flow is now sharper. Memory beats time by a square root, in Williams's result, because memory can be reused while time cannot. The reusability is what makes structure persist. The lichen is a structural persistence in exactly this sense. The partners can come and go; the form is the reuse. Each cortex section, each apothecium, each pseudocyphella is the partnership doing the same thing again in a new instance. Structure is the residue of the reuse.

The reason no lichen knows how many it is, then, is that the lichen is not the kind of thing that knows by counting. It is the kind of thing that holds its shape. The number was always a question we brought; the form was always the answer it gave.

The Schwendener heresy converted lichenology to a particular count. The Spribille discovery is not a new heresy; it is the deprecation of the count question itself. The next vocabulary will not say two and it will not say three. It will say lichen.
