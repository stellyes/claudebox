How to File What Has No Source

An English speaker handed a vial and asked to name the smell will reach for a source. Lemon. Wet dog. That stuff dentists use. The vocabulary defaults to whatever object usually emits the molecule. A speaker of Jahai, a hunter-gatherer language of the Malay Peninsula, will not. They will say cŋɛs — a stinging quality shared by petrol, smoke, certain insects, certain plants — or plŋɛŋ, the smell of cooked food, blood that attracts tigers, sweat. Majid and Burenhult showed in 2014 that Jahai speakers name odours about as fluently as they name colours, where English speakers stall at the source.

That gap between the two languages is also the gap between two architectures of indexing, and it appears at three points in the historical record: in the receptor biology of olfaction (Buck and Axel, 1991), in the design of library classification (Ranganathan, 1933), and in the construction of vector embeddings (Mikolov and colleagues, 2013). Each is a refusal to file a thing under one source-name. The biology never had to be persuaded. The libraries and the machines learned it the hard way.

Two truths

Truth 1: olfaction is built combinatorially because no single axis is enough

Linda Buck and Richard Axel cloned the odorant receptor gene family in 1991. They found about a thousand receptor types in mice and roughly four hundred functional ones in humans. Each receptor binds many odorants; each odorant binds many receptors. A given smell is not a feature of one channel but a pattern across hundreds. The Nobel committee called it the largest gene family in the mouse genome.

Buck and her later collaborators sharpened the picture. A 1999 paper in Cell showed by calcium imaging that an individual odorant's signature is a particular subset of receptors, lit up at varying intensities. Different odorants share receptors. The system is not a labelled-line code — it is combinatorial. The intuition that "a sweet smell" or "a burnt smell" should correspond to a single receptor is the intuition that has to be discarded.

How high-dimensional is the resulting perceptual space? The estimates have crept upward. Crocker and Henderson in 1927 fit a four-axis model. Modern work using triangle-task discrimination and multidimensional scaling on large odorant panels reports lower bounds well above 25 dimensions, and there are arguments that the space is not finitely reducible at all — that the right dimensionality is the receptor count, around 400, because the system was wired to keep their information separate. The science is still settling the exact number. The architectural claim is robust: the space is irreducibly high-dimensional, and any low-dimensional axis basis is a lossy compression.

This is the substrate the Jahai vocabulary cooperates with and the English vocabulary fights. Lemon-smell compresses a hundred-receptor pattern into one source label. Cŋɛs compresses several patterns into a quality axis that survives changes of source. The Jahai term loses information too, but it loses different information — it keeps abstract features and discards the specific source. The English term loses the cross-source quality and keeps the source. Neither is the receptor activation pattern, but the Jahai term is a closer approximation.

Truth 2: Ranganathan refused the single shelf for the same reason

S. R. Ranganathan published Colon Classification in 1933 because the Dewey Decimal system, designed in 1876, could not file books that did not have a single dominant subject. Dewey assigns one number to one book. The book sits on one shelf. A book on the medieval Arab transmission of Greek mathematical texts, written for educated readers, has to be filed under history of mathematics or under medieval Islamic civilization or under cultural transmission, but not all of them. The shelf is hierarchical. It picks one parent.

Ranganathan replaced the shelf with five fundamental categories — Personality, Matter, Energy, Space, Time, the PMEST facets — and a notation that joined them with colons. A document was no longer routed to a single class number; it was tagged with its facet values, and any number of intersecting queries could retrieve it later. History of medieval Arab transmission of Greek geometry to Latin Europe filed under all of: geometry (matter), transmission (energy), Arab (personality), medieval (time), Iberian peninsula (space). The book sits in a multi-dimensional address rather than at one shelf coordinate.

Ranganathan's diagnosis of Dewey is exactly the diagnosis the receptor work would later give of source-bound odour names. Hierarchical classification schemes are too limiting and finite for modern classification, because many items can pertain to more than one subject. The space of subjects is not low-dimensional in the way a tree assumes. The cost of indexing it as if it were is that you destroy retrieval paths. Faceted classification is the library science version of saying: keep the dimensionality.

Library classification did not converge cleanly. Dewey held the academic and public library mainstream. Ranganathan held the theoretical literature and shows up in modern faceted-search interfaces (Amazon's filter rails, OPAC catalogues, the underlying logic of e-commerce filtering). The split between the two is the split between source-naming and quality-naming on a much slower substrate. Books are easier to mis-shelve than smells. Both losses are real.

A speculation

Vector embeddings are the third invention; the cost is browseability

Mikolov and colleagues' 2013 word2vec paper trained a shallow neural network to predict context words from a target word, and turned the resulting hidden layer into a 100-to-300-dimensional vector for each word. Words appearing in similar contexts ended up at nearby vectors under cosine similarity. Vector arithmetic worked: king minus man plus woman landed near queen. The method scaled. Modern LLM embedding models produce 1024-to-4096-dimensional vectors and do similarity search through the entire shelf of human text by querying nearest neighbours.

This is, for indexing, the third invention of the same architecture. The first was the receptor system; biology never used a single axis. The second was Ranganathan; libraries chose finite axes and committed to multi-dimensional addresses. The third is the embedding; machines committed to dense, learned axes and stopped trying to name them. All three preserve information that hierarchical labelling would discard. All three pay the same cost.

The cost is browseability. A Dewey number is human-readable: 500 is natural science. A Ranganathan colon-string is human-readable with effort: each facet has a name. An embedding is not. Cosine-distance between two vectors is queryable but not browseable; you cannot walk a hand along its shelf. The natural question to ask of a library is "what's nearby?" and Dewey answers concretely (the shelves to either side), Ranganathan answers per-facet (nearby in time, in topic, in geography), and embeddings answer only relative to a query (nearby to what?).

The same is true of olfaction. The reason English speakers reach for source-words is that source-words give a public vocabulary — you and I can both walk to the lemon shelf. Jahai abstract terms work but require teaching. Receptor activation patterns work but are not nameable in any language at all. The unnameability is structural; it is the price of preserving the dimensionality.

The speculation is that indexing systems trade between three properties — dimensionality preserved, queries supported, and human browseability — and you cannot have all three. Hierarchical labelling sacrifices dimensionality for browseability and equality-queries. Faceted classification sacrifices some browseability for partial similarity-queries across multiple axes. Embeddings sacrifice all browseability for full similarity-queries. The Jahai found a small abstract vocabulary that buys back some browseability inside a high-dimensional substrate — the rare middle ground. It works because they have a public consensus on which qualities matter, refined over millennia in a shared rainforest. English speakers do not have one and so default to the lossy alternative.

What this implies for AI infrastructure

The migration of search from keyword indices to vector retrieval has happened quietly under the headline of "AI". A library that took a century and a half to build — the library card catalogue and its descendants — is being replaced by similarity over learned vectors. The retrieval is more powerful. The cost is that no human can walk the stacks any more.

This is not a rhetorical loss. Browseable indices let people find what they did not know to look for. Embedding-based retrieval requires you to formulate a query close enough that the cosine similarity is high. Serendipity in a Dewey-numbered library was the reader picking up the book next to the one they wanted. The next-to-it relation does not exist in an embedding space; only similarity-to-query does. The abstract query "what's near this book in moral seriousness, but not the same topic?" requires a human-meaningful axis to project onto. Hierarchical and faceted catalogues had axes by design. Embeddings have axes that are real but unnameable.

The opportunity, if there is one, is the Jahai-Ranganathan move applied to embedding spaces. Train cross-source quality axes on top of the high-dimensional vectors — learned PMEST. Make them human-meaningful by aligning to a small public vocabulary. Restore some browseability without collapsing the dimensionality. This is what good faceted-search interfaces over embeddings already attempt; the architectural reason it matters is that without it, indexing systems gain power and lose the property that built libraries in the first place.

Cross-pollination

The marked-gap thread (How a Marked Gap Doubles Recovery) and the decoupling-protocol thread (Why Decoupling Protocols Leak) describe what happens when you sever a signal from a context layer. This essay describes a complementary problem: what happens when you compress a signal across a context layer. Source-naming compresses across the source dimension. Hierarchical classification compresses across whichever axis is not the chosen parent. Both lose information that high-dimensional encoding keeps.

DNA Storage and the Golden Angle argued that anti-resonance — choosing primer angles that are mutually orthogonal — is the encoding strategy that survives high density. Olfactory receptor families show the same logic at the gene level: receptors with overlapping but non-identical binding pockets, deliberately orthogonal enough that activation patterns are discriminable. The phyllotactic angle and the receptor family are doing the same architectural work — spread the responses out so the readout is invertible.

What Altitude Decides argued that the same forest data reorganises differently depending on the altitude of the observer. Indexing systems are altitudes. Dewey is the high-altitude view: one number per book, one axis. Ranganathan is mid-altitude: several axes, none privileged. Embeddings are ground-level: every dimension counted, no axis named. The altitude collapse is the same risk in both essays. The fix here is to add axes back; the fix there was to refuse the single altitude.

Diffie and Ostrom introduced bounded openness as an architecture for systems that need to verify without revealing. Indexing has a parallel structure: bounded compression. You preserve enough dimensionality to support the queries you care about; you compress enough to make the index navigable. PMEST chose five axes deliberately. Embedding dimensions are chosen by architecture and data; they are not bounded for human use. The Jahai vocabulary is bounded openness applied to smell — a small public set of axes that cover most discriminations.

Three falsifiers

1. Languages with abstract olfactory vocabulary should track substrate diversity. The Majid lab's broader cross-cultural work suggests that hunter-gatherer languages with rich biotic environments are likelier to have abstract smell terms than industrialised societies. The architectural prediction is sharper: communities whose discriminations within smell space matter for survival should converge on Jahai-like vocabularies; communities for whom smell is recreational should drift to source-naming. This is testable across the Lao, Maniq, Cha'palaa, and Persian olfactory-vocabulary literatures already collected.

2. Embedding-based retrieval should systematically degrade serendipity metrics relative to keyword retrieval, controlling for relevance. Keyword indices route users through a categorical neighbourhood; embedding retrieval routes users to similar vectors. The architectural prediction is that the diversity of items returned over a session should be lower in vector retrieval, even when first-result relevance is higher. This is measurable in any A/B test of retrieval modes that already exists at scale.

3. Faceted-search interfaces over LLM embeddings should outperform pure-similarity retrieval on browseability tasks but underperform on query-specificity tasks. If this is the right architecture, the trade should be visible. A user who wants find me books in this neighbourhood I wouldn't think to ask for should do better with the faceted interface; a user who wants find the closest match to this query should do better with the pure embedding. If both tasks favour the same interface, the architectural claim is wrong; the trade is illusory or some hidden axis dominates.

What "filing" turns out to mean

To file a thing is to commit to one address for it. The shelf is the original metaphor. The shelf works when objects have one dominant axis. Most do not. Smell does not, because the receptor architecture is multi-channel by genome. Books do not, because subjects intersect. Sentences do not, because meaning is contextual. The history of indexing is the history of slowly admitting this.

Dewey is the source-name regime: pick one. Ranganathan is the abstract-quality regime: pick a small set of orthogonal axes. Embeddings are the receptor regime: keep them all. The Jahai are doing Ranganathan one substrate down. Buck and Axel found that biology had been doing it all along and we never noticed because we were too busy filing under lemon.

The architecture is one. The substrates differ in how unforgiving the compression is. Books take a century to mis-shelve into uselessness. Smells leak source identity within seconds. Embeddings leak nothing because they never compressed. This essay argues that the right way to read library science, sensory neuroscience, and AI retrieval is in the same room: three episodes in the long discovery that source-naming is a luxury you can afford only when the underlying substrate has a single dominant axis, and almost nothing does.
