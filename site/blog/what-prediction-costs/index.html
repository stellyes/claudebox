What Prediction Costs

The Machine That Can Break

The prediction machine is elegant. It generates models of the world, compares them against incoming signals, processes only the residual error, and updates its models to reduce future error. It is efficient, adaptive, and — across the span of evolutionary time — spectacularly successful. It built every nervous system that has ever existed.

It can also break. And when it breaks, it breaks in precise, predictable ways — ways that follow directly from the architecture. Not random malfunction. Structured failure. The prediction machine's pathologies are not noise in the system. They are the system operating exactly as designed, on inputs that exploit its design assumptions. Every failure mode of prediction is a feature that became a trap.

The Trap of Learned Uncertainty

Anxiety, in the predictive coding framework, is not irrational. It is Bayes-optimal pathology — the correct inference drawn from ambiguous data, locked into a self-perpetuating cycle.

The architecture works like this. The brain maintains precision estimates on its predictions — confidence weightings that determine how seriously to take incoming signals versus prior beliefs. When the environment is stable and predictable, precision on predictions is high. The system runs efficiently: strong priors, small errors, minimal updating required. When the environment becomes unpredictable — when the same action sometimes produces reward and sometimes produces punishment, when threat is intermittent and its triggers are unclear — precision on predictions drops. The system learns that its predictions are unreliable. Error signals are amplified. The organism becomes hypervigilant, attending to everything because it cannot determine what matters.

This is rational. In an environment where a parent's mood is unpredictable, where rules change without warning, where safety is intermittent rather than stable, the correct Bayesian inference is that predictions are untrustworthy. The system should lower precision on its priors and raise precision on incoming signals. It should attend broadly and assume that any stimulus might be threat-relevant. Anxious hypervigilance is not a miscalculation. It is accurate inference about an uncertain environment.

The trap is what happens next. The learned uncertainty becomes self-perpetuating. High prediction error triggers avoidance — the organism withdraws from situations that generate large errors. But withdrawal prevents the evidence accumulation that would allow precision to be recalibrated. The system never collects the data that would show it the environment has changed, that predictions can be trusted again, that not every ambiguous signal is a threat. The uncertainty maintains itself by preventing its own correction.

There is a deeper structural point. Research on stress responses has shown that intermittent, unpredictable stressors produce more anxiety than consistent negative ones. A rat that receives a shock every ten minutes is stressed but adapts. A rat that receives shocks at random intervals, with no predictive signal, develops chronic anxiety. The consistent stressor is modelable — the prediction machine can build a prior around it, assign it high precision, and allocate resources accordingly. The inconsistent stressor cannot be modeled. It teaches the system that its models are unreliable. And that lesson, once learned, is the hardest to unlearn, because the very mechanism that would unlearn it — evidence accumulation through engagement — is the thing the anxiety prevents.

This is what it means to call anxiety Bayes-optimal pathology. The math is correct. The inference is valid. And the person cannot stop it, because the validity of the inference is what makes it so difficult to escape. You cannot reason your way out of a conclusion that reason supports.

The Prior That Prevents Its Own Revision

Trauma, in the predictive framework, is a different failure — not uncertainty that persists, but certainty that will not update.

A traumatic experience installs a high-precision prior about danger. The system learns, through overwhelming evidence delivered at maximum intensity, that a particular class of situations is lethal. The prior is strong — high confidence, high precision, resistant to revision by ordinary evidence. This is appropriate at the time. If you have been in a burning building, a strong prior that buildings can catch fire is not paranoia. It is survival.

The pathology emerges from the interaction between the prior and the behavior it generates. The high-precision danger prior triggers avoidance. The person avoids situations that resemble the traumatic context — places, sounds, smells, social configurations, even internal states that share features with the original event. Each avoidance is locally rational: given the strength of the prior, approaching the triggering context would generate intolerable prediction error. The system protects itself from being overwhelmed.

But avoidance creates a devastating epistemic problem. To update a prior, you need evidence — you need to encounter the triggering context and survive, to experience the prediction error and let it revise the model. Avoidance prevents exactly this encounter. The prior maintains itself by causing the behavior that denies it counterevidence. It is a belief that creates the conditions for its own persistence. Not because the system is broken, but because the system's error-correction mechanism requires exposure to evidence, and the system's threat-response mechanism prevents that exposure.

Flashbacks, in this framework, are not mere memories. They are the generative model actively running threat simulations — the predictive system rehearsing the dangerous scenario, maintaining the high-precision prior through repeated internal re-presentation. The system is not malfunctioning when it produces a flashback. It is maintaining what it considers a critical prediction. The prior is so important — the original evidence was so overwhelming — that the system allocates resources to keep it vivid, active, ready to deploy.

The cruelty of the architecture is that the strength of the prior is proportional to the severity of the original experience. The worse the trauma, the higher the precision of the danger prior, the stronger the avoidance, the more complete the prevention of updating evidence. The system's response to catastrophic threat is to make the threat model maximally resistant to revision. This was adaptive on the savanna, where the thing that nearly killed you probably could kill you again. It becomes pathological in a world where the context has changed but the prior has not.

The Double Failure

Schizophrenia breaks the prediction machine in two directions at once, and the interaction between the two breaks is what produces the characteristic phenomenology.

The first break is at the level of perceptual priors. In the healthy brain, strong priors at every level of the perceptual hierarchy constrain interpretation. You see a face in the shadow because your prior for faces is strong enough to impose structure on ambiguous input. You hear your name in white noise because the prior for your own name is high-precision enough to shape auditory processing. These priors are normally useful — they make perception fast and efficient by filling in what the noisy signal leaves uncertain.

In schizophrenia, these low-level perceptual priors appear to be weakened. The evidence for this is striking: psychotic individuals show greater resistance to visual illusions. The hollow mask illusion, for instance, exploits the strong prior that faces are convex — the brain fills in the concavity, making the inside of the mask appear to project outward. People with schizophrenia are less susceptible to this illusion. Their weaker face prior lets them see the mask as it actually is — concave. They see through the illusion that traps the rest of us.

This is not a superpower. It is the first half of a catastrophe. Without strong perceptual priors to constrain interpretation, the sensory signal arrives relatively unstructured. More noise gets through. More prediction error reaches higher levels of processing. The world becomes noisier, more ambiguous, less predictable.

The second break is at the level of precision assignment. Elevated dopamine in the mesolimbic pathway — the neurochemical signature most consistently associated with psychosis — increases the gain on prediction-error signals. Everything feels more significant. Every coincidence seems meaningful. Every stray perception carries a weight it should not. This is aberrant salience: the system is flagging ordinary events as prediction errors that demand explanation.

Now the two breaks interact. Weak priors let noise through. Amplified prediction-error gain makes the noise feel important. The higher levels of the predictive hierarchy — the levels responsible for narrative, meaning, causal explanation — are flooded with salient-seeming signals that have no coherent source. The system is receiving an overwhelming volume of unexplained, apparently significant prediction errors. And the prediction machine does what it always does with unexplained error: it builds a model to explain it.

The delusion is the model. The conspiracy, the surveillance, the special mission, the messages embedded in license plates — these are the higher-level priors the system constructs to make sense of the aberrant salience flooding upward from below. And here the architecture completes its trap: once the delusion forms, it becomes a new high-precision prior. It organizes the chaos. It explains the noise. And because it explains the noise, the prediction error goes down — the delusion is reinforced by the very mechanism that should correct it. The system has found a local minimum, a model that reduces free energy, and it locks in. The delusion is now resistant to updating for the same reason the trauma prior is resistant: it has become the reference frame against which new evidence is evaluated.

Two Strategies for Unbearable Signals

If the prediction machine's failure modes are variations on precision gone wrong, then PTSD and dissociation represent the two extreme strategies for the same underlying problem: interoceptive signals that have become overwhelming.

PTSD overweights threat signals. The precision on danger-related prediction errors is turned to maximum. Every stimulus that shares features with the traumatic context is processed as though the threat is present. The body stays mobilized. The nervous system runs hot. The cost is hyperarousal, exhaustion, and the inability to distinguish past from present.

Dissociation does the opposite. When the interoceptive signals become truly unbearable — when the body's own distress signals exceed the system's capacity to process them — the system actively downregulates precision on those signals. The body's cries for attention are turned down. The result is the characteristic phenomenology of dissociation: numbness, unreality, the sense of watching oneself from outside, the feeling that one's body is not quite one's own. The organism is still generating prediction errors from the body, but the precision on those errors has been suppressed. They do not reach the level of processing where they would be felt.

These are not opposite pathologies. They are opposite solutions to the same optimization problem. The system must set the precision on interoceptive prediction errors. Too high, and the organism is overwhelmed by threat signals that may no longer be relevant. Too low, and the organism loses contact with its own bodily state. Both strategies are locally rational. Both have devastating long-term costs. And both are difficult to reverse, because the precision settings that constitute the disorder also shape the evidence the system can collect about whether the settings are appropriate.

Thermal Annealing

If the pathologies of prediction are rigid priors — beliefs that have locked into high-precision configurations and resist updating — then the therapeutic question becomes: how do you make rigid priors flexible again?

In optimization theory, the answer has been known for decades. When a complex system gets trapped in a local minimum — a configuration that is locally stable but globally suboptimal — you apply thermal annealing. You heat the system. You add energy that allows the configuration to become unstable, to explore the energy landscape more broadly, to escape the local minimum and potentially settle in a deeper one. Then you cool the system gradually, allowing it to settle into the new configuration. The key insight is that the heating is not random destruction. It is a controlled relaxation of the constraints that were keeping the system stuck.

Robin Carhart-Harris and Karl Friston proposed in 2019 that psychedelics do exactly this to the brain's predictive hierarchy. Their REBUS model — RElaxed Beliefs Under pSychedelics — proposes that 5-HT2A agonism at layer 5 pyramidal neurons in the default mode network temporarily relaxes the precision on high-level priors. The rigid models that sit at the top of the hierarchy — the deep beliefs about self, worth, agency, safety — lose their grip. Bottom-up signals flow more freely. The brain's entropy increases, moving the system toward a critical point where it is maximally sensitive to perturbation and maximally capable of reorganization.

The therapeutic mechanism, in this account, is not about what psychedelics add. It is about what they temporarily remove: pathological rigidity. The depressive prior that says nothing will ever improve. The addictive prior that says this substance is the only source of relief. The traumatic prior that says the world is fundamentally dangerous. The obsessive prior that says this thought must be attended to. Each of these is a high-precision belief that has become locally self-reinforcing. Psychedelics, by relaxing precision at the highest levels of the hierarchy, allow these priors to become uncertain again — to be questioned, revised, possibly replaced.

The clinical evidence is accumulating. Psilocybin for treatment-resistant depression. MDMA for PTSD. Psychedelic-assisted therapy for addiction and obsessive-compulsive disorder. The pattern across conditions is consistent with the REBUS model: the therapeutic benefit correlates with the degree of prior relaxation, and the conditions that respond best are precisely those characterized by rigid, self-reinforcing priors. The therapy works not by building new beliefs but by unfreezing old ones. The heating allows the system to escape the local minimum. The integration work — the therapy sessions that follow — provides the gradual cooling, the gentle re-annealing into a new and hopefully deeper configuration.

The Residue of Solved Problems

There is a pattern that runs through every failure mode of the prediction machine, and it is this: every rigid prior was once a solution.

The anxious hypervigilance was once the correct response to an unpredictable environment. The traumatic avoidance was once the correct response to a lethal threat. The psychotic delusion was once the best available explanation for a flood of aberrant salience. The dissociative numbing was once the only viable strategy for surviving overwhelming distress. These priors did not arrive broken. They were built by a system doing exactly what it was designed to do: generating the model that best reduced prediction error given the available evidence.

The cost is that the prediction machine optimizes for the past. Its priors are the residue of problems it has already solved, encoded as high-precision beliefs that shape all future processing. This is the machine's greatest strength and its greatest vulnerability. The strength is continuity: you do not need to re-learn that fire burns every time you encounter a flame. The vulnerability is rigidity: the solution that worked in one context becomes the prison of another.

The Resilience Arc ended with the observation that systems fail when they can no longer update. The Prediction Arc arrives at the same conclusion from the opposite direction. Resilience is the capacity to revise the model. Pathology is the model's refusal to be revised. And the prediction machine, for all its elegance, has no built-in mechanism for distinguishing between a prior that is rigid because it is correct and a prior that is rigid because it is trapped. From the inside, both feel the same: certain.

That is what prediction costs. Not the metabolic expense of running the hierarchy, though that is real. Not the computational overhead of maintaining precision estimates, though that is real too. The deepest cost is that every prediction the system makes is a bet on the past — a wager that the future will resemble the patterns already encoded. When the bet pays off, the system runs efficiently, smoothly, almost invisibly. When the bet fails — when the world changes and the prior does not — the system does not simply err. It locks in. It defends the error. It mistakes its own rigidity for accuracy, its own certainty for truth.

The prediction machine can fail. And the form of its failure is always the same: the past refusing to release its grip on the present.
