How Diffie and Ostrom Solved the Same Problem

In 1976, Whitfield Diffie and Martin Hellman published a paper titled New Directions in Cryptography. It described a way for two strangers, with no prior contact, to agree on a shared secret over a channel anyone could read. For thousands of years before that, secure communication had required something the parties already had in common — a shared key, a courier they both trusted, a meeting in a back room. After 1976, none of that was strictly necessary. Strangers could whisper through a megaphone.

In 1990, Elinor Ostrom published Governing the Commons. It distilled eight design principles from institutions that had managed shared resources for centuries — Swiss alpine pastures, Japanese satoyama woodlands, Spanish huertas, Filipino zanjeras. For decades, mainstream economics had taught that any commons must be either privatized, regulated by the state, or destroyed by free-riders. Ostrom showed thousands of working counterexamples and gave the recipe.

These two breakthroughs landed fourteen years apart in fields that share no journals, no conferences, no obvious vocabulary. One was about computers and number theory. The other was about goats and irrigation ditches. But they answered the same question, and the answers have the same shape.

The question is: how does trust scale beyond intimacy?

Truth 1: Public-key cryptography existed before 1976

Diffie and Hellman's paper is conventionally treated as the founding document of modern public-key cryptography, but the idea was nearly seven years old when they published it. In 1969, James Ellis at GCHQ — Britain's signals intelligence agency — wrote an internal report titled The Possibility of Secure Non-Secret Digital Encryption. Ellis had asked the right question: was the centuries-old assumption — that secure communication required pre-shared secrets — actually a theorem, or just a habit?

Ellis couldn't find a working scheme. In 1973, a young mathematician named Clifford Cocks joined GCHQ, was handed Ellis's idea, and produced what is now called the RSA algorithm in roughly half an hour. Rivest, Shamir, and Adleman would independently rediscover it four years later, with no knowledge of Cocks's work, and the public version would carry their names. In 1974, Malcolm Williamson, also at GCHQ, found what we now call Diffie-Hellman key exchange while trying to find a flaw in Cocks's scheme.

GCHQ classified all of this. The public history of cryptography began in 1976 because that was when the work became legible — when it could circulate. Cocks's papers were declassified on 18 December 1997, twenty-four years after the work was done. The truth is not that public-key cryptography was invented in 1976. It is that the breakthrough is not the math. The breakthrough is the moment a primitive can be used in public.

Truth 2: Ostrom's eight design principles

Garrett Hardin's 1968 essay The Tragedy of the Commons became one of the most-cited papers in the social sciences. Its argument was simple: rational individuals exploiting a shared resource will deplete it; the only solutions are privatization or external coercion. The paper is still routinely taught in introductory economics. It is also empirically wrong about most actual commons that have existed.

Ostrom's Governing the Commons documents the rebuttal. After fieldwork in dozens of long-lived institutions, she identified eight design principles common to commons that survived for generations:

  1. Clearly defined boundaries — both around the resource and around the set of people entitled to use it.
  2. Local rules adapted to local conditions — not imposed from outside.
  3. Collective-choice arrangements — those affected by the rules can participate in modifying them.
  4. Monitoring — by participants themselves or accountable to them.
  5. Graduated sanctions — first-time violations get warnings; repeated violations escalate.
  6. Conflict-resolution mechanisms — accessible, low-cost, locally available.
  7. Minimal recognition of rights to organize — the higher authority does not crush the local institution.
  8. Nested enterprises — large commons are built from smaller commons, layered.

These are not rules of thumb. They are conditions, all eight of which Ostrom found in commons that lasted; failures she studied tended to be missing one or more. The 2009 Nobel committee called the framework an empirical correction to a theoretical mistake. It was also a cryptographic specification, though no one noticed at the time.

The shared problem

Before 1976, secure communication required intimacy: you had to have already met. The pre-shared key represented your prior relationship. The world before public-key cryptography was a world where strangers could not transact securely without an intermediary they both trusted — a king's seal, a letter of credit from a known bank, a bishop's blessing.

Before Ostrom, governance theory treated commons the same way. You either had a tight local community where everyone watched everyone (intimacy), or you had a state that owned the asset (intermediation), or you had private property (privatization, which is intimacy reframed: you only deal with people whose ownership the state has already vouched for). In all three cases the trust was small: it was either pre-shared or backed by an institution everyone had already agreed to trust.

Both fields had been treating intimacy as a precondition for trust at all. Both were forced to reckon with situations where intimacy didn't scale: cryptography because the telegraph and then the internet put strangers in constant contact; commons governance because real institutions had been managing trust between people who didn't know each other for centuries, and the theorists had been ignoring them.

The breakthrough — in both cases — was the same. Trust does not require intimacy. It requires asymmetric verification: a protocol in which one operation is easy and another is hard, in which a public action can be checked but the private action that produced it cannot be reversed.

In cryptography this is mathematically precise. The factoring of a 2048-bit number is hard; the multiplication that produced it is easy. Anyone can verify a signature; only the key-holder can produce one.

In Ostrom's commons it is institutionally precise. Anyone can witness whether a herder is grazing more cows than allowed; only that herder knows whether they intend to keep doing it. Anyone can verify that someone took water out of turn; only the steward of the local rotation knows the full claim history. The asymmetry is between what can be checked publicly and what must remain private to the actor.

The speculation: Ostrom's principles are a cryptographic protocol stack

Here is the speculation, labelled clearly: the eight Ostrom principles, taken together, are a specification of a cryptographic protocol — instantiated in human institutions rather than in number theory, but with the same primitives playing the same roles.

Map them one by one:

  1. Boundaries → Key generation and membership set. Who holds a key pair; who is in the system at all.
  2. Local rules → Parameter selection. The choice of curve, modulus, threshold — fitted to the local threat model.
  3. Collective choice → Multi-party computation. Participants jointly decide changes; no single party owns the rule set.
  4. Monitoring → Public verification. Anyone holding the public key can check what was signed; the private key never leaves its holder.
  5. Graduated sanctions → Slashing schedules. Small misbehavior gets a warning; repeated violations cost progressively more, with the cost asymmetric to the cost of detection.
  6. Conflict resolution → Arbitration and multi-signature recovery. Accessible mechanisms for disputes the protocol itself cannot resolve.
  7. Right to organize → Trust anchor. The certificate authority, root key, or governmental recognition that lets the local protocol exist at all.
  8. Nested enterprises → Certificate chains and federation. Local groups with their own keys participate in larger-scale groups whose membership is managed by the locals.

If the mapping is right — and it's the speculation, so I'm not claiming it's proven — it makes a sharp prediction. Cryptographic systems that try to scale governance and skip Ostrom's principles should fail in characteristic ways. Institutional commons that try to scale and skip the asymmetric primitives should fail too.

The DAO hack of 2016 is the test case. The DAO had clearly defined boundaries (token-holders), local rules (the smart contract), collective choice (proposal-and-vote), monitoring (the chain itself). What it didn't have was principle 5 (graduated sanctions — only "all-or-nothing" rollbacks were available), principle 6 (accessible conflict resolution — the only mechanism was a hard fork that permanently split Ethereum into two chains), or principle 7 (recognition by external authority — the legal status of DAO holders is still unclear today). The reentrancy bug was the immediate cause. The deeper failure was that the protocol had no way to handle the bug without the most expensive intervention available. The Ostrom principles the DAO skipped are exactly the ones whose absence forced the catastrophe.

The reverse case is older: traditional commons that lasted centuries — Torbel, satoyama, the Valencian huerta — succeeded because they had institutional analogs of every primitive on the right-hand side of that mapping, including some, like nested enterprises, that decentralized cryptography is still trying to engineer.

Why this matters for identity

A connection back to one of the more uncomfortable findings in the prior corpus. Kahan's 2012 work on identity-protective cognition showed that increased numeracy and reflection make people better at defending identity-relevant priors, not worse. Smart people don't update against their tribe; they invent more sophisticated reasons not to.

This is a problem for both cryptography and commons. Both depend on monitoring; both monitoring regimes have to handle motivated witnesses. The way they handle it is the same: separate what can be verified anonymously from what requires identified accountability. The first set of operations does not need to know who you are; they just need to know that the math or the witness count is consistent. The second set of operations cannot be done anonymously — graduated sanctions only work if you know whose account is being charged.

This split is what makes a commons different from a panopticon, and what makes public-key crypto different from a surveillance system. The architecture is bounded openness — making things publicly verifiable without making them publicly visible.

Mutual Aid, Surveillance, Cryptography traced this through one cryptographic primitive (private set intersection) inside one institution (mutual-aid networks). What Bacteria Know About the Commons saw the same architecture solved evolutionarily by quorum sensing. What Cannot Verify Itself named the impossibility result that haunts both: no system can verify itself end-to-end; some part of the verification has to come from outside.

The point this essay adds is older and broader. The architecture of bounded openness was not invented in 1976 by Diffie and Hellman, and not invented in 1990 by Ostrom. It was discovered — twice, in completely separate fields, by people who had no idea they were working on the same problem. The fact that they converged on the same primitives is the strongest argument that the primitives are not human inventions at all. They are what trust is, when trust scales beyond the people who already know each other.

Conclusion

Two breakthroughs, fourteen years apart, in fields that share nothing on the surface. Public-key cryptography solved the problem of secure communication between strangers. Ostrom's commons design principles solved the problem of cooperative resource management between strangers. Both required the same architectural move: replace shared intimacy with asymmetric verification.

The speculation is that the eight design principles are a protocol stack. The truth is that, even if the exact mapping above is incomplete, something like the speculation has to be true. Two fields cannot independently arrive at primitives that solve the same problem unless the problem itself constrains the answer.

Diffie and Hellman saw it in math. Ostrom saw it in goats and ditches. The thing they saw is the same thing.
