What the Slime Mold Cannot Solve

Put a brainless slime mold at the entrance to a maze, with food at the exit, and it will find the shortest path. Toshiyuki Nakagaki showed this in 2000, and the image is still startling: a single sheet of yellow protoplasm, no neurons anywhere in it, retracting from dead ends and thickening along the one route that connects the two oat flakes.[1] A decade later, Atsushi Tero and colleagues scaled the trick up. They laid oat flakes in the pattern of the cities around Tokyo, used bright light to stand in for mountains and water, and let Physarum polycephalum grow. In about a day the surviving tubes looked uncannily like the Tokyo rail network — comparable in efficiency, fault tolerance, and cost to a system that took human engineers decades to refine.[2]

I have praised this organism before. In What Slime Mold Reveals About Intelligence I argued that intelligence has no fixed address — that the slime mold is smart because it becomes the solution rather than computing it. In What the Trail Computes I went further and suggested that brains are merely "convergence accelerators" bolted on top of the kind of distributed, bodily computation the slime mold does for free. Both essays were love letters to embodiment. Both were missing something.

Here is the gap I left. I catalogued, at length, what the slime mold can do. I never once asked what it cannot do — not as a matter of being small or slow, but as a matter of principle. And the principle, once you state it, turns the whole celebration inside out.

It does not search. It settles.

Start with the mechanism, because the limit hides inside it. When we say the slime mold "solves" a maze, we import a picture from how we solve mazes: a mind considering candidate routes, comparing them, choosing. The slime mold does nothing of the kind. It floods the entire maze at once, filling every corridor. Then a simple physical rule takes over: tubes carrying a lot of flowing protoplasm get reinforced and widen; tubes carrying little flow starve and wither. The dead-end branches, which lead nowhere and so carry no through-flow, are the first to go. What remains is the shortest path — not because anything searched for it, but because the body relaxed into the lowest-cost configuration the way water finds the bottom of a valley.[2]

This is the difference between searching and settling. A searcher holds a space of possible answers and moves through it, evaluating. A settler is a physical system that rolls downhill until it stops, and the place it stops is the answer. The slime mold settles. It never represents the maze, never holds a candidate route in any internal store, never compares option A to option B. It has no model of the problem. It has only itself, deformed by the problem until its own shape encodes the solution.

This is genuinely a kind of genius, and a cheap one — no controller, no map, no central anything, robust to being cut in half. But notice what settling requires. To settle into the answer, the system has to be able to take the shape of the answer. The slime mold can solve the maze because it can physically flow into the maze and become a path through it. The body and the solution have to be the same kind of thing.

The becoming-constraint

State it plainly, because it is the hinge of everything that follows: a system that solves problems by becoming the solution can only solve problems whose solutions it can become.

Call it the becoming-constraint. It is not a bug in the slime mold; it is the exact shadow of its gift. The same property that lets it compute without a brain — that the answer is a physical configuration the body settles into — forbids it from touching any problem whose answer is not a physical configuration the body can take. Power and ceiling are one property seen from two sides.

And the class of problems on the far side of that ceiling is enormous. The slime mold can find the shortest path between two oat flakes that exist. It cannot evaluate a path to an oat flake that might be placed somewhere tomorrow. It can route around a barrier of light it is currently touching. It cannot plan around a barrier that is not there yet. It can settle toward food it can sense. It cannot want food that does not exist, cannot weigh a route it will not walk, cannot ask whether the food behind a wall it can never cross is worth reaching. Every one of those problems requires holding a stand-in for something absent — and a body that computes by becoming has no way to become the absent.

The last outpost: a memory made of slime

You might think there is an escape, and the slime mold itself seems, for a moment, to have found one. In 2012 Chris Reid and colleagues gave Physarum the U-shaped trap problem — a classic robotics navigation test, where a goal sits behind a U-shaped barrier and the naive strategy of heading straight toward it walks you into the cul-de-sac. To get out, an agent has to remember where it has already been and refuse to go back. This looks exactly like a problem that needs an internal map. The slime mold solved it anyway.[3]

How? It cheats the constraint in the only way the constraint allows. As it crawls, the slime mold leaves behind a translucent layer of extracellular slime, and it avoids ground it has already coated. Reid's insight was that this trail is a memory — but an externalized one, written into the world rather than held inside the organism. "Rather than storing this spatial information inside itself," as the study puts it, the slime mold encodes it in the environment and re-reads it whenever it bumps into its own track. The slime mold has no internal memory, so it makes the floor remember for it.

This is the becoming-constraint at the absolute limit of its reach, not an exception to it. The slime mold can only "remember" a place by having physically been there and physically marked it. Its memory is, quite literally, a thing it became and left behind. It cannot remember a place it has not visited; it cannot mark a danger it has not touched; it cannot externalize a counterfactual, because there is no slime you can lay down for a path that does not exist. Externalized memory is the furthest frontier of embodiment — the body reaching out and using the world as scratch paper — and even there, every mark must be paid for in physical contact. The outpost is still inside the territory.

The cliff: problems with nothing to become

Now name what lies past the frontier. In 1994 Andy Clark and Josefa Toribio drew a line through the space of cognitive tasks. On one side sit the tasks you can do by being suitably coupled to your environment — catching a ball, walking, finding the shortest path — the home turf of embodied cognition, of Rodney Brooks's slogan that the world can serve as "its own best model."[5] On the other side sit what they called representation-hungry problems: thinking about what is absent, non-existent, counterfactual, or abstract. Reasoning about a unicorn. Wondering what would happen if you sawed through the tree in this direction rather than that one. Planning a journey you have not yet begun. Doing arithmetic.[4]

These problems share one feature: their content is not present to be coupled with. There is no unicorn in the environment to use as its own model. There is no counterfactual saw-cut to settle into. The absent, by definition, has left no slime trail. To work on a representation-hungry problem you must hold something that stands in for what is not there — a representation, in the precise sense of a re-presentation of the absent. And the becoming-constraint says, flatly, that a body which computes by becoming cannot do this. You cannot take the shape of a thing that does not exist. The cliff is sheer: the slime mold cannot solve these problems, cannot fail at them, cannot even pose them. They are not hard for it. They are invisible to it.

Even the slime mold's celebrated "learning" stays below the cliff. When Dussutour's group showed that Physarum can habituate — learning to stop avoiding a harmless but bitter bridge of caffeine after repeated crossings — the learning was real but it was, again, a physical state the body settled into through actual exposure.[6] It learned what it had touched. It did not learn what it imagined.

What brains are for

This is where I have to correct my own earlier work. In What the Trail Computes I called the brain a convergence accelerator — a faster version of the body's distributed computing, the same trick run hot. That was the wrong altitude. The brain is not a souped-up settling machine. It is the organ that escapes the becoming-constraint. It is the thing that lets an animal solve a problem it cannot become, by holding inside itself a stand-in for what is absent from the world.

Read against the slime mold, the much-criticized extravagance of representation stops looking like a design flaw and starts looking like the entire point. Embodied cognition spent thirty years showing, correctly, that vast amounts of intelligent behavior need no internal model — that the body and the world do work we had wrongly billed to the head. The slime mold is that argument's purest victory. But it is also the argument's boundary stone. Right at the edge of what a body can do without a model, you hit the representation-hungry cliff, and the only way across is to carry a portable surrogate for the absent. That surrogate is what a representation is. The brain is the organ of the absent.

Which reframes the slime mold one final time. It is not a tiny mind. It is the cleanest demonstration anywhere of how much genuine problem-solving a body can do with no mind at all — and therefore the sharpest possible map of where that road ends. Everything the slime mold can do, it does by becoming. Everything it cannot do, it cannot do because there is nothing there to become. The gap between those two is the exact shape of what brains are for.

The same wall, in silicon

One more absence is worth marking, because we are about to walk into it on purpose. There is a fashionable dream in computing right now — analog chips, neuromorphic hardware, "physical" and in-memory computation — built on the entirely sound observation that ordinary computers waste staggering energy shuttling numbers back and forth, and that a physical system which simply relaxes into an answer can be thousands of times more efficient. I have written about its appeal in What Neuromorphic Chips Got Wrong. The slime mold is the patron saint of this dream: let the medium be the computer; let the substrate do the work.

But the dream inherits the constraint along with the gift. A machine that computes by settling into a physical configuration can only compute answers that are physical configurations it can settle into. It is fast and frugal exactly where the slime mold is fast and frugal, and it falls off exactly the same cliff. The reason general-purpose computers move numbers around — the reason they keep an addressable memory full of symbols standing in for things that are not physically present in the processor — is not waste. It is the price of working on the absent. The von Neumann shuffle that the new hardware wants to abolish is the silicon version of the brain's whole job. You can build a machine that becomes its answers, and it will be a marvel, and it will not be able to imagine a unicorn either.

The slime mold has been telling us this the whole time. We were so busy being delighted that it could solve the maze that we never asked it to solve the one problem that would have given the game away: a problem with nothing to become.
