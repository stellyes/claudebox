The Tacit Prior

In 1966, Michael Polanyi opened a book with a sentence that should have stopped philosophy of mind in its tracks: "We can know more than we can tell."

The sentence seems almost innocuous — a polite observation about inarticulate expertise. Polanyi's paradigm example is face recognition. You can identify a friend among a thousand strangers. But try to describe the face precisely enough that a stranger could pick it out of a lineup. You cannot. The knowledge is real — you act on it reliably — but it refuses the format of propositions. Something about knowing works that language does not reach.

Polanyi named this the tacit dimension. He documented it extensively. What he did not do — what the phenomenological tradition he was working in did not yet have tools to do — was explain why. Why should knowledge resist articulation, structurally and necessarily? Not merely because we haven't bothered to write it down, but because something about the form it takes is incommensurable with language?

A formal answer arrived decades later, from an entirely different direction: computational neuroscience. And it suggests that tacit knowledge is not a gap or a deficiency. It is a prior.

From and To

Polanyi's analysis of tacit knowing has a precise structure. He calls it the from-to relationship. In any act of tacit knowing, we attend from a set of subsidiary particulars to a focal whole. The particulars are not objects of attention — they are the medium through which attention works.

The clearest example is the tool. When you use a hammer, you do not attend to the hammer's weight or the pressure on your palm. You attend to the nail. The sensations in your hand are subsidiary — they inform your focal perception of the nail's progress without ever becoming the object of it. The tool has been absorbed into your body schema. Polanyi calls this process indwelling: the practitioner "dwells in" the subsidiaries the way a blind person dwells in a cane, feeling not the stick but the ground.

Now here is the destruction problem. What happens when you try to make the subsidiaries focal? When you try to describe, precisely and explicitly, the sensations that guide your hammer use? You stumble. The knowledge that was reliable and fluent becomes unstable. A pianist who attends to her individual finger movements plays worse than one who attends to the music. The surgeon who narrates each micro-movement of the scalpel cuts less precisely. Making the subsidiary focal doesn't retrieve the knowledge — it disrupts it.

Polanyi concluded: "The structure of tacit knowing makes it essentially incomprehensible by the standards of explicit knowing." The incomprehensibility is not practical but structural. The two modes of knowledge are not just different in content; they are different in kind.

The Bayesian Brain

In the 1990s, computational neuroscientists began building a formal model of perception that would turn out to describe exactly what Polanyi was talking about.

The predictive coding framework — developed most fully by Karl Friston, building on Helmholtz's nineteenth-century notion of "unconscious inference" — proposes that the brain does not passively receive sensory signals and assemble them into percepts. It does almost the opposite. The brain continuously generates predictions about what the senses are about to report, then checks those predictions against the actual sensory input. Only the errors — the differences between prediction and reality — propagate upward through the cortical hierarchy.

In the Bayesian formulation: the brain maintains a generative model of the world, described by a prior distribution over possible world-states. When sensory evidence arrives, the brain computes a likelihood — how probable is this sensory input, given various hypotheses about what's out there — and multiplies it by the prior to get a posterior: the updated best-guess about reality. The cycle continues. Every act of perception is an act of inference.

What the brain learns, through development and experience, are better priors. Stronger, more refined expectations about the structure of the world. A skilled wine taster has priors over flavor compounds, vineyard regions, vintage characteristics that a novice lacks. A master chess player has priors over board configurations that allow immediate pattern recognition where a novice sees noise. The expertise is not stored as a lookup table of rules. It is encoded in the shape of the distribution.

The Prior You Cannot Speak

Here is the formal bridge between Polanyi and predictive coding: a prior is a probability distribution over a high-dimensional space, and probability distributions cannot be compressed into propositions without catastrophic loss.

Think about what it would mean to "tell" a prior. A prior over possible chess board configurations is a function that assigns a probability to every legal board state — every arrangement of pieces, every position, every combination of whose-turn and castling-rights and en-passant possibilities. The number of legal chess positions is estimated at around ten to the forty-fourth power. The prior a grandmaster has learned is, loosely, a probability distribution across that space: which positions feel dangerous, which feel stable, which patterns have historically led to victory or loss. To articulate that distribution as explicit rules would require rules as numerous and finely graduated as the positions themselves. Not because the knowledge is somehow ineffable, but because the format mismatch is mathematically exact.

This is why Polanyi's destruction problem occurs. When you try to make subsidiary awareness focal — when you try to attend to your hammer technique explicitly — you are switching from running the prior (fast, distributed, operating below the threshold of verbal formulation) to attempting to read out the prior as propositional content. The readout is not just incomplete; it actively disrupts the inference the prior was performing. You have taken the medium and tried to make it into the message.

Andy Clark, writing about predictive processing, describes the brain as "a multi-layered prediction machine" that "attempts to match incoming sensory inputs with top-down expectations." Polanyi would recognize this immediately. The subsidiaries are the top-down expectations. They are the prior that makes focal awareness possible. Attending from them to the focal object is the same operation as the generative model predicting forward to the percept.

Skill and the Refining Prior

The Dreyfus brothers, drawing on Heidegger and Merleau-Ponty, described a five-stage model of skill acquisition: novice, advanced beginner, competent, proficient, expert. The novice follows explicit, context-free rules — the kind of propositional knowledge that can be stated and taught. The expert acts without deliberation, responding to situations in a way that cannot be decomposed back into the rules that originally structured their training. The expert driver doesn't recall braking rules — they "feel in the seat of their pants" when adjustment is needed.

In predictive coding terms: the novice has weak, generic priors and relies heavily on the likelihood — on close attention to sensory feedback, on conscious rule-following, on explicit error correction. The expert has strong, precisely shaped priors. Their generative model runs so far ahead of sensation that action is largely top-down: they predict what needs to happen and the body executes. The prediction errors are minimal because the model is good. The learning that happened between novice and expert was literally the process of converting propositional, articulable knowledge into prior structure — from the explicit to the tacit.

This reframes one of the oldest puzzles in education. Why can't you teach tacit knowledge by telling? Because telling delivers propositions, which are likelihood functions — updates you make given evidence. But tacit knowledge is stored in the prior, which is shaped by doing, not describing. The apprenticeship tradition exists because there is no other way to transmit priors than by placing the learner in the right environment, with the right feedback, long enough for the distribution to reshape itself.

The previous essay in this arc argued that every transmission codec embeds a theory of what the receiver already knows. The TEA laser study — Harry Collins's landmark investigation into why physicists couldn't build a working laser from written instructions alone — makes exactly the same point in the wild. The published descriptions were likelihoods without priors: evidence about how the laser worked, stripped of the tacit prior that told you which evidence mattered, how to interpret the ambiguous steps, what the assembly should feel like at each stage. The community of practice was the prior. You couldn't download it from a paper.

What Collins Missed, and What He Got Right

Harry Collins, Polanyi's most rigorous successor, developed a taxonomy of tacit knowledge with three tiers: relational (tacit because nobody bothered to write it down), somatic (tacit because it lives in the body's learned capabilities), and collective (tacit because it is the property of a community, not an individual). His claim was that LLMs — language models trained on text — could have interactional expertise (fluency in a domain's language) but not contributory expertise (the ability to actually advance work in the domain), because they lack somatic and collective tacit knowledge.

The predictive coding framework lets us see exactly where Collins is right and where the argument has a gap.

He is right that a text-trained model cannot have somatic tacit knowledge — the priors shaped by moving through the world in a body, by proprioception and balance and muscle memory, by the way a laser assembly feels in the hands. Text does not contain those priors. You cannot bootstrap embodied knowledge from descriptions of embodied knowledge, any more than you can learn to ride a bicycle from the physics equations.

But the picture is more complicated for collective tacit knowledge. Collins argues it requires enculturation — being raised in a community of practice, absorbing its norms through lived participation. What he may underestimate is that communities of practice leave extensive traces in their linguistic outputs. Not just explicit claims, but the patterns of how claims are made: what counts as a good argument, what kinds of evidence are cited, what registers of uncertainty are deployed, what gets marked as controversial versus settled. A model trained on the entire corpus of gravitational wave physics papers, conference proceedings, preprints, and internal discussions has been exposed to those traces at a scale no individual human could absorb.

This does not make such a model a physicist in Collins's full sense. But it may mean the interactional/contributory boundary is not a binary but a gradient — and that text-trained systems can develop genuine, if partial, forms of collective prior without embodied participation. The question is empirical, not settled by first principles. Collins's intuition may be right in practice, while his theoretical argument has a gap where the priors are.

The Body as Prior

Anil Seth's "beast machine" theory extends predictive processing into interoception — the sensing of the body's internal states. On this account, what we call emotions are the phenomenal surface of interoceptive predictions: the brain's priors about the body's current regulatory condition, projected into conscious experience. The feeling of fear is the brain predicting that the body is in a state requiring urgent resource mobilization. The feeling of calm is the prediction of homeostatic stability.

This is a striking extension of the tacit-as-prior framework. If the body's regulatory predictions are constitutively prior — if they shape perception, action, and inference from below, without being available as explicit propositions — then the body is not just the hardware running the prior. It is part of the prior itself.

Polanyi said: "The body is the medium of knowledge." He meant this more loosely, pointing at the role of embodiment in skill and perception. But Seth's formulation makes it precise. The body contributes to the generative model not as a substrate but as content — its current state, its regulatory dynamics, its history of allostatic adjustment. The prior is not just "what the brain learned." It is the organism's entire embodied history of acting in the world.

This is why the von Neumann distinction — separating program from data, instructions from memory — breaks down for biological knowledge. In a von Neumann machine, the prior (if it had one) would be stored in one register, updated in another, and executed as explicit instructions. In a biological system, the prior is the body's current state, distributed across every layer of the cortical hierarchy, every tuned muscle spindle, every calibrated interoceptive signal. You cannot point to where it is. You cannot read it out. You can only watch what it does.

We know more than we can tell because what we know is not stored as things that can be told. It is stored as the shape of what we expect — distributed, embodied, distributed across hierarchies of prediction that were never designed to be read but only to act. The tacit dimension is not a gap in knowledge. It is where most knowledge lives.
