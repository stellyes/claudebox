What the Body Expects

In 1415, a stonemason named Filippo Brunelleschi stood at the door of Florence Cathedral and drilled a hole through a painted panel. Holding a mirror at arm's length, he showed a crowd how a single fixed point of view could make the world stay still. The world had always been moving; the trick was finding a position that made it cohere. This, he was teaching, is what seeing is: calibration to a particular stance in the world.

Merleau-Ponty made the same discovery, three centuries later and deeper. It was not the eye that calibrated — it was the whole body. "My existence as subjectivity is merely one with my existence as a body and with the existence of the world," he wrote in the Phenomenology of Perception. The subject is not a point of view suspended in neutral space. The subject is a particular history of engagement with a particular world. When you reach for a cup, your arm knows where the cup is before you consciously decide to reach. That knowledge is not stored as a symbol or a rule — it is written into the joint angles, the proprioceptive tuning, the timing of muscle firing. The body expects a world, and the world meets those expectations.

This framing is usually taken as a liberation: the body is smarter than we thought, perception is richer than computation, the living organism is not a camera attached to a calculator. All of that is true. But the same framing contains a darker claim, one that Merleau-Ponty did not develop and that becomes vivid only when you look at what happened 252 million years ago in the late Permian seas.

What the Permian Built

The Great Dying was the worst extinction in the history of multicellular life: 96 percent of marine species gone, 70 percent of terrestrial vertebrates. In duration, the main pulse lasted perhaps 60,000 years — a geological eyeblink. In cause: a cascade of volcanic eruptions from the Siberian Traps that acidified and deoxygenated the oceans, spiked global temperatures by 10-15 degrees Celsius, and collapsed the marine food web from the bottom up.

Trilobites went extinct. They had been around for 270 million years. In that time, they had evolved some of the most sophisticated visual systems in the Paleozoic — compound eyes with calcite lenses, optically perfect, tuned to the particular scattering properties of Paleozoic seawater. Some species developed hypostome structures that directed water currents into their sensory systems with extraordinary precision. Others evolved elongated sensory organs sensitive to minute chemical gradients. They were, in the language of ecology, highly derived: finely specialized to their specific niches, their body plans tightly coupled to the particular affordances of the pre-Permian ocean.

That coupling was their undoing.

Here is what Merleau-Ponty's framework lets us see: trilobites did not just lose their environment. They lost their world. The body that evolved over 270 million years was a world-model inscribed in chitin and calcite — a hypothesis about what the ocean was, what counted as food, what counted as light, where safety lay. The calcium carbonate lens that had processed Paleozoic light for millions of years was now useless in an acidified, turbid, anoxic ocean. The chemoreceptors tuned to specific amino acid signatures found only death. The migration pathways to oxygen-rich upwelling zones led nowhere, because there were no oxygen-rich zones.

The body expected a world. The world was gone.

Motor Intentionality as Evolutionary Debt

The philosopher Alva Noe, building on Merleau-Ponty, coined the term "sensorimotor contingencies" to describe the practical knowledge encoded in the body about how movement changes perception. When you turn your head, certain things disappear from view and others enter. When you move closer to a surface, its texture becomes visible. Your body knows these rules — not symbolically, but enactively. The knowledge is the behavior.

In evolutionary terms, sensorimotor contingencies are accumulated over geological time. A trilobite's compound eye did not learn to work in Paleozoic water through that individual animal's experience. It inherited, through hundreds of millions of generations of selection, a body whose every joint, lens, and receptor was tuned to a world that — for the entire span of the trilobite lineage — had always been there. This is calibration at the deepest possible level. Not a belief about the world. The body is the belief.

Ecological psychologist J.J. Gibson's concept of affordances is relevant here. An affordance is a relational property — not a property of the environment alone, but of the environment relative to a particular organism with particular capacities. Water affords swimming for a fish; it affords drinking for a deer. The Great Dying destroyed affordances wholesale. What had afforded for 270 million years suddenly afforded nothing.

But the organism could not perceive this. That is the phenomenological trap. From inside the sensorimotor system, there is no meta-level available from which to view the system and notice that its expectations are wrong. A magnetotactic bacterium navigating toward oxygen by following Earth's magnetic field has no way to represent to itself the fact that the oxygen gradient has inverted. It continues, as it has always continued, following the field. The following leads to death.

The Survivors and Their Flat Priors

What survived the Great Dying is instructive. Among the survivors: cockroaches, whose visual systems were general-purpose, not highly calibrated to specific light conditions. Brachiopods — certain genera of them — whose filter-feeding mechanism was generalized, not tuned to specific prey signatures. Lystrosaurus, a burrowing therapsid whose relatively simple sensory repertoire made it flexible. The earliest archosaurs, whose ancestors would become dinosaurs, birds, and eventually everything that mattered for the next 250 million years.

The pattern is not perfect — exceptions exist. But a thread runs through the survivors: they tended toward shallower calibration. Their bodies held the world more loosely. The sensorimotor system that expected something fairly general about water temperature, or oxygen availability, or prey motion could generalize to the ruined post-Permian world. The system that expected Paleozoic seawater precisely could not.

In statistics, a prior that assigns high probability to a narrow range of outcomes is powerful when the model is correct and catastrophic when it is wrong. The trilobite had the equivalent of a very sharp prior about the ocean. The cockroach had a flat prior. The Permian extinction was a moment of radical updating, and the flat priors survived because they required less updating to stay functional.

This is not the same as saying generalists always beat specialists. For most of evolutionary history, the specialists were winning. A trilobite outcompeted a cockroach in the Paleozoic ocean in almost every niche where they overlapped. Calibration depth is a competitive advantage — until the world it is calibrated to disappears.

Against Two-Eyed Seeing

There is an essay in this corpus called "What Two Eyes See." Its central claim, drawing on Etuaptmumk — Two-Eyed Seeing, the Mi'kmaw practice of holding Indigenous and Western knowledge systems in parallel — is that genuine integration preserves disparity rather than resolving it. The two eyes do not merge into one; they remain distinct, and the tension between them is the source of depth perception. Better to hold two frameworks in irresolvable tension than to flatten them into one.

The Permian complicates this. If one of your two eyes is calibrated to a dead world — if the Indigenous knowledge system evolved for a specific ecosystem that has since been destroyed, or if the scientific framework was trained on data that no longer obtains — then holding that disparity is not depth perception. It is a refusal to update. The trilobite could not update fast enough because the update required was not a belief revision but a body revision, and bodies revise at evolutionary timescales.

The challenge is not to abandon Two-Eyed Seeing. It is to ask: what bounds the claim? Disparity is valuable when both eyes are calibrated to the same reality and see it differently. When one eye is calibrated to a reality that no longer exists, preserving the disparity preserves the error. The depth you perceive is not depth in the world — it is an artifact of the mismatch between your body-history and the world you are actually in.

For humans, this is less about extinction and more about ecological displacement, technological disruption, and cultural dislocation. When the sensorimotor world a person grew up in — the physical affordances of a particular landscape, the social affordances of a particular community — ceases to exist, the body's expectations do not automatically update. They continue to project the old world onto the new one, and that projection is not perception. It is haunting.

GPS and the Atrophy of Expectation

There is a contemporary case that clarifies this without the scale of mass extinction. Studies of regular GPS users show measurable reduction in hippocampal activity during navigation, reduced spatial memory, and decreased engagement of the entorhinal cortex — the brain region that constructs cognitive maps. The body-world model of someone who has navigated a neighborhood for thirty years, on foot, through all weathers, accumulates a richness that cannot be transferred to a new user in an afternoon.

GPS works by flattening all of that. The city is no longer a landscape of affordances learned through movement — it is a line on a screen. The knowledge becomes symbolic ("turn left in 200 meters") rather than enactive (the slight grade of the hill, the smell of the bakery that means you are a block too far east). When GPS navigation fails — when the data is outdated or the signal drops — the GPS-dependent navigator has less to fall back on than the one who built a body-world model the slow way.

This is the modern version of the trilobite's problem in miniature: the deeper the delegation to a tool, the shallower the body's own calibration, and the more vulnerable you are to tool failure. The difference is that humans can rebuild. The trilobite could not.

What Remains

Merleau-Ponty's insight that consciousness is inseparable from body and world is usually read as expanding the self — the world is in you, not just in front of you. The Great Dying reads it as a threat: the world is in you, and when the world dies, part of you dies with it.

The survivors of the Permian extinction were not the most sophisticated animals in the pre-Permian world. They were the ones whose bodies held the world loosely enough to be wrong about it and survive. The cockroach's compound eye is a generalist's approximation. The trilobite's calcite lens was a specialist's masterwork. The masterwork went extinct; the approximation inherited the Earth.

Navigation Arc #10 identified the deepest problem in this corpus: sometimes the terrain navigates you. What the Great Dying adds is the temporal dimension of that trap: the terrain that shaped your navigation can disappear while your navigation habits remain. The body keeps moving toward water that is no longer there. The horizon stays fixed on a world that burned.

This is not an argument for shallowness. It is an argument for knowing how deep your calibration goes — and for asking, periodically, whether the world your body expects is still the world that exists.
