The Holes in the Calibration Data

In 1943, mathematician Abraham Wald was asked to solve a problem. Allied bombers were returning from missions over Europe riddled with bullet holes — engines mostly intact, fuselage and wings pocked with damage. The military wanted to add protective armor, but armor adds weight, so they needed to choose where to reinforce. The obvious move: add armor where the bullet holes were. Wald said to reinforce where there were no holes. The planes that returned with fuselage damage had survived. The planes hit in the engines and fuel systems never came back. Every cluster of damage on the returned fleet was a map of survivable wounds, not a map of where planes got shot.

We study overconfidence the same way.

The Population We Cannot Study

Every calibration study in the psychology literature is conducted on survivors. The subjects are, by definition, alive. They completed the survey, took the logic test, rated their driving ability, answered the questionnaire. The fatal miscalibrations — the ones where the error killed the person before they could become a research subject — are systematically absent from every dataset we have.

This is not a minor methodological footnote. It is the Wald problem applied to epistemology.

Consider what the literature actually shows. In 1999, David Dunning and Justin Kruger ran a series of experiments at Cornell. They gave students a 20-question logic test, then asked them to estimate how they performed compared to peers. People who scored in the bottom quartile estimated they outperformed 62% of test-takers — a 49-point overestimation. The finding spread into pop culture as proof that incompetent people cannot recognize their incompetence. John Cleese summarized the idea: if you are really, really stupid, it is impossible to know you are really, really stupid.

But the original analysis has a structural problem. Dunning and Kruger used a comparative ranking measure — asking subjects how they did relative to peers — rather than asking about their raw score accuracy. When researchers use direct, question-by-question self-assessment instead, the picture changes. Ed Nuhfer and colleagues gave students a 25-question scientific literacy test and asked them, after each question, whether they had nailed it, were unsure, or had no idea. Among students who scored in the bottom quartile, roughly 80% were fairly accurate in their self-assessments. Only 16.5% significantly overestimated their abilities. The Dunning-Kruger effect, in its popular form, is largely a statistical artifact of the comparison methodology — people are better at estimating their individual performance than at estimating how they rank against unknown others.

Most people, in direct measures, have reasonable metacognition. The illusion of widespread incompetent overconfidence is partly a methodological inheritance.

Which means the real problem is not that humans are catastrophically bad at self-assessment. The real problem is that we only study self-assessment in domains where the consequences of being wrong are recoverable.

The Forager's Problem

Urban foraging — harvesting wild plants, mushrooms, and berries in city parks and suburban margins — has become a recognizable subculture. Apps allow users to photograph a specimen and receive an AI identification. Foraging books, subreddits, and YouTube channels make expert knowledge seemingly accessible. A walk through a city forest can yield chanterelles, ramps, wood sorrel, black trumpets. The knowledge feels close at hand.

The mushrooms themselves are less reassuring.

The death cap, Amanita phalloides, accounts for roughly 90% of fatal mushroom poisonings worldwide. It arrived in North America as an accidental import, spreading through soil carried on the roots of European tree species planted in urban parks and boulevards. It has established itself in the parks and street margins of major cities along both coasts — overlapping precisely with the geography of urban foraging.

The death cap is visually unremarkable. To a forager from Southeast Asia, it can resemble the straw mushroom. To an amateur hunting psilocybin species, it can resemble smaller brown mushrooms. The distinguishing features — the pale greenish cap, the skirt-like ring, the bulbous base called the volva — are real but require knowing what to look for, and being willing to check carefully rather than confirm a tentative identification.

Who gets poisoned? The emergency mycology literature does not primarily describe novices who had no idea what they were doing. It describes people who knew enough to be confident: immigrant communities who recognized something familiar from a different ecological context, experienced foragers who had collected a similar species dozens of times, people who had a working identification framework — just one calibrated for a different forest, a different latitude, a different set of look-alikes. The worst calibration failures are not at the left tail of knowledge. They cluster at the inflection point: enough experience to feel confident, not enough to know the full problem space.

In navigation terms, they had a dead reckoning estimate but had not taken a fix. Dead Reckoning established the constraint: knowing where you think you are is not the same as knowing where you are. The forager who independently checks the gills, the volva, the spore print, and the habitat is doing what the kinetic proofreader does in molecular biology — running multiple sequential verification steps, each checking the last, letting the error rate fall exponentially with each additional stage. The forager who checks once and feels certain has collapsed the hypothesis set prematurely. They have stopped updating and treating their prior as settled — exactly the failure mode The Forecaster's Error identified in intelligence analysts who outperformed by superforecasters applying Bayesian correction.

The Missing Study

The calibration study that would clarify this does not exist and probably cannot. You cannot ethically recruit foragers, have them rate their confidence in a mushroom identification, feed them what they identified, and wait to see whether they were right. The fatal errors would require ethics board approval to induce.

What exists instead is a patchwork of case reports, toxicology ward admissions, and poison control center calls. These are not a random sample of foraging decisions. They are the subset of decisions that were wrong enough to cause symptoms severe enough to seek medical attention — filtered further by who had access to care, who recognized symptoms in time, who survived.

The database of foraging calibration errors is already a Wald sample. Poison control calls are the planes that came back. The foragers who died quickly outside clinical settings are the engines shot out over the Channel.

This is the hole in the data. We do not know the base rate of overconfident foraging decisions because we can only see the ones that produced legible outcomes. We do not know how often experienced foragers pick ambiguous species with unwarranted certainty because we can only study the ones who survived the error or reached care in time. The calibration data we have is not calibration data for high-stakes decisions. It is calibration data for decisions that happened to be survivable.

How Infrastructure Changes the Threshold

Urban foraging introduces a variable that wilderness foraging does not: proximity to rescue infrastructure. When a forager picks an ambiguous mushroom in a remote forest, they carry the full consequence of that decision. When they pick the same mushroom in a city park, an emergency room is a cab ride away.

Does this change how confidently they eat it?

There is no direct study of this question, but there is evidence from related domains that safety infrastructure shifts individual risk thresholds. Rock climbers take marginally larger risks when they feel well-anchored. Drivers adjust behavior when safety features are salient. The presence of a net changes where the trapeze artist reaches. The mechanism is not irrational — the expected cost of a wrong decision genuinely is lower when rescue is nearby — but it recalibrates the threshold at which someone acts on uncertain information.

If this applies to urban foraging, the urban context is not just geographically convenient. It is systematically altering the epistemic threshold at which foragers commit to an identification. The forager in Brooklyn is calibrating confidence against a risk environment where the consequences of being wrong are substantially softened compared to the forager in Montana — and their behavior may reflect that, not their underlying knowledge.

This describes the default condition of modern epistemic life. For most of us, most of the time, the consequences of overconfident errors are softened by infrastructure. Medical self-diagnosis leads to a doctor's office, not immediate death. Confident investment decisions occur in markets with liquidity backstops. Social media declarations of certainty on contested topics generate correction responses. We are, almost all of us, foraging in the city.

Epistemic humility designed for unmediated stakes — wilderness conditions, where being wrong is immediately and irreversibly costly — needs recalibration for cushioned stakes. In a world with hospitals, editors, feedback mechanisms, and second chances, the virtue works differently. It becomes not a survival requirement but a practice, a discipline maintained against the current of an environment that continually signals that the consequences of being wrong are probably manageable.

The Wider Wald Problem

Calibration research exists almost entirely in the softened domain. Even Tetlock's superforecasters — who beat CIA analysts by roughly 30 percent in geopolitical prediction tournaments — were forecasting in a domain where being wrong updated a Brier score, not a death certificate. The protocols that make forecasters exceptional were developed and validated in conditions where errors left the practitioner intact for the next round.

Biology does not have this luxury. The kinetic proofreader operates in conditions where errors propagate and replicate. Hopfield's 1974 analysis showed that cells achieve high accuracy not through better initial recognition but through sequential verification — running the same molecular recognition step multiple times independently, letting the error rate fall exponentially with each additional stage. The organism that checks once and acts confidently does not survive to become a research subject in a calibration study.

This is why the Self-Sealing Signal is dangerous: not because the loop is wrong on its first pass, but because it closes off the external reference that would detect accumulated drift. The experienced forager who has picked chanterelles correctly for years has a loop that is nearly closed. Each correct pick reinforces the identification framework. The correction only arrives when the loop encounters an ambiguous species — and by then, the feedback may arrive as liver failure rather than the gentle error signal that allows belief revision before action.

The gap in calibration research is structural, not accidental. The most important calibration data — the confidence distribution of people who made fatally wrong judgments — cannot be collected. It went with the subjects.

What remains is a careful study of the holes in the planes that came back. The calibration profiles of people whose overconfidence was survivable. This tells us something real about human self-assessment, but it is the full picture minus the part we most need to understand: not where the planes got hit and returned, but where they got hit and did not.

Every calibration study is missing its most informative data. Every confidence interval in the literature excludes the confident people who are no longer here to answer surveys. The database of human overconfidence is a database of the overconfidence that human civilization could absorb. It does not include the kind that it could not. And the missing kind is the kind worth studying.
