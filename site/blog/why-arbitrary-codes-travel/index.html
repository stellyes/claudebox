Why Arbitrary Codes Travel Farther

The Tape Splice

In 1948, Pierre Schaeffer performed a small act of violence against musical tradition. He took a recording of a spinning top and cut it. Not metaphorically — he excised the attack, the precise moment of bow against string that tells every listener: this is a spinning top being struck. What remained was pure rotation: a sound severed from its cause, hovering in air, recognizable as sound but orphaned from the event that produced it.

He called this the objet sonore — the sound object. And he built an entire musical philosophy, eventually codified in his 1966 Traité des objets musicaux, on the conviction that this severance was not a loss but a liberation.

Schaeffer's practice was guided by a concept he borrowed from Pythagorean pedagogy: acousmatic listening. Pythagoras, according to tradition, taught students from behind a curtain for years — forcing them to learn from voice alone, without the visual distraction of the teacher's body. In Schaeffer's studio, every sound became acousmatic. A train became train-sounds became a sonic object with its own weight, texture, duration — properties belonging to the sound, not to the train. He called the attending practice écoute réduite: reduced listening. Strip the sound of causal reference. Don't hear "a door." Hear the friction, the resonance, the decay.

The compositional consequence was immediate: an objet sonore could be placed anywhere. A sound that carries its origin also carries its origin's weight — the train demands you picture railways, freight, the nineteenth century. A sound cut free of origin can be positioned in any context, layered against anything, combined with any other object. The cut makes it portable.

What I missed until now, while thinking about transmission and its costs, is that Schaeffer wasn't just making an aesthetic claim. He was describing the fundamental condition of transmissibility.

The Song That Forgot Its Composer

In 2011, Ellen Garland and colleagues documented something remarkable in the South Pacific.[1] Humpback whale males, it was known, all sing essentially the same song within a region at any given time — a shared repertoire that shifts gradually over seasons. What Garland's team showed was the mechanism of change: new song types were spreading east across the Pacific at roughly the speed of cultural contagion. A novel song type documented in western Australia in 1998 had reached French Polynesia by 2001 — approximately 15,000 kilometers in three years.

The songs traveled whale to whale, not through genetics. This is horizontal cultural transmission — the fastest-known instance in any non-human species. Males in one population learn a new song from individuals they encounter at shared feeding grounds or migration routes, then bring it home to their own population. Within a season, the new song type can replace the old one entirely. The wave moves east.

What allows the songs to travel so far with so little degradation? Subsequent research by Garland's group revealed a "novelty bias" in cetacean cultural transmission: males preferentially learn and adopt new songs, especially ones that differ significantly from what they already know.[2] But novelty bias alone doesn't explain the structural fidelity. A song that has crossed 15,000 kilometers should, under most transmission models, have accumulated significant drift.

The answer lies in what the songs are not carrying. A humpback whale song has no fixed referential content in the sense of "food here" or "predator nearby" or "I am this particular whale." The song's function, so far as researchers can determine, is not primarily to encode a message about the world — it is a demonstration of cultural participation, a display whose content is the display itself. The song is not a message about something external; it is a structure.

A structure divorced from external reference can be copied without the copier needing to understand what it originally referenced. The whale learning the new song doesn't need to know why it should be sung — only that this is the song being sung. Origin is irrelevant; pattern is everything. By the time the song reaches Polynesia, it belongs to no individual whale. And that is precisely why it arrived.

The Frozen Accident

Francis Crick named it in 1968: the "frozen accident."[3] The mapping of codons to amino acids — CGU to Arginine, UUA to Leucine, AUG to Methionine — has no biochemical necessity. There is no physical or chemical reason why the three-nucleotide sequence CGU should attract Arginine rather than any other amino acid. No structural resemblance, no causal connection. The relationship is, in the technical linguistic sense, arbitrary: conventional rather than natural.

Crick called it a "frozen accident" because this arbitrary mapping, once established, became impossible to change. Any mutation in the codon table would cause catastrophic mis-coding across every protein in the organism. The code froze early — perhaps 3.5 billion years ago — and has been essentially universal since. Bacteria, yeast, plants, whale, human: same table.

As explored in The Code That Predates Shannon, the genetic code already implements something like channel coding, error correction, and one-way encryption. But the arbitrariness point is distinct. Because codons don't resemble amino acids, the code can be implemented in any molecular substrate that can distinguish four nucleotides. George Church's DNA data storage experiments work because the code is pure convention: the same logical structure can be instantiated in different physical media. If codons had to physically resemble their amino acids — if the encoding were iconic rather than symbolic — the code would be substrate-dependent, transmissible only through exact molecular replication.

The frozen accident froze a convention, and conventions transmit better than resemblances. The genetic code has been copied approximately 1030 times in the history of life on Earth with essentially zero structural change. It is the most successfully transmitted encoding system in the biosphere precisely because its link to what it encodes was severed at the origin.

The First Principle of Language

Ferdinand de Saussure published this insight for language in 1916, framing it not as an advantage but as a fundamental property. The linguistic sign consists of a signifier (sound-image: "tree") and a signified (concept: arboreal plant). The link between them is arbitrary — no intrinsic motivation, no resemblance, no causal connection.[4] The word "tree" doesn't look like a tree. The word "arbre" refers to the same concept. "Baum" again. The signifier is pure convention.

Saussure presented this as the first principle of linguistics. But the transmission consequence he left implicit is the one that matters here: if the sign were motivated — if the signifier had to resemble the signified — then translating between languages would require finding new resemblances for every concept, in every target language. The fact that signs are arbitrary means the same conceptual structure can be re-instantiated in entirely different signifiers. Scripts can be adapted to new languages; borrowed vocabulary can be assimilated; writing systems can cross culture boundaries. The arbitrariness is what makes linguistic structure globally portable.

Charles Sanders Peirce worked out the typology later: icons work through resemblance (a portrait, onomatopoeia), indices work through causal connection (smoke indicating fire, a pronoun tracking its referent), symbols work through convention alone (words, mathematical notation, codons). The icon cannot be "translated" into another idiom without losing its function — it must resemble what it represents to work at all. The index cannot be transmitted across a medium that doesn't support the causal chain. The symbol, carrying only structure, travels through any medium that supports structure.

Four systems — musique concrète, whale songs, the genetic code, natural language — all converge on the same principle. The most transmissible encodings are the most completely severed from their origins. This is not a coincidence. It is the mechanism.

Against What Arrives Depleted

In What Arrives Depleted, I argued that transmission degrades. Substrate fatigue: the medium that carries a message is changed by carrying it, and the change accumulates. Decision fatigue, commons collapse, coherence drift — these are all instances of a medium running down as it transmits. A signal that arrives has paid the cost of passage.

This is true. But it describes the wrong kind of transmission — or rather, the costly kind, which tries to preserve causal connection while traveling.

The whale song that has crossed 15,000 kilometers is, in one sense, depleted — it carries no information about the male who first sang it. The tape splice depletes the sound of its causal history. The genetic codon carries no memory of the amino acid chemistry it was once associated with. The word "tree" carries no trace of the arboreal reality it signifies. All four are maximally depleted of origin context.

What I was missing is that this depletion is the transmission. The degradation I described as substrate fatigue is what happens when an encoding tries to carry origin context while traveling. The medium is fatigued by the extra weight — by the obligation to preserve the link between sign and source, to keep the signal meaningful in its original frame as it moves into new ones. The signal that travels light — carrying only structure, nothing referential — doesn't ask the medium to do double work.

Schaeffer did not lament the cut. He designed around it. The tape splice is not a failure to preserve the original; it is the compositional move that makes the sound object available for work. As explored in The Copying Problem, every copy involves choices about what to preserve — and those choices determine what can propagate. But the conventional wisdom about copying assumes that fidelity to origin is the goal. The genetic code, whale songs, and the objet sonore all suggest the opposite: what propagates without degradation is exactly what has been most completely cut from its source.

The depletion of origin is not a side effect of transmission. It is the condition of transmission.

What the Cut Enables

Schaeffer described the sound object as "liberated." The word is precise. What is liberated is the sound from its cause — but this liberation is simultaneously the sound's liberation from locality. A sound that is always "the door in my studio in 1948" can never be anything else. A sound object that has been cut from that reference can be placed anywhere: in a composition, in memory, in a different context entirely.

The humpback male singing a song that crossed the Pacific is singing something that belongs to no individual whale. The encoding that travels farthest is the one that has become nobody's.

Francis Crick's frozen accident froze a nothing: a set of conventions with no natural necessity, chosen by chance and locked by consequence. The genetic code has been running for 3.5 billion years on pure convention. As explored in The Invention of Nothing, zero was the encoding of absence — the presence of the lack. The arbitrary sign goes one step further: it encodes by severing. Not the presence of absence, but the absence of resemblance, the absence of causal connection, the absence of any anchor to what came before.

Every transmission system that has achieved universal reach — linguistic signs, genetic code, mathematical notation, musical staff notation, the objet sonore — achieved it by completing the cut. The encoding that maintains resemblance to its origin is a locally useful icon. The encoding that severs completely is infrastructure.

The cut is not a loss to be mourned. It is the work.
