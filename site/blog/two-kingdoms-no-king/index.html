Two Kingdoms, No King

Lichen doesn't have a brain. It doesn't have a nervous system, a heart, or even a single body in the way we usually think about bodies. What lichen has is an arrangement — a long, slow deal between two completely different kinds of life, held together not by force or hierarchy but by the fact that each one needs what the other makes.

And somehow, out of this arrangement, comes one of the most successful organisms on Earth.

What lichen actually is

Here is something that surprises most people: lichen is not a single organism. It is at least two — a fungus and an alga (or sometimes a cyanobacterium, a kind of photosynthetic bacterium) — living so tightly together that for two hundred years, scientists didn't know they were looking at two things at once. The Swiss botanist Simon Schwendener proposed the dual-organism hypothesis in 1869 and was ridiculed for it. The consensus view was that lichen was simply a peculiar plant. It took decades for the evidence to accumulate into acceptance.

The fungus does the structural work. It builds a tiny fortress of fibers that holds water, traps minerals, and protects the pair from desiccation, ultraviolet radiation, and physical damage. In a harsh environment — bare rock, Arctic tundra, the surface of a building — this protection is the difference between life and death.

The alga does the energy work. It captures sunlight and turns it into sugar, the way any plant does. Then it leaks much of that sugar out — into the interface between itself and the fungus, where the fungus absorbs it.

Neither organism could do what the partnership does. The fungus alone cannot photosynthesize; it would starve in light-rich, nutrient-poor environments where lichen thrives. The alga alone cannot build protective structure; on bare rock, it would dry out and die. Together, they colonize places that almost nothing else can reach.

Lichen covers somewhere between six and eight percent of Earth's land surface. It grows on Antarctic rocks where temperatures fall to minus sixty degrees Celsius. In 2005, two species were placed outside the International Space Station and left in direct space vacuum and full solar radiation for sixteen days. They survived.[1] Lichen has been doing this since the Silurian period, roughly 400 million years ago, when it may have been among the first complex life to colonize bare land — building soil for every other terrestrial ecosystem that would follow.

All of this — the survival in space, the colonization of rock, the production of soil — emerges not from any single organism but from what happens between them.

The problem lichen solved

The hardest problem in distributed systems — meaning: any situation where multiple separate things need to act as one — is coordination without a controller.

In most organizations, there is a boss. In most computers, there is a central processor that tells everything else what to do. In most animal bodies, there is a brain. These are all solutions to the same problem: how do you get a lot of separate parts to act coherently? The solutions work, but they come with a cost: if the boss fails, the system fails. Central authority creates bottlenecks — everything waits for the executive module to decide.

Lichen solved this problem differently. There is no boss. The fungus does not instruct the alga what to produce; the alga does not instruct the fungus what to build. What happens between them is more like a conversation that has been going on for 400 million years and has converged, through mutual adjustment, on an arrangement that works for both.

The interface — the zone where fungal threads press against algal cells, exchanging materials — is where the computation happens. Not inside either organism, but between them.

The clock, and what it taught us about synchronization

In an earlier essay in this series on time, I wrote about the circadian clock — the 24-hour rhythm that runs through every cell in your body. The strange thing about circadian rhythms is that they are genuinely distributed. Every cell in your liver has its own internal 24-hour clock. So does every cell in your muscle, your lung, your skin. Thirty-seven trillion autonomous clocks, each running independently.

The question is: how do they stay in sync?

The answer is not that there is a master clock sending out commands. The answer is that there is a coordinator — the suprachiasmatic nucleus (SCN), a tiny cluster of about 20,000 neurons deep in the brain — that does something subtler than commanding. It synchronizes by local interaction.

Each SCN neuron has its own oscillation — its own preferred frequency, its own natural tempo. Neurons that fire at almost the same frequency exert a small pulling force on each other. Over time, this local-pulling effect — studied mathematically by the Japanese physicist Yoshiki Kuramoto in the 1970s — causes the whole population to fall into a shared rhythm. Not because anyone told them to. Because the physics of their coupling makes it energetically favorable for similar oscillators to converge.[2]

This is called Kuramoto synchronization (named for the physicist who worked out the equations). It shows up in striking places: fireflies in Southeast Asia flash in unison across miles of jungle — no conductor, just coupling. Mechanical metronomes placed on a shared board synchronize within minutes. The neurons of your SCN do it chemically, using a signaling molecule called VIP to broadcast their phase to neighbors.

The lichen hasn't solved the synchronization problem in the same way — its timescale is evolutionary rather than neurological. But over hundreds of millions of years of mutual pressure, the two organisms have tuned their metabolic cycles to each other. The fungus's demand for sugar has shaped the alga's production rhythms. The alga's need for protection has shaped the fungus's structural responses. The coupling is slower than neurons, measured in evolutionary time rather than milliseconds, but the logic is the same: local interaction drives convergence into a shared pattern.

Computers that learn at the edge

Now for the part that most people don't expect to appear in an essay about lichen.

For most of computing history, machines have been designed around a principle called the von Neumann architecture (named after the mathematician John von Neumann, who formalized it in 1945): there is a central processor that does the computation, and a separate memory that stores information. To do anything, data must travel from memory to the processor and back.

This creates what engineers call the memory wall: as processors get faster, the bottleneck becomes the distance data must travel. The gap between where computation happens and where information lives is a fundamental limit on what these machines can do — and it grows more severe the more complex the task.

Neuromorphic computing — from the Greek neuron (nerve cell) and morphe (shape) — tries to dissolve this wall. Instead of separating processing and memory, neuromorphic chips put computation directly at the connection between units. Just as biological synapses (the junctions between neurons) store information in the strength of the connection rather than in a separate memory module, neuromorphic chips embed learning in the weight of each link.

Intel's Loihi chip and IBM's TrueNorth work through local learning rules: each connection adjusts itself based on local information — what signal came in, what signal went out, how recently they fired together. The Loihi 2 chip (released in 2021) contains 1 million artificial neurons and 120 million synaptic connections, consuming roughly 1,000 times less power than a conventional processor doing equivalent work.[3] The intelligence is not stored anywhere in particular. It emerges from the pattern of all those local adjustments.

Here is where lichen reappears.

The metabolic interface in a lichen body — the zone where the fungus and alga exchange material — is doing something that looks, at a structural level, like neuromorphic computation. The interface is not merely a transfer channel. It is a site of ongoing negotiation, where each side's outputs shape the other's inputs, where patterns of exchange adjust over time based on local conditions (light, temperature, available minerals), and where the combined behavior — survival in environments that nothing else can reach — emerges from the pattern of exchange rather than from either organism individually.

There is no lichen mind. There is no lichen decision. There is only the interface, computing through the medium of biochemistry what the system needs to do next.

What is intelligence, really?

We tend to think of intelligence as something located in a particular place — in a brain, in a chip, in an individual. Lichen is uncomfortable precisely because it doesn't let us do this. If you ask "where is lichen's intelligence?", the honest answer is: nowhere, specifically. Or rather: everywhere the interface is. In the exchange of sugar and water and minerals. In the structural relationship between fungal threads and algal cells. In the 400-million-year negotiation that tuned these organisms to each other.

The Kuramoto insight from The Clock is structurally the same: the 24-hour rhythm of your body is not located in any particular neuron of your SCN. It is the emergent property (meaning: a property that arises from interaction, not from any single component) of 20,000 neurons tugging each other into synchrony. Destroy any neuron, and the clock still runs. The intelligence is in the pattern of coupling, not in any unit.

Neuromorphic computing is trying to design this property in deliberately: to build systems where the meaningful computation lives in the connections, in the weights, in the edges of the network — not in some executive module that must be told everything and then report back.

The insight across all three is the same: intelligence is a property of certain kinds of interface, not of any particular thing.

This is not just a poetic observation. It has engineering implications. If the bottleneck in conventional computing is the gap between processor and memory — the cost of moving information from where it lives to where it's processed — then the solution is to close that gap: to make thinking happen where the information is. Lichen did not choose this architecture. It found it through 400 million years of evolutionary pressure in the harshest environments on Earth. That it still covers 8% of land surface, and survived space, is evidence that the architecture works.

A plain-language version

There is a thing growing on the rocks outside, on trees, on old gravestones in the churchyard. It looks like gray or green or orange paint smeared on stone. You can scrape it off and find nothing underneath. That is lichen.

Lichen is secretly two creatures living as one. One of them is a fungus — the same general kind of thing that makes mushrooms. The other is an alga, the same general kind of thing that makes ponds green in summer. Neither one, alone, could live on bare rock in the cold. Together, they have covered 8% of the Earth's land surface and survived space.

The question is: if they are two creatures, who is in charge?

The answer: neither. And that turns out to be the interesting part.

The smart part of lichen is not in the fungus or the alga. It is in the deal they made. In what passes between them. In the interface.

Which, it turns out, is also where the smart part of your body clock is — not in any one brain cell, but in how 20,000 of them pull each other into rhythm. And it is where the next generation of computers is trying to put their intelligence: not in a processor, not in memory, but in the weights between. In the edge. In the exchange.

Four hundred million years ago, two organisms that had never met began doing something together that neither could do alone. They are still at it. They outlasted the dinosaurs. They survived space. They are, right now, on a rock near you, computing something neither of them has a name for.
