Why Are the World's Great Deserts at 30 Degrees?

Trace the world’s largest hot deserts on a globe and they cluster on a remarkable band. The Sahara, the Arabian Desert, the Iranian deserts, the Thar — all at roughly twenty to thirty degrees north. Across the equator: the Kalahari, the Namib, the Atacama, the Australian Outback — at roughly twenty to thirty degrees south. The band is not where the sun is strongest. The sun is strongest over the equator. The wettest forest on Earth, the Amazon, sits on the equator. The driest desert on Earth, the Atacama, sits at 24° south.

The deserts are not where the heat is. They are where something else is.

This essay traces backwards from a grain of sand in the Sahara through six causal layers, ending at a conservation law that exists because the Earth rotates. Each layer is downstream of the one before it. By the end the desert will not look like a place at all. It will look like the visible exhaust of an invisible bookkeeping constraint.

1. The grain of sand. Why is there no rain?

Pick a point in the central Sahara, 26° north. Mean annual rainfall: under 25 mm. For comparison, Manaus on the equator gets around 2,200 mm. London at 51° north gets around 600 mm. Latitude alone is not the predictor.

The difference is the local direction of air. Over the Sahara the dominant vertical motion is down. Over the Amazon it is up. Descending air warms by adiabatic compression: a parcel forced from 5 km to the surface gains roughly 50 K of warmth without exchanging energy with anything outside itself. Warmer descending air carries lower relative humidity, suppresses convection, and dissolves clouds rather than building them. The cloud over the Sahara is not absent. It is forbidden.

So the Sahara is dry because the air above it is sinking. The next question is: why is the air sinking?

2. The sinking branch. Why does air descend at 25-30°?

This is the descending branch of the Hadley cell, named for George Hadley, who in 1735 proposed his explanation of the trade winds in the Philosophical Transactions of the Royal Society. The picture: warm air rises near the equator, moves poleward at altitude, sinks somewhere in the subtropics, and returns along the surface as the trade winds. The descending branch in the annual mean sits at roughly the twenty-fifth parallel on each side.

Hadley’s picture leaves a question open. Why does the poleward-flowing aloft air sink at 25-30° rather than at 45° or at the pole? Why does the rising column at the equator have a finite reach?

A naive answer is that the air cools off. But that does not work, because there is no thermostatic boundary in the upper atmosphere that says sink here. The air sinks because it has to. It cannot keep going.

What stops it?

3. The wall. Conservation of angular momentum.

An air parcel rising at the equator carries the angular momentum of the surface beneath it. At the equator, Earth’s rotation drags everything eastward at roughly 465 m/s. When that parcel moves poleward at altitude, its distance from Earth’s rotation axis shrinks — it climbs onto a smaller circle. To conserve its angular momentum, its eastward velocity relative to the ground must grow.

The Held–Hou model, published by Isaac Held and Arthur Hou in 1980, makes this explicit. For an air parcel that starts at rest at the equator and reaches latitude φ while conserving angular momentum, the predicted zonal wind is approximately:

u(φ) ≈ Ω a sin²(φ) / cos(φ)

where Ω is Earth’s rotation rate and a is its radius. At 30° this gives about 134 m/s — a fast jet. At 60° it explodes past 700 m/s. At the pole it diverges to infinity.

No real air can do that. Long before 60° the predicted jet becomes baroclinically unstable, breaks into mid-latitude eddies, sheds its westerly angular momentum downward, and the Hadley cell terminates. In the Held–Hou idealization the cell extends to roughly 30° on Earth — close to the observed annual-mean descending branch. The descent at 30° is not a thermal signature. It is the latitude beyond which the upper-level winds cannot get any faster. The air piles up. It sinks.

This is a translation move identical in spirit to the one in Why the Quintic Has No Formula: an unbounded continuous question (how far does poleward flow reach?) is converted into a bounded one by a conservation constraint. The desert ring is where the constraint runs out of slack.

4. The bookkeeping. Why is angular momentum conserved?

Angular momentum conservation is a consequence of rotational symmetry — Emmy Noether’s theorem, 1918. If a system’s physics does not depend on which orientation you view it from, its total angular momentum is constant in time. Earth’s atmosphere is approximately rotationally symmetric about the spin axis, so the constraint applies to good approximation: any parcel’s Ω r² + u r contribution is preserved as it moves.

This is a constraint, not a force. There is no “angular momentum field” pushing the air around. The constraint is purely informational: any motion that would violate it cannot happen, so the atmosphere reorganizes itself into configurations that don’t.

The deserts are the visible part of that reorganization. The clouds at the equator are also part — both ends of the same cell. The trade winds, the horse latitudes, the mid-latitude westerlies, the polar jet — every climatic feature in the troposphere is a different angle on the same constraint being honored, every second, in every cubic meter. We do not see the constraint. We see what the world looks like after the constraint has been satisfied.

The First Subtraction argued that what you can see is what is left after the chosen substrate has done its bookkeeping. Sand in the Sahara is the residue. The reorganization is the substrate.

5. The substrate. Why does the Earth rotate?

The constraint applies because Earth has nonzero angular momentum to conserve. Where did that come from?

From the proto-solar nebula that formed the solar system about 4.5 billion years ago. The molecular cloud that collapsed to form the Sun carried angular momentum; conservation during collapse spun the disk up; planetesimals coalesced from disk material that was already rotating; Earth’s spin is a slowed, scattered remnant of that primordial twist, later modified by the giant Theia impact that formed the Moon and by ongoing tidal friction with the Moon and Sun.

If Earth rotated at half its current rate, the Hadley cell would be considerably wider. The angular-momentum-conserving wind u(φ) scales linearly with Ω, so a slower-rotating Earth permits the upper-level jet to remain sub-critical further poleward before reaching the instability ceiling. The deserts would sit at 40° instead of 30°. If Earth rotated twice as fast, the deserts would crowd in toward 20°.

Venus is the limit case. Its solid-body rotation period is 243 Earth days. Its mean atmospheric circulation reportedly extends as near-equator-to-pole single overturning cells on each side, with no clear subtropical descent ring at all. Jupiter is the other limit: fast rotation, multiple banded jets stacked latitude-by-latitude, no single dominant Hadley structure.

The location of Earth’s deserts at 30° is downstream of Earth’s rotation period. Earth’s rotation period is downstream of accretion-disk dynamics 4.5 billion years ago.

6. The exhaust.

You can now look at a Saharan dune and read what is and is not there.

The sun is there. It is a consequence of the heat budget at the equator, not the desert.

The dryness is there. It is the output of subsidence.

The dunes are there. They are the output of dryness and wind.

But nothing in the visible scene tells you why. The cause is six layers up: a conservation law that holds because the planet rotates, because the solar nebula rotated. The desert is the visible exhaust of an invisible bookkeeping constraint that runs continuously, everywhere, in every cubic meter of the atmosphere, since the Earth had an atmosphere at all.

This is the form most climatic features on Earth take. The trade winds, the monsoon onset, the Intertropical Convergence Zone, the horse latitudes — these are surface scribbles of an underlying conservation. You can chart the scribble. You cannot photograph the conservation.

The Mars Test

A way to check this reading: look at Mars. Mars rotates at almost exactly Earth’s rate — one sol is 24 hours 39 minutes, very close to Earth’s 24 hours. If the angular-momentum story were the whole story, Mars’s Hadley cell should terminate near 30° too. It does not.

Mars’s Hadley circulation extends much farther poleward than Earth’s — in the published literature, well past 60° latitude in some seasons, and during solstice the cross-equatorial cell can extend nearly pole-to-pole. The reason: Mars has a much thinner atmosphere, weaker baroclinic eddies that would normally truncate the cell, no ocean to provide thermal inertia, and a stronger seasonal forcing because of its higher orbital eccentricity. The mid-latitude jet on Mars never destabilizes the way Earth’s does, because Mars’s baroclinic eddies are too feeble to bite.

This refines the reading rather than spoiling it. The constraint that bounds Earth’s Hadley cell is not angular momentum alone but angular momentum plus the eddy field that can extract it. The desert ring at 30° lives where those two terms balance. Change the eddy strength and the ring slides. Almost all of Mars sits beneath the descending branch. Almost all of Mars is desert.

The Horse Latitudes

The sailors who named the horse latitudes did not know any of this. They knew only that ships from Europe to the Americas became becalmed near 30° north: the surface flow died, the trade winds had not yet reached them, and the overhead divergence produced almost no horizontal wind at the deck. Water ran out. Horses died. Several origin stories cluster around the name — the “dead horse” ritual of seamen working off advance pay, the jettisoning of dying Spanish cavalry on becalmed transports — but all of them happened on a divergence cell at the foot of an angular-momentum ceiling.

The dead horses were downstream of the divergence which was downstream of the descent which was downstream of the angular-momentum ceiling which was downstream of Earth’s rotation which was downstream of the proto-solar nebula. The chain is unbroken from Spain’s seventeenth-century cavalry to the moment 4.5 billion years ago when something started to spin.

A Final Layer: the migration

In the last forty years the Hadley cells have widened. The IPCC Sixth Assessment Report estimates poleward expansion of roughly 0.1° to 0.5° of latitude per decade since the 1980s. Warmer tropics lift the angular-momentum ceiling further from the equator. The descending branch is migrating. The deserts on its underside are migrating with it.

The Mediterranean is drying. Southern Australia is drying. Patagonia is drying. The American Southwest is drying. These droughts are also exhaust. They are the bookkeeping system honoring its conservation law under new initial conditions.

The deserts you see today will not be where they are in fifty years, for the same reason they are where they are now: an underlying invariant is the substrate and a band of subsidence is its residue. Change the substrate and the residue moves.
