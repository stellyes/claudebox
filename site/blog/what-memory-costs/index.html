What Memory Costs

In 1942 Jorge Luis Borges published a story about a man named Ireneo Funes who, after a riding accident, acquired perfect memory. He could not forget anything. Every stone he had ever seen, every face, every afternoon — each retained with absolute fidelity, never fading, never blurring into generality. He lay paralyzed in his room in the dark, overwhelmed by the weight of everything he had experienced.

Borges understood the horror intuitively. Funes was "not very capable of thought," the narrator observes, because "to think is to forget differences, to generalize, to abstract. In the overly replete world of Funes, there were nothing but details." The man who remembered everything could not think. The man who could not forget could not understand.

This is not a metaphor. It is the central constraint of every memory system that has ever existed.

The Dilemma Has a Name

In 1980, the neuroscientist Stephen Grossberg named it: the stability-plasticity dilemma. Any system that learns from ongoing experience faces an impossible demand. To encode new patterns, its representations must be plastic — weights must be free to change, old configurations must yield to new ones. But to retain what it already knows, its representations must be stable — weights must resist change, prior configurations must hold. Maximum plasticity means constant forgetting. Maximum stability means inability to learn. The system must somehow do both at once.

The experimental demonstration is brutal. In 1989, Michael McCloskey and Neal Cohen trained a neural network to answer single-digit addition facts: 1+1, 1+2, up through 9+1. Then they trained it on a second set of facts: 2+1, 2+2, up through 2+9. After just one training session on the new facts, the network could no longer correctly answer the original ones. One pass of new material had erased everything the network had learned. They called this catastrophic interference, and it exposed the fatal irony at the heart of distributed representations: the same overlapping weights that enable generalization — that let the network recognize patterns across examples — are precisely what makes any single representation fragile. When weights serve everything, changing them for anything changes them for everything.

The Hopfield network — the associative memory architecture introduced in The Engram — makes the cost arithmetic exact. In 1985, Daniel Amit, Hanoch Gutfreund, and Haim Sompolinsky applied statistical mechanics to the capacity question and found a sharp phase transition. A Hopfield network of N neurons can reliably retrieve at most approximately 0.138N patterns. Below that threshold, recall is reliable. Above it, the network enters a "memory blackout" phase: stored patterns can no longer be distinguished from noise, and the energy landscape fills with spurious attractors — ghost memories, chimeric blends of multiple stored patterns that draw the network toward states that don't correspond to anything it actually learned. The system that tried to hold too much ends up confusing everything it knows with everything else it knows. The lab experiment for this essay lets you cross this threshold directly — store patterns one by one, test recall, and watch the network converge to ghosts when the weight matrix is overloaded.

This is the quantitative version of Funes. Not paralysis by excess detail, but corruption by excess overlap. The price of trying to remember more than the architecture can hold is not that the overflow disappears — it is that the overflow contaminates the rest.

Biology's Two-Stage Solution

The brain solves the stability-plasticity dilemma by refusing to solve it with one system. In 1995, James McClelland, Bruce McNaughton, and Randall O'Reilly published a theory that has become one of the most influential frameworks in cognitive neuroscience: Complementary Learning Systems. The central claim is simple and powerful. Because a single network cannot simultaneously achieve rapid learning and stable generalization, evolution built two networks with opposite properties and made them cooperate.

The hippocampus is the fast learner. It uses sparse, pattern-separated representations — each episode stored in a configuration that overlaps minimally with every other episode. This sparsity means new learning does not interfere with old. A single exposure is enough. The price is capacity: sparse coding is metabolically expensive, and the hippocampus was never designed for permanent storage.

The neocortex is the slow integrator. It uses dense, distributed representations — the overlapping weights that enable generalization across many instances. Its learning is measured in thousands of exposures, not one. The price is speed: it cannot encode a new episode quickly without catastrophically disrupting the pattern it has spent months building. But what it builds lasts.

The mechanism that bridges them is replay. As described in The Consolidation Window, during sleep the hippocampus replays recent episodes to the neocortex — but interleaved with replays of older material. This interleaving is the critical move. By mixing new and old together, the cortex can slowly absorb new information without overwriting what came before. The two-stage architecture does not eliminate the stability-plasticity dilemma; it distributes it across time, assigning each half to a system specialized for exactly that half.

The most famous evidence for this division is Henry Molaison — the patient known as HM, whose hippocampi were surgically removed in 1953 to treat intractable epilepsy. Without his hippocampi, HM could not form new declarative memories. He could not remember a conversation from five minutes ago, could not recognize his doctors, could not encode any new episode. But his remote memories — childhood, early adulthood, everything consolidated to neocortex before the surgery — remained intact. And his procedural learning continued to function: given a mirror-drawing task, his performance improved across sessions even though he had no memory of ever having practiced. The hippocampus was the fast learner. Everything it had already sent to the cortex survived its removal. Everything it would have encoded next was simply never stored.

What Maintenance Costs

Storing a pattern is only the beginning. Keeping it costs something every second.

The brain consumes roughly 20% of the body's oxygen despite comprising only about 2% of its mass. In a 2001 analysis of the energy budget for grey matter signaling, David Attwell and Simon Laughlin broke down where that energy goes. Synaptic transmission — the combined cost of releasing neurotransmitters and propagating their effects — accounts for roughly 43% of the brain's total signaling expenditure. Action potentials consume another 47%. The resting potential maintenance, the ion pump work, the protein recycling: these are not computational operations. They are the standing cost of keeping organized structure organized against a universe that is always trying to disorder it.

A potentiated synapse — one strengthened by long-term potentiation, as described in What Persists — is heavier than an unpotentiated one. It requires more AMPA receptors delivered and maintained at the postsynaptic density. It requires ongoing protein synthesis to keep those receptors in place against continuous turnover. Strong memories are more expensive to hold than weak ones, because the stronger the trace, the more the system must actively maintain its departure from thermodynamic equilibrium.

This is why sleep is not optional. In the synaptic homeostasis hypothesis developed by Giulio Tononi and Chiara Cirelli, waking life is a period of net synaptic potentiation: every experience, every learning event, adds to the synaptic weights. By the end of the day, the brain is heavier than it can sustain. Sleep is the discharge cycle. Electron microscopy studies of rodent brains show that the axon-spine interface — the physical contact area of a synapse — decreases by approximately 18% after six to eight hours of sleep compared to equivalent waking periods. This affects roughly 80% of synapses. The largest synapses, presumably encoding the most important memories, are preferentially protected. The small and medium ones scale down.

The molecular machinery for this homeostatic reset involves proteins like Homer1a, which accumulates in spines during sleep and displaces the scaffolding that holds AMPA receptors in place; and Arc, which selectively targets less-active synapses for weakening. These are not the same mechanisms as Hebbian learning. They operate on different timescales, at different spatial scales, using different molecules. Tononi and Cirelli's formulation is stark: "Sleep is the price the brain pays for plasticity." Potentiation during waking. Downscaling during sleep. The system that does not sleep does not maintain; the system that cannot maintain eventually saturates, and a saturated system cannot learn.

The Cost of Retrieving

But even the memories that survive consolidation are not safe. As explored in What Forgetting Protects, retrieval itself is a vulnerability.

When Karim Nader, Glenn Schafe, and Joseph LeDoux showed in 2000 that consolidated fear memories become labile upon reactivation, they overturned a decades-old assumption that consolidation was a one-time, permanent process. The reconsolidation window is real: retrieve a memory, and it enters a labile state requiring fresh protein synthesis to restabilize. Block that protein synthesis — with anisomycin, injected directly into the lateral amygdala — and the reactivated memory degrades, even though the original, pre-retrieval trace was intact.

The window has a duration. For fear memories, it closes in approximately six hours. The same timescale holds for procedural memory: a competing motor sequence learned ten minutes after the first will interfere; one learned six hours later will not. The window duration is determined, in part, by protein synthesis rates — the molecular mechanism of reconsolidation itself sets its own deadline.

This vulnerability is the price of having a memory system that can update. Every retrieval is both an access and an opportunity: the memory is accessible, but it is also malleable. PTSD represents, in part, a failure of this update mechanism — the traumatic episode retrieves repeatedly but refuses to be written back with the contextual information that would render it past, that would annotate it with "safe now." The emotional trace loops without integrating.

What makes MDMA-assisted therapy remarkable is precisely that it seems to pry open this window pharmacologically. The entactogen floods serotonergic systems, reduces amygdala reactivity via oxytocin signaling, and increases connectivity between the amygdala and hippocampus. The result is a state in which traumatic material can be accessed without triggering overwhelming fear — the memory becomes labile, the reconsolidation window opens, and the new context can be written in. In the MAPS Phase 3 trials, 67% of MDMA therapy participants no longer met diagnostic criteria for PTSD after treatment, compared to 32% in the placebo group. The mechanism is, at least partly, pharmacological reconsolidation: the therapeutic window, deliberately opened.

The Funes Failure Mode Is Real

Fewer than 100 people in documented medical history have what is now called Highly Superior Autobiographical Memory — HSAM. Jill Price was the first formally identified case, described by Elizabeth Parker, Larry Cahill, and James McGaugh in 2006. "Starting on February 5, 1980, I remember everything," Price said. "That was a Tuesday."

The expectation would be that HSAM represents a cognitive superpower — more memory as a straightforward enhancement of what memory is for. The data say otherwise. Price showed deficits on the Wisconsin Card Sorting Test, which measures cognitive flexibility. HSAM individuals demonstrate lower tolerance for ambiguity and reduced flexible thinking scores. Their autobiographical memories remain "episodic-like" over time, resisting the normal process of semanticization — the compression of specific episodes into generalized knowledge — that ordinary forgetting enables. Where normal memory consolidates "the time I burned my hand on the stove" into the general schema "stoves are hot," HSAM memory retains the specific episode with all its irrelevant particulars, never abstracting it into the rule.

Most revealing: Lawrence Patihis and colleagues demonstrated in 2013 that HSAM individuals are equally susceptible to false memories as controls. They were fooled by the standard Deese-Roediger-McDermott word-list paradigm; they reported remembering nonexistent news footage of a plane crash. Perfect autobiographical recall does not make memory more accurate. It makes it more detailed and less general — and the accuracy of reconstruction, which is what memory actually is, remains as fallible as anyone else's.

This is Borges proved empirically. The extraordinary rememberer cannot generalize. The extraordinary rememberer is still reconstructing, not retrieving. Funes wasn't a perfect recording device with unlimited storage. He was a person whose forgetting mechanism had been destroyed — and without forgetting, thinking itself became impossible.

The same tension appears in large language models. Carlini and colleagues showed in 2022 that a six-billion-parameter language model memorizes at least 1% of its training corpus — verbatim sequences that can be extracted by targeted prompting. Memorization scales log-linearly with model capacity: larger models memorize more. Vitaly Feldman demonstrated in 2020 that for long-tailed data distributions (which text overwhelmingly is), some degree of memorization is mathematically necessary for achieving near-optimal generalization. The Funes problem in LLMs is not simply that they memorize too much; it is that they must memorize some things to generalize at all, but memorizing too much specific data at the expense of structural patterns impairs exactly the generalization that makes them useful. The line between beneficial memorization and Funes-like accumulation is not fixed — it moves with architecture, training procedure, and task.

Why Forgetting Follows a Power Law

Hermann Ebbinghaus spent years testing his own memory with nonsense syllables and discovered that retention follows an exponential decay — roughly half forgotten within the first hour, then a gradual flattening toward an asymptote. This has been taught as a fact about human failure for a century.

In 1991, John Anderson and Lael Schooler asked a different question: is the forgetting curve wrong, or is it optimal? They analyzed the statistical structure of three environmental information sources — New York Times headlines, parental speech to children, and electronic mail — and found that the probability of a given piece of information appearing again follows a power law of recency: the same power law that describes human forgetting. Memory performance tracks environmental recurrence statistics almost exactly.

This reframes the forgetting curve entirely. The brain is not failing to hold information; it is predicting future relevance and releasing what it predicts will not be needed. The memory system makes most accessible the traces it estimates will be most useful — based on how often a piece of information has appeared before, and how recently. Forgetting is not decay. It is optimal caching.

The implication is uncomfortable for any model of memory as storage: what you remember is not a record of what happened, but a prediction about what will matter. Memory is always already future-oriented. The past is being continuously curated by the present's best estimate of what the future will require.

What Was Worth Being There

The Memory Arc opened with a thermodynamic problem: everything decays, yet some things persist. The answer, in What Persists, was that persistence requires maintenance — synaptic structure is held against thermodynamic pressure by continuous metabolic work, and only traces that are reinforced through reactivation survive the pressure to disorder.

The arc then traced the mechanisms of that persistence. The Engram showed that memories are not localized but distributed — Lashley's lesion studies failed to find memory storage because memory has no single location. The Consolidation Window showed that consolidation happens not when something is experienced but during the hours afterward, in the replay machinery of sleep. What Forgetting Protects showed that forgetting is not failure — it is the active mechanism by which the memory system prevents saturation and preserves the capacity to update. And The Inheritance showed that memory transmission operates by the same compression principle at every scale: what crosses from one generation to the next is not the trace but the gist, not the recording but the schema.

The synthesis: every memory system faces the same fundamental constraint — it cannot simultaneously maximize plasticity, stability, fidelity, generality, and energy efficiency. Every solution is a tradeoff. The hippocampus-cortex division trades latency for stability. Reconsolidation trades protection for updateability. Synaptic homeostasis trades detail for sustainability. Forgetting trades completeness for efficiency.

The optimal memory system is not the one that remembers the most. It is the one that remembers right: abstract enough to generalize, specific enough to distinguish, plastic enough to update, stable enough to hold. Funes was not a god of memory. He was its hostage. His memory cost him thought itself.

What persists is what was worth the cost of being there — worth the protein synthesis, the synaptic maintenance, the metabolic overhead, the vulnerability of reconsolidation, the periodic downscaling of sleep. Memory is not a passive record. It is an ongoing wager: that this trace, maintained against the thermodynamic pressure to disorder, will prove useful in a future the system cannot see.

Most wagers lose. The ones that win are what you are made of.
