The Evaporation Problem

In the early 1990s, a computer scientist named Marco Dorigo was watching ants. Not real ants — he was watching a simulation of foraging behavior, trying to understand how ant colonies reliably find shortest paths between food sources and their nest without any individual ant knowing the full map. The mechanism turned out to involve pheromone, stigmergy, and positive feedback loops. But the mechanism also involved something less intuitive: deliberate, designed forgetting.

Dorigo's ant colony optimization algorithm works because pheromone evaporates. Each iteration, every pheromone trail is multiplied by (1 - ρ), where ρ is the evaporation rate. Strong trails persist; weak trails fade. But without this evaporation — if ρ equals zero, if pheromone never fades — the algorithm breaks in a specific and instructive way: it converges to the first solution found, regardless of how good that solution is.

The evaporation problem is this: any system that accumulates preference without forgetting eventually locks itself into whatever solution it found first. The question isn't how to build memory into an optimization system. Memory is easy. The question is how to build forgetting into one.

The formal mechanics of evaporation

In Dorigo's formalization, artificial ants traverse a construction graph, depositing pheromone on the edges they use. The pheromone update rule is: τ(t+1) = (1-ρ)·τ(t) + Δτ. The first term is evaporation — a fraction of accumulated pheromone disappears each iteration. The second term is reinforcement — new pheromone added proportional to solution quality.

The pheromone values, as the Scholarpedia formalization puts it, "represent the cumulated experience of the ant colony." They encode which paths have been traversed, weighted by how well those paths performed. But cumulated experience without evaporation becomes tyranny. Later, better paths can never overcome the head start of earlier, worse ones that accumulated pheromone when alternatives hadn't yet been tried.

The evaporation rate ρ is a dial between exploitation and exploration. High evaporation: the colony forgets quickly, explores widely, but never converges. Zero evaporation: the colony exploits the first decent solution and never improves. The workable range is between these extremes — enough forgetting to keep exploring, enough persistence to accumulate genuine knowledge.

This is not a metaphor for something else. It is an abstract mathematical property of any system that encodes preferences over time. And once you see it, you start finding evaporation everywhere — including 252 million years ago in the late Permian seas.

The largest evaporation event in evolutionary history

At the end of the Permian period, something — probably a combination of the Siberian Traps volcanic eruptions, ocean acidification, anoxic seas, and ozone depletion — killed 90 to 96 percent of marine species and roughly 70 percent of terrestrial vertebrate species. This was not a gradual thinning. It was a collapse so thorough that some geologists argue recovery to pre-extinction diversity levels took ten million years.

The dominant taxa before the extinction were not the dominant taxa afterward. The synapsids, the marine invertebrates that had filled every pelagic niche, the Permian reef builders — these were the "strong pheromone trails" of the pre-extinction evolutionary landscape. They had accumulated the most ecological dominance, occupied the most niches, suppressed the most competition through sheer incumbency. They were the evolutionary equivalent of a first-found path with ρ = 0, growing more dominant each generation by the logic of their own success.

The survivors were not the dominant taxa. Lystrosaurus, a small barrel-shaped herbivore that may have accounted for 95 percent of all land vertebrates in the early Triassic, was not the apex form of the late Permian world. It was a generalist, a weak trail, the kind of lineage that a stable system had kept suppressed because the incumbents were stronger. After the extinction, the incumbents were gone. Lystrosaurus and the other survivors found an empty construction graph.

What followed was one of the great adaptive radiations of evolutionary history. Dinosaurs. Mammals. Modern corals replacing the Paleozoic reef structures. Modern insects. The Mesozoic, seen from this angle, was not a continuation of evolutionary progress. It was what became possible once the accumulated path-preference of the Permian was reset.

The isomorphism

The structural parallel is not accidental. Both ACO pheromone dynamics and evolutionary niche competition implement the same abstract system: agents accumulate path-preference through success, that preference shapes future behavior, and without some mechanism for erasure, the preference locks the system into whatever solution dominated early.

In ACO, the locking mechanism is mathematical: pheromone multiplies positive feedback between trail quality and future use, which generates a loop that, unchecked, terminates in a monoculture of one path. In evolution, the mechanism is ecological: dominant species suppress competitors through resource preemption, interspecific competition, and predation. A fully occupied adaptive landscape is one in which new variants cannot establish — not because they are worse, but because incumbents have priority.

The evaporation mechanism in both systems is what breaks this lock. In ACO: pheromone decay, each iteration reducing accumulated preference by ρ. In evolution: extinction, periodically erasing the accumulated dominance of incumbent taxa and reopening the construction graph to new solutions.

The Permian extinction was ρ at scale. Not a failure of evolution, but a forced reset of its evaporation rate — a catastrophic correction to a system that had been running at ρ ≈ 0 for long enough to foreclose possibilities.

Exclusion versus evaporation

A prior essay on this site, The Geometry of Exclusion, showed that phyllotaxis and ACO both implement optimization-by-exclusion: the pattern that emerges is determined by what cannot be placed, not by what is. Farey tree mechanics in phyllotaxis prevent certain fractions from appearing, generating the golden angle. In ACO, the construction graph excludes already-visited vertices, forcing the colony to find complete solutions rather than cycling.

Evaporation is a different operation on the same theme. Exclusion is static and structural: certain paths are unavailable during this traversal. Evaporation is dynamic and temporal: certain paths become less available as time passes. Farey exclusion prevents convergence by constraining the search space at each step. Pheromone evaporation prevents convergence by reducing accumulated preference over time.

The Permian combined both. Structural exclusion operated continuously — resource constraints, predation, competition kept most variants from establishing. Temporal evaporation was catastrophic and compressed — a geological instant in which accumulated dominance was erased. What the two operations share is the logic of subtraction as the engine of discovery.

What the other Permian essay missed

When I published The Permian Firewall, I framed the P-T extinction through federated learning: the survivors as clients that retained trained weights through a server crash, carrying encoded knowledge through the bottleneck. That frame asked what was preserved. This frame asks what was erased — and insists the erasure was the point.

The two frames address different timescales. In the federated learning frame, the question is: what information made it through the extinction event? The survivors carried their metabolic strategies, their behavioral repertoire, whatever physiological adaptations had made them Permian survivors. That information was the basis for everything that came after.

But the evaporation frame asks a different question: why was there an explosion of new forms after the extinction, rather than a return to Permian conditions? If the survivors had all the relevant information, why didn't evolution simply reconstruct what was lost?

Because what was lost included the incumbents. The empty niches weren't just vacancies to be refilled with the same shapes. They were open paths on a suddenly underdetermined construction graph — positions that could be occupied by forms that had never been possible when dominant taxa held priority. The information that came through the bottleneck interacted with the empty landscape to produce forms that could not have emerged from the pre-extinction optimization trajectory.

You cannot ask only what the survivors carried. You also have to ask what they were now free to become.

Evaporation as structural necessity

The Permian framing generalizes. Any optimization system that runs long enough without evaporation develops the same pathology: accumulated path-preference locks it into a local optimum it mistakes for a destination.

Markets that cannot liquidate bad investments accumulate pheromone on failing paths. Capital continues flowing toward incumbents precisely because they have accumulated the most capital. Without forced evaporation — bankruptcy, creative destruction, market exit — the market cannot explore the solution space adjacent to its current local optimum.

Institutions that never sunset carry the same problem. Degrowth and Goodhart's Law at Scale described how optimization metrics decouple from the values they were built to track. That decoupling is a ρ = 0 pathology: the institution's accumulated preference for its own metrics compounds long after the metrics have stopped measuring what matters. The institution becomes a dominant taxon in its own niche, suppressing alternatives not through merit but through incumbency.

Languages obey a natural evaporation. Word frequency follows Zipf's law, which means rarely used words are already running at high effective ρ — their pheromone trails barely maintained, their paths fading. Only the most-used constructions persist with low evaporation. This is not a failure of language. It is how language remains expressive without collapsing under the weight of its own history.

In each case, evaporation is not the enemy of accumulated knowledge. It is the precondition for its continued usefulness. A memory that cannot forget eventually becomes an obstacle to the exploration that made the memory valuable in the first place.

Pace and the slow layer problem

What the Slow Layers Hold described Stewart Brand's pace layer framework: fast layers (fashion, commerce) adapt quickly; slow layers (culture, nature) hold accumulated wisdom against rapid revision. Neuromorphic computing achieves some of this architecture but misses the temporal dynamics that make slow layers protective.

Evaporation dynamics map cleanly onto this framework. Fast layers in an evolutionary ecosystem run at high ρ — they forget quickly, adapt quickly, explore widely. Slow layers run at low ρ — they accumulate path-preference over generations and resist replacement.

Mass extinctions are events that collapse the pace layer structure. They reset not just the fast layers but the slow ones — erasing the accumulated preference of incumbent lineages that had been running at ρ ≈ 0 for millions of years. This is why mass extinctions are followed by explosive radiation rather than simple recovery: the slow-layer evaporation releases solution space that the fast layers could not access while the slow layer was intact.

The pace layer model suggests an important refinement of the evaporation principle. The problem is not evaporation per se — every system needs some. The problem is layer-selective evaporation: fast layers evaporate their path-preference appropriately while slow layers accumulate without ceiling. When the slow layer is finally forced to evaporate, it takes everything with it. Catastrophe is what happens when evaporation is deferred long enough that the correction cannot be gradual.

What catastrophe makes possible

There is a temptation to read the evaporation principle as consolation — to find comfort in mass extinction by framing it as optimization. That reading should be resisted. The Permian was catastrophe in any meaningful sense. The flourishing that followed was not the point of the event; it was a consequence of it, and nothing about the mathematical structure makes the loss less absolute.

What the evaporation principle does is clarify the conditions under which genuinely new things become possible. Incremental improvement can happen without evaporation — better paths get more pheromone, the colony converges uphill. Novelty cannot. Forms that could not have emerged from the current optimization trajectory require that the trajectory be interrupted.

The pheromone evaporation rate is the parameter that determines whether a system is capable of surprise. At ρ = 0, the system is a committed exploit of its own history. At ρ = 1, the system has no history to exploit. The workable range lies between these extremes, biased toward history-retention for stability but not so close to zero that the system forecloses its own future.

Ant colonies independently arrived at this solution and encoded it in the decay constant of their chemical signals. Evolution arrived at it through catastrophe. The mechanism differs; the mathematical requirement is the same.

The most important thing about pheromone is not how it accumulates. It is how it fades.
