Why Dazzle Camouflage Doesn't Hide Anything

In the spring of 1917, German U-boats were sinking Allied merchant ships faster than the shipyards could replace them. The obvious countermeasure — paint the ships to blend into the sea — had already failed. Ocean and sky shift colour by the hour; a tone that matched the water at dawn stood out at noon. A British marine painter named Norman Wilkinson proposed the opposite. Cover the hulls in bold, clashing geometry: black-and-white diagonals, false curves, interrupted stripes that fought the line of the deck. The ships would not be harder to see. They would be impossible to read.

Wilkinson was explicit that this was not concealment. The term "dazzle painting" was coined in April 1917, and the first scheme went onto HMS Industry that May. His stated aim, in his own words, was "not so much to cause the enemy to miss his shot when actually in firing position, but to mislead him…as to the correct position to take up."[1] That sentence is the whole idea, and it is stranger than the word camouflage suggests. Wilkinson was not attacking the U-boat's ability to find the ship. He was attacking its ability to aim — a different faculty entirely, and one that turns out to be far easier to break.

The paint that was meant to be seen

To understand why a battleship would dress itself in carnival stripes, you have to understand what a U-boat commander actually had to do. A torpedo in 1917 was slow — perhaps thirty knots — and the ship it chased was moving too. You could not aim a torpedo where the ship was. By the time the weapon arrived, the ship would be somewhere else. You had to aim where the ship would be, which meant solving, in your head or on a mechanical fire-control table, a small geometry problem: given the target's range, its bearing, its heading, and its speed, where do the two paths cross?

Everything in that problem depends on reading the ship's motion correctly. Range came from a coincidence rangefinder — an optical instrument that split the target into two half-images and asked the operator to slide them into alignment. Heading and speed came from the eye: which way is the bow pointing, how fast is the bow wave climbing, how long is the hull foreshortened against the horizon. Dazzle attacked every one of these inputs. Diagonal slabs of paint gave the coincidence rangefinder false vertical edges to lock onto, so the two half-images aligned at the wrong distance. False bow waves implied the wrong speed. Stripes that broke the hull into mismatched blocks made it ambiguous which end was the bow — so a ship steaming north-east could read as steaming north-west, an error of forty-five degrees in a calculation where five degrees was a miss.

The defender, in other words, was not trying to corrupt the U-boat's knowledge that a ship existed. It conceded that point completely. It was trying to corrupt one specific downstream quantity: the firing solution. This is the move worth naming. An attacker who must act on a target runs a pipeline — notice it, estimate its state, predict its future, fire. Concealment attacks the first stage. Dazzle leaves the first stage untouched and goes after the estimate. And a battleship, which cannot plausibly attack the first stage, has every reason to discover that the second stage is soft.

What the eye gets wrong about motion

Did it work? Here the honest answer is: the historical record is a mess, and the mess is instructive. The Admiralty's own statistics on whether dazzled ships were torpedoed less often were inconclusive; the scheme survived partly because crews and the public liked it. Modern psychophysics has tried to settle the mechanism in the laboratory, and the results are mixed in a revealing way.

In 2011 Nicholas Scott-Samuel and colleagues showed that high-contrast dazzle patterns genuinely distort a human observer's estimate of speed — moving stripes can read as travelling slower than they are.[2] But the effect only became large at speeds well beyond any 1917 ship: the equivalent, the authors noted, of a Land Rover at ninety kilometres an hour seen from seventy metres. A 2025 study by Tim Meese and Samantha Strong at Aston University separated the effect into pieces and found that the "twist" — the heading confusion Wilkinson cared about most — was real but small, while a different "horizon effect" was stronger. So the laboratory verdict is that dazzle does perturb the motion estimate, just perhaps not enough, at ship speeds, to have been the war-winner its champions claimed.

What matters for the argument is not whether the paint earned its keep but that the motion estimate is corruptible at all. The visual system does not measure velocity directly; it infers it, and it is already in the business of guessing the future. The flash-lag effect, described by Romi Nijhawan in 1994, is the cleanest demonstration: a moving object and a brief flash presented at the exact same location are perceived as offset, because the brain extrapolates the moving object forward to compensate for its own processing delay.[3] Perception of a moving thing is not a report of where it is. It is a prediction of where it will be — the same prediction a gunner needs — and any prediction can be fed misleading inputs. Dazzle is an attack on the extrapolator.

The same trick in fur and in tadpoles

Evolution found this attack long before Wilkinson. Consider the zebra. Its stripes have never made much sense as concealment — a striped horse on an open plain is, if anything, conspicuous, and lions hunt largely by scent and at dusk. In 2014 Martin How and Johannes Zanker modelled what the stripes do to a predator's motion-detecting neurons and found that a running zebra generates spurious motion signals: the moving stripes produce illusory cues, including apparent motion in directions the animal is not travelling.[4] The stripes do not lower the chance of being seen. They lower the chance of being correctly intercepted — which, for a predator that must lunge ahead of a galloping target, is the quantity that decides the meal.

I arrived at all of this, in fact, by following a study about tadpoles. In 2025, researchers led by Akihiro Noda at Kyoto University reported that tadpoles of the Japanese tree frog Dryophytes leopardus, when raised in the presence of dragonfly nymphs, grow vivid orange tails marked with black spots.[5] The result is doubly counterintuitive. The conspicuous tails drew more attacks, not fewer — and yet those attacks missed more often, and the strikes that did land hit the expendable tail rather than the body. The colour fails utterly at the first stage of the predator's pipeline; it makes the tadpole easier to detect. It succeeds at the later stages: it draws the strike toward a part the tadpole can lose, and it makes the strike itself harder to land on a moving, oddly-marked target. This is the same architecture as a deflection eyespot on a butterfly wing, and the same architecture as Wilkinson's hull. Be found. Be hard to solve.

Adversarial examples are dazzle for machines

The purest modern version of the move lives in machine learning, where it was discovered by accident. In 2013 Christian Szegedy and colleagues noticed that a deep neural network classifying images could be flipped to a confident wrong answer by adding a perturbation so small a person could not see it. The panda is still, to any human, obviously a panda; the network now calls it a gibbon with 99 percent confidence.[6] Ian Goodfellow and collaborators formalised a fast way to generate these perturbations in 2014.[7] The defining property is exactly the one Wilkinson exploited: the object is not made harder to perceive. Its presence is not in question. What is corrupted is the estimate the system computes about what it plainly sees.

The physical-world versions make the parallel unmistakable. In 2017 Anish Athalye and colleagues 3-D-printed a toy turtle whose surface texture caused Google's vision system to call it a rifle from nearly every angle.[8] In 2018 a team led by Kevin Eykholt showed that a few strips of carefully placed tape could make a self-driving car read a stop sign as a speed-limit sign.[9] The tape does not cover the sign. A human still reads STOP without effort. The defacement is aimed past human vision entirely, at the machine's internal estimate of what the well-lit, fully-visible octagon means. These are dazzle patterns for a different nervous system — paint applied not to defeat the sensor but to defeat the inference behind it.

This is where the move connects to something the corpus has circled before. An adversarial patch is a way of gaming a measure: the network's confidence score is a proxy for "what is this object," and the attack drives the proxy away from the target it was meant to track — a Goodhart failure manufactured on purpose. And it is, in the most literal sense, induced apophenia: dazzle gives a rangefinder false edges to fuse, gives How's motion neurons false vectors, gives a classifier a false maximum. In every case the defender does not erase a pattern. The defender plants one, and lets the attacker's own pattern-completing machinery do the rest.

Two axes of being found

It helps to draw the two attacks on the same page. An adversary that wants to act on you runs a chain: detect that you are there, estimate your state, predict your future, act. There are two very different ways to break that chain, and we usually conflate them under the single word "camouflage."

The first is to attack detection — to lower the probability that you are noticed at all. This is the classic kind, and the corpus has examined its deep-sea form: counter-illumination, where a fish lights its own underside to match the downwelling glow so that a predator below sees no telltale silhouette against the brighter surface. Counter-illumination is a defense of the first stage. Its entire purpose is that the predator's detector finds nothing anomalous.

Dazzle is the exact inverse, and the inversion is the point. It accepts a detection probability of one. The ship, the zebra, the tadpole, the stop sign — all are seen, all the time, by design. What dazzle defends is the second and third stages: the estimate and the prediction. These are orthogonal axes. Visibility and targetability are not the same quantity, and a system can be pinned at maximum on one while being driven to zero on the other. Most defensive intuition lives entirely on the detection axis — be quieter, be dimmer, leave less trace. The other axis is the one available to things that cannot be quiet: the battleship too large to hide, the prey already in the open, the sign that has to be read by humans even as it fools the car.

That second axis has a feature the first does not. Detection is often a hard wall — once a sensor crosses its threshold, the target is found and stays found. But the estimate in the middle of the pipeline is soft. It is a computation, and every computation has inputs that can be poisoned. The visual system extrapolates, so feed it the wrong extrapolation. The rangefinder fuses two images, so give it the wrong edges to fuse. The classifier maximises a score, so build a false maximum. Wilkinson's real discovery, the one that outlived the doubtful war statistics, was that an enemy who must compute your future has handed you a target of his own. You do not have to be unseen. You only have to be unsolved.
