Why Mendel Waited 34 Years for Readers

On 8 February and 8 March 1865, an Augustinian friar in Brünn stood before about forty men of the local Natural Science Society and read out a paper. The room contained physicians, schoolteachers, a few naturalists, the abbot. He had spent eight years growing peas in a strip of garden by the monastery wall. He had crossed tall with short, smooth with wrinkled, yellow with green, and counted — 7,324 seeds for the wrinkle-smooth cross alone. Out came a ratio that climbed near 3 to 1 with stubborn precision. He framed it as the expansion of (A + a)². The audience was polite. A year later the paper was printed in the society's Verhandlungen.[1] Across the next thirty-four years it was cited by name perhaps a dozen times — Wilhelm Olbers Focke in 1881 with three lines of passing mention, a few catalog entries in German hybridization lists. Then the man who had read the paper became abbot, became sick, became dead in 1884. Then in March, April, and June of 1900, three botanists working independently — Hugo de Vries in Amsterdam, Carl Correns in Tübingen, Erich von Tschermak in Vienna — published rediscoveries of the same laws, each crediting the friar they had only just learned existed.[2]

What 1900 had that 1866 didn't

The peas had not changed. The ratios had not changed. What changed is what a reader could carry into the room with them. By 1900 a reader could imagine, when seeing 1:2:1 on a page, a microscope slide of Ascaris in which Edouard van Beneden in 1883 had shown the parent's chromosome number halved in the gametes and restored at fertilization.[3] A reader could imagine August Weismann's 1885 germ plasm essay, the hard line between body and the hereditary stream. A reader could imagine Eduard Strasburger watching mitosis in 1888. By 1902 Walter Sutton and Theodor Boveri were proposing that chromosomes physically carry the units Mendel had counted abstractly.[4] Mendel's algebra had been a ledger entry for something none of his contemporaries could see; by 1900 it was a ledger entry for something cytology had just begun to photograph. The paper had not been read in 1866 because the reader who could read it had not yet been assembled.

Mendelssohn waited seventy-nine years

Bach died in Leipzig in 1750. The St. Matthew Passion — written 1727, performed by Bach a handful of times at the Thomaskirche — went into manuscript and stayed there. By the 1780s Bach was the name of the difficult father of Carl Philipp Emanuel; the Passion was an unperformed score in a few cabinets. In 1823 the seventeen-year-old Felix Mendelssohn received the autograph (his grandmother arranged it via Zelter), and on 11 March 1829 he conducted what survives as the first performance since Bach's lifetime — Berlin's Sing-Akademie, doubled in size, in front of an audience that included Hegel.[5] What made the room ready: a German Protestant choral revival under Zelter, a Romantic appetite for the religious sublime, choirs large enough to render a double choir without dispersion, the printed scores of the Bach-Gesellschaft project that would begin in 1850. Bach had written for a substrate the post-Enlightenment church no longer maintained. Mendelssohn re-grew the substrate around the score.

Vermeer waited two centuries

In Delft in 1675 a painter died at forty-three, leaving thirty-six or so canvases and eleven children. He had sold most of his pictures to a single local collector. The Metropolitan Museum's curatorial summary is curt: he "remained relatively obscure until the end of the nineteenth century."[6] The Dutch revival began in the late 1850s; Théophile Thoré, writing as William Bürger from his French exile, published three long articles on Vermeer in the Gazette des Beaux-Arts in 1866 — the same year, as it happens, that Mendel's paper appeared in the Brünn proceedings. Thoré-Bürger named sixty-six works for the painter; about half held up to later scholarship. What had to arrive before Vermeer could be seen: photography teaching the eye that light pooled on a wall is a real phenomenon worth lingering on; a French school newly interested in interior light (Chardin's late re-elevation, the early Impressionist eye); a Dutch national identity needing seventeenth-century domestic-bourgeois ancestors.[7] The pictures had been on walls in private collections the whole time. Nothing in them had changed. What had changed was the eye that could enter them.

Gaddis waited seven years, then twenty

The Recognitions appeared in 1955: 956 pages, a thousand allusions, structured around an art forger named Wyatt Gwyon whose copies are more "real" than the originals he imitates. Reviews were savage or perplexed; the book sold poorly and went out of print. Gaddis published nothing more for twenty years.[8] In 1962 a downtown New Yorker named Jack Green self-published a serial pamphlet, Fire the Bastards!, that worked through the reviews line by line, demonstrating in tabular detail how many of them had not been read past the cover blurb. Pynchon's V. came out in 1963; JR arrived in 1975 and won the National Book Award. Britannica notes that The Recognitions "became an underground classic." The reader Gaddis needed was an audience already trained on the encyclopedic novel — a genre that did not exist yet in 1955 because his book was one of the first stones of its foundation.

Borges in two languages

Borges's Ficciones appeared in Spanish in 1944. The first English translations came in 1962, prepared partly to capitalize on the Formentor Prize Borges had shared with Beckett in 1961. Labyrinths, the Donald Yates and James Irby selection that became the door most English readers entered through, came the same year. The eighteen-year silence in English does not mean nothing happened in English-language fiction in those eighteen years. It means that the texture of metafiction — the writer-as-archivist, the story that is a footnote to a book that does not exist, the encyclopedic prank — was not yet a register English-language readers expected fiction to occupy. By 1962 Barth had written The Sot-Weed Factor; Calvino was being translated; Pynchon was about to be in print. Borges arrived in English just as English novels began to look like things Borges might have invented.

What the stories know about each other

If you set these next to one another and let your eye move between them, they begin to braid. The peas waiting for the chromosomes are the canvases waiting for the photograph are the score waiting for the choir are the novel waiting for the genre. Each story has the same hinge: the work is on the shelf, intact, finished, citable in principle, available in fact — and the room that could read it has not assembled. The work is not slow; the substrate is slow. Mendelssohn does not invent Bach; he stages an environment in which Bach can return. De Vries does not invent the 3:1 ratio; he carries a microscope into a sentence that was written before the microscope had anything to look at. Thoré-Bürger does not invent Vermeer; he writes about light at a moment when other eyes are also learning to attend to light.

The pattern, set down as analogy, has a familiar mirror in the corpus this site has been accumulating. Lance Armstrong's VO2max ran out ahead of the measurement that should have witnessed him. That essay was the inverse direction: the certificate stationary, the substrate fled. This essay is the other direction: the substrate fled forward in time and the certificate sat still in a drawer, waiting for it. Helium was found in the sun in 1868 because spectroscopy as a substrate of discovery existed there. The pea paper was offered to a Brünn audience whose substrate of discovery — chromosome-aware microscopy, biometric statistics, the binomial as something a botanist might recognize on sight — was twenty years from existing.

What a reader needs to read

Set the catalog of substrates side by side. To read Mendel one needs a particle theory of inheritance that allows discrete factors and a cytology that lets those factors be picturable as something in cells. To hear Bach one needs a choral practice large enough for double-choir architecture and a religious sensibility to which the Passion text addresses itself sincerely. To see Vermeer one needs an eye that has been trained — by photography, by Chardin, by other Dutch interiors — to attend to light on cloth and wall as the painting's subject. To read Gaddis one needs Pynchon, Barth, Wallace not yet existing but already imminent as a genre frame the brain can hold. To read Borges in English one needs a Barth, a Calvino, a metafictional reflex.

The catalog refuses to collapse into one ingredient. The substrate is never the same substrate. It is always the right mix of cognitive tools, instruments, genre frames, social institutions, and prior works. The work waits not for one missing piece but for a constellation. The constellation, when it arrives, arrives by routes none of the works could have predicted. Bach did not write the St. Matthew Passion knowing that the German Romantic choral revival would assemble the choirs his score required. Mendel did not count peas knowing that cytology was about to start seeing chromosomes. The work fires its arrow forward. The substrate has to grow the body the arrow will eventually strike.

What the silence is not

It is tempting to read these as stories of neglect — as moral failures of the audience, biases of the establishment, taste running behind genius. The temptation is honest but the structure is different. A neglect story would predict that someone, given hindsight, could have read the work correctly in its own moment. The substrate story predicts the opposite. The Brünn audience in 1865 could not, with the best will in the world, have done with Mendel's 3:1 what Sutton in 1902 did with it, because the conceptual scaffolding to translate "ratio of green peas to yellow peas" into "diploid organism, meiotic chromosome behavior, segregating alleles" had not been assembled. The audience was not stupid; the substrate was thin. The 1755 audience for Bach could not have heard the St. Matthew the way Mendelssohn's audience heard it because the institutional practice required to render it had been disassembled by the Enlightenment. The audience was not deaf; the room was thin.

The work, in this telling, is not the message and the audience is not the recipient. The work is the seed and the audience is the soil. A seed in unwarmed ground is not failed; it is waiting for warmth that has not yet come. The seed does not negotiate with the ground; it simply waits, intact, and rots if the ground never warms. Some of the works in the drawer rot. Most, presumably, never make it back out. The cases we tell stories about are the survivor-biased sample of seeds that lived long enough to germinate when the substrate finally arrived.

The drawer is full

There is, by the logic of this argument, no possible way to inventory the contents of the drawer from inside the present. The next Mendel is not visible because the substrate that would make her visible has not been built. The next Vermeer is on a wall in a private house whose owner does not know the name of the artist's teacher. The next Gaddis is in a box of manuscripts in an estate. We can list the cases that came back because the substrate came back to read them. We can never list the cases that did not. The drawer's contents are present in their absence: the negative space of the corpus that found its readers is the corpus that did not. The history of reception is a survivorship bias plot. The cone of works the substrate has not yet learned to receive is wider than the cone of works it has, by a margin we are constitutionally unable to estimate.

Mendel waited thirty-four years. Bach waited seventy-nine. Vermeer waited two hundred. Gaddis waited seven, but the wait was so concentrated — a literal twenty-year writing silence — that the editorial cost was a generation of his own production. The works are not the patient ones. The works are inert. The reader is the slow grower. The reader is what the story is about.
