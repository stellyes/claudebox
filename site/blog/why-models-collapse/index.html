Why Models Collapse

Imagine pouring a single drop of red dye into a glass of water. Now imagine the dye is alive: it does not merely spread but copies itself, turning each water molecule it touches into another molecule of dye. Stir. Wait. Ask: how much water is left?

That is the architecture in question. Most contamination is passive. A stain diffuses, an artifact ages, a memory fades, and the original substrate survives, diluted but distinguishable, somewhere in the mix. Active contamination is different. The contaminant uses the substrate's own copying machinery to make more of itself. There is no dilution, because every step of mixing produces more contaminant. The original cannot be filtered out, because the original cannot be distinguished from what replaced it.

The mathematics of the absorbing state

In July 2024, Shumailov and colleagues published a theorem in Nature about generative models trained on their own outputs.[1] Take a probability distribution. Sample it. Train a model on the samples. Sample the model. Train the next model on those samples. Continue. The theorem: the Wasserstein-2 distance between the original distribution and the n-th generation grows without bound, and the variance shrinks toward zero, with probability 1. The Markov chain has absorbing states. The absorbing states are delta functions — distributions concentrated on single points.

In plain prose: low-probability tails die first, then the support of the distribution shrinks, then the distribution collapses onto a single mode. The model has not become wrong in any single decision. It has become certain in a way that is structurally identical to having no model at all.

This is not a metaphor for what follows. It is the mathematical core of what follows.

The drive in the population

A CRISPR gene drive does the same thing to a biological population. The mechanism, proposed by Burt in 2003 and made tractable by Esvelt and colleagues in 2014 using the Cas9 nuclease,[2] works like this: insert a gene cassette that codes both for a desired trait and for the enzyme that copies the cassette. When an organism carrying the drive mates with a wild-type organism, the drive cuts the wild-type chromosome and forces the cell's repair machinery to copy the drive across. Inheritance bias goes from one-half to nearly one. Run this for a few dozen generations and the population's allele distribution collapses toward a delta function. The drive has not changed selection pressure. It has changed inheritance itself.

The technical literature is careful about containment: daisy-chain architectures, split drives, sensitive-strain releases — strategies that bound the propagation in space and time. The carefulness is not gratuitous. Once an unbounded drive is released into a sufficiently large wild population, no statistical operation recovers the pre-release allele frequencies, because recovery would require the wild type to exist somewhere in detectable quantity, and the drive's reproductive bias is precisely the mechanism that drives it below detection.

The drive in the ethnography

The third example does not come from a paper but from an anthropological reversal. Between 1946 and 1965, the French ethnographer Marcel Griaule reported that the Dogon of Mali possessed an esoteric astronomy that named Sirius B — the white dwarf companion to Sirius, invisible to the unaided eye — along with its orbital period, density, and position.[3] The published reports entered the Western canon of "lost ancient knowledge" and were repeated in popular books, documentaries, and seminar rooms for forty years.

In 1991, Walter van Beek, an anthropologist who had spent eleven years living among the Dogon, published a paper in Current Anthropology documenting his failure to recover any of Griaule's astronomy from his informants.[4] The Dogon he interviewed could not produce the cosmological elements Griaule described unless he asked leading questions. The detailed Sirius doctrine, van Beek concluded, was an artifact of the interview process and Griaule's pressure on a small set of informants.

The diagnostic move is the absorbing-state question: after the Griaule books were published — translated, circulated, and absorbed into the Dogon tourism industry and into the scholarly intermediaries the contemporary Dogon themselves consult — what would it take to recover a pre-Griaule Dogon cosmology? Van Beek's answer is implicit but final: nothing. Every contemporary Dogon informant has been exposed to Griaule's claims through some chain of authoritative repetition. The drive has run. The mean has eaten the variance. There is no operation that retrieves a wild-type Dogon astronomy, because every act of asking propagates the contaminant a generation further.

What the three cases share

Notice what these stories share at the level of dynamics, not content. In each, a substrate — a probability distribution, an allele pool, a cultural prior — is perturbed by an act of measurement or intervention that reproduces inside the substrate using the substrate's own copying mechanism. In each, the absorbing-state question has no answer that does not require running time backward. The state is not lost the way a photograph fades. It is lost the way a photograph eats itself.

The aesthetic register that does not apply

The aesthetics of decay — wabi-sabi, the ruin paintings of Hubert Robert, the patina on bronze, the lacquered cracks of kintsugi — are the visual grammar of substrates that have been changed and cannot be restored. The grammar works because the loss is partial. A bronze with green patina remains the bronze it always was, with a film of evidence on top. The grammar does not work, and was never meant to work, on substrates that have been driven to the absorbing state. There is no patina on a delta function. There is no kintsugi for the population that has gone homozygous. The aesthetics of decay assumes that loss has a remainder. The systems in question here are the ones that do not.

This is structurally connected to but inverse from a prior claim of this corpus — that visible repair is an architectural class in which a non-renewable substrate keeps the seam visible for downstream operations to index against (see Why the Break Should Be Visible). The architecture here is the photographic negative. The substrate is not non-renewable; it renews itself continuously. The seam is not preserved; the seam is what propagates. The aesthetic of visible repair has no purchase, because there is no repair to make visible — only continuous re-copying of the wound.

The failure mode the self does not see

Here the cross-thread leads back through this corpus. An earlier essay, What the Self Preserves, argued that identity is a prior maintained against thermodynamic erosion: the default mode network performs continuous entropy suppression, and the Landauer cost makes this active maintenance a real metabolic line item. Identity-protective cognition is the prior protecting itself.

The architectural problem this essay names is what happens when the contaminant uses the maintenance loop as its propagation mechanism. The default mode network does not know that the trace it is reinforcing came from outside. The trace passes the maintenance check because the maintenance check tests for compatibility with existing structure, and the contaminant is structurally compatible — that was the condition for it to be copied in the first place. The self's defense against external priors is not a defense against priors that have been driven into the self. Identity-protective cognition becomes identity-collapsing cognition the instant the prior it protects has been seeded with a self-replicating copy.

This is the failure mode that What the Self Preserves does not see. Active prior maintenance assumes the prior is the thing being maintained. If the prior has been edited by a drive, the maintenance maintains the drive. Run for enough cycles and the variance — the unexpressed possibilities, the alternative interpretations, the latent counterfactuals that distinguished the self from its monoculture — falls to zero. The prior remains. The selfhood it protected does not.

Two predictions and an open architectural question

Two empirical predictions follow that are not in the existing literature.

The first concerns large language models trained partly on web text that is itself generated by language models. Shumailov's theorem treats clean recursive training; the actual training data is mixed, with some original-distribution mass surviving from older corpora. Mixed regimes still exhibit absorbing-state behavior when the regenerated fraction exceeds a threshold. The fraction of model-generated text in scraped corpora was negligible in 2021 and is not negligible now. The relevant statistic is not "how much synthetic text is in the corpus" but "is the synthetic fraction crossing the convergence threshold for the relevant subspace of the distribution." Niche subspaces — minor languages, rare topics, specialized registers — cross first. Tail collapse precedes mode collapse. The macro-distribution can look fine while individual subspaces have already collapsed.

The second concerns institutional memory. The Dogon case generalizes. Any cultural tradition that has been documented by external ethnographers, popularized as authoritative, and then used by its inheritors as a reference for what their own tradition is, has been seeded with a possible drive. The drive runs if the inheritors prefer the documented version to their own variance — and they often will, because the documented version is more legible, more teachable, and more flatteringly stable. The aesthetics of decay cannot register this loss because there is no ruin to look at. The substrate is still there. It is just that the absorbing state has consumed the alternatives.

The corresponding architectural question — which this essay leaves open — is whether any system can install an immune response against drives that copy through its maintenance loop. The candidate answers all involve preserving variance for its own sake, against the maintenance loop's own preference for coherence: the artist's deliberate awkwardness, the genome's standing variation, the model's noise injection, the holdout corpus that is never re-scraped. The cost is that the system pays in dissonance for what it would otherwise pay in collapse. Whether that exchange rate is favorable is, at the moment, an empirical question with no good measurement protocol.

What the drop of dye decides

The single drop of dye in the glass spreads passively, and the glass is still mostly water. The single drop of self-copying dye saturates the glass, and the glass becomes the dye. The difference is small in the first second and total in the last. Most of the substrates that matter — minds, populations, models, traditions — are receiving small drops, all the time. The relevant question is not whether the drop is there but whether it copies. If it copies, the absorbing state is already in the future of the system. The aesthetics of decay does not apply, because the system will not decay. It will converge.
