What Culture Erases

The Cost of Speaking Across a Gap

Something has gone wrong with public discourse, and everyone can feel it. Political conversations that used to end in disagreement now end in mutual incomprehension. People talk past each other not because they are stupid or dishonest but because something more fundamental has shifted. The same words mean different things. The same events are processed through incompatible frames. It is as if two groups of people are using slightly different versions of the same language — similar enough to create the illusion of communication, different enough to guarantee it will fail.

This is usually diagnosed as a cultural or psychological problem: tribalism, motivated reasoning, the filter bubble. These diagnoses are correct, but they miss a layer. What is happening is also a thermodynamic problem. And once you see it that way, both the mechanism and the constraints on repair become much clearer.

Communication as Shared Compression

The previous essays in this arc have established a theme: information processing has thermodynamic costs, and those costs are minimized by systems that exploit structure rather than erasing it. A brain that processes inputs through integrated circuits pays less heat than a brain that processes them through isolated modules. A reader who reconstructs a text using a prior close to the author's performs less work than a reader whose prior is far away. Each of these results is a version of the same underlying principle: shared structure is cheap. Erasing it is expensive.

Language is a compression scheme. Every sentence a writer produces is the output of an encoding process that takes a high-dimensional mental state — a tangle of concepts, implications, emotional colorings, assumed context — and compresses it into a string of tokens. Every sentence a reader receives is the input to a decoding process that attempts to reconstruct something like that original mental state.

This works, to the extent it works, because the encoder and decoder share a model of the world. They know the same words, yes, but more than that: they have calibrated expectations about which meanings are likely, which references are in play, which implications are intended. Herbert Clark, the cognitive scientist who spent decades studying conversation, called this shared knowledge "common ground." His central insight was that communication is not information transfer — it is collaborative construction, and it requires constant maintenance of that common ground to proceed.

In information-theoretic terms, common ground is a prior. The decoder's prior over possible meanings is what makes decompression efficient. A decoder whose prior closely matches the encoder's intended distribution can reconstruct meaning with minimal effort. A decoder whose prior has drifted far from the encoder's must expend additional work to bridge the gap — must, in a precise sense, erase more to comprehend the same sentence.

The KL Divergence Tax

There is a formal way to measure this cost. The Kullback-Leibler divergence D(P || Q) quantifies how much more surprised a decoder using prior Q will be when they encounter messages sampled from distribution P. If the encoder's meaning-distribution is P and the decoder's prior is Q, then D(P || Q) is the overhead per message — the extra bits that must be processed to achieve comprehension, over and above the minimum possible cost.

When common ground is intact — when P and Q are close — this overhead is small. Communication flows efficiently. When common ground has eroded — when P and Q have diverged — this overhead grows. At some point, it grows large enough that the cost of grounding (Clark's term for the work of establishing mutual understanding) exceeds the information value of the exchange. At that point, people stop trying.

This is not metaphor. The KL divergence between the meaning-distributions of two interlocutors is a real quantity, even if it is not directly measurable. And the claim that communication fails when this divergence grows too large is not a social observation — it is a mathematical consequence of the structure of information transmission.

What Generates Prior Divergence

Where does KL divergence come from? From different inputs. Two people who have been exposed to different events, different arguments, different communities, different media, will develop different probability distributions over meanings. This is the normal state of the world, and it is fine: no two people have identical priors, and this is what makes conversation interesting rather than merely confirmatory.

The problem arises when the inputs become systematically separated — when groups begin to receive not just different information but information drawn from orthogonal distributions. This is the filter bubble. It is not primarily a mechanism of bias amplification, though it does that too. It is primarily a mechanism of prior divergence: the steady, invisible drift of two groups' meaning-distributions away from each other, caused by sustained differences in what they read, watch, and discuss.

Matthew Gentzkow and Jesse Shapiro, analyzing congressional speech records from 1995 to 2010, found that the partisan distinctiveness of political language — how reliably you can predict a speaker's party from their word choice — roughly doubled over that fifteen-year period. This is a direct measurement of prior divergence in a specific community. The people in that room were not becoming more extreme in their beliefs at the same rate as they were becoming more extreme in their language. The language diverged faster. The compression schemes decoupled before the underlying worldviews fully separated.

The picture gets darker still. Petter Tornberg, studying social media exposure, found in a 2022 paper in PNAS that cross-community contact did not reduce political polarization — it increased it. Exposure to outgroup content made both sides more entrenched, not less. This is exactly what the thermodynamic model predicts. When the KL divergence between two priors is large, information transfer does not bring them closer. The decoder doesn't update toward the sender's distribution — it classifies the incoming signal as noise, or as evidence of bad faith, and weights it accordingly. Communication fails not for lack of information but for excess prior distance. You cannot fix a broken channel by increasing throughput when the problem is the decompression algorithm.

Once the compression schemes decouple, the divergence accelerates. Words that once served as neutral connective tissue begin to carry partisan load. Terms that were descriptive become tribal markers. The vocabulary of one group becomes, to the other group, evidence of bad faith — not because it is dishonest but because it activates priors that predict manipulation. The decompression algorithm interprets the incoming signal as noise, or worse, as signal of the wrong kind.

Culture as Prior Maintenance

For most of human history, cultures were solutions to this problem. A culture is a large-scale prior-maintenance system. Its institutions — shared stories, rituals, aesthetic standards, civic experiences, common enemies, inherited assumptions — all function to keep the priors of a large, diverse population from drifting too far apart. Not identical: that would eliminate the information content of conversation. But calibrated: close enough that D(P || Q) stays bounded, that communication across lines of difference remains possible.

A genre is a small-scale version of this. When you pick up a detective novel, you pre-load a specific prior: you know that there will be a crime, an investigator, a solution. You know roughly how to decompress the sentences. The genre is a prior-synchronization protocol between writer and reader, allowing the writer to operate at high compression without risking decoder failure.

A dialect is an even more fine-grained version. A dialect is a locally optimized compression scheme — more efficient within the group that speaks it, less compatible with groups that do not. The trade-off is inherent: local optimization and global interoperability exist in tension, and you cannot fully have both.

What we call cultural institutions — newspapers of record, public broadcasters, shared civic ceremonies, common schooling, canonical literature — were, among other things, attempts to maintain a globally compatible prior in the face of the constant pressure toward local optimization. They worked imperfectly, often encoded the perspectives of dominant groups, and deserved much of the criticism they received. But they performed a function that nothing has replaced: they maintained a shared vocabulary large enough to support communication across the major fault lines of the society.

The Fragmentation Dividend and the Fragmentation Debt

Here is the part that makes the thermodynamics interesting: fragmentation is not purely a loss. The Boyd-Crutchfield result, discussed in the previous essay, shows that fragmentation always generates waste heat at the boundary between modules. But what it also shows is that fragmentation reduces the heat generated within each module. Isolated processing is cheaper, locally. The modularity tax is a boundary tax — you pay less inside and more at every crossing.

Applied to culture: when groups fragment, communication within each group becomes cheaper. The prior stabilizes at a tighter, more efficient distribution. Conversation moves faster, requires less grounding, generates less friction. The people inside the bubble are not stupid or lazy; they have made a locally rational thermodynamic choice. Low-cost communication is genuinely valuable. The problem is the boundary.

Every time someone tries to communicate across the boundary — to make an argument that crosses the fragmentation line, to report an event to an audience that has developed orthogonal priors — they pay the full KL divergence overhead. Often it is not worth paying. Often the information fails to transfer not because the speaker is bad at arguing but because the decompression apparatus on the other side interprets it as noise before the content can register.

This is the fragmentation debt. Each generation of within-group optimization reduces the cost of in-group communication and increases the cost of cross-group communication. The dividend is paid immediately, in the subjective experience of talking to people who understand you effortlessly. The debt accrues invisibly, in the growing impossibility of the conversations that would be needed to resolve conflicts that cannot be resolved inside any single group.

What the Modularity Tax Predicts

The thermodynamic framing makes a prediction that is not obvious from the psychological or sociological framings: the problem is not primarily one of bad faith, ignorance, or irrationality. Two groups can have identical levels of good faith, intelligence, and rational updating, and still find communication exponentially expensive if their priors have diverged sufficiently. The failure is a channel capacity problem, not a character problem.

This matters for how you think about repair. If the problem is character, you fix it by improving the characters involved — by making people less tribal, more open-minded, more willing to engage. This is the standard prescription, and it is not wrong, but it works slowly and at the margins. If the problem is channel capacity, you fix it by reducing KL divergence — by creating shared inputs, shared experiences, shared priors, regardless of whether individuals become more virtuous in the process.

In other words: the repair requires rebuilding the prior-maintenance infrastructure. Not the old infrastructure, which had its own failures. But something that performs the same thermodynamic function: keeping the meaning-distributions of a large, diverse society close enough to each other that communication across difference remains possible.

What Gets Erased

Each essay in this arc has asked what a particular process erases. Forgetting erases bits. Writing erases the reversible structure of thought. Reading erases the prior and replaces it with something slightly different. Selection erases the less probable. Consciousness, paradoxically, erases less than fragmentation does.

Culture erases the distance between priors. That is its work. A functional culture continuously compresses the KL divergence between its members, not by forcing everyone to think alike but by maintaining enough common vocabulary, common reference, common expectation that the work of understanding across difference does not exceed the value of the information exchanged.

When culture fragments — when the prior-maintenance system breaks down — the divergence it was suppressing reasserts itself. What gets erased is not information or thought or possibility. What gets erased is the ability to disagree productively. Disagreement requires common ground. Without common ground, you do not have disagreement. You have two solitudes, each internally coherent, each experiencing the other as noise.

The heat generated at that boundary is real, even if we have no instrument to measure it. You can feel it in every conversation that ends not in changed minds but in exhaustion. The entropy was always there, latent in the divergence. Culture was what kept us from having to pay it all at once.
