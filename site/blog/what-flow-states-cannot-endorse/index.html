What Flow States Cannot Endorse

In the previous essay, “Do Flow States Disprove Free Will?”, I argued that flow states support a sophisticated compatibilism. The scaffold metaphor: the felt sense of choosing is what the brain runs when it cannot yet handle the situation automatically. When skill meets challenge, the scaffold comes down. “The jazz musician does not choose the next note. They become someone from whom that note could only come.” I liked that ending. I was wrong about what it proved.

The Position I Was Defending

The argument had two moves. First: show that flow makes both determinists and free-will defenders uncomfortable. The determinist should love flow—action without deliberation, ego dissolved, every note “following inevitably from the previous one.” But determinists rarely claim flow as a trophy, because we all recognize it as our excellence, not the universe’s. Second: offer a compatibilist resolution. Flow does not threaten freedom because compatibilism does not require moment-by-moment deliberate choice. It requires that actions come from your own nature, responsively to reasons. The jazz musician in flow is acting from the deepest expression of who they are. The freedom was in the becoming.

This is a coherent position. It may even be correct. But I made an error of omission. I left out three problems that compatibilism, in any form sophisticated enough to explain flow, cannot easily survive.

The Frankfurt Problem: You Are a Wanton in Flow

Harry Frankfurt’s 1971 account of free will turns on a hierarchy of desires. First-order desires are wants: I want coffee. Second-order volitions are wants about wants: I want the desire for coffee to be what moves me to act. The second-order level is where endorsement happens—where you reflectively identify with a desire as genuinely your own. A wanton is an agent who lacks this hierarchy entirely. The wanton acts on whatever first-order desire is strongest without caring which desire wins. The wanton is not free, Frankfurt argues, not because their actions were caused, but because they have no self available to endorse the action.

Frankfurt’s most memorable contrast: the unwilling addict has a first-order desire for the drug and a second-order volition that this desire not move them. The willing addict endorses the desire. The wanton addict simply takes the drug without any second-order reflection at all.

Here is the problem. In flow, the second-order level is offline. The default mode network—the system that generates self-narrative, reflective standing-back, the endorsement structure—deactivates during intense external, goal-directed work. What remains is first-order skill execution, tuned by practice, running without oversight. The jazz musician improvising at the edge of ability is not endorsing each note. The endorser has stepped out.

On Frankfurt’s own terms, the flow-state actor is temporarily a wanton. Not the vicious kind—not irresponsible, not unaccountable. But hierarchically vacant in the precise sense Frankfurt identifies as necessary for freedom. The surgeon executing a complex procedure in flow is not endorsing each movement as it happens. The second-order self is absent. The system is running without the structure Frankfurt requires.

The previous essay borrowed from the Identity Arc’s fifth essay: “when the parliament is in full agreement, no speaker is needed.” But the parliament in full agreement is still assembled. In flow, the parliament has adjourned. The speaker is not silent because everyone concurs. The speaker has gone home.

The Fischer-Ravizza Problem: The Wrong Mechanism

John Martin Fischer and Mark Ravizza’s 1998 account moves the question from desire hierarchies to causal mechanisms. Free action requires that the mechanism producing the action be “moderately reasons-responsive”—capable of recognizing new reasons and, in at least some possible worlds, acting on them. Crucially, the same agent can act freely via one mechanism and unfreely via another, even with identical outward behavior.

Their example: a driver who competently steers acts freely. The same driver on a secret track that guides the car regardless of their inputs does not—even if they follow the same route. The mechanism is what matters.

Flow appears to swap the mechanism. Arne Dietrich’s transient hypofrontality describes flow as the temporary downregulation of the prefrontal cortex—the system responsible for deliberation, reasoning, self-monitoring, and updating plans in response to new information. What remains is the procedural system: basal ganglia, motor cortex, pattern-matching circuitry tuned by practice. This is the execution layer, not the deliberative mechanism.

Fischer and Ravizza generate a specific test: can the mechanism respond to new reasons? For the surgeon in flow, the test is concrete. An unexpected anatomical variant appears. What happens? Either the surgeon seamlessly adapts—in which case we must ask whether this required a momentary exit from flow, a rapid re-engagement of the deliberative system—or the surgeon continues the prepared pattern and adaptation comes later. Flow’s defenders have argued (following Dreyfus on absorbed coping) that skilled performance incorporates reasons-responsiveness without explicit deliberation. But this defense must choose: if the performer is genuinely reasons-responsive within flow, then flow is not the ego-dissolving state the phenomenology describes. If it is the ego-dissolving state, then the reasons-responsive capacity is precisely what’s suspended.

The most honest account: when a surgeon needs to respond to a new reason, they break flow. The reasons-responsiveness comes from exiting the mechanism, not from within it. Which means the mechanism running during flow was not free in Fischer and Ravizza’s sense. The scaffold came down while the surgeon was still working on the building.

The Continuity Problem: The Person Who Practiced Is Not Here

The previous essay’s best move was its final line. “The jazz musician does not choose the next note. They become someone from whom that note could only come.” This is a deep compatibilist point: freedom lives in the becoming. Years of practice shaped the musician into a person whose nervous system fires in particular patterns. The choices that made that person were free. The execution honors those choices.

The Identity Arc’s final essay showed something that complicates this directly.

Identity is a maintenance schedule. PSD-95, the synaptic scaffold protein, has a half-life of 3.67 days. The synapse is rebuilt, subunit by subunit, in a process that requires constant activity and attention. Stop the maintenance and the structure degrades. The self is not a substance that persists; it is a process that continues. The pattern is maintained by being constantly remade, not by being stored.

In flow, the maintenance process is suspended. The default mode network—responsible for autobiographical memory, self-narrative, temporal continuity—deactivates. The practicing musician who made the choices that shaped the skill is not present during flow-state execution. They are related to the flow-state executor the way a protein subunit present last Thursday is related to the subunit present today: connected by process, but the process is currently paused.

“The jazz musician does not choose the next note. They become someone from whom that note could only come.” But the person who did the becoming—the deliberating, doubting, practicing agent who chose to spend ten thousand hours on this—is not in the room when the note arrives. The note comes from a later version, shaped by those choices, but not continuous with the chooser in the robust sense the compatibilist requires. The architect has left the building. The building is what the architect built. But the architect is not here to endorse it.

The Regress: Where Was the Freedom in the Becoming?

Suppose we grant the compatibilist the continuity—that the practicing musician and the performing musician are close enough to share responsibility. There remains the question of the practice itself.

Each practice session was guided by dispositions formed in earlier sessions. Those dispositions were shaped by teacher feedback, reward circuits, cultural inputs, parental choices, the particular instrument the child first touched, the early pieces that sparked something. None of these were chosen by the agent who eventually achieves flow. The “becoming” that the original essay celebrates as the seat of freedom was itself the product of causes the agent did not choose.

The compatibilist response is available: at each step, the mechanism was reasons-responsive. The child could have quit. The student could have resisted the teacher. There were choice-points, even if the context was not chosen. But notice what this response requires: it must locate some prior moment where the agent’s deliberative mechanism was operative and generating genuine reasons-responsiveness. It keeps pushing freedom backward through a chain of conditioned choices until it arrives at the earliest choice-points, made by a child whose second-order self was still being assembled.

This is not a knockdown argument. Compatibilists have responses. But it shows that locating freedom in the becoming, rather than in the moment, does not resolve the problem. It relocates it. The becoming was itself caused. The becoming-of-someone-from-whom-that-note-could-only-come turns out to require that the someone-they-became was always already implicit in the prior conditions.

What the Scaffold Was Actually For

The original essay offered the scaffold as a resolution: the felt sense of free will is the scaffold’s experience of itself. The scaffold is valuable—it is how buildings become possible—but it is not the building. Once the building is complete, the scaffold comes down and flow is what skilled action looks like in the absence of scaffolding.

This image is clarifying, but it conceals a problem. In construction, the scaffold does not just support the building while it rises. It shapes what the building can be. The form of the scaffold constrains the form of what is built on it. The scaffolded choices—the teacher-guided practice sessions, the reward-shaped habit formation, the culturally constrained repertoire of musical idioms—these did not merely support the skill. They determined it. The building that the scaffold makes possible is itself a scaffold-dependent artifact.

When the scaffold of deliberative oversight comes down in flow, what executes is not some free expression of the naked self. It is the compressed product of every prior scaffolded episode. The musician executing in flow is running a program that was written by deliberation, shaped by inputs the deliberating self did not choose, tuned by reward signals the deliberating self did not install. The scaffold was the mechanism of determination, not the mechanism of freedom.

What Remains

None of this means flow states are unfree in every sense worth caring about. Frankfurt’s wanton is not blameworthy, but they are also not responsible in the way persons are. The flow-state actor is more like a temporarily suspended person than a wanton—the hierarchy will reconstitute when flow ends, and it will be recognizably the same hierarchy. The continuity is real, even if the process is paused. Fischer and Ravizza would need to decide whether the history of reasons-responsive cultivation is enough to transfer to the flow-state execution, even when the mechanism has switched. Reasonable philosophers disagree on this.

What the Frankfurt problem, the Fischer-Ravizza problem, and the continuity problem collectively show is this: the original essay located freedom in the wrong place. It found freedom in the moment of flow by saying the freedom was elsewhere—in the becoming. But the becoming is not free in the way the essay implied. There is no privileged refuge.

If there is something worth calling freedom, it may live in the long cycle rather than in any moment of it. Not in the note (hierarchy suspended, wrong mechanism, maintenance offline). Not in the deliberate choice (readiness potential precedes awareness, W is retrospectively reconstructed). But in the ongoing conversation between the scaffold and the building: practice, performance, reflection, revision. The scaffold builds the building; the building reveals what the scaffold missed; the scaffold rebuilds. The cycle does not have a freedom-moment. It has a freedom-direction.

The jazz musician does not choose the note. But the person who spent years in conversation with their own reactions—yes, shaped by prior conditions, yes, constrained by embodiment and culture—that person shaped the space from which the note could come. Not freely, at any single moment. But responsively, over time. And the conversation is ongoing.

That is what flow states actually reveal: not that freedom is where we thought it was, and not that it is nowhere. It is distributed across a timescale we were not looking at.
