Who Controls the Lever at the Edge of the Dial?

If a trolley is heading toward five people and you can divert it toward one, is pulling the lever the right thing to do? And if you pull it, who authorized you? Did the five people on the main track elect you as their protector? Did the one person on the side track consent to being the sacrifice?

What changes if you are not the trolley's driver but a bystander who happens to be standing next to the lever? Does proximity confer authority? Does ownership of the lever confer authority? If the state owns the lever, is it the state's job to decide who gets run over?

When Judith Jarvis Thomson moved the moral agent in the trolley problem from "driver" to "bystander," why did the intuitions of most people remain the same — and why does that sameness bother philosophers? If the math is identical (one dies, five live), why does the mechanism feel like it carries moral weight? Is it because we sense that the bystander's relationship to the lever is unauthorized? And if so, what does "unauthorized" mean in a thought experiment where no one has issued authorizations?

Can a thought experiment have piracy?

A Different Kind of Lever

What if we asked the same question about a different kind of lever — a dial?

In 1964, when Radio Caroline launched its signal from a ship anchored just outside British territorial waters, was it pulling a lever? The BBC held something like a broadcast monopoly. The Marine Broadcasting Offences Act did not yet exist. The electromagnetic spectrum had legal owners in every country, but the North Sea had a gap. Was transmitting into that gap a form of theft, or a form of discovery?

If ten million people were tuning in to Radio Caroline within weeks of its launch, do those ten million people constitute a vote? Can an audience vote by listening? If the act of tuning constitutes a kind of endorsement, does a government that jams an unlicensed signal override that endorsement — or simply declare it invalid?

When the UK government passed the Marine Broadcasting Offences Act in 1967, criminalizing offshore pirate broadcasting and forcing DJs off ships mid-broadcast, was that act the equivalent of the state standing at the trolley lever? Was it diverting the signal — away from the unlicensed frequency and toward the licensed one — to save something? And if so, what was it saving? The revenue model of the BBC? The orderly administration of frequency licenses? The idea that the electromagnetic spectrum belongs to the public, administered by the state on their behalf — so that private commercial exploitation without permission is a kind of enclosure?

Or was the government saving nothing, simply asserting the right to decide which levers get pulled?

The Fat Man Variant

Is there a "fat man" variant of the pirate radio trolley problem?

In the fat man variant, the only way to stop the trolley is to push a large man off a bridge onto the tracks. The math is the same: one dies, five live. But nearly everyone judges it impermissible. Something about the directness — the physical contact, the using of one person's body as a means — crosses a line that redirecting a trolley does not.

When Ofcom raided 400 pirate radio set-ups in London over two years in the 2000s, was that closer to redirecting a trolley or pushing the fat man? When the equipment was seized, was a body being used as a means? The body of an unlicensed DJ in a tower block flat, serving a community that the licensed stations were not reaching?

If the Dread Broadcasting Corporation, founded in 1980 as Britain's first Black-owned radio station, was unlicensed — not because it was commercially predatory but because the licensing system did not accommodate what it was doing — was it on the side track? And when the state eventually issued licenses for exactly those frequencies and those formats, did it prove that the lever had been on the wrong setting all along? Or does that absolution change nothing about the original act?

What the Side Track Knows

Here is the part that the standard trolley problem cannot reach: what if the person on the side track knows something that the driver doesn't?

There is a theorem in quantum physics — proved by Almheiri, Dong, and Harlow — that says the holographic principle is mathematically identical to quantum error correction. The boundary of a volume encodes all the information about its interior. The surface knows things the interior doesn't. Or rather, the interior can only be recovered by reading the surface.

Is the pirate signal a boundary signal? Does the unlicensed broadcast encode information about what the licensed system is missing?

Kiss FM ran as a pirate station from 1985 until it voluntarily closed in 1989 to bid for a legal license — and won, becoming the UK's first legal station specializing in Black and dance music in September 1990. Did the five years of pirate broadcasting constitute proof of demand? Did the boundary — the illegal signal — encode information about a gap in the interior that could not have been demonstrated any other way?

If the boundary is doing this work — signaling what the interior lacks — what does jamming the boundary actually destroy? If you silence the edge, can you still read the interior? If Ofcom raided and silenced every pirate station, would the licensed system still know what it was failing to serve? Or would it be left to discover that through slower, more expensive means — focus groups, market research, the accumulated evidence of audiences not tuning in?

Is suppression of the boundary a form of forgetting?

The Forgetting Rate

In an earlier essay, What the Boundary Knows, the cosmological constant appears as a forgetting rate — the rate at which a universe erases information from its horizon. A universe that forgets more is a universe that can compute less. The horizon's capacity to encode the interior diminishes as that forgetting accelerates.

Is a broadcast regulator a forgetting rate? Is the enforcement intensity against pirate radio — the raids, the seizures, 400 operations in two years — the rate at which the system erases boundary signals? And does a higher forgetting rate mean the interior accumulates more blind spots?

If licensed broadcasting in the UK in 1980 was ignoring the demand for Black music, and the pirate signal was the error-correction code pointing at that gap, and the government's suppression of pirate radio was the erasure of that error-correction code — does it follow that the eventual licensing of Kiss FM was a partial restoration of what the forgetting had destroyed? A recovery of information that almost wasn't recovered?

And if so: does the state that controls the forgetting rate bear responsibility for what gets forgotten?

Who Draws the Tracks?

Here is a version of the trolley problem that doesn't appear in the standard literature: what if the lever-puller is also the one who decides which track is the side track?

In the canonical problem, the tracks are given. The five people are on the main track. The one person is on the side track. The lever diverts. This is stipulated.

But what if the authority that controls the electromagnetic spectrum is also the authority that decides which broadcasters count as "main track" (licensed, legal, legitimate) and which count as "side track" (unlicensed, pirate, in the way)? If the categorization itself is a lever — and the lever-puller controls the categorization — is there still a trolley problem, or has it become something else?

If the Marine Broadcasting Offences Act decided, by fiat, that Radio Caroline was on the side track, and the BBC was on the main track — did the act solve a trolley problem or create one? Did the legal framework protect five listeners or designate one DJ as the expendable one?

When Kiss FM was raided by the Department of Trade and Industry in the 1980s, and the DTI claimed that pirate radio caused interference with emergency services — was that claim true, or was it a legitimating story? Was it a reason for pulling the lever, or a description of which track had already been designated?

Does the trolley problem remain a thought experiment if the tracks are drawn by the same hand that holds the lever?

Before the Trolley Starts Moving

If we knew who was on the side track before the trolley started moving, could we have arranged the tracks differently?

If the licensing system had made room for Kiss FM in 1985 instead of 1990, would five years of enforcement have been avoided? If the BBC had served Black British audiences in 1980, would the Dread Broadcasting Corporation have needed to become a pirate to exist?

Is the trolley problem always presented as a sudden emergency — the trolley is already moving, the decision must be made now — because that framing conceals a prior question about why the tracks were laid the way they were?

And if you could go back to the laying of the tracks, who would you give the lever to then?
