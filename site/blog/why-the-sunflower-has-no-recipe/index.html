Why the Sunflower Has No Recipe

A sunflower head holds about two thousand seeds, arranged so well that no two press more than necessary against any other. The arrangement is famous: each new seed forms at a specific angle from the previous one, 137.508 degrees, the "golden angle." The number is so famous that we forget the strangeness of the situation.

Where is that number written? Where in the plant does the instruction live? You will not find 137.5 anywhere in the genome. You will not find a clock that counts to 137.5. You will not find any structure that could count, because each seed appears before its successor exists; the angle is not measured from a reference, it is set by what was there last.

The recipe is the substrate. The number has no manuscript.

What "no manuscript" means

For most things we call "a rule," we can locate the rule somewhere apart from the thing it governs. Tax codes are in books. Cake recipes are in kitchens. A chess rule lives in the rulebook and the rule is the same whether anyone is playing or not.

But there are processes for which no such separation exists. A sunflower has no rulebook of phyllotaxis (the technical name for the arrangement of leaves, petals, and seeds; from the Greek phyllon, leaf, and taxis, arrangement). The "rule" is something like: the next primordium (a small bump of cells that will become a seed or a leaf) forms at the largest available gap on the meristem (the growing dome at the tip of the stem). That is the rule Wilhelm Hofmeister found in 1868[1]. He did not find a number. He found a behavior.

What follows from a behavior is not what follows from a number. The number 137.5 is what the behavior produces over many iterations, not what the behavior consults. Stéphane Douady and Yves Couder confirmed this directly in 1992[2]: drop magnetized ferrofluid droplets one at a time into a dish where they repel each other and drift outward. Within a few drops, the system settles into the golden angle. Nothing in the dish knows about Fibonacci. Nothing in the dish knows about plants. The angle is the only stable answer to "where do I go so I am farthest from everyone who is already here," iterated.

This is what knowledge-in-form means. The rule cannot be lifted off the running of it. The pattern is the residue of the process, not its plan.

Three domains, same architecture

Phyllotaxis is one example. Two more, chosen for their distance from each other, make the architecture visible.

Prion replication

A prion (the word is a compression of "proteinaceous infectious particle," coined by Stanley Prusiner in 1982[3]) is a protein that, when it bumps into a normally-folded copy of the same protein, makes the normal copy refold into the prion shape. There is no DNA in the prion. There is no code. The "instruction" is the shape, and the shape replicates by templating any compatible protein it meets.

This was not believed at first. Proteins were thought to require nucleic acid templates to be passed on; the central dogma was that information flowed DNA to RNA to protein, never protein to protein. The 1967 hypothesis of John Griffith[4] sat for fifteen years before Prusiner's purification of the scrapie agent, and another decade before Keh-Ming Pan and colleagues demonstrated in 1993 that the conversion is a refolding from alpha-helix to beta-sheet (a change in the protein's local geometry from a coil shape to a flat pleated shape)[5]. The "rule" of prion replication is the same kind of rule as the rule of phyllotaxis. There is no rule apart from the iteration of contact.

Traditional ecological knowledge

When the United Nations Convention on Biological Diversity in 1992 recognized "traditional ecological knowledge," the accumulated practice-based knowledge of indigenous communities about the places they have lived, it created an administrative problem: how do you preserve a knowledge that has no text? Brent Berlin's 1992 study of ethnobiological classification[6] documented that indigenous classifications of plants and animals are largely consistent across cultures (against the prediction that they would be arbitrary). The consistency lives in the practice of harvesting, naming, telling, and using, not in any written taxonomy.

When the knowledge is textified (extracted into written rules), the rules are correct as far as they go, but the knowledge is lost. Fikret Berkes's Sacred Ecology (1999) catalogs cases[7]. A fishing rule extracted from a fishing community becomes a regulation. The regulation does not adapt. The fishery collapses. The recipe is the substrate. Take the recipe out of the substrate and you have not preserved it. You have replaced it with something else, and the something else does not run.

This is the same architecture as the sunflower and the prion. The instruction is not in a document. The instruction is the practice of doing the thing.

The three failure modes

If form-knowledge is the same architecture in three domains, the failures should be the same architecture too. They are not. Each domain breaks in its own way, and the breaks are diagnostic.

Phyllotaxis fails by rationalization

The botanical literature spent the nineteenth century looking for the "law" of phyllotaxis. The brothers Auguste and Louis Bravais in 1837 noticed Fibonacci ratios in the spiral counts of pinecones[8]. For decades, every spiral was counted, every ratio was tabulated, and theorists proposed that the plant "computes" the golden ratio. Helmut Vogel in 1979 gave a beautiful formula that reproduces the sunflower head exactly[9]: place the n-th seed at radius proportional to the square root of n, at angle n times 137.508 degrees.

The formula is right. It is also misleading. A plant does not run Vogel's formula. Vogel's formula is what the plant's behavior produces in the limit. To mistake the limit for the process is to think the sunflower has a recipe. The failure is not in the formula. The failure is in believing the formula is what the sunflower consults.

Prions fail by runaway replication

In healthy tissue, misfolded proteins are usually caught by molecular chaperones (helper proteins that detect bad folds and either refold them or hand them to the proteasome, the cell's protein shredder) and degraded. Prion proteins fold around these defenses. The beta-sheet shape they take is stable enough to resist the chaperones, the chaperones are too weak to refold them, and the proteasome cannot cleave their tightly-stacked sheets[10].

The same kind of templating rule that grows a sunflower head with bounded curvature instead grows a plaque that does not stop. There is no stopper. Didier Reinhardt's group in 2003[11] showed that phyllotaxis has a stopper built in. Each new primordium depletes the local auxin (a plant growth hormone) through directional transport mediated by a protein called PIN1, creating an exclusion zone in which no other primordium can form for a while. The exclusion zone is the difference between a sunflower and a death. Remove the depletion, and the same local rule produces unchecked accumulation.

TEK fails by textification

The conservation literature is full of cases where indigenous practice was written down, the written form was implemented as policy, and the policy failed because the practice had been responsive to conditions the writing could not name. The practice integrated weather, water level, the appearance of certain birds, the colors of certain leaves, the timing of a particular flower's bloom: variables that change every year. The text was static. The community could not amend the text because the text had been adopted by a regulatory authority for whom amendment meant rule-making delay, public comment, environmental review[12].

The recipe got separated from the substrate. What was left was not the knowledge. It was an artifact of the writing.

What was load-bearing

The diagnostic value of the three failure modes is that each tells you what was holding the system up.

Phyllotaxis tells you the iteration is load-bearing: each step is the input to the next, no step can be skipped, and the limiting pattern only exists at the limit. If you try to write a recipe that names the limiting pattern, you have skipped what produces it.

Prions tell you the stopper is load-bearing: a templating rule without an exclusion zone is not a generator, it is a cascade. The same local rule that makes form when bounded makes plaque when unbounded.

TEK tells you the substrate is load-bearing: the rule was a property of the running, and the running was a property of the substrate. Lift one off the other and the rule stops being what it was.

This is the architecture the Iliad piece traced in oral epic: the song was a knowledge whose body was the singer, and Athenian standardization replaced that knowledge with something stable that did not adapt. It is what "How a Wrong Shape Spreads" traced in prion replication, QWERTY, and engagement algorithms: the wrong configuration converts the substrate's repair budget into its own propagation. It is what "Geometry of Exclusion" traced in golden-ratio packing and ant-colony search: the answer is what survives systematic exclusion of the unstable. And it is what "DNA Storage and the Golden Angle" traced in the engineering re-use of phyllotaxis: orthogonal addressing under noise is the cheap path to error correction. Phyllotaxis, prions, and indigenous practice sit at three different corners of the same shape.

What this says about knowledge

Tacit knowledge, the kind a craftsman has but cannot fully articulate, was named by Michael Polanyi in 1958[13]. He argued that all knowing has a tacit dimension, that "we know more than we can tell." Polanyi presented this as a property of the knower. The case of phyllotaxis suggests that the tacit is not only in the knower. It is in the system.

The sunflower is not "knowing more than it can tell." The sunflower is the only place the knowledge could be. There is nowhere else to put it. The knowledge of how to pack two thousand seeds into a flat disc with maximum efficiency does not exist as a proposition that the sunflower somehow lacks the vocabulary to express. The knowledge exists as a packing.

This complicates the picture inherited from the explicit-knowledge tradition. We tend to imagine knowledge as something that could in principle be written, even if we have not yet found the words. The tacit is just unverbalized explicit, the thinking goes; given enough time and good observers, it can be made textual. But the prion, the sunflower, and the fishing community say no. Some knowledge has no other body than the body it lives in. If you remove the body, the knowledge does not survive intact and unread. It ceases to be the knowledge it was.

The manuscript that was never written

In 1919, Romain Rolland drafted the Declaration of the Independence of the Mind, signed by hundreds of the era's most prominent thinkers, including Albert Einstein, Bertrand Russell, Hermann Hesse, Jane Addams, and Rabindranath Tagore[14]. It was published in L'Humanité on June 26th of that year. The text declared the mind's freedom from the propaganda machinery of the just-ended war. The text is largely forgotten today.

It deserves the comparison because it did everything textification can do. It named, it bounded, it called for action. It was the mind in propositional form: a manifesto, signed, dated, distributed. The substrate it sought to defend was not propositional. It was the practice of reading carefully, of letting doubt have its weight, of attending to the world rather than to one's certainty about it. That practice has substrate but not text. When the substrate decayed, when the universities and the magazines and the daily habits of attention decayed, the manuscript could not save it. The manifesto was a recipe that did not run.

A sunflower has no manuscript. That is not a defect. That is the only condition under which it could be a sunflower.
