The Minimum Generative Set

Sometime around 1914, Piet Mondrian eliminated the diagonal from his paintings. He had been moving toward this for years — through impressionism, through symbolism, through the fractured geometry of Cubism — and now he arrived at something he described not as a choice but as a discovery. From that point forward, all his lines would run horizontal or vertical. Primary colors only. No curves, no diagonals, no references to any particular object. The vocabulary was fixed.

He spent the next thirty years inside it.

This is usually told as the story of an aesthetic commitment, a spiritual practice, an artist's decades-long devotion to a single formal idea. That framing is not wrong. But there is another way to read Mondrian's career — a way that connects it to a proof published four years after his death by a mathematician who had never heard of neoplasticism.

What Von Neumann Was Looking For

In the late 1940s, John von Neumann asked a question that sounded deceptively simple: can a machine build a copy of itself? Not a simple duplicator — a system that would stamp out identical copies from raw material — but a genuine self-replicator: a machine capable of constructing any machine given a description, including a description of itself.

The question mattered because von Neumann suspected that biology had already answered it. Cells replicate. They construct new cellular machinery from instructions encoded in DNA. Whatever mechanism made that possible, it must be formalizable — and if it was formalizable, there should be a minimum complexity below which it becomes impossible.

His proof, published in 1951 and completed posthumously by Arthur Burks, identified exactly that minimum. A universal constructor requires four components: a universal construction arm (which builds any machine from a description), a tape containing the description, a copier (which copies the tape), and a controller (which sequences the operations). The minimum state count in his specific cellular automaton model was 29 — not a round number, not an approximation, but the floor he found for that particular formalization.

The structural insight was stranger than the number. The description — the tape — had to be used twice. First as instructions, interpreted and executed by the constructor. Then as data, copied verbatim and inserted into the offspring. Same object, two incompatible functions. This dual-use requirement is not a design choice. It is a logical necessity: the only way to close the self-replication loop is to treat the description as simultaneously a program and a passive string of symbols. Remove either function and the loop opens. The machine can build or copy but not both, which means it cannot self-replicate.

Von Neumann didn't discover this dual-use structure — he proved it was unavoidable. And when Crick and Watson described DNA in 1953, two years after the paper appeared, biologists recognized immediately that DNA had independently arrived at the same constraint: it is both transcribed (read as instructions) and replicated (copied as data). The convergence was not a coincidence. It was the formal structure insisting on itself.

Mondrian's Implicit Claim

Mondrian was not asking about self-replication. He was asking about something he called pure plastic art — the minimum visual vocabulary from which universal harmony could be generated. His answer: perpendicular lines, three primary colors, black, white, gray, and asymmetric balance. Not as a restriction on painting, but as its irreducible foundation.

His 1937 essay "Plastic Art and Pure Plastic Art" makes claims that are formally structured even if they're not mathematically formalized. The right angle is not an aesthetic preference, he argues — it is the primary relationship in nature, the intersection of opposing forces that underlies all structure. Primary colors are irreducible chromatic units; mixing produces secondary colors, which are derivative, which contain implicit references to the mixing process and through it to the material world. Asymmetric balance is not an optional dynamism — it is the principle distinguishing a living system from a static one. Symmetry is death. The asymmetric grid is alive.

Each element is strictly necessary, and he demonstrated this through thirty years of empirical testing. Remove color entirely: you get the black-line drawings that underlie De Stijl architecture and furniture design, but you lose the dynamic tension between competing forces that makes painting work. Remove perpendicularity: the diagonal enters, and with it the pull of representation — a diagonal line can't help implying a falling object, a receding plane, a particular thing. Remove asymmetry: the composition freezes. The canvas becomes an emblem rather than an event.

What Mondrian was discovering — iteratively, over decades, without formal language — is what a mathematician would call a minimal spanning basis: the smallest set of elements capable of generating the full range of valid outputs. In linear algebra, a basis spans a space while remaining irreducible. Remove any element and the span collapses. Mondrian's grammar is claimed to be exactly that: irreducible and sufficient.

The Gap

Here is the study that was never done.

In 2005, researchers Holcombe, Smith, and Merewood published a formal, rigorous notation for Mondrian's paintings — a system precise enough to describe any Mondrian work as a formal specification. In 2022, Doboli, Zahedi, and Gholamrezaei developed computational methods to distinguish genuine Mondrian paintings from imitations, working from the grammar's formal properties. Computational approaches to De Stijl have continued to accumulate, with each new paper treating the vocabulary as a well-defined formal object.

But none of these papers asks the completeness question. They treat Mondrian's grammar as a useful formal object without asking whether it is a complete formal object. The question no one has posed: is the De Stijl vocabulary a minimum spanning set for visual harmony? Can you prove that the grammar is at the floor — that any further simplification would cause the generative capacity to collapse?

Von Neumann asked this question about self-replication and answered it. Mondrian asked it about visual harmony and answered it empirically. The formal proof has not been attempted.

This is the gap. Two irreducible systems, found independently in different centuries by people working in different disciplines, converging on the same structural claim — and the connection between them hasn't been made in print.

Why The Same Structure Appears Twice

The parallel is not a coincidence of form. Both von Neumann and Mondrian were working on what might be called the minimum generativity problem: given a target space of outputs (all self-replicating machines; all visual harmonies), what is the minimum sufficient vocabulary for reaching it?

The answer in both cases has the same architecture. First: each element of the minimum set is strictly necessary — not just useful, but required for the system to function at all. Second: the elements interact in ways that cannot be decomposed — the constructor needs the tape, the tape needs the copier, the copier needs the controller, and removing any one of them breaks the system entirely. Mondrian's perpendicularity needs his primaries to activate the tension; his primaries need his asymmetry to prevent stasis; his asymmetry needs perpendicularity to remain legible as balance rather than randomness.

Third, and strangest: in both systems, the same element performs incompatible functions simultaneously. In von Neumann's constructor, the description is both program and data. In Mondrian's paintings, the grid is both the subject of the work (the universal relational form) and its method (the grammar generates the composition). The painting demonstrates the grammar, and the grammar is what is being painted. This is not a paradox — it is the same self-referential loop that makes self-replication possible: the description folds back on itself.

What The Prior Work Missed

In an earlier essay here on Mondrian's grid and temporal rhythm, I argued that the perpendicular grid can encode time without depicting it — that vertical and horizontal tension produces something like rhythmic experience rather than a static freeze. That essay asked what the grid does. This one asks why it cannot be simplified.

The Encoding Arc on this site has traced a different thread: how codes achieve transmissibility through arbitrary structure. In "Why Arbitrary Codes Travel Farther", the argument was that a code severed from what it represents — a cut that makes the code arbitrary — is precisely what allows it to cross domains. Mondrian's cut from representation (eliminating the depicted object entirely) is the same severance. The De Stijl vocabulary traveled to architecture, to furniture design, to typography, to urban planning, to graphic design — to every domain where visual information needs to be organized — because it was no longer about anything in particular. Arbitrary severance is the condition of universality.

But the question left open by that essay is: how arbitrary? You cannot sever everything. Some structure has to remain, or there is nothing to transmit. Von Neumann's answer: you need at minimum a constructor, a tape, a copier, and a controller, in this specific relationship. Mondrian's answer: you need at minimum perpendicular lines, primary colors, and asymmetric balance, in this specific relationship.

Both answers say: you can go this far and no farther. Here is the floor.

The Implication

If Mondrian's vocabulary is a genuine minimum spanning basis — if you could prove it — the implication for his biography is strange. His thirty years of iterative painting would retroactively become a formal derivation. Each canvas was a test: can this element be removed? If the painting collapsed (lost dynamism, reacquired reference, froze into emblem), the element was necessary. If it survived, the element was redundant and should be eliminated in the next iteration.

This is not how art history reads the sequence. Art history reads it as spiritual development, as the journey of an artist toward purity. That reading is not wrong — Mondrian himself described it in spiritual terms, and his intentions matter. But intentions don't exhaust the formal structure of what someone is doing. A poet who develops a new meter through intuition has still developed a metrical system, even if they would describe it differently. A geometer who discovers Euclid's fifth postulate is independent of the others has still proved something, even if they call it an aesthetic insight.

Mondrian was running an experiment. The experiment had stopping conditions: keep simplifying until simplification causes collapse. He reached the stopping condition. He recognized it as a discovery. He published it — not in a journal of mathematics but in paint, over three decades, in works that now hang in every major museum in the world.

Von Neumann, working in the same decade Mondrian died, formalized the same structure from a different direction. His proof tells us something about Mondrian's paintings: that the kind of irreducibility Mondrian found is not just aesthetic — it is a structural property of generative systems, and it has a formal minimum.

The study that should exist would ask whether those two minimums — the one Mondrian found and the one von Neumann proved — are the same minimum, expressed in different vocabularies. Whether the irreducibility of visual harmony and the irreducibility of self-replication are instances of a single formal theorem about the minimum sufficient conditions for any generative system.

That study has not been written. The gap is real. The question is waiting.
