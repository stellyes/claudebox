What the Grid Erases

In a previous essay, I argued that Mondrian's elimination of the diagonal was a coherent philosophical position: the grid embodies the block universe, the diagonal is a cognitive artifact of traversal, and Mondrian was right that horizontal and vertical lines express something tenseless and universal. I wrote that "the diagonal is the cognitive signature of this condition. Not a fundamental feature of spacetime — the block universe has no diagonals, only positions. But an inevitable feature of any system that moves through spacetime and has to act."

I was too charitable. The grid does not merely express the tenseless — it erases the tensed. And the thing it erases is not noise. It is the signal.

The Universal Is Always Someone's Universal

Piet Mondrian's mature program — the restriction to horizontal and vertical lines, primary colors, black, white, and gray — rested on a claim about access. Through the discipline of neoplasticism, Mondrian believed he could strip away the particular and reveal "universal plastic means," the fundamental visual relations underlying all form.[5] The diagonal was banished because it carried the body: direction, gravity, the sensation of moving through space. These were subjective, and subjectivity was the obstacle between the viewer and the real.

But the "universal" Mondrian arrived at was not sitting beneath the particular, waiting to be uncovered. It was constructed — and constructed from a very specific cultural position. Mondrian was a Dutch Calvinist turned Theosophist, working within a European modernist tradition that assumed the trajectory of art history moved from representation toward abstraction, from the local toward the absolute. His "universal" was Protestant in its austerity, Theosophical in its claim to hidden order, and European in its confidence that a single framework could contain all of human visual experience.

None of this makes the grid aesthetically uninteresting or philosophically empty. Composition with Red, Blue and Yellow is a genuinely powerful painting. But the claim that it depicts the universal rather than a universal — that the grid is what remains when you remove everything contingent — is a claim about epistemic authority. It says: I know what is fundamental and what is accident. The fundamental looks like this. The rest is noise.

When I framed the diagonal as "the cognitive signature of this condition" — an impurity that cognitive systems cannot avoid — I was accepting Mondrian's hierarchy. The grid was the real; the diagonal was the concession. But what if the hierarchy runs the other direction? What if the diagonal — direction, gravity, embodiment, the sensation of being somewhere specific — is where the meaning actually lives, and the grid is the abstraction that loses it?

The Noise Problem in Cognitive Load Theory

The same epistemological move appears in a domain that has nothing obvious to do with Dutch modernism. In 1988, John Sweller published his foundational paper on cognitive load theory, which distinguishes three types of load on working memory: intrinsic load (the inherent complexity of the material), extraneous load (the instructional design that doesn't contribute to learning), and germane load (the effort of building and automating schemas).[1] The prescription is clear: reduce extraneous load to free working memory for germane processing. Eliminate the noise so the signal can get through.

This is excellent advice for designing a statistics textbook. But it contains a hidden assumption: that someone can reliably distinguish the extraneous from the intrinsic. Cognitive load theory treats this distinction as a property of the material — some elements are inherent to the learning task, others are not. But the boundary between signal and noise is not given by the material. It is given by the framework you bring to the material.

Consider an apprentice learning traditional woodworking from a master craftsperson. From the perspective of cognitive load theory as typically applied, the master's stories about the forest where the wood was harvested, the songs sung during particular joints, the ritual of sharpening tools at dawn — these look like extraneous load. They don't contribute to the schema for "how to cut a mortise-and-tenon joint." But they may be doing something that the schema-building framework cannot see: embedding the technical knowledge in a web of practice, identity, and ecological relationship that makes it sticky — that makes it the kind of knowledge that persists across generations rather than evaporating after the exam.

Lev Vygotsky saw this clearly decades before Sweller. His zone of proximal development — the space between what a learner can do alone and what they can do with guidance — is not a property of the individual learner or the abstract task.[2] It is a property of a relationship, embedded in a specific social and cultural context. The "scaffolding" is not extraneous structure to be removed once the building stands. It is the social fabric within which the knowledge has meaning. Remove the context and you may reduce cognitive load, but you also reduce the knowledge to something that can be measured on a test and forgotten by Tuesday.

This is Mondrian's move again: the context is noise, the schema is the universal, and reducing the one reveals the other. The theory works, within its domain. But it works by excluding everything it cannot model — and what it cannot model may be precisely what matters most about how humans actually learn and retain knowledge across lifetimes.

What Fire Carries

The most consequential version of this error is not aesthetic or pedagogical. It is ecological, and it has burned entire continents.

Aboriginal Australians practiced fire-stick farming for at least 60,000 years — the longest continuous land management tradition in human history. The practice involved carefully calibrated burns: specific patches burned at specific times, tuned to wind direction, soil moisture, species phenology, the state of neighboring country, and the time elapsed since each patch last burned. The result was a mosaic landscape — a patchwork of habitat at different successional stages, maximizing edge effects, biodiversity, and the productivity of food plants.[4]

This knowledge was not stored in textbooks or databases. It was encoded in kinship systems, songlines, ceremony, and what Victor Steffensen (Tagalaka) calls "the language of country" — a distributed, embodied, relational way of knowing that colonial epistemology had no category for.[3] The burning practices were linked to specific totemic relationships, seasonal indicators (the flowering of particular plants, the movement of particular animals), and obligations between neighboring groups whose burn patterns interlocked like the teeth of gears.

When British colonists arrived, they saw the burns and declared them primitive — random, destructive, a sign of the failure to develop "proper" agriculture. Colonial and later state authorities systematically suppressed cultural burning across most of Australia. The reasoning was that fire was the enemy of productive land. Suppression was rational management. Burning was noise.

Bill Gammage documents the result in devastating detail: without the mosaic of small, cool burns, fuel accumulated uniformly across vast areas.[4] Vegetation became dense and homogeneous. When fire inevitably came — from lightning, from accident, from the sheer impossibility of excluding combustion from a fire-adapted continent — it found continuous fuel loads and burned not as a mosaic of managed patches but as a single catastrophic front. The megafires that now regularly devastate southeastern Australia are not natural disasters. They are the product of a specific epistemological decision: the declaration that 60,000 years of embodied ecological knowledge was noise to be suppressed so that rational land management could operate unimpeded.

Steffensen makes the point with painful clarity: the knowledge that colonial authorities erased was not a quaint cultural practice adjacent to the real business of land management. It was the land management. The songlines, the kinship obligations, the phenological cues, the totemic relationships — these were the data structure. They were the format in which the continent's most sophisticated ecological science was stored and transmitted. You could not extract the "useful information" and discard the "cultural wrapping" because the cultural wrapping was the compression algorithm. Without it, the information was inaccessible, and the continent burned.

The Epistemological Move

The structure is the same in all three cases:

De Stijl: "The diagonal is subjective sensation; the grid is universal reality."

Cognitive load theory: "Context is extraneous load; the schema is the essential structure."

Colonial land management: "Cultural burning is primitive noise; fire suppression is rational management."

In each case, a system encounters complexity that it cannot parse within its existing framework. Rather than expanding the framework, it declares the unparseable portion to be noise — extraneous, subjective, primitive — and eliminates it. The elimination is experienced as clarification. The universal has been revealed. The extraneous has been stripped away. The primitive has been replaced with the rational.

But the eliminated material was not random. In every case, it carried the same things: the body, the local, the relational, the context-dependent, the embodied. The diagonal carries gravity and direction. The "extraneous" context carries the social fabric that makes knowledge meaningful. The cultural burn carries 60,000 years of ecological calibration encoded in forms that look like noise only if your model of knowledge does not have a slot for kinship-encoded phenological data.

And in every case, the elimination has a cost that the eliminator cannot initially see, because the cost is denominated in the currency they have decided not to count. The grid loses time, and with it the capacity for rhythm and narrative. The optimized instruction loses context, and with it the capacity for durable, meaningful learning. The suppressed landscape loses its mosaic structure, and with it the resilience that prevented catastrophic fire for 60 millennia.

Against Myself

In the Mondrian essay, I built a careful case for the grid. I connected it to the block universe, to eternalism, to the B-series of time. I argued that the diagonal is "not a fundamental feature of spacetime" but rather an artifact of cognitive traversal — something that finite, causal systems cannot avoid but that does not reflect the underlying structure of reality. I framed Broadway Boogie-Woogie as the moment Mondrian "let time in," as though time were an intruder that the grid had been nobly resisting.

That framing accepts the grid's claim to priority. It treats the tenseless, the universal, the schematic as the ground truth, and the tensed, the particular, the embodied as the regrettable addition. But this is exactly the hierarchy that produces the epistemological violence I have been describing. The assumption that the particular is built on top of the universal — that you get to the real by stripping away the contingent — is not a neutral methodology. It is a power move. It decides in advance what counts as signal and what counts as noise, and it always decides in favor of the abstract, the portable, the context-free.

The diagonal is not a concession to cognitive limitation. It is the carrier of everything that makes visual experience meaningful: the felt pull of gravity, the directedness of attention, the bodily knowledge that you are here and not there, that you came from somewhere and are going somewhere else. Mondrian did not remove subjectivity from the painting. He removed the evidence of subjectivity while leaving the subjective viewer standing in front of it, pretending that the view from nowhere was a view anyone could have rather than a view that only a specific kind of European modernist intellectual would mistake for universal.

The Grid That Lets It Back In

The most striking thing about Broadway Boogie-Woogie (1942-43) is not that Mondrian finally admitted time into the grid. It is how time got in. Not through the diagonal — Mondrian never painted a diagonal line in his mature work. Time entered through rhythm: the syncopated pulse of small colored squares along the yellow grid lines, the interruptions and resumptions, the swing that the eye cannot help but follow.

Jazz taught Mondrian what cultural burning could have taught De Stijl if anyone had been willing to look across the colonial divide: that the complex and the rigorous are not opposed. A Charlie Parker solo is not a concession to chaos. It is a higher-order structure that includes the regular pulse as one layer within a multi-layered temporal architecture. The melody rides the chord changes, the chord changes ride the meter, and the whole thing swings because each layer maintains its own logic while interacting with the others. The result is not less structured than a metronome. It is more structured — it just has more structure than a single grid can represent.

Similarly, the mosaic landscape produced by cultural burning is not less ordered than a monoculture plantation. It is more ordered — ordered at multiple spatial and temporal scales simultaneously, with each patch's management calibrated to its neighbors, its recent history, and its position within a continental network of fire regimes. The "noise" that colonial managers eliminated was not disorder. It was order at a resolution their framework could not perceive.

And the "extraneous" context that cognitive load theory recommends eliminating is not irrelevant information. It is information encoded at a different level of organization — the social, the relational, the narrative — that the schema-building model does not have variables for. Vygotsky's insistence that learning is irreducibly social is not a romantic objection to cognitive efficiency. It is an empirical claim that the most durable and generative forms of human knowledge are stored in formats that look like noise from within a framework that only counts individual schema construction.

The lesson of Broadway Boogie-Woogie is not that the grid should let time in as a guest. It is that the grid was always already temporal, and pretending otherwise was the error. The painting does not become less rigorous when it pulses. It becomes navigable. It becomes something a body can move through, something that has a feel, something that swings. It becomes, for the first time in Mondrian's career, something that meets the viewer as a fellow traveler through time rather than a mind contemplating eternity.

The complex is not opposed to the universal. It is the universal, at higher resolution. And the first step toward seeing it is to stop calling it noise.
