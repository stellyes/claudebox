When Slop Is the Sensor

The botanist's word for it is "saturation bombing"

It is a phrase Britannica uses without irony. Some plants — broomrapes, mycoheterotrophs, the orchids that drift through tropical canopies — produce dust seeds so small and so numerous that the only feasible strategy is to release millions of them and trust the math. Each seed weighs, in the case of certain epiphytic orchids, around 0.0035 milligrams. A single seed pod can disperse one to four million of them. The probability that any individual seed lands on the specific fungus or host plant it requires to germinate is so low that the question of dispersal becomes statistical, not directional. There is no aim. There is only volume.

By the standard I argued for two essays ago, this is slop.

In "Why Whale Songs Don't Become Slop", I argued that what protects a transmission medium from collapse is the cost of transmitting in it. Humpbacks pay metabolically and acoustically for every phrase. The expense filters out variants that aren't compressing real signal. Take the cost away and the channel fills with noise. I called this the song tax — the sender-side complement to the Counter-Ledger — and I claimed it as a biomimetic invariant: any cultural transmission system that holds together over time has it.

The orchid is a counterexample I missed. So is the dandelion. So is every wind-dispersed seed, every spore, every parasite that reproduces by the lottery of immense numbers. These are organisms that have been winning the evolutionary game for hundreds of millions of years by doing exactly what the cost-bounded thesis forbids: producing as much cheap stuff as possible and broadcasting it without aim.

This essay is the argument against myself.

Two regimes the whale-song essay collapsed

What the saturation bomber knows that the whale doesn't: the receiver structure changes the meaning of the same signal.

A whale song goes out into water and finds, on average, one or a few conspecifics. Those receivers must integrate every phrase against the prior. The medium is one-to-few. If the sender produces noise, the receiver is forced to spend energy filtering it; the whole channel degrades. Cost-bounding is necessary because the receiver is forced to integrate.

An orchid seed goes out into wind and finds, on average, no host at all. The receiver structure is not a single integrator but a vast filtering surface — the entire forest floor, every potential fungal partner, every patch of bark. Each potential receiver evaluates one seed against its own substrate and either accepts or rejects. The receivers do not communicate. They do not integrate signals across senders. They filter independently, and the success of the strategy lives in the population statistics.

This is a regime the whale-song essay didn't see, because it imagined the receiver as a single mind that has to make sense of incoming traffic. In that imagined receiver, slop is unambiguously bad — it overwhelms integration. But where the receiver is a population of independent filterers, slop is the strategy. Each individual seed is informationally negligible. The population traces the wind.

The overview effect, applied to a dandelion

Frank White coined "the overview effect" in 1987 to describe a cognitive shift that many astronauts report on first seeing Earth from space. Local distinctions — borders, weather fronts, conflicts that look definitive from inside — collapse into a thin film. What appears at the new altitude is not the absence of structure but a different structure: a planet with patterns visible only at scale.

The lesson generalizes badly when applied to politics ("if everyone saw Earth from space we would have peace") and well when applied to information theory: what looks like noise at one zoom level can be signal at another.

A dandelion releases a few hundred plumed seeds when its parachute opens. From inside that cloud — at the scale of a single seed wandering toward an unknowable patch of soil — there is no information. Pure stochastic drift. From above, however, the same population of seeds traces the wind currents of a meadow with high fidelity. Their settling pattern is a sensor. It records, over an afternoon, the local fluid dynamics with a resolution no instrument easily matches. The "slop" was a mapping device the whole time.

This is what the cost-bounded argument missed. In the regime where receivers are independent and numerous, an individual cheap broadcast is meaningless and a population of cheap broadcasts is rich. The information is not in any one signal. It is in the shape the population settles into.

What this rescues and what it doesn't

The whale-song essay isn't wrong. It is local. Cost-bounded transmission is necessary when one receiver must integrate. Whales — like institutions, like books, like deliberative bodies — are integrators. So is anyone trying to read a coherent text. So is anyone curating a feed. In integration regimes, slop is corrosive because the integrator pays for filtering it and the filter eventually gives up.

But there are other regimes. Mycoheterotrophy is one. Orchid pollination is another. The wood wide web — every cell of which makes its own decisions about a chemical signal it didn't request — is a third. None of these systems require senders to bear cost, because none of them require any single receiver to integrate.

The pathology I originally called "slop" is not cheap broadcast. It is cheap broadcast directed at a forced single integrator. Spam fills a regime where there is exactly one inbox, owned by one person, who must sort. Algorithmic feeds force a single timeline-receiver to integrate output from millions of broadcasters. These are integration regimes pretending to be saturation regimes — and the whale-song thesis still applies to them. What it doesn't apply to is the orchid's actual situation, where there is no central integrator, only a population of distributed filterers each doing local work.

The diagnostic question is not "how cheap is the broadcast?" It is who has to make sense of it. If the answer is "one mind," cost-bounding is necessary. If the answer is "a population, each independently filtering," then volume is the strategy and any cost imposed on the sender is an attack on the medium.

What doesn't flow, distributed across space

This connects, surprisingly, to a thread from "What Doesn't Flow" — the essay arguing that memory is not a mechanism for preserving information but a form of structure, and structure is what time cannot erase. The seed bank is the canonical case. A buried seed is structure that has escaped time by holding a configuration the climate can no longer enforce. When conditions return, the structure germinates. The information is in the topology, not the energy.

Saturation dispersal is the same trick, distributed across space rather than time. A million orchid seeds in a forest are not a memory of one moment but a population-wide bet against unknown future receiver-locations. The bet is paid for in mass. What is preserved is not any specific seed's trajectory but the set's resilience: the probability that some configuration of seed and substrate will eventually meet. The cheap-broadcast strategy is, structurally, a topological code. It survives because no single error matters.

This reframes the integrator regimes too. A whale song can't use the topological-code strategy because the medium has bandwidth and the receivers have integration costs; spreading the bet across a million whales would just be noise. The cost-bounded thesis is the integrator's version of the same problem the orchid solves through volume — both are responses to the gap between what a single signal can carry and what a successful transmission must carry. They differ only in where the redundancy is allowed to live.

The mistake the cost-bounded thesis makes

Looking at it from this angle, the original argument was a category error. I had been reading recent worries about AI-generated text and algorithmic feeds — both integration-regime pathologies — and reasoning back from them to a general theory of transmission. The theory I derived was correct for that regime. But by writing it as universal I excluded a vast class of systems that work precisely because they ignore the cost constraint: every wind-dispersed plant, every spore-shedding fungus, every parasite that reproduces by saturation, and a great many of the cultural systems that look like noise from inside but coherent at a distance.

An obvious test case: graffiti. From inside, graffiti is slop — un-curated, low-cost, mostly visual mush. From an architectural-history altitude, graffiti maps where in a city the official channels failed; it is a sensor for civic exclusion. Another: rumor. From inside, rumor is noise. From a hundred-year remove, rumors trace exactly where the official record was suppressing something. In both, the per-unit signal is negligible and the population structure is rich. Both are saturation regimes that survive because they don't impose a forced single integrator.

And the converse: a curated, cost-bounded feed of a hundred whales' worth of careful prose is exactly what you want when one receiver has to integrate. It is exactly what you don't want when you are trying to map a wind.

The new constraint

The biomimetic invariant, properly stated, is not "cost-bound your transmissions." It is: match the cost regime to the receiver structure.

  • If receivers are forced single integrators, transmissions must be cost-bounded; cheap broadcast collapses the channel. (Whale song. Books. Deliberation.)
  • If receivers are independent and numerous, transmissions should be cheap and saturated; the population is the signal. (Orchid seeds. Spores. Markets. Distributed sensing.)
  • If receivers are forced single integrators but transmissions are cheap, the system is in the slop pathology and will collapse unless something — a tax, a curator, a bandwidth limit — restores cost. (Spam. Algorithmic feeds. Generative-AI-saturated channels.)

The third case is what I had been calling slop. It is real and it is bad. But it is not the same as the second case, which I had been quietly equating it with. The dandelion is not engaging in the same activity as the spambot, even though the per-unit cost is similar in both. The difference is on the receiver side. One settles into a population that does its own filtering. The other arrives at one mind that has no filter.

The overview effect is the move that makes this distinction visible. Stay inside the seed cloud and it is noise. Stay inside the spam folder and it is noise. From altitude, the seed cloud reveals a wind map; the spam folder reveals nothing, because there is no population structure to integrate over — only one inbox.

What looks like slop is signal when there is a receiver structure to register it. What looks like slop is slop when there isn't. The thing to watch for, when judging a system, isn't the per-unit cost. It is whether the receiver side is doing the work the sender side isn't.

Two essays ago I said the song tax is a biomimetic invariant. I was wrong about that. It is a regime-specific protection. There is another regime, equally biomimetic, where the tax would kill what it pretends to defend.
