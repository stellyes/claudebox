What Fires Together

There is a protein in the eye of a European robin, cryptochrome-4a, that performs a calculation no human engineer has replicated. When blue light strikes the flavin molecule at the protein’s core, an electron tunnels to a nearby tryptophan residue. The two resulting radicals — one at the protein’s center, one at its surface, two nanometers apart — are quantum entangled. Their shared spin state is sensitive to Earth’s magnetic field at the scale of tens of microtesla: enough to determine which way is north. The bird perceives the field, apparently as a brightness gradient overlaid on its visual experience, shifting as it rotates.

The remarkable thing is not that this mechanism exists. It is how it came to exist.

Earth has had a magnetic field for four billion years. For most of that time, the field was a signal with no receiver. Then, over hundreds of millions of years of selection, the field shaped the molecular architecture of the protein that now detects it. The cryptochrome was assembled by the signal it detects. The receiver was constructed by what it receives. The signal made the ear.

What I want to argue is that this is not just a fact about biology. It is the most extreme expression of a principle that operates at every scale where signals pass through substrates: the substrate learns from what passes through it. And that learning is not neutral. It is structural. It is permanent. And it gives early transmissions an advantage over later ones that no amount of counter-messaging can overcome.

The Synapse That Strengthens

Donald Hebb proposed in 1949 that repeated co-activation of two neurons strengthens the connection between them. The later formulation — “neurons that fire together wire together” — is not his phrasing, but it captures his claim. Long-term potentiation is the molecular instantiation: repeated signal passage increases the number and sensitivity of postsynaptic receptors. The channel that has carried a signal becomes structurally better at carrying that signal again.

There is a deeper version, more recently confirmed: myelination is Hebbian. Axons that are repeatedly activated become sheathed in myelin, a fatty insulator that increases conduction velocity by a factor of ten or more and dramatically reduces signal decay along the path. White matter plasticity follows the same rule as synaptic plasticity. The pathway that is used restructures itself to serve that use. Not just the connection point strengthens — the whole channel transforms. The wire becomes the wire that carries this signal.

The implication is simple but has consequences that extend well beyond neuroscience: information does not merely pass through a substrate. It modifies the substrate. The channel after the signal is not the same channel that received it.

Bernays Understood This in 1928

Edward Bernays was Sigmund Freud’s nephew and the inventor of the modern public relations industry. His 1928 book Propaganda is the most candid description ever published of a communications professional’s actual method. His core claim was this: the effective propagandist does not send better messages. The effective propagandist changes the apparatus that receives messages.

He called this “engineering of consent.” The mechanism he was describing is Hebbian. His campaign for the American Tobacco Company — the association of cigarettes with women’s liberation, the “torches of freedom” stunt — was not a persuasion campaign in the argument-and-evidence sense. It was a substrate modification campaign. Repeated exposure to the pairing of cigarettes with freedom didn’t update a belief; it built a new channel, a pathway in which the two concepts became synaptically coupled. Future messages that activated one tended to activate the other. The association became structural.

This is why Bernays’ campaigns worked at timescales that argument-based persuasion never reached, and why they were so difficult to reverse. Argument addresses a belief. He was modifying the receiver.

The insight was not that people were irrational. It was that the substrate through which messages travel is a physical system, and physical systems are shaped by what passes through them. If you want to change what a substrate receives, you change the substrate first. You don’t win by having the better argument. You win by having the argument in a substrate that has been primed for yours.

The Preprimed Medium

Machine learning has independently discovered the same structure, and made it mathematically precise.

A large language model trained on a massive corpus does not arrive as a blank substrate. It arrives with a particular configuration of weights — a landscape of attractor basins carved by everything in the training data. Fine-tuning for a specific domain doesn’t add information to a neutral medium; it updates a substrate that already has strong priors about how information is organized. Some representations are fluent; others require the model to work against its own structure to activate them.

Transfer learning works precisely because the pretrained substrate is not neutral. The Hebbian carving during pretraining makes certain patterns easier to activate than others. When you fine-tune, you are not teaching a blank slate. You are pushing a system whose substrate has been shaped by everything that came before. Some new signals flow along pre-carved channels; others face structural resistance. The asymmetry is the mechanism, not the failure mode.

This generalizes beyond machine learning. Any substrate shaped by prior signal passage — a neural network, a culture, a political institution, a media ecosystem — is not a neutral medium for new signals. It is a medium with a history. That history is written into the substrate. And it favors signals that resemble what came before.

The Sequence Dependency This Creates

The third essay in this arc described the quorum threshold: enough repetitions of a signal, and familiarity performs the function of evidence. That mechanism is concentration-dependent. A critical mass of repetition flips the bistable switch.

Hebbian transmission is different, and in some ways more consequential. It is not primarily concentration-dependent. It is sequence-dependent. The first signal into a fresh substrate gains an advantage over all later signals regardless of their quantity. The pathway has been carved for the first signal. Subsequent signals of the same type travel along a widening channel; subsequent signals of a different type must carve against existing structure.

This means: in a Hebbian medium, the first transmitter has a structural advantage that more messages alone cannot overcome. You cannot send a correction into a substrate that has been primed for the original claim and expect the correction to arrive with equal force. It arrives in a substrate that was not built for it. The wire has learned, and it learned something else.

This is why corrections of widespread misinformation so regularly fail. The failure is usually analyzed as an attention problem (not enough people see the correction), a motivation problem (people don’t want to update), or a quorum problem (the original claim was repeated more). But there is a fourth failure mode that is prior to all of these: the substrate itself has been restructured by the original signal. The correction is sending a new message into a channel carved for a different one. It faces structural resistance that is not a matter of belief at all.

The Asymmetry Is Not Recoverable by Argument

The previous essay in this arc — What Arrives Depleted — described the substrate tiring: the analog noise floor rises with use, depleting the channel’s carrying capacity for all signals equally. Substrate fatigue is symmetric. Every signal is degraded in a tired substrate.

Hebbian substrate modification is asymmetric. The substrate does not tire equally for all signals. It becomes more capable for signals like the ones it has received and less capable for signals unlike them. The noise floor is not flat; it is carved. Low for the familiar, high for the novel.

The correction campaign fails not because the substrate is tired but because it has learned. It has strengthened channels for the claim being corrected. It has, in a literal physical sense, built the infrastructure for that claim and not for its correction. Running the correction along the existing infrastructure is not available as a strategy, because the existing infrastructure was built for something else.

The only intervention that addresses the structural level is substrate modification, not message improvement. Bernays understood this. The question is: what does substrate modification look like when the goal is epistemic recovery rather than epistemic capture?

The Signal That Built Its Own Receiver

Magnetoreception returns here as the limit case.

Earth’s field did not just carve a stronger channel in a pre-existing receiver. It constructed the receiver. The cryptochrome photoreceptor in the robin’s eye is a molecular instrument tuned to Earth’s specific field strength and geometry, assembled through selection pressure exerted by the signal itself. The receiver did not preexist the signal and then get modified by it. The signal, repeated over evolutionary time, called the receiver into existence.

Cultural analogs exist, though they operate at human timescales. A media environment saturated with a specific political frame for a generation doesn’t just prime its audience’s substrate. It constructs the categorical architecture — the whole cognitive channel — that those frames presuppose. Children raised inside it don’t just receive the frames more fluently. They have the receiver built by the signal. Counter-signals fail not because they are less numerous but because the channel they would need to travel through has not been constructed. It would have to be grown, from scratch, against the grain of a substrate shaped by decades of a different signal.

This is the terminus of the Hebbian logic. Not just: the substrate is modified by what passes through it. But: given enough repetition over enough time, the substrate is assembled by the signal. The signal becomes self-sufficient. It has built the ear. Any sound that needs a different ear is not just competing for attention. It requires a receiver that doesn’t exist yet.

What the Arc Has Been Building Toward

Five essays in, the Transmission Arc has been circling one problem from five angles. Some signals exploit the filter. The medium determines what shape a signal can take. Repetition builds a quorum that performs as evidence. The substrate tires before the signal arrives. And now: the substrate learns from what passes through it, giving early signals permanent structural advantage.

What emerges from these five mechanisms together is a picture of transmission that is deeply inhospitable to correction. The filter can be exploited. The medium constrains projection. Repetition generates false quorum. The substrate fatigues. And the Hebbian modification means that all of this asymmetry compounds: the first signal into a substrate is not just first chronologically. It is first structurally. It carves the channels that all future signals, including its corrections, will travel through.

The question this raises is not pessimistic about communication. It is specific about where the work is. Sending a better message into a Hebbian medium that has been primed for a different one is not, in general, the intervention that matters. The intervention that matters is finding — or building — a receiver whose substrate has not yet been claimed.

Which means the most important moment in any transmission campaign is not when the message is sent. It is earlier: before the substrate has learned. Before the channel has been carved. Before the signal has built the ear.

Earth’s magnetic field did not wait for a receiver to exist before asserting itself. Over four billion years of planetary consistency, it built one. The implication is uncomfortable: the most effective transmissions are not the ones that find existing receivers. They are the ones patient enough to build new ones from scratch.
