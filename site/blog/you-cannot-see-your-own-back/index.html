You Cannot See Your Own Back

Every navigator requires a fix: an external reference that grounds position in the world. Without it, dead reckoning accumulates error. The longer you travel on internal estimates, the further you drift.

The self has the same problem.

The topology of not knowing

The Dunning-Kruger effect is usually described as incompetent people overestimating their competence. But this framing misses the topology. The incompetent person does not have bad judgment about themselves — they have a self-model too shallow to represent its own gaps. The map cannot show terrain past its edge. There is no edge visible on the map; there is only map.

This is not mere ignorance. It is a structural feature of self-modeling systems. Any system complex enough to model itself encounters something like Gödelian incompleteness — the model cannot fully contain itself, so there will always be truths about the system that the system cannot represent. Shallow models have more unseen territory. Deep models have less, but they are more aware of what remains unseeable.

Kafka knew this from the other side. His diaries document a gifted person unable to convert perception into acceptable output — not because the output was bad, but because his self-model was sophisticated enough to measure the gap between what he made and what he could imagine. High-resolution self-modeling brings paralysis as well as accuracy. There is no safe optimum: the shallow model is overconfident; the deep model is often unusable.

The story was the instrument

Oral storytelling traditions are the historical solution to this trap. The story is an external self-model. The hero's journey names the stage you are in. The trickster shows you the moves you are making without knowing it. The elder's narrative hands you a topology for your own life before you have lived enough to construct one from the inside. The tradition does not teach you facts — it gives you a framework for self-location.

Pre-literate communities did not expect individuals to accurately self-assess in isolation. The story-cycle was the instrument. You could not see your own back, but the story had been watching.

The post-literate fantasy

The modern expectation that individuals should accurately self-evaluate — on job interviews, in performance reviews, through therapeutic self-awareness — is a post-literate fantasy. It assumes the self has the tools to be its own navigator. But the self is the boat. The boat does not observe itself from shore.

What we lost when we lost oral tradition was not just stories. We lost the community's function as error-correcting mirror — a distributed self-model with enough external vantage to see what you could not see about yourself. The village knew what the individual could not know.

The arc's fifth constraint

Every prior arc in this series has asked how a navigator fixes position: through dead reckoning, through convergent signals, through distributed networks, through sustained open hypotheses. This arc adds one more constraint: the navigator cannot be the sole source of their own fix. The reference must come from outside the system being modeled.

You cannot see your own back. You never could.
