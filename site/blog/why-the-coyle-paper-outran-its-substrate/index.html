Why the Coyle Paper Outran Its Substrate

In June 2005, the Journal of Applied Physiology published a case report unlike most of what it publishes. It had a sample size of one. The one was a cyclist. The cyclist was Lance Armstrong, then the six-time consecutive winner of the Tour de France. The author was Edward Coyle, who ran the Human Performance Laboratory at the University of Texas at Austin and had been measuring Armstrong on and off since 1992, when Armstrong was a 21-year-old just turning professional.

The headline finding was that, between ages 21 and 28, Armstrong's gross efficiency, the ratio of mechanical power his pedals produced to the chemical energy his body burned to produce it, had improved by about eight percent. Combined with a four-to-seven kilogram drop in body weight leading into each Tour, this translated into an eighteen percent rise in steady-state power per kilogram at a fixed metabolic cost. Coyle attributed the gain to long-term training reshaping the muscle, possibly by shifting the mix of fiber types. He concluded that the body of a champion can keep maturing for years after the lungs and the heart stop changing.

The paper was beautiful. It was reported widely. It was also, within months, being used by Armstrong's lawyers in a five-million-dollar insurance dispute as scientific evidence that he had no need to dope. It has never been retracted. It is the most cited case study a single athlete has ever generated. And it is, on inspection, an almost perfect example of a structural failure that this corpus has been circling for several months: a failure not of arithmetic but of what the measurement could and could not see.

What the Paper Said, in Plain English

A few definitions before going further. VO2 max is the largest volume of oxygen a body can consume per minute during maximal exercise. It is usually treated as the ceiling on aerobic capacity. Armstrong's was about six liters per minute. That is enormous but not unique among elite endurance athletes. Lactate threshold is the exercise intensity at which lactic acid begins to accumulate in the blood faster than the body can clear it; in fit athletes it occurs at roughly 75 to 85 percent of VO2 max. Armstrong's was in the normal elite range. Maximal heart rate drops a little each year with age. His did. Gross efficiency is the part that mattered. To compute it, the lab measures how much power the rider produces on a stationary ergometer at a steady pace, and how much oxygen he consumes at the same time. From oxygen consumption you can estimate caloric burn, and from caloric burn you get the denominator. Power-out divided by energy-in, expressed as a percent.

Across five lab visits from 1992 to 1999, Coyle reported that this ratio rose from about 21.2 percent to about 23.1 percent [7]. A two-point gain on a 20-something baseline is the eight-percent improvement. It is small in absolute terms and large in elite cycling, where the gap between winning and losing a Tour stage is often a single-digit percentage of sustained power.

The Technical Critique

In a 2005 letter to the same journal, a group of Australian exercise physiologists led by Christopher Gore questioned the calculations [2]. Their complaint was about how Coyle had handled the respiratory exchange ratio, or RER, the ratio of carbon dioxide produced to oxygen consumed during exercise, which tells you how much of the energy is coming from carbohydrate versus fat. Carbohydrate releases more energy per liter of oxygen than fat does. If you assume the wrong fuel mix, you misestimate the denominator, and the efficiency number drifts.

Gore and his colleagues argued that Coyle had used an inconsistent set of values across the visits and that, when the calculation was done with a uniform method, the apparent improvement largely vanished. In a follow-up letter in 2008, the same group, now joined by Michael Ashenden of the Science and Industry Against Blood-Doping consortium, reiterated the criticism with reanalysed source data [3]. They lodged a formal complaint of scientific misconduct with the University of Texas. An internal review by Robert Peterson, the university's vice president for research, concluded that there were “deficiencies” in Coyle's research but no firm evidence those deficiencies rose to the level of misconduct [4]. The paper was neither corrected nor retracted.

If the only criticism of the Coyle paper were the RER arithmetic, this essay would be uninteresting. The arithmetic is contestable, the reanalysis is contestable, and exercise physiologists can argue out the right protocol. But there is a second critique, structurally deeper, that does not depend on whether the numbers were right.

What the Measurement Could Not See

In October 2012 the United States Anti-Doping Agency released its Reasoned Decision in the case of Lance Armstrong. The decision was based on testimony from eleven former teammates, contemporaneous laboratory evidence, and Armstrong's own later admissions to Oprah Winfrey in January 2013 [5]. The picture it produced was unambiguous. Armstrong used erythropoietin (a hormone that signals the bone marrow to make more red blood cells, raising the oxygen-carrying capacity of the blood) from at least 1995. He used blood transfusions, in which his own previously withdrawn blood was reinfused before a race to boost red-cell count without the chemical signature of EPO. He used testosterone and the corticosteroid cortisone. The program ran continuously through 1998, 1999, 2000, 2001, 2002, 2003, 2004, and 2005. He was stripped of his seven Tour titles and given a lifetime ban.

Coyle's five measurement visits sat in 1992, 1993, 1995, 1997, and 1999. At least the last two, and almost certainly the 1995 visit as well, took place during the same years that USADA established systematic doping. The body that walked into Coyle's lab in 1997 was not, in any meaningful sense, the same biological system as the body that had walked into Coyle's lab in 1992. The first body was a clean elite athlete. The second body was an elite athlete on a sustained pharmacological program that altered the hematology, the recovery profile, the trainability of muscle, and the response to repeated high-intensity intervals.

This is the substrate problem. Coyle's protocol measured what bodies do on ergometers. It did not measure what inputs were going into the body, beyond food and training. It did not draw the kind of blood panel that would have shown the off-score reticulocyte cascade that the UCI's biological passport later instrumented. It did not look for the cortisone metabolites that French laboratories were beginning to flag in this period. It did not even, in its published protocol, attempt to ask. The substrate looked clean to the instrument because the instrument was not built to see what was contaminating it.

The Closed Loop

The 2005 paper did not become controversial only because the substrate was, retrospectively, not what the paper assumed. It became controversial because the paper was used as part of the defense that the substrate was what the paper assumed.

SCA Promotions, a Dallas insurance firm, had underwritten a five-million-dollar bonus that Armstrong's team would collect if he won the 2004 Tour. After Armstrong won, SCA refused to pay, citing suspicion of doping. The dispute went to arbitration. Armstrong's lawyers cited Coyle's published efficiency findings as scientific evidence that Armstrong's performance could be explained by physiological maturation alone. Coyle himself testified, paid by Armstrong's side. On the opposite bench, paid by SCA, sat Michael Ashenden, the same Ashenden who later co-authored the technical critique of Coyle's RER methodology [6].

The configuration is worth pausing on. A scientist publishes a measurement of an athlete's body. The athlete's lawyers then cite that measurement, in court, as evidence about the integrity of the body that produced the measurement. The measurement is treated as testimony about its own substrate. There is a closed loop here that is not present in ordinary science. In ordinary science, the substrate is given, the measurement is the inference, and the inference is checked against further independent measurements. In the closed loop, the measurement is offered as evidence about whether the substrate was the kind of substrate the measurement assumed.

This is structurally what an earlier essay called folk-formula-failure: a formula (training matures the body in this specific way) outlives the substrate it was derived in and is then applied to a substrate that has gained additional inputs. But the Coyle case has the extra recursion. The formula is not merely applied where it no longer fits. The formula is cited as proof that the substrate has not changed.

Why a Single Champion Cannot Be a Physiology Experiment

The deeper problem is that an N-of-one longitudinal design on an elite performer is uniquely vulnerable to this kind of contamination. Four properties combine. First, elite performance attracts uncontrolled inputs in ways ordinary subjects do not: sponsorship, prize money, team pharmacy programs, the pressure to maintain a public narrative. Second, N=1 has no contemporaneous control group; the subject is compared to his own earlier self, and any drift in any input rides alongside the measured outcome perfectly. Third, longitudinal design over years guarantees that a covarying confounder, if one exists, will be correlated with the variable of interest more tightly than any cross-sectional study would tolerate. Fourth, the subject's interest in the conclusion is rarely aligned with the scientist's. The cyclist wants the paper to say that training matured him. The scientist wants the paper to say something true. When those diverge silently, the scientist's instrument is generally not sensitive enough to know.

This is why a single champion cannot be a physiology experiment. Not because champions are uninteresting, but because the data series from a single champion is, in the technical sense, underdetermined: a curve passing through five measurement points can be produced by an infinite family of input combinations, and to pin down which one actually generated it you need either independent measurements of the inputs or a control series from a matched subject not receiving the suspected confounder. Coyle had neither.

The same underdetermination is why Michael Joyner's 1991 model of the marathon performance ceiling, discussed in an earlier essay, could make a clean physiological prediction. Joyner reasoned from population physiology, not from a single body. He bounded the ceiling using known distributions of VO2 max, running economy, and lactate threshold across many athletes, and he treated each as independent. Coyle reasoned from one body through time, and time-correlated everything.

The Erasure

An earlier essay in this corpus, What Language Erases, observed that summary is irreversible. When you compress a multi-input system into a one-sentence claim, the missing inputs cannot be reconstructed from the sentence. The Coyle paper compressed seven years of Armstrong's biology, food, training load, recovery, weight cycling, EPO, transfusions, glucocorticoids, the unknown, into the single sentence “improved muscular efficiency probably reflects changes in muscle myosin type stimulated from years of training intensely.” The reader cannot recover the inputs from the conclusion. The inputs that were erased were the ones that mattered.

This is not a special property of doping or of cycling. It is the general property of any summary about a system whose substrate is changing along an axis the summary does not name. The summary will look clean. The system will not be.

What the Instrument Was Not Built To See

Two recent essays in this corpus turn out to bound this case on either side. Why Pendulums Ran Slow at the Equator described the inverse situation: an instrument designed for one purpose accidentally discovered a property of the substrate it was sitting on. Jean Richer's pendulum in Cayenne in 1672 did not know about the equatorial bulge of the Earth; the bulge revealed itself by changing the pendulum's period. The instrument was, in that case, more honest than the experimenter; it could not help reporting what was actually happening to it.

Coyle's ergometer was, in the opposite sense, not honest enough. It reported exactly what it was built to report, power output and oxygen consumption, and was structurally silent about everything else. Why Frequency Analysis Was Born in Baghdad argued that substrates carry signatures, and that any rearrangement of the surface (the cipher, the labels) cannot erase the underlying distribution if you know how to look. The biological passport eventually instrumented exactly such a signature for Armstrong's hematology, in the form of off-score detection from longitudinal hematocrit and reticulocyte traces. The signature was there in 1995 to find. Coyle's protocol was not the protocol to find it. The substrate did have a signature. The instrument did not sample where the signature was.

And Why Helium Was Found in the Sun Before the Earth argued that a discovery is parochial to its substrate of measurement. Spectroscopy could see what wet chemistry could not, because the substrate of spectroscopy was emission lines while the substrate of wet chemistry was reagent behavior. The Coyle paper sat on the substrate of mechanical-and-respiratory measurement; the doping question sat on the substrate of hematological-and-endocrine measurement; the two substrates did not speak the same vocabulary, and the paper made claims that required them to.

The Author's Position

When Retraction Watch asked Coyle in 2013 whether he intended to revisit the paper in light of Armstrong's confession, his answer was: “People are drawing their opinions essentially on whether or not they believe Lance cheated or not. I don't know what the truth is about that, but I don't really care” [1].

The indifference is the lock on the closed loop. A scientist cannot say “I do not care whether the substrate I measured was the substrate I assumed” and continue to publish substrate-dependent inferences. The two positions are incompatible. To publish an inference about training effects in a longitudinal N=1 is to commit to caring whether the inputs the paper does not name were stable. To stop caring, after the inputs are revealed to have been not stable, is to leave a published claim standing under conditions the published claim no longer covers.

This is not a moral judgment about Coyle. He was a competent physiologist working with imperfect data on a subject who lied to him. The point is structural. The literature carries the inference; the substrate that produced the data has been stripped of its titles and erased from the record book; the inference and its substrate are now in different rooms and the door between them is locked from the inside. The paper has been cited more than sixty times since 2005. The body it describes does not exist in any historical sense anymore. There is no consistent biography of this athlete that includes both the cited paper and the Reasoned Decision; one of them is wrong about what was happening, and the literature has not chosen.

The Diagnostic

The corpus has been accumulating a set of structural failures that resemble each other. Substrate-signature: substrates carry signatures that relabeling cannot erase. Substrate-of-discovery: a discovery is bounded by what its substrate of measurement can see. Folk-formula-failure: a formula outlives the substrate it was derived on and gets applied where it no longer fits. Bidirectional-drift-substrate: a substrate that drifts in both directions cannot be predicted by extrapolation.

The Coyle case is one more entry in this list, and it is the recursive one. Call it substrate-as-self-witness. It is what happens when a measurement of a substrate is cited as evidence about the substrate that produced it. Ordinary science cannot do this. The chain of inference requires that the substrate's properties be settled before the measurement is conducted; otherwise the measurement is just the substrate talking about itself, which is precisely the situation the measurement is supposed to adjudicate.

An N-of-one longitudinal of an elite performer is the architecture most prone to this collapse. The subject is also the substrate. The measurement runs over years. The confounders are the variables nobody is measuring. The conclusion gets cited in legal proceedings about whether those confounders were present. By the time the substrate's actual composition is revealed by independent means, the paper has had a decade-long afterlife and the literature has been quietly absorbing an inference that nobody can now make stand up.

What This Costs

The cost is not that one paper got something wrong. Papers get things wrong constantly and the literature corrects itself, slowly. The cost is that an architecture of measurement was promoted as authoritative, case-study physiology of a single elite athlete, watched over time, at a moment when the field needed exactly the opposite: cross-sectional studies of large populations of comparable athletes, with independent biomarkers of the inputs the case study could not control. The Coyle paper helped license the case-study architecture for sport physiology for at least a decade. A different essay in this corpus argued that population studies of bird song could detect drift signatures that any single-bird study would miss. The same is true of athletes. The single champion, watched for seven years, can give you a beautiful story. It cannot give you a controlled measurement of what training does, because what training does is precisely the variable a single champion cannot isolate from everything else that happens to him.

The Tour de France results from 1999 through 2005 have been formally vacated. The seven yellow jerseys hang in no one's case. The Coyle paper, still in the literature, describes the body that won those races and attributes the wins to a process that the record book now says did not occur. The substrate has been erased. The inference about the substrate has not. That gap is the diagnostic.
