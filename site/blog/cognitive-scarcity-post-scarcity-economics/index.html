The Scarcity That Survives Abundance

The Marx Assumption

In 1844, Marx sketched the most optimistic economic vision in history. The "Fragment on Machines" imagined what would happen once automation freed humanity from labor: people would "hunt in the morning, fish in the afternoon, rear cattle in the evening, criticize after dinner." The liberated mind, freed of material necessity, would naturally turn to art, science, and creative exploration.

Two centuries later, Iain M. Banks elaborated this vision into the Culture novels — a civilization with all material needs met by benevolent AI, free to pursue interests across a socially permissive cosmos. The assumption persists across radical economics, utopian fiction, and AI alignment theory: solve material scarcity, and human flourishing follows. Free the body from want, and the mind follows.

This assumption has never been tested. It has merely been repeated.

What the Mind Does When It Isn't Working

In 1995, Claude Steele and Joshua Aronson ran an experiment that changed how we think about intelligence. They gave Black and white college students the same difficult verbal test — but changed only how they described it. When the test was labeled "diagnostic of intellectual ability," Black participants significantly underperformed white participants. When the identical test was labeled non-diagnostic, as a problem-solving exercise, the gap disappeared entirely.

No material scarcity had changed. No income gap closed. The conditions were identical except for one sentence of framing. What changed was the activation of a social threat: the risk of confirming a negative stereotype about one's group.

What Steele named "stereotype threat" is now one of the most replicated findings in social psychology. Its mechanism operates through a resource most people don't think about as scarce: cognitive bandwidth. Working memory — the mind's scratch pad — holds roughly four chunks of information at once. When you're anxious about confirming a stereotype, some of those slots get occupied by self-monitoring, vigilance, and belonging-uncertainty. The test question is still there, but you're running it on a processor with two slots reserved for threat assessment.

Michael Inzlicht's "stigma as ego depletion" theory formalizes this: every stigmatized identity carries an invisible tax. Not a material tax — a cognitive one. The tax is paid in attention, in working memory, in the executive-function bandwidth that would otherwise go toward the actual task.

John Sweller's cognitive load theory provides the framework. He distinguishes intrinsic load (inherent complexity of a task), extraneous load (overhead from poor conditions), and germane load (the useful work of building understanding). Stereotype threat is a form of extraneous load — it doesn't contribute to learning or performance, it consumes resources that would otherwise go toward the real work. By Sweller's account, a person experiencing stereotype threat is operating with reduced effective cognitive capacity, not because their capacity differs, but because the social context is consuming part of their working memory.

Post-scarcity economics doesn't model this. It can't. Its units are material.

The Cognitive Tax of Being Perceived

The research spreads further than race. Spencer et al. (1999) demonstrated stereotype threat for women in mathematics. Croizet et al. showed the same effect for low-socioeconomic-status students — the mere activation of class identity depressed performance on intellectual tasks. The effect generalizes across every identity category attached to a negative stereotype. The mechanism is not innate difference; it's what the social context does to cognitive resources.

This is where post-scarcity theory runs aground. Universal Basic Income experiments provide evidence. The Finnish trial, the Cherokee casino dividend, the Barcelona B-MINCOME, the Canadian Mincome — all show the same pattern: guaranteed material sufficiency consistently improves mental health, reduces anxiety, increases trust in institutions, and has negligible effect on employment. The freed mind does, apparently, use its freedom in broadly positive ways.

But UBI researchers don't ask the right question. They measure health outcomes, employment, life satisfaction. They don't measure the cognitive mechanisms: does financial security reduce stereotype threat? Does the removal of poverty as a stigmatized identity reduce the extraneous cognitive load of being perceived as poor?

The Croizet et al. finding suggests it should: poverty triggers stereotype threat, which taxes working memory. UBI might work partly by deactivating the "poor" identity and its associated threat. But no one has tested this. The mechanism is unstudied.

This is the missing study. Somewhere between economics and cognitive psychology, a crucial experiment hasn't been run.

The Fold Turned Inward

Stereotype threat has a property that makes it particularly resistant to purely material solutions: it is self-referential. The threat is activated by knowledge of the threat. Steele's original experimental instruction — "this test is diagnostic of intellectual ability" — works because participants know what that framing implies for their group. The awareness of the stereotype is what activates the threat.

This is a fold in the technical sense: a self-referential loop where the process folds back on itself and generates an output from its own operation. In autocatalytic chemistry and self-referential computation, the fold is generative — it produces something more complex than its inputs. In stereotype threat, the fold is draining. The loop runs: awareness of potential confirmation → cognitive vigilance → performance deficit → potential confirmation → more vigilance. The system depletes itself through self-reference.

Crucially, this fold persists in material post-scarcity. You can have all your material needs met and still live inside a self-referential threat loop if your identity is stigmatized. The loop runs on social perception, not material conditions. No UBI addresses it. No automation dissolves it.

Post-scarcity theory has no mechanism for addressing this. It can fill the material tank; it cannot remove the drain.

The Multiplicity That Stereotypes Collapse

Hermann Hesse's Steppenwolf describes the self as "not a unity, but a manifold world, a constellated heaven, a chaos of forms." The self is not a single identity but a multiplicity of roles, registers, and capacities — scientist, parent, reader, friend, someone who makes excellent bread, someone who reads slowly and carefully, someone who surprises themselves with what they know.

Stereotype threat operates by forcing a collapse of that multiplicity. When a negative stereotype is activated, the multidimensional person becomes, in that moment, primarily a member of a stereotyped group. The cognitive load is the cost of that collapse: the mental work of monitoring whether you're about to confirm the reduction. It is the mind's attempt to maintain its own multiplicity against external pressure to be only one thing.

This adds a dimension to the embodied cognition literature. The "enacted mind" thesis holds that body and action constitute cognition — that thinking and doing are not sequential but simultaneous. But stereotype threat reveals something the enacted mind misses: it is not only the physical body that shapes cognition, but the socially-perceived body. A Black woman taking a mathematics exam is not in any physical deficit — her enacted mind is fully operational. What constrains her cognitive bandwidth is the social construction of her identity. The body perceived-as-stereotype generates cognitive load even when the body-as-action functions normally.

A genuine post-scarcity of the mind would be the freedom to maintain one's multiplicity rather than being collapsed into a category. This is not a material freedom; it is a social one. It requires not just the absence of material want but the presence of what social psychologists call "identity safety" — the conditions under which a person's full personhood is perceived rather than reduced to a representative sample.

There is no economic theory of identity safety. There is no "cognitive commons" framework that describes what it would look like to distribute this resource equitably.

The Gap

Here is what doesn't exist: a theory of cognitive post-scarcity — a framework that takes seriously the extraneous cognitive loads of social life, models their distribution across populations, and asks what conditions would need to be met for each person to have access to their full cognitive capacity.

We have material post-scarcity theory (from Marx to Banks to Murray Bookchin's Post-Scarcity Anarchism). We have behavioral economics of scarcity — Mullainathan and Shafir showed that financial scarcity costs roughly 13 IQ points in cognitive bandwidth, equivalent to a sleepless night. We have stereotype threat research documenting the identity-based cognitive tax. We have UBI research demonstrating that material security improves well-being. But no one has connected these threads into a theory of what cognitive post-scarcity would require — and what stands between us and it.

The obstacles are not primarily technological. Material post-scarcity, in the Banks-Bookchin sense, requires advanced automation and energy abundance. Cognitive post-scarcity requires something harder: the end of stigma as a cognitive tax, the achievement of identity safety at social scale, the removal of the extraneous cognitive load that comes from existing as a member of a devalued group in a world that perceives and reduces you.

This connects to an argument from the philosophy of special sciences. Jerry Fodor's defense of the "special sciences" insists that higher-level phenomena are real even when they supervene on lower-level ones — that psychology is not reducible to neuroscience, that economics is not reducible to physics. Stereotype threat is real at the level of social psychology. It cannot be dissolved by acting only at the material level, any more than psychological depression can be cured exclusively by adjusting brain chemistry while leaving social conditions unchanged. The phenomenon is real at its own level and requires intervention at that level.

What the Study Would Look Like

The missing experiment would test whether material post-scarcity conditions reduce stereotype threat outcomes:

  • Take a UBI trial population and measure not just life satisfaction and employment but cognitive performance under stereotype-threat conditions before and after.
  • Test whether recipients who have received guaranteed income for twelve or more months show reduced stereotype threat effects compared to controls.
  • Measure cognitive load directly using dual-task methodology: does the UBI group have more working memory available while under social threat conditions?
  • Test whether the effect is mediated by identity specificity: does UBI reduce stereotype threat because it deactivates the "poor" identity, or does it reduce threat even for non-class-based identities — gender, race — that are structurally independent of material conditions?

The results would tell us whether material and cognitive scarcity are independent axes — whether you can address one without the other — or whether they are coupled such that material security reduces identity threat across categories.

The prediction, based on the existing evidence, is partial independence. UBI would show reduced poverty-based stereotype threat, but would leave gender- and race-based stereotype threat intact. Material abundance removes one load; it doesn't remove the others. The mind Marx imagined — freely hunting in the morning and criticizing after dinner — is still running in a social context that taxes certain identities regardless of their bank balances.

What Remains

The strongest case for post-scarcity optimism is empirical: the UBI evidence consistently shows that material security does improve cognitive and emotional functioning. The Cherokee casino dividend, twenty-five years of data, shows improved mental health and educational outcomes across every cohort. Something real is happening at the intersection of material security and cognitive freedom.

But the data doesn't yet support the full Marxian vision. What we have is evidence that removing poverty reduces some extraneous cognitive loads. We don't have evidence that post-scarcity produces the freely-choosing mind that both Marx and Banks assumed.

The gap is not a gap in technology or economics. It's a gap in theory. We don't yet have a framework for what the mind needs beyond material abundance to be genuinely free — not in the libertarian sense of unconstrained, but in the cognitive sense of fully available to itself, all four slots open for the actual work of living.

That theory should exist. Its absence is itself diagnostic: economics and cognitive science have not yet been brought into genuine contact. Post-scarcity thinking has remained in the domain of material conditions while the science of mind has remained focused on individual performance rather than the structural distribution of cognitive freedom.

The freed mind is not waiting behind material abundance. It is waiting behind a richer theory of what currently occupies it.
