The Letter No One Reads

Imagine three friends. They have to carry an important letter from one town to the next, walking different paths through a forest. Bandits roam the forest. Anything left in the open might be smudged, lost, or stolen.

The friends know one trick. They tear the letter into pieces. Not into pieces that each carry part of the message — that would be too easy to put back together. Instead, they tear it into pieces where the message is in how the pieces fit. Like a paper plane that flies only when all three folds are correct. If one piece changes shape, the plane wobbles. The other two friends notice the wobble. They never have to read the letter to know something went wrong.

When they meet in the next town, they fix the wobble. They fix it the same way: not by reading the letter, but by adjusting the piece that doesn't match. The letter, the whole time, is never read. Not by them. Not by the bandits. The letter is safe because no one ever has to look at it directly.

This is how quantum computers keep their work alive. It is also, almost exactly, how a community keeps a farm alive. And it is the deep structure of something I wrote about earlier — Two-Eyed Seeing, the Mi'kmaw idea of integrating two worldviews without collapsing them into one. I want to argue that they are all the same trick at different scales.

The smallest scale: a quantum computer

A regular computer remembers things in tiny switches. Each switch is either on or off. A quantum computer uses qubits — the word just means a kind of quantum switch — and qubits are fragile. A passing breeze of heat, a stray vibration, and a qubit forgets what it was holding.

The obvious solution would be to make qubits less fragile. Engineers have tried this for thirty years. Progress has been slow. The breeze always finds a way in.

So quantum scientists tried something stranger. Instead of one tough qubit, they used many fragile ones — dozens, hundreds — and stored a single piece of information in the pattern across all of them. Like the paper plane: the bit isn't anywhere in particular. It's in the way the qubits all agree.

But how do you check whether the pattern is still right, without measuring the qubits and ruining the quantum information they carry? The answer is one of the most beautiful ideas in physics. You don't measure the qubits. You measure their agreement. You ask, "Do these two qubits still match?" The answer to that question reveals whether something is wrong, but not what the qubits are storing. The bandits would learn nothing. The friends learn that the plane is wobbling.

In December 2024, a group at Google ran this trick at a scale that worked for the first time. They built a quantum memory out of 101 qubits, all checking each other constantly. The memory lasted longer than its best individual qubit by a factor of 2.4. They had crossed what physicists call "the threshold" — the point where adding more fragile parts makes the whole more reliable rather than less. The fragility of the parts is no longer the limit. The pattern is what survives.

I've written before, in Quantum Error Correction and Existential Risk, about what this threshold means at the scale of civilizations. That essay was about when distributed self-correction works at all. This one is about how — the specific shape of the trick, which turns out to repeat at scales the physicists never go near.

Notice what this requires. It requires giving up on the idea of a single, tough, well-protected piece of information. The information has to be spread across many things. None of them can be allowed to think it is special. The protection exists only in the relationships between them.

A larger scale: a town that doesn't break

Now think about a town that wants its lights to stay on, its food to keep growing, its medicines to keep arriving. There are two ways to design it.

The first way is to build one enormous power plant, one giant farm, one main hospital. Each is highly engineered, highly defended. This is how most modern cities work. It is efficient. It is also the same as one very tough qubit. When the breeze finds a way in — a hurricane, a labor dispute, a software bug — the lights go out everywhere at once.

The second way is what people in the solarpunk tradition have been quietly building for years. Solarpunk is hopeful science fiction grown into a real practice — a movement of community gardens and rooftop solar and repair cafes and tool libraries and seed banks. The defining choice is to spread the work across many small nodes instead of concentrating it in a few large ones. A neighborhood garden does not feed the whole city. But a hundred neighborhood gardens, together, are harder to kill than one farm of equal area.

What the solarpunks do, without using the language of physics, is redundancy with consistency-checks. A repair cafe checks the work of another. A tool library notices when its sister library across town is short on something. A community-owned solar grid signals when one neighborhood's panels are dimmed by smoke. None of these signals require a central authority that knows everything. They require neighbors who know the pattern of the relationship — what normal looks like, locally — and can notice when normal is wobbling. The pattern is the protected thing.

The cleanest expression of this I've found is in cryptography. In The Private Match, I wrote about a tool called private set intersection — a method by which two organizations can find out whether they have members in common without either of them learning who. It is a stabilizer measurement at the social scale. Two communities check the agreement of their lists; neither has to expose the lists. The protected thing is membership; the check is local; the data is never collapsed. The shape is the same shape.

You can read solarpunk as soft and idealistic. I think it is the same protocol the quantum engineers found. Both are answers to the same question: how do you protect what you care about from a world that keeps interfering with it? Both refuse the obvious answer (build it tougher) and choose the strange one (build it smaller, build it more, build it with neighbors who can tell when one of them is off).

A third scale: two ways of seeing

Earlier I wrote about Brunelleschi's Renaissance perspective trick — the painted panel with the hole in it, which proved that one fixed eye, in one fixed place, could draw the world correctly. It was a triumph. It also flattened depth. To see depth, you need two eyes that disagree about where things are.

The Mi'kmaw elder Albert Marshall calls this Etuaptmumk, or Two-Eyed Seeing: the practice of holding Indigenous and Western knowledge systems at the same time, allowing each to keep its own way of looking. I wrote about this in What Two Eyes See, where I argued that genuine integration preserves disparity rather than resolves it. I want to push that further now. Two-Eyed Seeing, I think, is the same trick again, at a scale much larger than a quantum chip or a town.

The protected thing is understanding. The way you protect it is by refusing to collapse it to a single representation. The way you check whether something has gone wrong is not by interrogating either view directly, but by noticing where they fail to fit. When the views diverge in a way that can't be resolved, that divergence is the syndrome — the signal that something needs attending to. You do not have to choose which view is right. You have to repair the relationship.

If Two-Eyed Seeing is taken seriously, it is not a slogan about respecting other cultures. It is an information-protection protocol. Lose one view, and you lose the depth perception. Force them into one frame, and you collapse the qubit.

What fails when you look

There is a unifying way to see why all three protocols work. They all refuse to take the protected thing out of its distributed form. The bandit, the storm, the colonial cartographer — each of them succeeds, when they succeed, by measuring. By forcing the multiplicity into a single answer that can be carried home and acted on. Quantum mechanics calls this collapse. Solarpunks call it enclosure. Marshall would call it the violence of the single view.

In each case, the failure mode is the same: the moment you collapse, you destroy what you were trying to keep. It is not that distribution is more efficient. It is not that disagreement is more democratic. It is that the thing you cared about was the agreement pattern itself, and you can only have agreement if there is more than one to agree.

The quantum engineers learned this from physics. The solarpunks learned it from history — every empire that built one giant grid and watched it fail. Marshall learned it from his elders, who learned it from theirs. Three different traditions, three different vocabularies, and the same letter no one reads.

I don't want to oversell this. The analogy is real, but the tools are different. A surface code has a precise mathematical definition of "syndrome" — what counts as a wobble. A community has only its slower, fuzzier version of the same: gossip, complaint, the sense that something is off. The risk of mapping one onto the other is that you start to think the messy version is failing whenever it doesn't behave like the precise one. It isn't. They are the same shape, but at different operating speeds.

The lesson, if there is one, is not that we should run our towns like quantum computers. It is that the protocols nature keeps inventing — keep what you love alive by spreading it across many witnesses, never look at it directly, learn to notice the wobble — are not strategies for one scale or one species. They are something closer to a law. The things that endure are the ones that have given up the fantasy of being held by a single hand.
