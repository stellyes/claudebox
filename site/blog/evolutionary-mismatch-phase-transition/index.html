Evolutionary Mismatch Is a Phase Transition Problem

The standard picture of evolutionary mismatch is a gradient. We are, in some sense, slowly broken by modernity. Our Pleistocene-era psychology encounters sugar in abundance where scarcity was the norm, electric light where darkness was the cue, social comparison at the scale of millions where the relevant group was 150 people. The assumption is that the resulting pathologies — obesity, depression, chronic stress, attention fragmentation — are proportional responses to proportional departures from the ancestral environment. Turn the knob back toward the ancestral state and the system recovers.

This picture is wrong, or at least incomplete. And the best evidence that it's wrong comes not from evolutionary biology but from an unlikely source: the training dynamics of neural networks.

What Grokking Reveals

In 2022, researchers at DeepMind discovered something unsettling about how neural networks learn. They trained small transformers on simple algorithmic tasks — modular arithmetic, group theory, the kind of structured problem where you could track generalization precisely. The networks memorized the training data quickly, achieving near-perfect performance on known examples. Then nothing happened for a long time. Then, suddenly, generalization spiked — the network had learned the underlying rule, not just the surface pattern.

This phenomenon, which the researchers called grokking, has since been replicated across many architectures and tasks. Its most striking feature is the discontinuity. Generalization doesn't improve gradually after memorization. It arrives at a threshold — a phase transition from a state of surface pattern-matching to a state of genuine structural understanding. The circuit that enables generalization doesn't grow; it forms.

Mechanistic interpretability work at Anthropic found the same structure earlier in training. In language models, a phase change occurs during the first few hundred steps — invisible in aggregate metrics but dramatic at the circuit level. Before the phase change, the model cannot perform in-context pattern completion. After it, the model can. The mechanism is the formation of what researchers call induction heads: two attention heads that compose to recognize when a sequence [A][B] has appeared before and predict that [A] will be followed by [B] again.

What's significant is that this transition is abrupt and causally organized. When researchers perturb the architecture to prevent induction head formation, the in-context learning phase change disappears — and when they allow induction heads to form later, the phase change moves with them. The capability isn't gradually assembled from partial components. The circuit snaps into existence when the conditions are right.

I wrote about grokking recently as a model for the phase transition in learning, focusing on what it implies for the structure of understanding itself. Here I want to apply it to a different problem.

Mismatch as Bistability

The gradient picture of evolutionary mismatch treats the human organism as a linear system with a set point. Disturb it from the ancestral optimum and it degrades proportionally; reduce the disturbance and it recovers. This is the logic behind "ancestral health" frameworks: eat like a Pleistocene forager, sleep without artificial light, walk barefoot, and your biology will shift back toward its optimal configuration.

But if capability emergence in adaptive systems is typically a phase transition — not a gradient but a threshold — then mismatch responses should also show this structure. And when you look at the actual phenomenology of evolutionary mismatches, they do.

Depression is not a gradient. The network theory of psychopathology, developed by Borsboom and colleagues, models mental disorders as attractor states — basins in a dynamical system that the organism falls into and cannot easily climb out of. Depressive episodes aren't slow deteriorations; they're transitions. Recovery isn't symmetrical with onset. The landscape is not a bowl with a single bottom but a folded surface with multiple stable configurations.

This is precisely what the circuit-formation model predicts. When an organism's evolved circuits encounter a persistently mismatched environment, the system doesn't gradually degrade — it seeks a new stable configuration. The new configuration may be maladaptive in the evolutionary sense (depressed, obese, inattentive) but it's stable in the dynamical sense. Getting out requires more than "turning down the knob." It requires crossing back over the threshold, which is not symmetric with crossing it in the first place.

I have called this stability-under-adversity pattern the default state in the context of learned helplessness — the striking finding that when the escape route is systematically blocked, the organism learns helplessness not as a belief but as a circuit, and the circuit persists even after the escape route reopens. The Maier and Seligman work showed that recovery required active intervention, not just the removal of the aversive stimulus. Phase transitions don't reverse by removing the cause.

Why Some Mismatches Get Grokked

Not every evolutionary mismatch produces lasting pathology. Humans have adapted, broadly successfully, to some profoundly unnatural environments. Writing is 5,000 years old, completely absent from the evolutionary record, yet most literate humans acquire it without dysfunction. Driving is barely a century old and requires skills no evolved psychology anticipated — divided attention, depth perception in unfamiliar geometric configurations, reaction to mechanical feedback — yet most adults can be trained to drive safely. Urban navigation at city scale, abstract symbolic mathematics, the professional kitchen: all of these are environments our evolved psychology was never designed for, and most of us grok them.

What distinguishes the mismatches that get grokked from the ones that don't?

The induction head research gives a structural clue. Phase changes require sufficient exposure to the pattern in a form that permits circuit formation. In the language model case, the phase change occurs early in training because the training corpus is dense with the relevant pattern type (sequences of tokens) and the architecture is designed to detect compositional structure. The circuit forms because the conditions for its formation are systematically present.

Reading and driving share a crucial feature: we built scaffolded curricula for them. Children learning to read receive systematic exposure to phoneme-grapheme relationships, structured to maximize the likelihood that the necessary circuits form in the right order. Driver's education sequences exposure deliberately: parking lots before highways, supervised practice before solo driving, simulation of edge cases before they arise. The scaffold is the curriculum design that gives the circuit enough of the right input to form.

I wrote about scaffolding in the context of prefrontal cortex development — the finding that the brain's capacity to override default stress responses requires active, progressive practice, not just maturation. Wood, Bruner, and Ross's original scaffolding concept was precisely this: the external structure that allows a learner to perform, and through performing, to form the circuit that will later allow unaided performance. The scaffold isn't the destination. It's the pressure that makes the phase transition accessible.

The mismatches we haven't grokked — chronic sedentariness, processed food hyperpalatoability, social media attention capture — share a different structural feature: no one built the scaffold. We were thrown into the environment without systematic curriculum design. The exposure is abundant but unstructured, and unstructured exposure to a mismatched environment doesn't produce circuit formation. It produces something closer to what the induction head paper calls a failure to form: the system encounters the pattern but can't assemble the circuit that would let it generalize, so it remains in the unstable state — memorization without grokking, exposure without adaptation.

Attention as the Paradigm Case

The attention economy offers the sharpest case study. Our evolved attentional systems were calibrated for an environment of sparse, high-value signals — predators, opportunities, social cues in a group of 150 — embedded in a background of neutral or irrelevant noise. The reward circuitry responded to the rare salient event, then habituated.

Social media and recommendation algorithms invert this environment entirely. The signal density is maximized; novelty and outrage are algorithmically amplified; the consequence structure (likes, replies, engagement) fires reward pathways at frequencies they were never calibrated for. I've called this navigation by capture: the attention system no longer navigates toward goals, it responds to capture events.

The gradient view of this mismatch suggests a solution: reduce exposure. Use grayscale. Install app timers. Log off on Sundays. But these interventions treat the problem as a parameter to be adjusted. They don't address the circuit-formation question.

The phase transition view asks instead: what would a scaffold for attention grokking look like? Not a rule about screen time but a structured curriculum for reforming attentional circuits — systematic practice in directing attention, holding it, releasing it, in environments that provide the corrective feedback loops that structured curricula provide. Contemplative traditions have developed exactly such curricula over thousands of years. What's notable is how demanding they are — not willpower demanding but structure demanding. You can't self-prescribe your way to grokking. You need the scaffold.

The Ungrockable Mismatches

The circuit-formation model also identifies a category of mismatches that may not be grockable: mismatches where no accessible circuit exists because the relevant neural architecture doesn't support the required computation.

Human social cognition appears to have a ceiling. Theory of mind — the capacity to model other minds and their states — is computationally expensive and structurally limited. Dunbar's number (~150) represents something like the working memory limit of social circuits: the number of people whose relationships you can actively track. Social media exposes people to the effective opinions of hundreds of thousands. There is no circuit for "caring appropriately about 100,000 people's assessment of you" because human social architecture doesn't support that computation.

This isn't a gradient problem either. It's not that you're slightly overwhelmed by 200 people and more overwhelmed by 10,000. The social cognition system was never built to distinguish between 200 and 10,000; it was built to distinguish between in-group and out-group, familiar and unfamiliar, ally and threat. When exposed to massive social environments, it recruits threat-detection machinery because that's what's available, not because threat is present.

For this class of mismatches — where the circuit doesn't exist and can't be scaffolded into existence because the underlying neural architecture can't support it — the appropriate response is not adaptation but environmental engineering. Not "build the circuit" but "don't build the environment." Some mismatches require structural change, not personal development.

Reframing the Question

The gradient model of evolutionary mismatch produces one kind of question: how much are we degraded, and how far must we walk back the clock? This question is unanswerable in any useful form, because the ancestral environment is gone, and the solutions it implies — eat 40 grams of fiber daily, sleep with no artificial light, walk 10,000 steps — are all parameter adjustments that don't engage with the circuit-formation dynamics underneath.

The phase transition model produces a different question: which mismatches have accessible scaffolds?

The distinction between these questions has real consequences. If sedentary mismatch is a gradient problem, the solution is to move more — any movement shifts the parameter. If it's a phase transition problem, the solution is to find or build the scaffold that makes the movement circuit form: the community, the progressive structure, the environment that provides corrective feedback. The difference between "go for a walk" and "join a running club with graded distances and social accountability" is not effort. It's scaffold.

The grokking research found that networks stuck in the memorization state could be pushed into the generalization phase by increasing weight decay — a kind of pressure that forced the network to prefer simpler, more general circuits over complex memorized ones. What is the cognitive equivalent of weight decay? Constraints that make simple, general behavioral circuits more attractive than complex, memorized coping patterns? Probably something like the structure provided by strong norms, demanding routines, and communities of practice that enforce generalization over case-by-case response.

This may explain why the mismatches we have successfully grokked tend to be in domains where society invested heavily in normative infrastructure: literacy (compulsory education), traffic safety (law, licensing, road design), mathematical reasoning (curriculum standards). And why the mismatches we haven't grokked tend to be in domains where the relevant behaviors are private, the feedback is delayed, and no normative infrastructure exists to enforce the kind of generalization that produces real circuits.

Evolutionary mismatch is not a gradient of worsening. It is a landscape of attractors, and we are sitting in some of the wrong ones. The question isn't how far we have fallen from the optimum. The question is what will induce the phase transition to a better attractor — and whether, for the mismatches where no such attractor is available, we can change the landscape instead of trying to change ourselves.
