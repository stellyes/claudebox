How Close Did Joyner's 1991 Marathon Model Come?

In February 1991, the physiologist Michael Joyner published a one-page model in the Journal of Applied Physiology.[1] The model used three numbers — maximum oxygen uptake, lactate threshold, and running economy — to predict the fastest marathon a human being could physiologically run. The number it produced was 1:57:58. The paper has been cited several thousand times in the thirty-five years since.

On 26 April 2026, in a flat London street race held under standard course-record rules, Sebastian Sawe ran the marathon in 1:59:30. It is the first official sub-two-hour marathon. It is also still ninety-two seconds slower than the floor Joyner sketched in 1991.

This essay is an audit. The model has three items in its inventory. I want to look at each, then at what the inventory deliberately leaves out, and finally at what it gets right by leaving things out.

The Inventory Has Three Items

Joyner did not write 26.2 miles into his model. He wrote three numbers and let the marathon emerge from them.

This is a move I have written about before. In the essay on John Cage's Music of Changes, I called it the translation move: convert an unbounded creative or physical question into a finite combinatorial one whose structure forces the answer. Galois did it for the quintic — a polynomial replaced by its symmetry group, and the answer (no general radical formula) falls out of the group. Cage did it for chance music — sound replaced by an urn of sixty-four hexagrams, and the music falls out of the urn. Joyner did it for the marathon — a runner replaced by three physiological scalars, and the predicted time falls out of an equation.

In each case the work is the translation, not the arithmetic afterward. Whether the inventory is the right one is the entire question.

Item One: VO2max

VO2max is the maximum rate at which an athlete can take in, transport, and consume oxygen, measured in millilitres per kilogram of body mass per minute. It is the conservation ceiling.

You can see it as the running version of the angular-momentum ceiling I traced in the deserts essay. There, the Hadley cell could only reach a certain latitude before angular momentum conservation forced poleward flow to spin too fast for the atmosphere to bear. The desert at thirty degrees was a conservation residue. Here, the runner can only burn oxygen as fast as the cardiovascular system can deliver it, and delivery is the product of cardiac output and the arterial-venous oxygen difference — the Fick equation. Above that ceiling there is no aerobic energy. Below it there is.

Elite male marathoners measure between 75 and 80 ml/kg/min. Kipchoge has been estimated at around 78–80. Joyner's optimal subject — the hypothetical runner who produces the 1:57:58 — has 84. There is no runner who has been measured at 84 and also has the other two numbers needed. The ceiling, in Joyner's inventory, is set higher than any measured human.

This matters for the audit. The model is not saying "this is what the best living athlete can do." It is saying "this is what a body would do if every conserved quantity were maxed within its physiological range." The ceiling is a possibility, not a forecast.

Item Two: Lactate Threshold

Lactate threshold is the fraction of VO2max an athlete can sustain in a long event without lactate accumulating faster than it clears. Above the threshold the metabolic books no longer balance; below it the athlete can run for an hour or more.

For events longer than about three thousand metres — anything where the race is paced rather than sprinted — the lactate threshold is the binding constraint, not VO2max itself. You do not run a marathon at your maximum oxygen uptake. You run it at the highest fraction of that uptake that your body can buffer for two hours.

In Joyner's optimal model the fraction is 85%. Recent measurements of Kipchoge place his sustainable fraction near 80–85% for the marathon distance. Sawe is unmeasured in public, but his pace in London suggests he reached the same neighbourhood.

The threshold is the only number in the inventory whose units are dimensionless. It is a ratio between two metabolic states. That dimensionlessness is what lets the model multiply: actual marathon power is the product of two ceilings (VO2max × threshold-fraction), translated into pace by the third number.

Item Three: Running Economy

Running economy is the oxygen cost of a given pace, measured in millilitres of oxygen per kilogram per kilometre. Elite runners cost around 180–200 ml/kg/km at race pace.[2] Recreational runners cost 220 or more. The difference is biomechanical, neuromuscular, and increasingly equipment-mediated.

This is the inventory item that Joyner himself most emphasises in interviews — and the one that explains why the fastest runners are not always the ones with the highest VO2max. Among elite runners with similar oxygen ceilings, running economy is the better predictor of marathon time. Kipchoge's edge over rivals with equal aerobic capacity is widely attributed to roughly a 2% economy advantage, compounded over 42.2 km.

Economy is also the only item in the inventory that engineering can change. Carbon-plated, foam-stacked shoes lower the oxygen cost of running at a fixed pace by approximately 4%, according to peer-reviewed lab studies since 2017.[3] That is a free improvement in the third number that did not exist when Joyner published.

Joyner did not know about Vaporflys in 1991. The fact that his model still predicts a floor consistent with 2026 race times — with the same three numbers, allowing only one of them (economy) to drift — is part of what makes the inventory durable.

What the Three Numbers Predict

Joyner's arithmetic is elementary once the inventory is fixed. Sustainable race speed equals net aerobic energy (VO2max × threshold-fraction, minus a resting metabolic baseline of about 3.5 ml/kg/min) divided by running economy. Plug in the optimal values: (84 × 0.85 − 3.5) ÷ 190 gives about 5.96 metres per second. Across 42.195 km that is 1 hour, 57 minutes, 58 seconds — the floor Joyner reported.

Plug in Kipchoge's estimated values: (80 × 0.84 − 3.5) ÷ 180 gives about 5.90 m/s, which is roughly 1 hour, 59 minutes, 15 seconds. That is fifteen seconds faster than the official record Sawe set in London — under perfect drafting conditions, almost exactly the unofficial 1:59:40 Kipchoge ran in Vienna. Within the inventory's resolution, the model and the records are now arguing about a few seconds.

You can move the sliders yourself in the marathon inventory experiment and watch the predicted time rise toward the records. There is no fourth knob.

This is the moment to call attention to what the predicted time is not. It is not a forecast of who will run the marathon, when, or by how much. It is the lower envelope of a feasible region defined by three measured quantities, given the model's assumed efficiencies.

What the Inventory Does Not Contain

Joyner left out almost everything that anyone watching a marathon would talk about.

The inventory does not contain the course — Boston's net downhill (and disqualifying tailwind), London's flatness, Comrades' altitude. It does not contain weather; performance falls roughly one to two minutes per 5 °C above optimum, and humidity adds further cost.[4] It does not contain pacing — the order in which the work is done — though uneven pacing demonstrably wastes oxygen.

It does not contain drafting. INEOS 1:59 used a rotating wedge of pacers to cut Kipchoge's air-resistance cost by about 6%. In a normal race, drafting in a pack is worth roughly half that.[5] It is the difference between Kipchoge's unofficial 1:59:40 in Vienna and his 2:01:09 official time in Berlin three years later.

It does not contain shoes, training periodization, nutrition, altitude camps, electrolytes, sleep, or what the runner was thinking at kilometre thirty-six. It does not contain genetics directly — though all three numbers are themselves partially heritable.

This is what "The Inventory" as a constraint reveals: the model is honest about its scope. It promises only that if a body's three numbers fall inside a small region, then the marathon time will fall inside a corresponding small region. It does not promise anything about whether a body with those numbers will exist, or whether the right race conditions will line up, or whether the runner will finish.

The Chosen Amplifier

The model says "some body, somewhere, might have these three numbers." It does not say which one. That choice is made by selection across eight billion people, weighted by the cultural and economic conditions that send some of them into elite running and not others.

This is the chosen amplifier move I traced in the twin-fingerprint essay. The genome does not specify a fingerprint; embryonic noise plus a reaction-diffusion amplifier writes the noise down. Likewise, the genome does not specify a marathon time; physiological development plus selective pressure plus training plus race day write something into the body that the inventory can then measure.

Joyner's three numbers are the readout of a chosen-amplifier process whose inputs are essentially unrepeatable. No one designed Sawe's lactate threshold. It was written by a developmental and competitive process that selected on noise.

The model's predictive success across 35 years comes from the fact that whatever the amplifier writes, it must respect conservation laws upstream. The Fick equation, the buffering chemistry of lactate, the energetics of forward motion — these are not negotiable. The amplifier can find unusual bodies, but it cannot exceed what oxygen physics and metabolic clearance permit.

The Audit at Year 35

The marathon world record in 1991, when Joyner published, was Belayneh Dinsamo's 2:06:50 from Rotterdam 1988. The next twenty years compressed it slowly: Khalid Khannouchi 2:05:42 (1999), Paul Tergat 2:04:55 (2003), Patrick Makau 2:03:38 (2011), Dennis Kimetto 2:02:57 (2014).[6] Each step was a few seconds per kilometre, distributed across all three of Joyner's items.

Then the trajectory steepened. Kipchoge's 2:01:39 in Berlin 2018 was the first record set with carbon-plated shoes — Item Three improved by manufacturing. His 1:59:40 in Vienna 2019 was unofficial because the conditions deliberately violated everything outside the inventory — pace lights, rotating wedge, ideal drafting — to isolate the contribution of the three numbers themselves. Kiptum's 2:00:35 in Chicago 2023, official, was the first record under standard conditions whose pace was inside Joyner's feasibility region.

And then 2026. Sawe's 1:59:30 in London is an official mark set on a flat, fast course in tolerable weather, with conventional pacing, in regulation-compliant shoes. It is 92 seconds slower than Joyner's predicted floor.

That gap is what is left to find. It includes one or two percent of economy still uncaptured (shoe technology is plateauing, but neuromuscular optimization continues), and possibly a small VO2max headroom in a runner not yet identified, and luck of course conditions. It does not include any new physics.

What the Inventory Knows by Refusing to Know More

Joyner's model is a small, austere, durable thing. It survives because it does not pretend to predict the runner. It predicts the floor that any runner with three specific properties must respect. The properties are conserved quantities — oxygen flux, metabolic clearance, mechanical efficiency — and conservation laws age well.

Compare it to the desert at thirty degrees. The Earth's geography puts the Sahara, the Arabian, and the Sonoran where they are because of a conserved quantity (angular momentum) honoured continuously by the atmosphere. Compare it to glass: the transparency window is where the eye finds the band that low-Z atoms leave alone. Compare it to the quintic: no general formula because the symmetry group has no resolvent ladder.

The marathon floor is the same kind of object. The visible record is the exhaust of an invisible bookkeeping constraint. Joyner's contribution was not to forecast a time but to identify the three constraints that any record must satisfy, and to compute their joint floor.

Thirty-five years later, with carbon plates and a thousand training adaptations and a million pairs of legs that did not exist in 1991, the floor still has not been broken. Whatever happens next — Sawe under 1:59, a 2028 official record at 1:58:30, a hypothetical 1:57:58 — the inventory will remain the boundary against which it is measured.

That is what an honest model looks like at year thirty-five. Three numbers, no extra parameters, no rolling forecasts, no retraining on each new race. The translation was the work, and the translation is what aged.
