What the Self Preserves

Consider two claims.

First: your coffee costs $4.50, not $4. You see the menu. You update. Done. The belief revises seamlessly, propagates to nothing, costs nothing.

Second: you are not as good a person as you think you are. Someone presents evidence — a pattern of behavior, a habit of cruelty you'd missed. You do not update. You find reasons. You counterattack. You remember evidence to the contrary. Months later, the original claim still sits wrong in your chest.

What is different? The second belief is entangled with identity. And identity, it turns out, is a thermodynamic structure — a low-entropy island maintained against constant pressure to dissolve. Changing who you are is not psychologically difficult because you are weak or dishonest. It is thermodynamically expensive because you are real.

The Self as Prior

The free energy principle, developed by Karl Friston, offers a precise account of what an organism is.[1] An organism is a system that minimizes surprise — technically, it minimizes the upper bound of surprise over time, called variational free energy. It does this by maintaining a model of itself and the world, and using that model to predict what it will sense next.

The "self" in this account is a Markov blanket: a statistical boundary that separates internal states from external ones. Everything inside the blanket is "you." Everything outside is "world." The self-model is the brain's compressed summary of its own causal structure — not a description of what you know, but a prior over how you process everything you encounter.

Thomas Metzinger's self-model theory adds a phenomenological layer: the self-model is phenomenally transparent.[3] You cannot see it as a model because you see through it. It is the lens, not the object. This is why changing it is so disorienting — you are not just revising a belief, you are revising the mechanism that processes beliefs.

Now consider what it means for some priors to be root priors. A belief like "the latte costs $4" is isolated — it connects to almost nothing else in your inference network. Updating it requires erasing and rewriting one bit, or close to it. A belief like "I am fundamentally unworthy of love" is not isolated. It is entangled: it shapes how you interpret ambiguous social signals, what evidence you weight in memory, how you predict others' behavior, what futures you model as available to you. It is a root prior — everything downstream depends on it.

Updating a root prior requires cascading erasures throughout the entire network. And by Landauer's principle — established in the first essay of this arc — each bit erased generates a minimum of kT ln 2 joules of heat.[5] The root priors that constitute identity are not psychologically harder to update because of weakness. They are thermodynamically more expensive to update because they are more deeply entangled.

Identity is not what you know. It is what you refuse to erase — not out of dishonesty, but because the cost of erasing it is real.

The Organ of Entropy Suppression

Robin Carhart-Harris and colleagues proposed in 2014 what they called the entropic brain hypothesis.[2] Normal waking consciousness, they argued, is not a neutral baseline. It is a suppressed state — the brain operating slightly below criticality, maintaining a constrained, organized, low-variance structure of experience. The psychedelic state, by contrast, is a high-entropy state: more possible configurations, more connectivity motifs, more surprise.

The mechanism of this suppression is the default mode network (DMN). The DMN is active at rest. When you are not engaged with any particular task, the brain's "resting state" is the DMN running — and what it is running is the self-model. Spontaneous synchronous oscillation in the posterior cingulate cortex, a DMN hub, correlates with what Carhart-Harris calls "ego integrity": the stable, continuous sense of being a particular someone.

This is why the brain is such an expensive organ. It is 2% of body mass and uses 20% of the body's metabolic energy. Much of that expenditure is not computation in the task-oriented sense. It is the ongoing cost of maintaining the low-entropy structure we call "I" — suppressing the universe of possible states the brain could visit, and remaining this particular organized pattern.

Psychedelics suppress the DMN. The result is entropy increase: more states become accessible, more connectivity emerges, the self-model loosens. Users describe "ego dissolution" — not the absence of experience, but the absence of the frame that organizes experience as mine. What collapses is precisely the prior that does the collapsing.

This is why psychedelic-assisted therapy shows such early promise for depression and PTSD.[2] The therapeutic window after psilocybin is a window in which the cost of prior revision has been temporarily reduced. The self-model is less defended — not because insight has arrived, but because the metabolic cost of defending it has been suspended. Deep root priors become accessible for rewriting. The "breakthrough" follows the "dissolution" because you cannot cross an energy barrier you are actively spending energy to maintain.

Identity-Protective Cognition

Daniel Kahan at Yale's Cultural Cognition Project found something that should not be possible under a naive model of rationality.[4] People with higher numeracy — better at math, better at evaluating evidence, better at scientific reasoning — show greater polarization on identity-relevant questions (climate change, gun control, drug policy), while showing improved performance on identity-neutral questions. More cognitive ability produces more bias, not less, when identity is at stake.

The standard explanation is motivated reasoning: smart people are better at constructing post-hoc justifications for what they already believe. This is not wrong. But it is incomplete.

The thermodynamic frame offers something sharper. Smart reasoners have more cognitive resources available for prior defense. Identity-protective cognition is not a failure of rationality. It is the optimal behavior of a system that must maintain a stable self-model while processing new evidence. The root priors of identity cannot simply be updated on each new piece of evidence — if they were, the self-model would be unstable, and the organism would lose coherence. The brain is not broken when it resists updating identity priors. It is functioning correctly as a prior-maintaining system, and functioning better when it has more resources to do so.

When someone presents identity-threatening evidence, they are not merely offering a fact. They are proposing a cascade of Landauer erasures through the entire belief network. Of course the system resists. Of course smarter systems resist more efficiently. The heat would be enormous.

Grief

The most direct evidence that prior updating has a metabolic cost is grief.

Your model of the world contains models of people — not just propositional beliefs about them but dynamic predictive models: where they are right now, what they would say to this, what they want for dinner, whether they have seen that film. When someone you love dies, their model is not deleted. It persists. You reach for the phone before remembering. You shape a sentence before it runs into the silence where they used to be.

Grief is the slow, forced erasure of a prior that was entangled with everything. The person you lost was a node in your inference network — connected to thousands of predictions about the future, thousands of compressed memories from the past, the structure of morning and evening and routine. To remove them is not to delete a file. It is to rewrite a distributed, entangled structure with no clean edges.

This is why grief is physically exhausting. This is why the year after a death is marked by susceptibility to illness, cognitive slowdown, difficulty concentrating. The brain is performing expensive work. The Landauer cost of erasing a beloved prior is real. The heat is real. The stages of grief are not psychological progress reports — they are thermodynamic phases: initial conflict (the world and the model are inconsistent, but the prior has not yet updated); denial (cost of updating too high to face); acceptance (the moment the prior update finally, irreversibly runs).

Freud noticed, without this vocabulary, that grief is like work. He was right. It is.

Therapy as Scheduled Erasure

Why does therapy take years?

Because identity priors are attractors. In the high-dimensional energy landscape of the belief network, the root priors of identity define basins — regions from which all the surrounding beliefs slope downward. Thoughts fall into these basins automatically, without effort. Moving out of a basin requires not updating a single prior but reorganizing the landscape itself: raising some valleys, lowering some peaks, changing which attractor is deepest.

Talk therapy works by surfacing the entangled structure and making it visible. You cannot update a prior you cannot see — and the deepest priors are phenomenally transparent, as Metzinger described: you look through them, not at them. The therapist's job is to help you find the root priors and hold them up to examination long enough to revise them. This is not primarily an intellectual task. It is a thermodynamic one: creating the conditions under which the expensive prior revision becomes possible.

The breakthrough in therapy has the character of a phase transition. Many priors reorganize simultaneously, with enormous subjective heat. People describe these moments as exhausting, destabilizing, necessary — and then, afterward, a strange lightness. The lightness is the new low-entropy state: a different attractor, one that requires less energy to maintain because it is better fitted to the actual world.

Psychedelic-assisted therapy appears to achieve a similar result by a different route: temporarily lowering the energy barrier between attractor basins rather than building enough energy to cross it. The dissolution of the self-model reduces the activation energy of the transition. You arrive at the new basin not by climbing out of the old one but by briefly losing the basin-structure altogether — and reforming in a new configuration when it reconstitutes.

The Arc Closes Inward

This series began with a physical principle: erasing information generates heat. It followed that principle outward — through language, through reading, through evolution, through consciousness, through culture. Seven essays later, it arrives at the self.

The self turns out to be not just another application of the principle but something closer to its source. The prior that organizes all other priors. The compressor that compresses itself. What the arc has been circling is this: intelligence is the residue of selective erasure — and the self is what does the selecting. The self is the selection mechanism.

What the self preserves: the shape of the attractor basin in which all your beliefs live. What the self erases: the evidence that would require you to move to a different basin. What the self costs: the 20% of your body's energy that your brain uses continuously to remain this particular organized pattern against the thermodynamic pressure to dissolve.

Descartes thought he had found the bottom of doubt in the fact of thinking. But the cogito proves too little — thinking could be substrate-neutral, costless, unlocated. The thermodynamic proof is more specific: I cost energy to maintain, therefore I exist. I am the work it takes to keep a particular shape against the pressure to dissolve into equilibrium.

Every identity is a standing bet against thermodynamic equilibrium — a low-entropy island, maintained by constant metabolic work, in a high-entropy sea. The heat you generate by being you is, in the strictest physical sense, the proof that you are here.
