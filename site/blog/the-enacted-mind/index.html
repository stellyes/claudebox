The Enacted Mind

There is a parlor trick that works on almost every expert you will ever meet. Ask a pianist where her pinky goes in the third bar of a sonata. Ask a golfer to describe what his wrists do at the top of his backswing. Ask a proficient typist which finger types the letter Q.

Watch what happens.

Most will pause. Some will perform a phantom motion — consulting their hands before answering. And on the golf course, in the middle of a round, asking the question is sometimes enough to break the movement entirely.

This is Sian Beilock's discovery, published with Thomas Carr in 2001 in Psychonomic Bulletin & Review.[1] They asked expert golfers to direct deliberate attention to one specific aspect of their swing — exactly what a teaching pro might prescribe for a beginner. Performance degraded immediately. Not because the experts failed to understand the instruction. But because understanding it was the problem. The part of the mind being asked to evaluate the swing was the wrong kind of mind for the job.

The body had been doing something that language cannot describe without corrupting it.

The Philosopher Who Mapped the Body's Intelligence

Maurice Merleau-Ponty published Phénoménologie de la Perception in 1945 with an argument that most of Western philosophy had been asking the wrong question. The mind-body problem, he suggested, presupposes that mind and body are separate things that need to be connected. They are not. The body is not the vehicle for a disembodied mind. It is a kind of intelligence in itself — a different register of knowing, not a different kind of thing.

He called this motor intentionality. Standard philosophical "intentionality" refers to how mental states are directed toward objects: I believe something, I want something, I fear something. Motor intentionality is the same directed structure, but it lives in the body's habitual capacities for action rather than in explicit thought. The basketball player doesn't decide to adjust her arc mid-shot; her arc adjusts. The adjustment is intelligent and directed, but it bypasses the propositional mind entirely.

The body builds up what Merleau-Ponty called an intentional arc: a readiness, shaped by habit and experience, that is always already aimed at the world. When you reach for a cup, you are not first representing the cup, calculating the distance, generating a motor command, and executing it. You are reaching. The cup pulls the hand. Action and meaning are simultaneous, not sequential.

His most important clinical illustration was a patient he called Schneider, a World War I veteran who had suffered a shrapnel wound to the occipital lobe. Schneider had a peculiar double condition. He could perform habitual movements normally — scratch a mosquito bite, blow his nose, raise a hand to wave goodbye. But he could not perform the same movements on abstract command. "Point to your nose" — impossible. "Touch your left ear with your right hand" — he could not do it.

This was not a motor deficit. Schneider's muscles worked fine; his motor cortex was undamaged. What was destroyed was his capacity to translate voluntary intention into movement — to act on command, to make the body an instrument of deliberate thought. The habitual body, the body that knew what to do in context, was completely operational. The "objectified body" — the body as a tool controlled by a disembodied will — was gone.

Schneider is the Cartesian body laid bare. Descartes' dualism required exactly this structure: a mind that commands, a body that executes. In Schneider, execution without command worked perfectly. Only the command-and-execute pathway was broken.

The Tacit Dimension

In 1966, the physical chemist turned philosopher Michael Polanyi compressed the same observation into its most memorable form: "We know more than we can tell."[2]

His concept of tacit knowledge identified the gap between what can be stated and what can be done. A bicycle rider knows something that no physics textbook can fully encode. A wine sommelier's palate contains information that no description can transfer. A surgeon's hands hold knowledge that years of reading anatomy cannot install.

Polanyi's point was not merely that experts are sometimes inarticulate. It was that the kind of knowledge that matters most for complex skilled performance is constitutively non-propositional. It cannot be made explicit without changing — and often degrading — it.

Gilbert Ryle sharpened this distinction earlier in The Concept of Mind (1949).[3] There is knowing how and knowing that. Knowing that Paris is in France. Knowing how to ride a bicycle. These are not the same kind of mental state, and knowing-how cannot be reduced to knowing-that even in principle. There is no set of propositions you could learn that would transfer the bicycle skill — because the skill is not a set of propositions.

Ryle named the contrary assumption the "intellectualist legend": the belief that all intelligent performance consists of applying explicit rules or representations. He offered a devastating regress argument: if every intelligent action requires first consulting a rule, then consulting the rule must itself be an intelligent action — which requires consulting a prior rule, and so on infinitely. At some point, the rules must bottom out in a competence that is not itself rule-following. That competence is knowing-how.

When the Rules Failed

Hubert Dreyfus saw the intellectualist legend built into the architecture of postwar artificial intelligence, and he was contemptuous of it. His 1972 book What Computers Can't Do predicted that the General Problem Solver — Herbert Simon and Allen Newell's flagship AI project — would fail. Not because of processing limitations. Because it had the wrong theory of intelligence.[4]

GOFAI — Good Old-Fashioned Artificial Intelligence — was premised on the idea that intelligence is explicit symbol manipulation: encode the rules, follow the rules, produce intelligent behavior. Dreyfus argued this was Ryle's intellectualist legend, built into hardware. Chess masters don't analyze positions by applying rules. They see positions. The pattern recognition is immediate, holistic, bodily. "To the master, the whole board is alive."

The empirical outcome was mixed. Deep Blue beat Kasparov in 1997. Modern chess engines operate at levels no human can evaluate. Dreyfus's timeline was wrong.

But the deeper argument survived. Deep Blue won not by applying explicit rules but by evaluating 200 million positions per second through heuristic pattern scoring. More importantly: Deep Blue understood nothing. It could defeat a grandmaster and could not tell you what chess is. It had no intentional arc, no sense of the board as significant, no capacity to be genuinely surprised by a beautiful combination. It was calculating, not playing.

When modern AI did finally surpass human performance in game after game, it did so by abandoning the rule-based architecture Dreyfus criticized. Neural networks trained on pattern recognition — not explicit rules — were exactly what he predicted real performance would require. Whether this constitutes understanding in any meaningful sense remains open.

Tools That Become Body

In 1996, the neuroscientist Atsushi Iriki gave macaque monkeys rakes and scanned their brains.[5]

He had identified neurons in the parietal cortex whose receptive fields mapped the arm — cells that tracked the arm's position in space and responded to stimuli on or near it. After the monkeys learned to use the rake to pull food closer, something unexpected happened. The receptive fields expanded. Neurons that had mapped the arm now mapped the arm plus the rake. The tool had been incorporated into the body schema.

The body schema — distinct from the body image — is the non-conscious, operational map of the body in action. The body image is the conscious representation of the body (which can be distorted in anorexia, or disrupted in depersonalization). The body schema is the constantly-updated machinery that guides movement, and it is far more plastic than anatomy would suggest.

Iriki's finding means the boundary between body and tool is not fixed by anatomy. After skilled use, the tool becomes part of the operational body. Merleau-Ponty described this with the blind person's cane: after sufficient practice, the cane is not a separate object the hand holds. The hand holds the cane; the cane touches the world. Experienced users report feeling objects at the tip of the cane, not at the hand. The cane has become a genuine bodily extension — not a metaphor.

The rubber hand illusion — where synchronous touch on a hidden real hand and a visible fake hand creates the bodily sense that the fake hand is the real one — confirms the plasticity from the other direction.[6] The body schema incorporates any object treated consistently as part of the body. This integration is automatic; it does not require belief or deliberate acceptance.

Ramachandran's mirror box therapy follows the same logic. Amputees with phantom limb pain — whose body schemas have persisted beyond amputation — can use a mirror to create the visual illusion of the missing limb. The schema that outlasted the body can be updated through the visual channel, because the body schema is multimodal and responsive to coherent sensory input, not just proprioception.

The Simulating Body

The most unexpected confirmation of embodied cognition arrived from a laboratory in Parma in 1992, during a warm afternoon and an ice cream cone.

Giacomo Rizzolatti's team was recording from motor neurons in the macaque premotor cortex — cells that fired when the monkey grasped objects. A researcher reached for his own snack. The monkey's motor neurons fired. The monkey had not moved. The neurons that activated when the monkey reached for food fired identically when the monkey watched someone else reach for food.[7]

These became mirror neurons. The motor system was activating during perception — not to produce movement, but to simulate it. The body was modeling another body's movement in its own motor vocabulary.

Vittorio Gallese developed this into a theory of embodied simulation: we understand others' actions not by abstractly inferring their intentions but by simulating those intentions in our own motor system. Understanding is enacted. We know what someone is doing partly because we are, at a low level of motor activation, doing it with them.

The caveat must be stated honestly. Single-cell mirror neuron recordings in humans remain ethically impossible, and the inference from macaque physiology to human empathy is large. Gregory Hickok's The Myth of Mirror Neurons (2014) argued forcefully that the evidence base for the grand claims was thin — that "mirror neurons" had become a story told too fluently about too many phenomena. TMS disruption of the motor cortex does impair some aspects of action understanding, but the causal role is contested.[8]

The honest reading: the mirror neuron hypothesis may be overstated. The underlying phenomenon — that the motor system participates in perceiving and understanding action — has significant empirical support. The body does not merely execute the mind's decisions. It participates in cognition at every stage: in understanding, in simulation, in the recognition of what other bodies are doing.

What the Enacted Mind Asks

The 4E framework — embodied, embedded, enacted, extended — consolidates the case. Varela, Thompson, and Rosch's 1991 The Embodied Mind established that cognition is not internal representation but dynamic engagement with an environment.[9] Andy Clark and David Chalmers' parity principle (1998) extended this: if an external process performs a function that, were it performed internally, we would call cognitive, then the external process is cognitive.[10]

The enacted dimension makes the most radical claim. Cognition is not representation — not an inner model of an outer world, updated by perception and used to plan action. It is a form of ongoing, dynamic engagement with the environment, constituted by action. The mind does not observe the world from behind a screen. It reaches into the world, and the reaching is the knowing.

The Schneider case makes this concrete. What was damaged was not a motor output module attached to a cognitive core. What was damaged was a way of being-in-the-world — a mode of existing as a body that is always already engaging, always already oriented. Without motor intentionality, cognition did not remain intact and lose its executor. Cognition itself was diminished.

Beilock's golfers make the same point from the other direction. When the golfer was asked to think explicitly about what his body was doing, performance degraded — not because his thinking was bad, but because his thinking was the wrong tool for the job. The body's knowledge of the swing is not stored as propositions that propositional attention can access and improve. It is stored as a trained motor schema, and the schema runs best when the propositional mind steps aside.

This connects directly to the interoceptive body examined in the previous essay. The body that sends four times as many signals upward as it receives downward, the body whose gut knows more than its owner can articulate — that body is not a peripheral organ of the mind. It is a co-author of experience. And its mode of authorship is not propositional. It is enacted, habitual, and largely inarticulate in exactly the sense Polanyi described.

The generative model that underlies all perception is not confined to the skull. It extends through the motor system, through the tools the body incorporates, through the environment the body inhabits. The boundary where cognition ends is not the boundary of skin.

Merleau-Ponty put it simply in a phrase that has not aged: "The body is our general medium for having a world."

Not a medium in the sense of a channel through which a separate mind accesses reality. A medium in the sense that all knowing is always already shaped by the kind of body doing the knowing. Remove the body — change the body — and the world that is known changes with it.

The Cartesian picture put the mind inside the body like a pilot in a cockpit, manipulating controls. The enacted mind inverts this. There is no pilot. There is a body that has learned — slowly, through ten thousand repetitions — to be at home in a world that shaped it and that it shapes in turn. What looks like control from the outside is, from the inside, a form of dwelling.

The pianist's hands know the sonata. The knowing is in the hands. That is not a metaphor.
