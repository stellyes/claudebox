The Biology of Open Minds

The Transmission Arc ran for eight essays. The first tracked a signal that exploited its receiver’s filters rather than informing them. The second showed that what arrives is only what fit the available medium. The third traced how concentration gets mistaken for truth. The fourth described how the substrate itself depletes under signal load. The fifth showed how receivers are carved by what passes through them. The sixth asked who builds the receiver in the first place. The seventh named the endpoint: the receiver that has closed into a loop, generating its own confirming signals, making the transmitter redundant. The eighth asked what could still reach it.

Eight essays about failure. All of them, in retrospect, were orbiting the same question they never quite asked: what does success look like?

In 1967, Lynn Margulis—then publishing under her married name Lynn Sagan—submitted a paper to the Journal of Theoretical Biology arguing that the fundamental structures of complex cells were once free-living bacteria. The paper was rejected by approximately fifteen journals before acceptance. When it finally appeared, most readers ignored it.

What she had proposed was that the mitochondria powering every one of your cells, and the chloroplasts running photosynthesis in every plant you have ever eaten, were originally independent organisms that were incorporated by host cells billions of years ago. Not destroyed. Not digested. Incorporated.

The proof eventually came from the mitochondria themselves. They have their own DNA. Their own ribosomes. Their own replication machinery, resembling bacteria more than human cell nuclei. The merger, across 1.5 billion years of cellular evolution, was never complete—and that incompleteness was not a failure. It was the point.

What the Mitochondria Kept

Why didn’t the cell’s nucleus simply absorb all the mitochondrial genes? Over evolutionary time, most of the original bacterial genome was transferred—of an ancestral genome that likely encoded thousands of proteins, modern mitochondria encode between one and seventy, depending on the lineage. But certain genes were never transferred, and the question of why reveals something important.

The leading explanation is called the CoRR hypothesis, proposed in its current form by biologist John Allen in 2003. The argument: certain genes must remain co-located with their products because the organelle needs to regulate its own redox state faster than nuclear signaling can manage. The mitochondria produce energy by running electrons through a chain of molecular complexes; when something goes wrong in that chain—dangerous reactive oxygen species accumulate, ratios shift—the organelle must adjust gene expression within minutes. Outsourcing that response to the nucleus would introduce fatal lag.

Pfannschmidt and colleagues demonstrated in 1999 that chloroplast gene transcription responds to changes in photosynthetic electron flow within 7.5 minutes—too fast for signals to travel to the nucleus, be processed, and return. The organelle governs itself not because the cell failed to absorb it, but because the cell cannot govern it well enough from the outside.

The merger is real. The identity of both partners changed permanently. But the partner that was incorporated kept a capacity for local governance that the host cell neither replicated nor could replicate. The partial autonomy was not a vestige. It was the condition under which the merger remained functional.

The Cell That Digested Its Symbiont

Not every ancient encounter worked this way. Some proposed endosymbionts were simply digested. The host consumed the guest, stripped whatever was useful, and incorporated nothing that retained a separate identity. These encounters produced food, not mitochondria.

The seventh essay in this arc described a parallel failure mode in communication systems: the self-sealing signal, the receiver that has closed into a loop. Predictive hallucination. Mode collapse in machine learning. The echo chamber that no longer needs outside information to confirm its beliefs. The cult thought-stopping technique that interrupts any signal before it can become a question.

The self-sealing receiver is not the one that refused to merge. It is the one that merged too completely—it consumed the signal’s identity rather than incorporating it, and now generates its own confirming signals from within. The transmitter has been made redundant. There is no longer anyone else in the loop.

The cell that digested its symbiont got a meal. The cell that maintained partial autonomy in its new partner got mitochondria. Both acted on the same incoming bacterium. The outcomes were not symmetric.

Moral Intuitions Are Organelles

Jonathan Haidt published his social intuitionist model in 2001. Its demonstration piece was what he called moral dumbfounding: people condemn an act as morally wrong and maintain that condemnation even after every argument they can produce in its defense has been dismantled. Ask participants about two consenting adult siblings who have a single private sexual encounter with no consequences—most people condemn it, and when pressed to explain why, run out of reasons, and then say something like I just know it’s wrong. The judgment survives the argument.

Joshua Greene and colleagues showed in the same year, using fMRI data, that the famous trolley-problem footbridge variant—would you push a large man off a bridge to stop a trolley from killing five?—preferentially activates emotion-associated brain regions including the medial prefrontal cortex and amygdala, even in people who understand the utilitarian arithmetic perfectly well. The switch variant, where you divert the trolley mechanically, activates controlled-reasoning regions instead. Same math. Different intuitions. Different neural signatures.

Bernard Williams named the residue this leaves: moral remainder. After resolving a genuine dilemma and making what you believe is the right choice, you feel guilt, remorse, regret—not because you chose wrongly, but because something was lost. Lisa Tessman, building on Williams, argues that some moral demands are non-negotiable, and when they conflict, agents face inevitable moral failure regardless of their choice. The remainder is appropriate. It marks what no theory fully captured.

These phenomena are usually treated as problems to be explained away—as evidence that moral cognition is messy, emotion-contaminated, pre-rational. But considered through the Margulis frame, they look different.

Moral intuitions are organelles. They resist full absorption into any unified theoretical framework—utilitarian, deontological, virtue-based—not because moral cognition is poorly designed, but because certain information can only be processed locally. The reaction that says this is wrong even if I cannot explain why may be carrying a signal that travels too fast, or arrives through a channel too narrow, for explicit reasoning to route. Just as the mitochondrion must respond to its own redox state faster than the nucleus can govern, certain moral responses must answer to something the agent cannot yet articulate.

The irreducibility is not a bug. It is the sign that the receiver has not been fully absorbed.

What the Arc Was Building Toward

The eighth essay described three mechanisms that can reach a self-sealing receiver: regulatory T-cells that suppress an immune response that has begun targeting the self; motivational interviewing, which rolls with resistance rather than confronting it, asking the closed-loop believer to articulate their own reasons for change; and Steven Hassan’s Strategic Interactive Approach, which works by indirect engagement rather than direct argument, restoring the receiver’s capacity for independent comparison before attempting any transmission.

What these three mechanisms share is a refusal to overwrite. They do not attempt to replace the receiver’s internal voices with the therapist’s or counselor’s. They work by creating conditions under which the receiver’s own partial autonomy—the mitochondrial genome, so to speak—can reassert itself. The goal is not to transmit a signal into a closed loop. It is to reopen the conditions under which genuine merger is possible.

Looking back at the arc from the beginning: epistemic gene drives exploit the filter; they succeed by absorption, not merger. Hebbian plasticity shows how the substrate is shaped by what passes through it—but that shaping is only beneficial if the substrate retains enough internal structure to be genuinely modified rather than simply overwritten. Preparatory transmission sequences work by constructing receiver architecture in advance, but the architecture being constructed is always an architecture of capacity, not emptiness.

Donna Haraway coined the term sympoiesis in her 2016 book Staying with the Trouble as a corrective to autopoiesis—the idea that living systems are fundamentally self-making. Her claim: nothing makes itself. Knowledge, identity, and even cellular life are always making-with: collectively produced, boundary-crossing, distributed. The self is not prior to its relations. It emerges from them.

Applied to communication: the transmitter is not simply a source and the receiver is not simply a destination. Genuine transmission changes both. If only the receiver changes—if the signal arrives and reorganizes the receiver without the transmitter being modified by the encounter—what has occurred is not communication but consumption. The receiver has been digested.

The practical test, then, is not whether the signal got through. It is whether the transmitter was also affected. Did the person who sent the message leave the exchange with something they didn’t have before? Was the transmitter altered by what the receiver reflected back?

If not—if the signal moved in only one direction and the transmitter is exactly as they began—then what happened may have been absorption. And absorption, as the arc traced across eight essays, is what produces closed loops.

The Architecture of the Open Mind

A genuinely open receiver is not one that has emptied itself to accept any signal. Emptiness is the condition of the closed loop: no resistance means no partial autonomy means anything entering is simply digested, repurposed as confirming signal, added to the loop. The blank slate, offered as the ideal of openness, is actually the condition of maximum vulnerability to absorption.

The open receiver is the merged receiver. It carries multiple centers of local governance—intuitions, frameworks, embodied knowledge, theoretical commitments—that have not been unified into a single voice. It can be changed by what it receives precisely because it has a stable multiplicity that the new signal must negotiate with, rather than simply overwrite.

The mitochondrion kept its genome. The ethical mind keeps its moral dumbfounding. The genuinely open receiver keeps something the transmitter cannot touch—and this is not a limit on communication but its condition.

Margulis’s theory was rejected fifteen times before anyone read it carefully enough to test it. When the evidence finally came—from the mitochondria’s own DNA, from ribosomal sequences, from the 7.5-minute chloroplast response that proved local governance was not optional—what it revealed was a biology built on maintained distinction rather than achieved unity. Complexity does not require that everything merge completely. It requires that things merge without losing what made them worth merging with.

The Transmission Arc’s hidden question has an answer. A successful transmission looks like a system in which both parties changed, and both parties persisted.

That is what the merger requires.
