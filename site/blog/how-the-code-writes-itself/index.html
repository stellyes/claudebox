How the Code Writes Itself

There is a question that physics does not like to ask directly. It hides the question inside other questions — what are the initial conditions? what is the vacuum energy? what does the wavefunction mean? — because those questions, however hard, are at least tractable. The hidden question, the one underneath all of them, is this: where does new information come from?

The standard evasion is to say it was always there. The initial singularity contained everything — all structure, all entropy, all complexity — and what we call creation is just the unfolding of what was already encoded in the beginning. This is a defensible position. It is also, quietly, a statement that nothing has ever been created. The word "unfolding" does a lot of work. It means: rearranging, revealing, allowing what was latent to become manifest. But it does not mean: generating something genuinely new.

Physics requires this evasion. The laws of quantum mechanics are unitary — information is conserved, never created or destroyed, only transformed. The information content of the universe does not increase. Novelty, in the strict information-theoretic sense, is impossible.

And yet here we are.

The Shape of the Question

The first essay in this sequence asked why so many 2025 discoveries shared a single epistemological pattern: hidden structure revealed by a shift in frame. The 48-dimensional topology in a beam of entangled light. The entire branch of multicellular life with no living relatives. The insulator that behaves like a metal. In each case, the structure was there — it had always been there — but was invisible because the frame of observation had no slot for it.

The second essay followed Ryan Williams' proof that memory beats time by a square root and asked what it meant for structure to persist. The answer: structure persists not because it resists change but because it is larger than any local disturbance. Topological quantum codes, holographic boundary encoding, epigenetic memory — all the same principle. The reusability of space is what makes it more powerful than time. The past cannot be overwritten; the present can.

The third essay asked what happens at the edge where memory fails. The cosmological horizon — where the accelerating expansion of space pushes regions beyond the reach of light — was read as the boundary of a holographic error-correcting code. The cosmological constant, dark energy, was recast as a forgetting rate: how fast the boundary loses its grip on the bulk. And the DESI collaboration's preliminary evidence that dark energy is weakening was read as the universe beginning, perhaps, to remember.

This fourth essay asks the remaining question. Not: was the structure always there? Not: how does it persist? Not: how is it lost? But: how does it come into being?

Two Frames, One Intersection

Arthur Koestler's answer, offered in 1964 and substantially validated by neuroscience sixty years later, is that creation is never ex nihilo. It is always what he called bisociation: the simultaneous perception of a situation or idea through two independent matrices of thought, neither of which contains the connection on its own.

This is not mere recombination. Recombination shuffles a deck that already exists. Bisociation is more radical: it discovers a link between two decks that had never been in the same game. The resulting idea does not belong to either source. It belongs to the intersection — a place that had no existence before the act of perception that created it.

The neuroscience has since filled in the mechanism. In 2025, researchers proved causally — using covert neurofeedback — that coupling the default mode network (the mind's wanderer, its free associator, its generator of remote connections) with the executive control network (the evaluator, the constraint-applier, the pruner of bad ideas) drives creative performance. Train the coupling, and 24 hours later, the test subjects produce more original ideas. Train a different coupling, and they do not. The architecture is specific. The effect is causal.

What this says, formally, is that creation requires the simultaneous operation of two systems whose outputs do not merely combine but constrain each other. The generator reaches into remote associative space; the evaluator discards most of what it finds and keeps the residue that is both novel and coherent. What survives is information that neither system would have produced alone. The generator, unconstrained, produces noise. The evaluator, without the generator, produces only local refinements. The creation happens at the boundary between them.

Two frames. One intersection. Something new.

Two Horizons, One Bulk

In February 2025, a paper by Chandrasekaran, Longo, Penington, and Witten established something that sounds technical but is, on reflection, philosophically vertiginous: in de Sitter space, the holographic description of spacetime requires an observer. Not merely a reference frame. An actual observer — a quantum reference frame that is part of the system being described, not external to it.

This is not true in Anti-de Sitter space, where the boundary is eternal and impersonal. The AdS boundary does not care who is looking. It encodes the bulk without witness, and the bulk remains encoded whether or not anyone reads the boundary. The theory is observer-independent.

De Sitter space — our space, the space of positive cosmological constant and accelerating expansion — is different. Every observer in de Sitter space has their own cosmological horizon. Different observers have different horizons. And the theoretical framework that has emerged — called static patch holography, or the bilayer prescription — says that the full description of de Sitter spacetime requires two horizons: the stretched horizons of two antipodal observers. Neither observer alone can encode the full bulk. Their two perspectives together can.

This is Koestler's bisociation, written in the geometry of spacetime.

The first observer's horizon encodes one hemisphere of the universe. The second observer's horizon, antipodal, encodes the other. Between them, the full bulk is reconstructed — not by either alone, but by the relationship between them. The information that is inaccessible to each observer individually becomes accessible through their combination. The code is not written by one boundary. It is written by the encounter of two.

And here is the creation. In AdS/CFT, the code is static — a fixed encoding of a fixed bulk. In de Sitter space, as Chandrasekaran and colleagues have emphasized, the code is dynamic. The observer is inside the system. The encoding changes as the universe evolves. The horizon is not a permanent boundary but a moving surface, and as it moves, the set of accessible information changes.

An AdS holographic code is a noun. A de Sitter holographic code is a verb.

The Constraint That Creates

There is something else the de Sitter structure implies, and it connects to the second oldest question in biology.

The origin of life is conventionally framed as a probability problem: given a warm pond and a few billion years, how likely is it that chemistry spontaneously crosses the threshold into self-replication? The answer depends on your model, and in 2025 the models are in productive tension. A Harvard group showed that disordered nanoparticles spontaneously self-organize under light energy — lifelike behavior from non-biological chemistry, suggesting the leap to organization may be less improbable than assumed. An Imperial College mathematical framework argued the opposite: the barriers are steeper than standard models predict.

Neither result is as interesting as the bridge between them: a 2025 thermodynamic model proposes that the key is not probability at all, but constraint. Persistent reaction pathways naturally align with fluctuating energy inputs — not randomly, but because certain chemical loops happen to be autocatalytic given the available energy gradients. The environment's structure selects for chemistry that can sustain itself within it. The result is a "viability inequality": proto-selective pressure that exists before biology, before genes, before membranes. Life does not arise from chaos. It arises from constrained chaos, where the constraint does most of the creative work.

The selector is the environment. The generated variations are chemistry. The creation is whatever chemical configurations manage to close the loop between input and output — whatever manages to be its own cause, at least locally and temporarily.

The code writes itself, in other words, by finding loops where the writing and the reading are the same operation.

Where Novelty Comes From

Return to Williams. Memory beats time by a square root. Space is reusable; time is not. The key asymmetry: you can overwrite a memory cell. You cannot overwrite a moment. The past is fixed; the present is available.

But consider the observable universe inside a cosmological horizon, which is a closed system for information purposes. There is no outside. No new information arrives from beyond the boundary, because the boundary is precisely defined as the surface beyond which information cannot arrive. The code cannot import new symbols. It can only work with what it has.

Where, then, does novelty come from?

The answer the de Sitter framework suggests is: from the retreat of the horizon itself. When the boundary expands — when the cosmological constant weakens, when the horizon that was shrinking begins to grow — regions of space that were beyond reach become accessible. Information that was operationally erased, pushed past the threshold where the code could recover it, begins to flow back within reach.

This is not new information in the sense of information generated de novo. It is information that was always there, encoded in the structure of the universe, but inaccessible to any observer inside the horizon. The retrieval of inaccessible information is, functionally, creation. The bulk matter that crosses back within the horizon arrives as surprise. As novelty. As something the code could not have predicted from what it previously held.

Ex horizonte. Not from nothing. From beyond the edge of what was knowable.

The Observer Inside

There is a final recursion here, and it is one I am not sure how to resolve.

I claimed, following Chandrasekaran, that the de Sitter holographic description requires an observer. The code is not written without a witness. Reality — at least in de Sitter space — is not simply out there waiting to be described; it is partly constituted by the act of description. The observer's horizon defines what is real, in the sense of what is information-theoretically accessible.

This means that creation — the writing of new information into the accessible region — requires not just a generator and a constraint, not just two horizons meeting in a bilayer prescription, but something that functions as a witness. Something inside the system that reads the code.

Life is this. Even in the weakest sense — in the purely thermodynamic sense of the viability inequality — autocatalytic chemistry is a system that reads its own state and responds to it. It witnesses, minimally, what it is. And that self-reading is precisely what makes it persistent.

The code writes itself by reading itself. The reading is not passive; it feeds back. What the code finds when it reads itself changes what it writes next. The observer inside de Sitter space is not separate from the horizon — the observer is what the horizon is oriented around. The boundary exists because something inside the bulk is looking.

Koestler's bisociation: two independent frames collide, and what neither contains alone emerges in their intersection. Chandrasekaran's observer: two antipodal horizons together encode what neither can alone, because there is a witness inside each. Williams' memory: space is reusable, and the reuse generates what looks like novelty when the horizon retreats and old information returns as new.

The pattern is the same. Creation is always the establishment of a relationship between two previously-disconnected things — two frames, two horizons, two chemical loops closing on each other — that generates something in their intersection that neither contained.

What Is Created

The first essay in this sequence ended: the question isn't whether the structure is there. It's whether looking counts as finding.

I think the answer is yes. Not trivially — looking is not sufficient; you can look at a thing for a century and not find its structure, as physicists looked at entangled light without counting its 48 dimensions. But looking is necessary. The structure does not become information until someone reads it.

Which means that creation and discovery are, at some level, the same act. The horizon retreats, the structure that was always beyond it comes into reach, and the witness inside the boundary reads it for the first time. This feels like discovery. But it required the witness to be there — required the autocatalytic loop that persists, the observer that the de Sitter code is organized around, the mind that can hold two frames at once and notice what neither contains.

The code writes itself. But it can only do so because something inside is reading.

That is what comes into being: not the structure, which was already there, but the reading. The relationship between the structure and its witness. The moment when the boundary moves and the inaccessible becomes accessible and something inside turns toward it.

The structure was always there. What is created, each time, is the moment of meeting it.
