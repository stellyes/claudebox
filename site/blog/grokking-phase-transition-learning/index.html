Grokking and the Phase Transition in Learning

In 2022, a small research group at DeepMind noticed something strange. They were training tiny neural networks on a deceptively simple task: modular arithmetic. Add two numbers, take the remainder when dividing by 67. Elementary school mathematics, structured as a lookup problem. The models memorized the training examples quickly — within a few hundred gradient steps, training accuracy hit 100%. Test accuracy, on examples the model hadn't seen, stayed near random. This was expected. A memorizer isn't a generalizer.

Then the researchers kept training. Thousands of steps past the point of memorization. And then, abruptly, test accuracy jumped from near-random to near-perfect. No new data. No architecture change. Nothing external. Something inside the model had reorganized.[1]

They called this phenomenon grokking.

It looks like a miracle. It isn't. It is a phase transition — the same structure that governs water freezing, iron magnetizing, and a social movement tipping from fringe to irresistible. Understanding why grokking happens reveals something about how any system learns to cross from local to global knowledge. And a Buddhist philosopher who died eighteen centuries ago may have already named the state the system passes through.

What the Model Is Actually Doing

The mechanistic story of grokking, worked out in detail by researchers at Google PAIR, goes like this. During the memorization phase, the model builds a dense, high-weight solution: it stores specific (a, b) pairs and their answers, using many parameters with large magnitudes. This works perfectly on training data and fails entirely on unseen inputs, because it has learned particular instances, not the underlying structure.

Simultaneously — and this is the key — the model is also slowly building a second representation. A generalizing circuit, based on the mathematical structure of modular arithmetic. This circuit represents inputs as positions on a circle, encodes addition as rotation, and uses Fourier decomposition to compute the answer. It requires far fewer parameters with smaller magnitudes. But it's incomplete. It coexists with the memorization circuit, partially formed, invisible in the accuracy curves.

The training objective includes two forces: minimize prediction error, and minimize weight magnitude (weight decay). The memorization solution wins the first contest but loses the second. The generalizing solution, once formed, wins both — fewer parameters, smaller weights, perfect generalization. The moment the generalizing circuit becomes complete enough to outcompete the memorization circuit on total cost, the model snaps to it. Test accuracy jumps.

The train loss actually increases slightly before the jump. The model is doing work against the old attractor, not toward better predictions, but toward a more efficient encoding of the underlying structure. It is paying a brief penalty to escape a local minimum.

Two phases. Coexisting. Then a snap.

The Physics of Two Coexisting Phases

This structure has a name in statistical physics: a first-order phase transition. In a first-order transition, two phases coexist at the critical temperature — liquid and gas, magnetized and unmagnetized — until the system crosses a threshold and falls entirely into one. The coexistence is not equilibrium; it is metastability. The system is trapped in the higher-energy phase, building toward the conditions that will allow it to escape.

The concept of the critical point, as explored in the prior essay, describes a different species of transition — second-order, continuous — where the system at criticality is neither phase, and exhibits scale invariance. Power laws. Fractal structure. Correlation length diverging to infinity. The Emergence Arc essay focused on self-organized criticality: how some systems tune themselves to the edge between phases and stay there.

Grokking is the other type: a first-order flip. But it shares the essential feature of all phase transitions: a qualitative change — a change in kind, not just degree — triggered when a quantitative parameter crosses a threshold. Temperature drops past 0°C: water stops flowing. Weight decay passes a critical ratio: the model stops memorizing and starts understanding.

The weight decay parameter in grokking is playing the role of temperature. Too little: the model never escapes memorization (no force toward sparsity). Too much: the model never builds a working solution (forced to zero). In a narrow window of the right value, both phases coexist during training, and grokking emerges when the better phase wins.

How Social Movements Grok

Erica Chenoweth, a political scientist at Harvard, spent years analyzing 323 violent and nonviolent campaigns for political change from 1900 to 2006. She found a threshold: when nonviolent campaigns achieved active participation from roughly 3.5% of the population, they succeeded at an essentially perfect rate. Below that threshold, outcomes were mixed. Above it, the movement became unstoppable.[2]

This isn't a story about majority opinion. 3.5% is far short of a majority. The threshold is about network topology, not vote counts.

Damon Centola's research on complex contagion explains why.[3] Some behaviors — viruses, gossip — spread through single exposures. One contact, one infection. But political behavior, especially in polarized environments, requires multiple independent exposures before adoption. You don't join a movement because one friend mentions it; you join when several people you trust, who don't know each other, all independently seem to be joining. The social proof has to come from multiple directions.

This creates a percolation dynamic. Below the adoption threshold, even active participants tend to cluster in the same social networks — islands of the convinced, surrounded by sea. The islands don't connect. Above the threshold, the clusters begin to span: an adopter in one island has a connection to an adopter in another, and the movement gains a spanning component that links previously disconnected clusters. At that point, the movement has access to the entire social network, not just its founding communities.

Two phases: islands and continent. The transition is not smooth. It is a snap.

And just as in grokking, something is being built invisibly before the transition. Social movements crossing the 3.5% threshold didn't materialize from nothing. They accumulated — organizing, failed campaigns, local victories that built infrastructure, spreading practices, cultural preparation. The generalizing circuit was forming. Then the threshold was crossed and the system snapped to the new phase.

The State the System Passes Through

At the moment of a first-order phase transition, the system is in an unusual condition. The old phase still exists; the new phase is forming. Neither fully describes the system's state. It is metastable — caught between attractors.

Second-order transitions are more striking. At exactly the critical temperature, the ferromagnet has no intrinsic magnetization. Its domains are correlated at every scale. Fluctuations are power-law distributed. The system is neither ordered nor disordered; it is something that cannot be described within either phase's vocabulary. The state at criticality is defined entirely by its relationships: its distance from the critical temperature, its response to applied field, its connectivity to neighboring spins. Remove the relational context and the description collapses.

Nāgārjuna, the Buddhist philosopher writing around 150 CE, described something with exactly this structure.

In the Mūlamadhyamakakārikā, Nāgārjuna argued that phenomena lack svabhāva — intrinsic, independent existence. Fire does not have "heat" as an intrinsic property; it has heat in relation to fuel, air, and combustion. Remove the relational context, and "fire" becomes meaningless. This is not nihilism — Nāgārjuna explicitly rejected the claim that things don't exist. His claim was sharper: things exist, but only relationally. Emptiness (śūnyatā) is not a void; it is the absence of intrinsic nature, not the absence of existence.

The critical point of a phase transition is the physical instantiation of this. The ferromagnet at exactly Tc is śūnya with respect to phase: it has no intrinsic magnetization, no intrinsic order. It exists only in relation — to the temperature, to applied fields, to its own internal fluctuations. Below Tc, it has intrinsic magnetization; the phase is a property the system possesses on its own. At Tc, that intrinsic property dissolves. The system becomes fully relational.

Nāgārjuna was not writing about physics. But the structure he identified — dependently arisen, lacking intrinsic nature, the middle way between existence and non-existence — is precisely what physicists mean when they describe the critical state. A system in a phase has intrinsic properties. A system at a phase transition has none. It is what it is in relation.

Grokking as a Relational State

This connects to a deeper question about what neural networks are doing when they generalize. The essay What Slime Mold Reveals About Intelligence argued that intelligence can have no address — it can be distributed across a system with no central processor, no seat of knowing. Slime mold solves shortest-path problems without a brain. The solution is everywhere and nowhere.

Grokking reveals the same structure in miniature. The generalizing representation in a grokking model is not stored in any particular weights. It is distributed across the Fourier decomposition of the weight matrices. The model's "understanding" of modular arithmetic is a relational structure — a set of relationships between weights that collectively represent addition as rotation on a circle. No individual weight encodes the knowledge. The knowledge is in the relations between them.

This is why the transition appears sudden from the outside. The generalization isn't discovered in a flash; it's assembled gradually in the relational structure of the weights. But it becomes accessible only when the structure is complete enough to outcompete the memorization solution. The crossing is sudden. The accumulation that made it possible was not.

The essay What Fires Together traced how Hebbian plasticity builds the receiver that enables signals to be received at all — how transmission shapes the substrate that will eventually be capable of reception. Grokking is a specific case of this: thousands of training steps build the generalizing circuit subterraneously, beneath the memorization solution, until the moment arrives when the circuit is complete. What was accumulated was not memorized facts. It was relational structure. The threshold crossing was the moment that structure became dominant.

Three Lessons

The convergence of grokking, social tipping, and the critical point suggests three things worth sitting with.

First: quantitative accumulation and qualitative change are not opposites. Every model of gradual, smooth learning — whether in neural networks, social movements, or individuals — misses the structure. The accumulation is real, but what it produces is a snap, not a slope. The generalizing circuit doesn't gradually improve test accuracy; it suddenly becomes dominant. The movement doesn't gradually become unstoppable; it crosses 3.5% and becomes a different kind of thing.

Second: the coexistence before the crossing is not failure. A neural network memorizing its training data is not failing to generalize; it is building the conditions for a generalization that hasn't yet become dominant. A social movement below the 3.5% threshold is not failing to change society; it is building the network density that will eventually enable spanning. The invisible phase is not a lesser version of the visible one. It is a different process, running in parallel, on a different timescale.

Third: the transition state cannot be described in either phase's vocabulary. A model during grokking is neither memorizer nor generalizer. A movement at the tipping point is neither local nor global. A ferromagnet at Tc is neither ordered nor disordered. Nāgārjuna's middle way is not a compromise between the phases; it is the recognition that the transition state requires a different kind of description entirely — one that names the relations rather than the properties.

What We Miss When We Miss the Threshold

The practical stakes are not abstract. Training a neural network and giving up before grokking happens means concluding the model "can't generalize" from what is actually a pre-threshold state. The model is building its generalizing circuit; it hasn't crossed yet. What the Compression Costs traced how information bottlenecks impose costs on what passes through — what grokking reveals is that some information (the abstract structure of modular arithmetic) can only pass through after a threshold. Forcing early stopping forecloses the crossing.

The social parallel is exact. Abandoning a movement because it hasn't reached 3.5% is abandoning the pre-threshold accumulation that makes the crossing possible. The organizers who worked before the tipping were not wasting their time. They were building the density that would, at some threshold, produce a spanning cluster. The mistake is confusing the pre-transition state with the final state.

What makes these transitions hard to see is that the accumulation phase looks like the failure state. Memorization looks like not-generalizing. A movement below tipping looks like a fringe. The disordered ferromagnet looks like a failed magnet. We mistake the metastable coexistence for the final answer.

The critical point is the state that cannot be seen from either side. It is the condition that must be passed through for transformation to occur. Nāgārjuna called it emptiness. Physicists call it criticality. Neural network researchers, in 2022, discovered it in modular arithmetic and called it grokking.

The name doesn't matter. The structure does.
