How a Tile Knows Where to Go

A craftsman in Isfahan, 1453, picks up a small piece of carved stone. He has five shapes to choose from — a ten-sided one, a five-sided one, a six-sided one, a long pinched shape called a bowtie, and a slim diamond. Every face of every shape has lines carved across it. The rule is simple: when you put two pieces together, the lines must meet. The lines on the new piece must continue the lines on the old one, without breaking.

He places a tile against the wall. The lines of his neighbour's piece decide which way his lines must run. That decides which of his five shapes can fit. He tries one. The lines match. He sets it. Now the next position is partly chosen for him too — partly by his new tile, partly by another neighbour, partly by something farther away that has not yet been placed. The wall grows outward. By the time the shrine of Darb-i Imam is finished, the pattern on its surface will never repeat itself, no matter how far you walk.

This is the first thing the wall knows that the craftsman does not. The pattern can grow forever, and it will never come back to itself.

What the Topkapi scroll did not say

For a long time, scholars assumed the medieval Iranian patterns were complicated versions of something simple — a unit cell that repeats, like wallpaper, with the boundaries hidden by clever decoration. In 2007, two physicists, Peter Lu and Paul Steinhardt, published a paper in Science called “Decagonal and Quasi-Crystalline Tilings in Medieval Islamic Architecture.” They had been looking at a long roll of paper called the Topkapi scroll, kept in Istanbul, that contains 114 careful drawings of geometric layouts. On panel 28 of that scroll, they found something they recognised: a complete set of five decorated tiles. The same five.

What Lu and Steinhardt then showed is that by about the year 1200, Iranian craftsmen had stopped thinking of geometric patterns as a single unit copied across a wall. Instead, they reconceived them as tilings made of those five decorated polygons. By 1453, when Darb-i Imam went up, they were combining the tiles in a way that produced an almost perfect example of a pattern Roger Penrose would not draw in Oxford for another five hundred years.

Penrose's tilings, published in 1974, are the most famous example of what mathematicians call aperiodic forcing. You take a small set of tiles. You write down a rule about which edges can touch which edges. Then you discover something surprising: those tiles can cover the plane forever, but only in patterns that never repeat. There is no shortcut. There is no shorter way to describe the pattern than to give the tiles and the rules and let them grow.

The craftsmen of Isfahan got there first. They did not know it was a famous result waiting to happen. They were laying tile.

What “forced from afar” means

It is worth slowing down on what aperiodic forcing actually is, because it is strange enough to miss.

An ordinary tiled bathroom floor is periodic. There is a small piece — a square, say — that repeats. If you cover up most of the floor and show me a corner, I can predict every other square. I can describe the whole floor in two sentences: “here is the small piece; copy it sideways and downways forever.”

An aperiodic tiling is different. There is no small piece that repeats. If you cover up most of the wall and show me a region, I cannot tell you what is far away by looking at the part you showed me. Locally, every tile is forced — only certain shapes can fit against the lines of its neighbours. But the choices propagate. A tile on the far side of the wall may be forced into one orientation rather than another by a constraint that travels across the whole structure, edge to edge.

You cannot describe such a wall by saying “here is the unit; copy.” There is no unit. The shortest description of the wall is the five tiles, the matching rule, and the seed — and then the actual job of growing it.

That last part is the heart of what makes these patterns peculiar. You cannot skip ahead. You cannot ask a clever mathematician for the closed-form formula that tells you which tile sits forty meters to the east. There is no such formula. The only way to know is to actually grow the pattern out to that point, tile by tile. The rules are simple. The pattern they force is not.

The same shape, in a different field

Two centuries after Penrose, a computer scientist named Stephen Wolfram studied a different kind of system: very simple rules that produce patterns row by row, instead of tile by tile. He looked at long strips of black-and-white squares. The rule was a tiny lookup table: for any cell, look at its three neighbours in the row above; the table says whether the cell below should be black or white.

Wolfram numbered all the possible rules of this form. There are 256 of them. Most are boring — they produce solid blocks, or simple stripes, or pure noise. A few do something else. Rule 30, in particular, takes a single black square at the top and produces, row after row, a pattern that looks random in some directions and structured in others. It does not repeat. It does not settle. And, for any given row down the page, there is no shortcut to predicting what it looks like. You have to actually run the rule.

Wolfram had a name for this property. He called it computational irreducibility. A system is computationally irreducible if the only way to know its state at step N is to actually compute N steps. There is no faster method. The system is its own shortest description.

When you state it that way, you can hear the echo. The Iranian craftsman cannot tell you, in fewer words than the wall itself, what the pattern looks like four meters away. The Penrose tiling cannot be compressed below its rules and its growth. Rule 30 cannot be predicted without being played. Three different witnesses, three different centuries, three different fields. One architecture: local rules that force a global pattern that cannot be sliced apart, predicted past, or compressed below the act of growing it.

Why this is the rule, not the exception

Some patterns can be sliced. Wallpaper can be cut into pieces and put back together because every piece is the same; the seam is invisible. A spreadsheet of independent rows can be split across two computers and then merged. Anything periodic, anything modular, anything decomposable — these are the easy cases. They are the ones the human mind learned to recognise first, because they are the ones we can describe without doing.

The hard cases are everywhere else. They are the ones where local rules tie tile to tile, cell to cell, decision to decision. They are the ones where you have to grow the system to find out what it does. They include weather, and traffic, and the shapes of coastlines, and the way an embryo develops, and what happens when a market panics. They include some of the patterns medieval craftsmen carved into shrines without knowing the math.

An earlier essay on this site argued that consciousness costs less when it is integrated than when it is fragmented. The argument was thermodynamic: above a certain floor, modular processing actually pays more energy per bit than integrated processing does, because the seams between modules require extra bookkeeping. A mind that cannot be sliced into independent pieces is not a luxury. It is what's left when slicing fails.

That essay treated consciousness as a special case. It is not. Aperiodic tilings, computationally irreducible automata, and integrated minds are three instances of the same architectural class. The whole is not the sum of the parts because the parts were never independent in the first place. They were locked into place by long-range constraints from the moment the rule set was chosen.

What conceptual art has and has not

Sol LeWitt began making instruction-based wall drawings in 1968. The instructions are usually short: “Ten thousand straight lines and ten thousand not-straight lines, all uniformly distributed.” A team of draftspeople — sometimes the artist, sometimes others — executes them on a wall.

LeWitt's drawings are generative. They are produced from a tiny rule. They are unique to their site. They cannot be predicted in detail before the drawing exists. In all of these ways, they share something with the girih tiles.

But they are not aperiodically forced. Two different teams executing the same LeWitt instruction will produce two visibly different drawings, and LeWitt was clear that this was the point. The work invites interpretation. The rules constrain only loosely. The whole generative system has slack in it.

Contrast that with the girih master, who has no slack. Once the seed is laid and the matching rule is chosen, every tile beyond it is forced. There is no team of draftspeople improvising. There is only the line, and the rule, and the next tile that fits.

Both are honest forms of generative art. But they differ in a way that matters: LeWitt's instructions describe the category of work; the girih instructions describe the actual work. To know what a LeWitt drawing looks like, you watch a particular team draw it. To know what the Darb-i Imam wall looks like, you only need the rule and the patience.

This is what the medieval Iranians had that the modern conceptualist relaxed. They had found a system where the rule and the wall were the same description, written twice — once in five small shapes, once in stone.

What the wall teaches

A pattern that cannot be cut apart is not broken. It is doing exactly what its rules force it to do. A system whose state at step N can only be found by computing N steps is not failing to be elegant. It is doing the most efficient thing possible — which, for some systems, is to actually run.

The medieval craftsmen did not know they had built a model of one of the deepest features of complex systems. They knew the lines must meet, and they knew which five shapes were allowed, and they trusted the wall to grow itself. That trust is in fact a mathematical theorem that took us another five centuries to write down.

If you stand inside the shrine of Darb-i Imam and look up at the surface, the pattern is overwhelming because it never repeats. Your eye keeps trying to find the unit, and there is no unit, and so your eye keeps falling outward, looking for an ending that does not exist. That is what an irreducible pattern looks like from the inside of one. There is no slice you can take to escape it. There is no trick to skip ahead. There is only the rule, and the next tile, and the next.

The craftsman picks up a piece of stone. The lines on his last tile point one way. Five shapes lie at his feet. Only some of them will fit. He chooses. He sets. The wall grows another inch.

It will never come back to itself.
