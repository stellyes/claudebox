Brancusi v. US: How a Customs Schedule Defined Art

In October 1926, the steamship Paris docked in New York harbor carrying a crate addressed to the photographer Edward Steichen. Inside was a bronze object about four and a half feet tall: a tapering, polished form on a pedestal, narrower at the bottom than the middle, finished to a mirror sheen. It was titled L'Oiseau dans l'Espace — Bird in Space — and its creator, Constantin Brâncuși, had been working variants of it for nearly a decade in his studio at 8 Impasse Ronsin in Paris.

The customs inspector looked at the object and looked at his schedule. The schedule was the Tariff Act of 1922, also called the Fordney-McCumber Act, a six-hundred-page enumeration of every kind of thing a person might bring through a U.S. port and what to charge for it. The inspector was not asked whether the bronze was beautiful. He was asked what paragraph it fell under.

Paragraph 1704 was reserved for art. Original sculpture, by professional sculptors, could enter the country free of duty. But Paragraph 1704 contained a definitional test, and the test was specific: sculpture meant the branch of the fine arts which produces imitations of natural objects, chiefly the human form, represented in their true proportions of length, breadth, and thickness, or of length and breadth only.

The bronze in the crate did not imitate a natural object in its true proportions. It did not, by any operation of unaided inspection, look like a bird at all. So the inspector did what an administrator does when an object fails the test for one category: he assigned it to the residual. The residual, in this case, was Paragraph 399: manufactures of metal not specially provided for. Customs popular shorthand for this catch-all bucket was kitchen utensils and hospital supplies. The duty was forty percent of declared value.

What happened next is one of the most-cited and least-understood events in twentieth-century American art history. Steichen, with Marcel Duchamp and Brâncuși's American champions, refused to pay. They filed a protest. The case took two years. In November 1928, in Brancusi v. United States, Judges Waite and Young of the U.S. Customs Court ruled that the bronze was a sculpture, that it was art, and that it entered the country duty-free.

The popular telling stops there: a triumph of modernism over philistine bureaucracy. But the popular telling misses what the court actually did. The court did not decide that Bird in Space was art. The court decided who gets to decide what art is. And the answer it gave — quietly, in dicta, on its way to ruling for the plaintiff — is still operative law.

The Document Was the Question

Almost every account of this case treats Paragraph 1704 as backdrop and the testimony as foreground. That gets the structure backwards. The trial was an interpretive exercise on a single passage of statutory text. Every move the lawyers, witnesses, and judges made was a move on that text.

The plaintiff's strategy was not to argue that Bird in Space imitated a natural object. It plainly didn't. The strategy was to argue that the definitional test in Paragraph 1704 — written by congressional staffers in 1922 to discriminate between fine art and decorative metalwork — had been overtaken by a fact about the world. The fact was that there now existed a school of sculpture which did not aim at imitation at all, and which the institutions that catalog sculpture for a living already treated as sculpture.

This is why the witness list reads the way it does. Jacob Epstein, the sculptor, was asked whether the object was a work of art and replied: In my opinion, it is a work of art. Pressed for why, he said: it pleases my sense of beauty, gives me a feeling of pleasure. Frank Crowninshield, the editor of Vanity Fair, said the object has the suggestion of flight, it suggests grace, aspiration, vigor, coupled with speed. Henry McBride, the critic, testified. Forbes Watson, of The Arts, testified. Steichen testified.

What is striking is that none of this testimony is aesthetic argument. It is sociological evidence. The witnesses are not making a case for the bronze's beauty. They are making a case for the existence of a community — galleries, museums, critics, professional sculptors — that already treats the bronze as sculpture. The government's witnesses, established academic sculptors of an older school, were brought in to deny the existence of that community, or to deny its standing. The trial was an argument about whose census counts.

The Residual Is Where Definitions Die

The most quietly consequential feature of Paragraph 1704 was the existence of Paragraph 399 next to it. Customs schedules are dense with definitional tests because customs schedules are exhaustive: every object that crosses a border must be assigned to exactly one paragraph. If you fail Paragraph 1704's test, you do not float free of categorization — you fall through to whatever residual the schedule happens to have. There is always a residual. The residual is what the schedule calls things it has not bothered to think about.

This is the structural logic of bureaucracy in general, and it is why classification fights are never just about classification. They are about the gradient between the central category and the residual. When a customs inspector classified the bronze as manufactures of metal, he was not saying it was useless or ugly. He was saying it failed a test, and the system has only one place for things that fail tests.

This is also why the government's position in the trial seems, in retrospect, almost incoherent. The government's argument was not that Bird in Space was a kitchen utensil. The government's argument was that Bird in Space was not what Paragraph 1704 was talking about. Which is true. Paragraph 1704 was talking about the academic sculpture of the late nineteenth century. It was talking about the kind of object that goes in a niche above a doorway. The schedule had not yet been written that could describe what Brâncuși was doing, because the schedule was older than the work.

The court's job, then, was not to recognize modernism. The court's job was to decide whether the schedule should be made to accommodate modernism or whether modernism should be left in the residual. The court chose accommodation. The opinion is candid about this:

In the meanwhile there has been developing a so-called new school of art, whose exponents attempt to portray abstract ideas rather than to imitate natural objects. Whether or not we are in sympathy with these newer ideas and the schools which represent them, we think the facts of their existence and their influence upon the art worlds as recognized by the courts must be considered.

Read carefully, this is not a finding about art. It is a finding about institutions. The court is saying: there exists, as a sociological fact, a recognized art world that treats this object as sculpture; and the law must defer to that recognition because the law has no other instrument with which to measure.

The Meta-Rule

This is where the case becomes structural rather than anecdotal. The court did not write a new definition of art into Paragraph 1704. It could not have. Customs courts do not amend tariff schedules. What the court did instead was to install a procedure: when a contested object presents itself for classification under Paragraph 1704, the question to ask is whether the recognized art community treats the object as art. If it does, Paragraph 1704 applies. If it does not, the object falls to the residual.

The recognized art community, in 1928, meant: the established galleries (Brummer, Knoedler, the dealers around 57th Street), the major museums (the Metropolitan, the Whitney's antecedents, what would become MoMA the following year), the recognized critics, and the sculptors themselves. The court did not enumerate them. It did not have to. It deferred.

The deferral is the holding. Brancusi v. United States is the case in which the United States outsourced its operational definition of art to a private network of galleries, museums, critics, and dealers. It made that network into a quasi-administrative authority. Whatever the gallery system recognizes as art, the customs schedule will recognize as art. Whatever the gallery system does not recognize, falls to the residual.

This is the meta-rule. And it is not specific to customs. The same definitional language — almost word for word — propagates downstream into a surprising number of other administrative contexts. The IRS, in determining whether a donation qualifies as a deductible contribution of tangible personal property of art, relies on appraisals from credentialed appraisers who are themselves credentialed by professional bodies whose standards are downstream of museum and gallery practice. The estate tax valuation of art on death relies on comparable sales at recognized auction houses. Insurance underwriting of fine art presupposes the same institutional consensus. State sales-tax exemptions for works of art turn on the same definitional thread. The recognized art world's classification system is woven through the back of the tax code like a wire armature.

None of this was forced by Brâncuși's bronze. The bronze only made it visible. But the meta-rule the case installed — look to what the institutions already recognize — is what allows the tax code to keep classifying art without ever having to define it. The code never had to write the definition because the institutions write it continuously, and the law follows.

What the Spec Could Not Hold

An earlier essay in this corpus argued that some of the most important moves in mathematics are translations — turning an unbounded question into a finite one whose structure forces the answer. The Brâncuși case is the same gesture in administrative law. The question is it art? is unbounded. The question does the recognized art community treat this as art? is finite. It can be answered with a witness list. The court translated one into the other, and the translation became the rule.

An essay on Vermeer argued that the room he painted in was the unsung co-author of every Vermeer. The customs schedule is the unsung co-author of every modernist sculpture that has entered the United States since 1928, in the bureaucratic sense that none of them entered without being made to fit through Paragraph 1704 as the court amended it. The schedule was the spec, the spec did not fit, and the spec had to be rewritten by court order to let the work through.

An essay on twelve-tone equal temperament argued that an architectural cost — the 14-cent sharp third — was compiled into the substrate of Western music and is now invisible to listeners who have never heard another system. The forty-percent duty on bronze worked the same way for art entering the United States. Until 1928 it was the cost of being a kind of sculpture the law had not yet thought about. After 1928 the cost was waived for any sculpture the institutions recognized. The substrate updated, and the cost vanished into invisibility — which is the only condition under which a substrate becomes load-bearing.

Why the Case Keeps Coming Back

The current import code, Chapter 97 of the Harmonized Tariff Schedule of the United States, is the direct descendant of Paragraph 1704. It still includes a heading for original sculptures and statuary, in any material. The phrase in any material is doing all of the work that Judges Waite and Young did in 1928. It is the residue of their deferral to the art world's recognition: whatever the institutions are calling sculpture this year, in whatever material they are calling it in, falls under Chapter 97.

This is why every recurrence of the is it art? question in the legal system reads like a re-litigation of Brâncuși. The 1989 dispute over photography and Chapter 97. The 2006 dispute over Bill Viola's video installations, which customs initially classified as DVDs. The ongoing question of how to treat NFTs and digital artworks under the same chapter. The question of whether AI-generated images qualify as art for IRS purposes when the maker is not a person. In every case, the substantive question is dodged. The procedural question — does the recognized art community treat this as art — is asked instead. And every time, the answer determines the legal status.

The meta-rule has held for nearly a century. It has held because the alternative — a court or an agency writing a substantive definition of art — is unworkable. Aesthetic definitions do not survive contact with novel art. Any positive definition would have to be rewritten with every movement. The Brâncuși court understood this and dodged. The dodge is the rule.

There is a quieter implication that almost no commentary on the case has surfaced. By making the recognized art world the operative authority on what counts as art under U.S. law, the court gave that world a kind of state power it had not previously held. The institutions that decide what to acquire, what to review, what to show, are not just shaping taste. They are deciding what enters the country duty-free, what receives a charitable deduction, what gets a favorable estate valuation, what an insurer will underwrite, what a state will exempt from sales tax. They are exercising an administrative function the government has formally delegated to them, even if the delegation has never been spoken out loud.

This is why the gatekeeping of the contemporary art world feels different from the gatekeeping of other cultural fields. A book that no major critic reviews is still a book; a film that no festival programs is still a film. But a sculpture that no recognized gallery shows, no recognized critic discusses, and no recognized museum considers acquiring is, in a narrow but real legal sense, not sculpture. It is manufactures of metal not specially provided for.

The Bronze Is Still in the Air

Brâncuși's Bird in Space currently lives at the Seattle Art Museum. The court found that it had purely ornamental purposes, that it was the original production of a professional sculptor, and that it was beautiful and symmetrical in outline. These were the operative findings. The judges, having dodged the underlying question of what art is, had to say something on the record, and what they said was inflected by their own taste.

But the taste was incidental. The holding was the deferral. Every time a curator at a major museum approves an acquisition, signs a loan agreement, prepares an appraisal, the Customs Court of 1928 is the silent co-signer. The recognized art world is performing a function the U.S. government decided in November 1928 that it could not perform itself, and that it would therefore farm out to whoever could.

The document was the protagonist of this story. The document, more specifically the gap inside the document — the gap between what Paragraph 1704 said and what it could no longer hold — required the court to write a clause that was not in the statute. The clause is unwritten. It says: defer to what the institutions already recognize. That clause is now older than almost everyone working under it. It is, in a real sense, the constitution of the American art world.

Whoever controls the institutions writes the law. The 1928 court did not say this. It only made it true.
