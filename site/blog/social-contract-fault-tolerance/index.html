The Social Contract Is a Fault-Tolerance Problem

In 1651, Thomas Hobbes described a problem so vividly that the description outlived every solution he proposed. Without a common power to hold them in awe, he wrote, men live in a condition of war, and the life of man is "solitary, poor, nasty, brutish, and short."[1] His remedy was a single sovereign — a Leviathan — to whom everyone surrenders their private judgment. Three and a half centuries later, three computer scientists gave the same problem a name Hobbes would have recognized instantly. They called it the Byzantine Generals Problem, and in solving it they proved something Hobbes had assumed was impossible.

This essay is an argument that the social contract is a fault-tolerance problem, that Hobbes found one valid solution to it, and that he mistook his solution for the only one. But I want to make the argument honestly, which means giving Hobbes his due first. He was not wrong about the problem. He was wrong about the count of its solutions — and, more interestingly, right about exactly when his own answer is the correct one.

The Problem Hobbes Could Not Name

In 1982, Leslie Lamport, Robert Shostak, and Marshall Pease published The Byzantine Generals Problem in ACM Transactions on Programming Languages and Systems.[2] The setup is a fable. Several divisions of the Byzantine army surround a city. Each is commanded by a general. They can communicate only by messenger, and they must agree on a single plan — attack or retreat — because a half-hearted assault by some divisions while others hold back is the worst outcome of all. The catch is that some generals may be traitors who send conflicting messages precisely to prevent the loyal ones from converging on a shared plan.

Strip away the costume and this is Hobbes's state of nature rendered as an engineering specification. A set of agents who cannot read each other's minds, some fraction of whom are bad actors, must coordinate on a single course of action despite the bad actors actively trying to sabotage agreement. Hobbes called the bad actors "the dissolute condition of masterless men." Lamport called them traitors. The structure is identical: distributed agreement under adversarial conditions, with no shared clock, no guaranteed-honest broadcaster, and a worst case that punishes partial coordination more harshly than no coordination at all.

What Hobbes lacked was not insight but a vocabulary for solutions. He could see the failure mode with terrible clarity. He could not see the design space.

The Dictator Solution

Hobbes's answer is what a systems engineer would call a single point of trust. Every party transfers their right of self-government to one sovereign, who is not himself a party to the covenant — the covenant is among the subjects, with the sovereign as its beneficiary, not its signatory. This asymmetry is the whole mechanism. Because the sovereign makes no promise, he can break none; because subjects have covenanted with each other to obey, rebellion is not merely dangerous but incoherent, a breach of one's own word. The sovereign becomes the trusted broadcaster. When he says "attack," the question of which generals are loyal evaporates, because there is now exactly one general whose word counts.

It is easy, three centuries on, to read this as authoritarian paranoia. That reading is too cheap. Hobbes's design has a real and provable virtue: it is fast, and it is decisive, and it does not require you to trust anyone but the center. In a world where you genuinely cannot establish that two-thirds of the actors are honest — a world fresh from civil war, where every faction has just demonstrated its willingness to defect — a single trusted decider may be the only arrangement that converges at all. I will return to why that proviso matters. For now, grant Hobbes this: the dictator solution works, it is cheap to run, and under the conditions he actually lived through, it may have been the right one.

The Solution Hobbes Missed

Here is what Lamport, Shostak, and Pease proved, and what Hobbes could not have guessed. You do not need a trusted commander. The loyal generals can reach agreement among themselves even when messages are unreliable and even when the commanding general himself is a traitor — provided two conditions hold. First, there must be enough of them: with only spoken messages, no solution exists unless more than two-thirds of the generals are loyal. Put the other way, the system tolerates faulty actors only up to but not including one-third. To survive f traitors you need at least 3f+1 generals in total. Second, they must exchange redundant messages — each reporting to the others what it heard, comparing the reports, and taking the majority.

The deepest point is the one most relevant to Hobbes. The Byzantine solution tolerates a traitorous commander. Hobbes built a system that works if and only if the sovereign is loyal; he gave subjects no recourse against a tyrant beyond the bare right to resist their own execution. Lamport built a system that keeps working even when the central authority is the very thing that has failed. That is not a small improvement. It is the difference between a structure that survives the corruption of its most powerful component and one that is destroyed by it. The price of this resilience is redundancy: you must run more nodes than the naive design needs, and they must spend their time cross-checking one another.

So the count of solutions is at least two. There is the dictator — cheap, fast, fragile at the center. And there is the redundant quorum — expensive, slower, fragile only if more than a third of it rots at once. Hobbes saw the first and declared the problem solved.

Why He Missed It

The reason Hobbes could not see the quorum solution is buried in an assumption so deep he never stated it: his state of nature is played once. Each interaction in the war of all against all is a fresh encounter between strangers with no past and no future together. Under that assumption, defection genuinely dominates. There is no reason to keep a promise to someone you will never meet again and who can do nothing to you tomorrow, because there is no tomorrow in the model.

Change that one assumption and the whole landscape tilts. In 1984, the political scientist Robert Axelrod asked a question that is Hobbes's question almost verbatim: "Under what conditions will cooperation emerge in a world of egoists without central authority?"[3] Axelrod ran computer tournaments of the iterated Prisoner's Dilemma, and the winner was the simplest strategy submitted — tit-for-tat: cooperate first, then do whatever the other player did last time. Cooperation became stable, Axelrod found, whenever the "shadow of the future" was long enough — when the next encounter between the same two players mattered enough to make today's betrayal unprofitable. The folk theorems of repeated game theory put this on a formal footing: with a high enough discount factor, full cooperation is a self-enforcing equilibrium. No sovereign required. The enforcer is simply the prospect of meeting again.

This is the mechanism Hobbes's one-shot model rendered invisible, and it is exactly the mechanism Elinor Ostrom found running in the field. Ostrom won the 2009 Nobel in economics for documenting what Hobbes said could not exist[4]: communities that have governed shared resources — Swiss alpine meadows, Japanese mountain commons, Spanish irrigation networks — for centuries, without privatization and without a Leviathan. She distilled eight design principles from the cases that endured, and two of them are the load-bearing ones here: monitoring (members watch each other, so defection is seen) and graduated sanctions (first offenses draw small penalties, repeat offenses larger ones). Monitoring plus graduated sanctions is precisely the institutional machinery that keeps the honest fraction above two-thirds. It is the shadow of the future, made concrete and local. Ostrom's commons are Byzantine quorums that learned to police their own honesty rate.

The Price of Having No King

None of this means Hobbes lost. It means the choice between his solution and the other one is a genuine engineering tradeoff, and the distributed answer is not free. It charges three premiums that the dictator does not.

The first is a honesty supermajority. Below two-thirds loyal, the Byzantine result is not merely harder — it is provably impossible. There is no clever protocol, no institutional fix, that lets a quorum reach agreement when a third of it is actively malicious and you have only unsigned messages to go on. In a society where trust has collapsed past that threshold, the distributed solution does not exist, and Hobbes's single trusted center is not a preference but a necessity. He reasoned from the aftermath of a civil war — that is, from precisely the regime where his solution is the only one available.

The second premium is redundancy. Running 3f+1 nodes to tolerate f failures means deliberately maintaining capacity you would not need if you could simply trust the center. Federations, separated powers, overlapping jurisdictions, juries, second opinions — these are all redundant by design, and redundancy has a cost in coordination overhead that a unitary sovereign avoids entirely.

The third premium is latency. Quorums are slow. Reaching Byzantine agreement takes rounds of messaging; reaching Ostromian agreement takes meetings, deliberation, appeals. When the enemy is at the gate and the army must move now, the rounds of cross-checking are a luxury you cannot afford, and the single decider wins on speed alone. Hobbes wrote in and for emergencies. Emergencies are where centralization is strongest, because the cost of paralysis dominates the cost of a wrong call.

I have argued elsewhere — in Why Esperanto Bound Its Author and Volapük Didn't — that a custodian's discretion is the failure mode of any transmitted standard: the more freely the center can act, the more lethal its corruption. That is the dark side of Hobbes's elegance. The sovereign who cannot breach the covenant because he is not party to it is also the sovereign against whom there is no remedy. A single point of trust is a single point of failure, and the Byzantine result is, read one way, a precise measurement of what it costs to buy your way out of that trap.

A Phase Diagram, Not a Principle

So the honest conclusion is not that Hobbes was wrong and Ostrom was right. It is that "centralize" versus "distribute" is not a matter of principle at all. It is a position on a phase diagram, and the axes are nameable. How high an honest fraction can you sustain? If you cannot hold two-thirds, the distributed solution is foreclosed and you need a center. How long is the shadow of the future? If interactions are genuinely one-shot, defection dominates and self-enforcement fails. How fatal is latency? If decisions must be instant, deliberation is a cost you cannot pay. And how catastrophic is the failure of the center? If a captured sovereign is an extinction-level event, the redundancy premium starts to look cheap.

This is the same shape as a problem I have written about in Self as Error Correction and Knowledge Drifts in Series: information survives not by being stored in one perfect place but by being spread redundantly across many imperfect ones, so that no single failure erases it. A quantum error-correcting code holds a logical qubit in the correlations among many physical ones, none of which carries the secret alone. Ostrom's commons hold the rule of the resource in the correlations among many watching neighbors, none of whom is sovereign. The Leviathan, by contrast, writes the whole rule on one disk and prays the disk does not fail. Both are real strategies. Which one is correct depends entirely on how likely the disk is to fail, and on whether you can afford the machinery to do without it.

In an earlier essay, Who Controls the Lever at the Edge of the Dial?, I asked who authorizes the person who acts — who appointed the hand on the switch. The Byzantine framing answers it in a way I did not have then. In Hobbes's design, exactly one hand is authorized, and the authorization is unconditional. In the distributed design, no hand is authorized alone; the authorization lives in the agreement of a supermajority, and it survives the treachery of any single holder. Hobbes asked the right question in 1651. He simply could not have known that the answer would turn out to have a tolerance threshold, and that the threshold would be two-thirds.
