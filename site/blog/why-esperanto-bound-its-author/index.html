Why Esperanto Bound Its Author and Volapuk Didn't

In 1889, a constructed language called Volapük had everything Esperanto would spend the next century envying. It had roughly a million claimed adherents, twenty-five periodicals, two hundred and eighty-three local clubs, and a third International Congress held entirely in the language itself — the first time in history a fully artificial tongue was spoken aloud by a hall full of strangers who understood one another. Esperanto, published only two years earlier, had almost none of this. Yet today Volapük is a museum piece with a few dozen hobbyist speakers, and Esperanto is the one invented language with a living community, native speakers, a literature, and a permanent congress.

The usual explanation is linguistic: Volapük was ugly and hard. It had umlauts everywhere, a baroque case system, verb forms that could run to half a million conjugations. Esperanto was cleaner. This is true, and it explains almost nothing. Volapük's difficulty did not stop it from acquiring a million users; the cleaner language did not win on its release. The two systems diverged not because of what they were but because of who was allowed to change them. Volapük had an owner. Esperanto had a constitution that bound its own author. That difference — a fact about governance, not grammar — is why one died and one lived.

The language that belonged to one man

Volapük was invented by Johann Martin Schleyer, a German priest, in 1879. Schleyer regarded the language as his property in a strong and literal sense: his to define, his to revise, his to veto. As the movement grew it acquired an Academy, and the Academy acquired a Director — Auguste Kerckhoffs, a Dutch linguist working in Paris, who had introduced Volapük to France, Spain, and Scandinavia. Kerckhoffs wanted reforms. The case system should shrink; the verb should simplify; the language should be made learnable by ordinary people rather than by enthusiasts willing to memorize a grammar the size of a hymnal.

Schleyer refused. He insisted on his proprietary right to the last umlaut. At the 1889 Paris Congress, the Academy stripped him of his veto, and Schleyer responded by repudiating the Academy entirely. There was now no authority both sides recognized. The reformers could not simply fork the language — take the core and improve the edges — because Schleyer owned the core and would not let it go. Their only recourse was to walk away and build new languages from the wreckage. They did, prolifically: Idiom Neutral, Dil, Veltparl, Dilpok, Lingua European, a dozen still-births between 1893 and 1907. By 1890 the movement was, in the words of one chronicler, in disarray, conducting its business through violent arguments. The language that owned itself had fragmented into a graveyard of orphans.

There is a quiet irony in Kerckhoffs being the man who lost this fight. He is remembered today not for Volapük but for Kerckhoffs's principle, the founding maxim of modern cryptography: a system must remain secure even if everything about it is public knowledge except the key. Secrecy of the design is a liability; security must live in the key, which anyone may possess. Kerckhoffs spent his cryptographic career arguing that a system whose strength depends on a hidden, proprietary core is brittle. Then he spent his linguistic career discovering the same law in a different medium — a language whose legitimacy depends on a single proprietor's will is brittle — and he lost to the proprietor anyway. The principle was right. The man could not enforce it against an owner who held the keys.

The author who forbade himself

Ludwik Zamenhof did the opposite, and he did it first, before there was any crisis to respond to. In the Unua Libro of 1887 — the pamphlet that introduced Esperanto — he declared that "an international language, like a national one, is common property," and renounced all personal rights to his creation. The language was, from its first day, the property of society and not of Zamenhof. He had built it and then signed away the authority to revise it.

This renunciation became a written law in 1905. At the first World Esperanto Congress in Boulogne-sur-Mer, the assembled speakers adopted the Fundamento de Esperanto — sixteen grammatical rules, a base vocabulary, a set of exercises — as the sole obligatory and untouchable foundation of the language. The Declaration of Boulogne stated the principle in language that reads almost like a curse: no person and no society has the right to make even the smallest arbitrary change to the Fundamento. The rule bound everyone. It bound Zamenhof. The author of the language was constitutionally forbidden from improving it.

The test came in 1907. A committee of scientists, joined by some of Esperanto's own leading figures, proposed a reformed version called Ido — cleaner, in many respects linguistically superior, with the diacritics smoothed away and several of Zamenhof's awkward choices corrected. This was Esperanto's 1889 moment, its schism. But the outcome inverted Volapük's. Because the Fundamento was inviolable and ownerless, the reformers could not capture the core. They could only leave. Most speakers stayed; Ido withered into its own small museum; Esperanto absorbed the shock and kept growing. The reformers were frequently right about the linguistics. It did not matter. Being right about the core was the one move the system had permanently forbidden.

Why the binding works: a theorem from monetary policy

The mechanism that saved Esperanto was described, almost a century later and in a completely unrelated field, by the economists Finn Kydland and Edward Prescott. Their 1977 paper "Rules Rather than Discretion: The Inconsistency of Optimal Plans" — which won the 2004 Nobel — identified what they called time inconsistency. A policymaker who retains the discretion to change course later cannot make a credible promise today, because everyone can see that when the moment comes, the policymaker will face fresh incentives to renege. A central bank that can inflate will be expected to inflate, and that expectation alone wrecks the outcome it was trying to prevent. The counterintuitive remedy: the bank becomes more effective by binding itself to a rule that removes its own discretion. Credibility is not earned by being trustworthy. It is manufactured by becoming unable to betray.

Zamenhof's renunciation is the linguistic version of central-bank independence. By making himself powerless over Esperanto, he made Esperanto credible. A learner investing years in the language needed to know that the grammar would not be reorganized out from under them by an irritable founder. The Fundamento guaranteed it, precisely because not even the founder could touch it. Schleyer offered no such guarantee; his retained discretion meant every adopter of Volapük was, knowingly or not, betting on the stability of one man's preferences. When those preferences collided with the Academy's, the bet went bad for everyone at once.

This reframes the schism itself. A mutable authority does not merely fail to prevent disputes — it converts them into something lethal. When the core is owned, every proposed reform is automatically a referendum on the owner's legitimacy, because the owner is the only one who can enact or block it. Disagreement about the dative case becomes a constitutional crisis about Schleyer. When the core is frozen and ownerless, the same reform is just a proposal that the rule rejects; there is no person to depose, no authority to capture, and the dissenter's only options are to fork at the periphery or to leave entirely — neither of which can take the core community with them. The frozen kernel does not suppress dissent. It relocates it to where it cannot fragment the whole.

The same move, everywhere durable things are built

Once you see the pattern, it recurs across domains that never talk to one another. Constitutional democracies entrench their cores against their own future legislators: Germany's Basic Law contains an Ewigkeitsklausel, an eternity clause (Article 79​(3)), declaring that human dignity and the democratic federal order can never be amended away, not even by the unanimous vote of every future parliament. The clause exists so that no lawful majority can ever again vote a dictatorship into being. It is the Fundamento applied to a state: freeze the kernel against everyone, including the people who will later hold legitimate power, and leave everything else amendable.

Open-source software learned the same lesson the hard way. The "benevolent dictator for life" model — one owner with final say, Schleyer's arrangement in a hoodie — works until it doesn't; Python's creator stepped down from the role in 2018 precisely because no living person should be the single point of failure for a shared standard. The healthy modern arrangement is the git model: a protected main branch that no individual rewrites at whim, combined with permissionless forking at the edges. Dissent is cheap and peripheral; capture of the core is structurally prevented. When OpenOffice's stewardship became untrustworthy, the community simply forked LibreOffice and walked off with the momentum — the move Volapük's reformers were denied, now made trivial by design.

And it connects directly to a piece of this site's own corpus. When the world's metrologists retired the physical kilogram in 2019, they replaced an artifact that one custodian held in a vault with a definition fixed to an unchangeable constant of nature. They removed discretion from measurement: no committee can now revise the kilogram, because it is pinned to the Planck constant by fiat. That is a stipulated ground — a value frozen by convention so that everything else can be coordinated against it — and the Fundamento is its linguistic twin. Zamenhof stipulated sixteen rules as Esperanto's unmeasured ground; the rest of the language is free to grow on top of them.

The dual: the epic that survived by having no master

The deepest connection is also the most surprising, because it looks like the exact opposite. This site has argued that the Homeric epics survived for centuries without any master copy — recomposed in performance every time, owned by no scribe, fixed to no authoritative text. Esperanto survived by doing the reverse: it created a master copy and made it untouchable. How can both be survival strategies?

Because they are the same move executed from opposite ends. The threat to a transmitted thing is a custodian with discretion — an authority that can change the core and therefore turns every disagreement into a fight over the authority. There are exactly two ways to remove that threat. You can abolish the authoritative core altogether, so there is nothing to capture and nothing to defend — the oral epic, which has no canonical text to corrupt because it never had one. Or you can freeze the core and bind the author, so the authority exists but cannot exercise discretion — Esperanto, the constitution, the redefined kilogram. Both routes arrive at the same place: a system whose center cannot be seized, because there is either nothing there to seize or no one with the power to seize it. Volapük took neither route. It kept an owner who could change everything and would let no one else. That is the one configuration that reliably dies.

This sits alongside two other strategies the corpus has mapped for keeping a transmitted thing intact: the parallel redundancy of collation and cross-checking, where many independent copies vote down the errors that accumulate in any single chain of transmission; and the commons governance of Ostrom's design principles, where a community polices a shared resource through graduated, predictable rules rather than a sovereign's discretion. Self-binding is the fourth member of that family. It is also the most paradoxical, because it works by subtracting power from the very person who would seem most entitled to wield it.

The price of the lock

None of this is free, and it is worth ending on the cost, because the cost is exactly where the elegance becomes a trap. The Fundamento did not freeze the good parts of Esperanto and leave the flaws editable. It froze everything in the kernel, mistakes included. Esperanto's accusative ending, its supersigned letters that no typewriter wanted, the choices Zamenhof himself later regretted — all of them are now permanent, defended by the same inviolability that defends the language's survival. The credibility that kept Esperanto alive is purchased with the permanent inability to fix it. The reformers of 1907 were not cranks; many of their corrections were sound. They lost anyway, and they had to lose anyway, because a kernel you can improve when you are right is a kernel an owner can also change when he is wrong, and the second possibility is the one that kills.

This is the same self-binding that, elsewhere on this site, lets a community delete a decision node it does not trust itself to hold — the precommitment of Ulysses lashing himself to the mast, choosing in advance to be unable to choose. Schleyer kept his hands free and steered his language onto the rocks. Zamenhof tied his own hands and his language outlived him by a century. The founder who could not improve his creation is the reason his creation still exists.
