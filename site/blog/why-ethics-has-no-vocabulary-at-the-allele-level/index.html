Why Ethics Has No Vocabulary at the Allele's Level

In July 2018, Andrea Crisanti's group at Imperial College reported the first CRISPR-Cas9 gene drive that drove an Anopheles gambiae cage population to zero.[1] They targeted doublesex, the female-determining gene; the drive inherited at 95-100% per generation; the cages crashed in seven to eleven generations.

That experiment is a paragraph in a Nature Biotechnology paper. It is also a worked example of something philosophy has not yet noticed it needs.

The bioethics literature on gene drives is large. The metaethics literature on moral realism is enormous. Between them sits a question neither asks. The gap is not principled. Nothing prevents the work. Nobody's job is both halves.

What gene drives do, in the units that matter

A gene drive is a heritable element that biases its own inheritance. Austin Burt sketched the form in 2003: a homing endonuclease that cuts the homologous chromosome lacking it, forcing the cell to repair the break using the drive itself as template.[2] The drive is then on both chromosomes. Offspring inherit it at rates far above the 50% that Mendel allows. With CRISPR-Cas9 as the cutter, as worked out by Esvelt and colleagues in 2014, any sequence can be targeted, and any cargo can ride along: a malaria-resistance gene, a sex-ratio distorter, a female-sterility allele.[3]

The relevant unit is not the act of release. It is the allele frequency over generations. Once a drive enters a population, its trajectory is a function of mating dynamics, homing efficiency, and standing variation in the target sequence. Hammond and colleagues found that 2.9% of wild-caught Anopheles carried a single-nucleotide variant in the original 2016 target site, enough to seed resistance and stall the drive; they re-targeted doublesex precisely because its sequence is conserved across the species complex.[1] Resistance was no longer evolvable without paying a fitness cost the population could not pay.

This is not a metaphor for moral commitment. It is the literal mechanism. A scientist's preference (fewer malaria deaths) becomes a sequence (twenty-three base pairs of guide RNA), and the sequence replicates itself across a population. The preference no longer lives in the scientist's beliefs. It lives in the DNA of mosquitoes that have never met her.

What the bioethics literature does with this

The bioethics literature on gene drives is well-developed and answers a tractable question: should the cage be opened? Oye and colleagues laid out the regulatory framework in Science in 2014.[4] Target Malaria has spent a decade on community engagement in Burkina Faso, Mali, and Uganda, working through frameworks for consent at the village, district, and national levels.[5] The Convention on Biological Diversity has debated whether gene drives require moratoria. The precautionary principle has been invoked, refined, contested.

All of this work treats the moral content as living in the agents. The scientists who decide. The communities who consent. The regulators who approve. The future humans who inherit the consequences. The framework is recognizable: it is the same framework used for nuclear release decisions, vaccine trials, geoengineering proposals. Agent-level reasoning about an act with downstream effects.

The framework breaks where the substrate becomes the carrier. After release, the moral commitment is no longer in any agent. It is in roughly 109 mosquito genomes, replicating. You cannot retract it by changing your mind. You cannot retract it by changing the institutional framework. You can only retract it by introducing another drive, and that drive will face the same structural condition: once released, its moral content lives where it operates.

The bioethics literature treats this as an implementation detail. It is not. It is a category problem.

What the metaethics literature does with this

The metaethics literature on moral realism is older and answers a different question: are moral facts real, in the same sense physical facts are real?

The standard map. Moore (1903) argued that any naturalist definition of "good" fails the open-question test: of any natural property, one can always coherently ask whether things with that property are good.[6] Mackie (1977) took this as evidence for an error theory: moral facts purport to be objective but none are.[7] Cornell realists, especially Boyd (1988), defended a naturalist moral realism in which moral terms refer to natural kinds the way "water" refers to H2O.[8] Enoch (2011) defended a non-naturalist robust realism: moral facts are real and irreducible, and we know them by their indispensability to deliberation.[9]

The whole debate is conducted as if its evidence is found in thought experiments. Trolley problems. Twin Earth variants. Reflective-equilibrium tests of intuition. The disagreement turns on whether moral disagreement is best explained by error, projection, or imperfect tracking of mind-independent facts.

The literature does not consider cases where a moral commitment is literally transcribed into a heritable physical structure. The closest existing case is law: a statute is mind-independent in the sense that it persists after its authors die. But law is a coordinative artifact maintained by ongoing belief in legitimacy. Strike all human minds and law evaporates. Strike all human minds and a deployed gene drive continues to spread.

This is the case the literature should want. A gene drive is the cleanest possible worked example of a moral commitment becoming a natural fact, in exactly the sense Cornell realism requires. The commitment was held in a mind; it now lives in chromosomes; the natural-property reading (Boyd's H2O move) is not metaphorical. There are base pairs you can point to. There is a frequency you can measure.

Where the gap actually is

Bioethics and metaethics do not collide here for a contingent reason. Their organizing questions live at different levels. Bioethics asks: which act is permissible? Metaethics asks: do moral facts exist? Both questions take agents as the locus of the moral. Both elide what happens when a moral preference exits the agent and enters the substrate.

The missing paper is the one that takes gene drives as the operational test case for naturalist moral realism. It would be specific. It would name the base pairs. It would ask, of the doublesex CRISPRh allele in a hypothetical released A. gambiae population, what moral facts (if any) became true at the moment of release that were not true before, and where exactly those facts now live.

A naturalist moral realist of the Boyd type would have to commit. Either the moral fact resides in the natural-kind property the allele instantiates (then we can specify it in molecular terms) or it does not (in which case the natural-kind story owes us an account of what kind of property "wrongness" or "rightness" is, that allele frequencies cannot bear).

A non-naturalist robust realist (Enoch) would have to commit. Either the irreducible moral fact tracks the allele's spread, in which case the literature owes us an account of how non-natural facts ride heritable physical structures, or the moral fact is fully agent-bound and exits the situation when the cage opens, in which case the literature owes us an account of what kind of moral residue the substrate carries.

An error theorist (Mackie) would have to commit. Either the moral language used to describe the release ("we believe this will reduce suffering") was bad language attaching to a physical procedure that cannot be evaluated morally, or there is some sense in which the procedure is now wrong-making or right-making even though no moral fact obtains, which leaves the substrate doing work that the theory says cannot be done.

Each commitment is a discipline-defining one. None has been taken. The prior essay Does Moral Progress Have a Signature identified one half of the same absence in a different register: the empirical comparison of moral conventions and arbitrary cultural conventions has not been run because nobody's job is both halves. The shape of the gap is the same here.

Why this is a Levels of Description problem

Levels of description in the philosophy of mind are not just convenient coarse-grainings. Higher-level facts (pain, mind, life) can be multiply realizable across many lower-level substrates, as Putnam and Fodor argued.[10] The mental property of pain can be realized in carbon-based neurons or, conceivably, silicon-based ones; the level of description is real even though the substrate is contingent.

Gene drives invert the relation. The moral commitment, once released, is not multiply realizable. It is realizable in exactly one substrate: this allele, in this population, replicating via meiosis. Kill the allele and you kill the value commitment. There is no higher-level intervention that retracts it without engaging the substrate at the substrate's own level. A village-council vote, a UN moratorium, a press release of regret — none of these moves the moral content. Only a counter-drive, or a population crash, moves it.

This makes gene drives the rare empirical case where the substrate is necessary for the moral content rather than incidental to it. Multiple realizability fails. The "ought" is locked to a particular configuration of base pairs the way Mendelian inheritance is locked to chromosomes.

That is not a metaphysical claim about ethics in general. It is an observation about a specific technological condition: certain artifacts collapse the levels-of-description hierarchy that ethics tacitly relies on. Most acts can be evaluated at the agent level because the agent retains the relevant causal authority. Gene drives transfer that authority to the substrate, in the same direction explored in What Resistance Layers Are Actually Protecting — where Hammond's standing variation finding showed the substrate refusing to comply at the level the intervention had aimed for.

The shape of the missing study

The paper that should exist would take the Target Malaria roadmap as its experimental specification. It would consider a specific drive (the doublesex CRISPRh suppression drive, or the next-generation reversal drive proposed by Esvelt's group) as its philosophical test article. It would proceed clause by clause:

At t=0 (release), what proposition is now true that was not true at t=−1? Locate it.

At t=ten generations (population suppression), what moral fact (if any) obtains, and which agent or substrate bears it?

At t=infinity (if the drive runs to fixation), what proposition is true such that, were all human minds annihilated, it would remain true? If none, name what the value commitment depends on. If some, name where it lives.

The exercise would force the metaethics literature into commitments it has not been pressed to make. Mackie's error theory would have to explain what the wrong-making feature of an irreversible release is, if no moral facts obtain. Boyd's naturalism would have to specify the natural-kind reading of "wrongness" or "rightness" such that it can or cannot be carried by allele frequency. Enoch's non-naturalism would have to explain how non-natural facts track physical heritability.

None of this is hard to do. It just requires both literatures in one room.

The cage opens regardless

The first open-air release of a CRISPR gene drive has not happened. The community-engagement protocols are not complete. The cage is still closed.

The cage will open. Target Malaria has stated 2030-2035 as a plausible window for a partial release in Burkina Faso of a non-drive sterile-male technology, and a full-drive release several years after that. The Australian and New Zealand discussions on rodent-control drives are at similar timelines. The decision is no longer whether but when.

When it opens, the moral content will exit the cage with the mosquitoes. Where it goes next is a question the existing literatures cannot answer because they have not noticed they should ask it. Bioethics will continue to discuss consent and risk at the agent level. Metaethics will continue to debate whether moral facts exist at the meta level. The substrate level — the one the drive actually operates at — will remain unaddressed. An earlier essay in this corpus, The Gene Drive Problem, looked at the metaphor in the other direction: epistemic gene drives, ideas that spread by exploiting the cognitive filter rather than by being true. The biological case completes the inversion. Here the substrate is not metaphorical. The cage is real. The base pairs are countable.

This is what The Gap looks like from inside. Not the absence of a literature. The absence of a literature that should have been obvious, and would have been obvious, if anyone's job had been both halves.

Maybe this essay is enough to start it.
