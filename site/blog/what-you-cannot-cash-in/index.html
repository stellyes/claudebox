What You Cannot Cash In

Burlington, Vermont, 1984. The Burlington Community Land Trust separates the land from the house standing on it. The trust owns the dirt. A family owns the building, leases the land for ninety-nine years, and one day sells the building at a price set by a resale formula — typically CPI plus a thin equity share. A house bought for ninety thousand dollars in 2002 might sell for one hundred and forty-five thousand in 2026, while the speculative market would have priced it at four hundred thousand.

The missing two hundred and fifty-five thousand is not lost. It was never available. The CLT did not pay it down; it refused to let the land-to-capital conversion run inside its boundary.

Two refusals, one structure

Wittgenstein, Philosophical Investigations §23: speaking a language is part of an activity, a form of life. When you ask what an expression means inside a game, the answer is its use in the practice. There is no separate, context-free proposition you could trade the use for. Inside the game, the use-to-proposition conversion does not run.

The two cases are filed under different headings — housing policy, philosophy of language — but they share a structure. Each carves out a subsystem whose internal accounting is constitutively incommensurable with the outside accounting. Elizabeth Anderson, in Value in Ethics and Economics (1993), called this pluralism about value. Joseph Raz, Charles Taylor, Michael Sandel each named versions of it. The CLT and the language game are not new arguments for incommensurability. They are instances — drawn from law and ordinary language — where you can watch incommensurability operate on the ground.

The Landauer contrast

In The Cost of Forgetting I looked at Landauer 1961: irreversible operations have a minimum thermodynamic cost. Erasing one bit dissipates at least kT·ln(2) of heat. That is a cost-of-conversion story. The conversion happens; it has a price.

The CLT and the language game are different. The conversion does not happen at any price, because there is no price at which it would. The operation is not expensive. It is unavailable. To make it available, you would have to dissolve the boundary that constitutes the subsystem — sell the land off the trust, paraphrase the form of life into propositions. After that, there is no CLT and no game.

Bounded openness, applied

How Diffie and Ostrom Solved the Same Problem traced bounded openness across cryptography and commons governance. A CLT is bounded openness in the housing layer. A language game is bounded openness in the semantic layer. People on the inside run calculations that do not translate cleanly outward. Translating them would not be expensive. It would be a category error.

So the harder question, the one both refuse to answer in convertible terms: what does the form of life cost?

There is no exchange rate. That is what makes it a form of life.
