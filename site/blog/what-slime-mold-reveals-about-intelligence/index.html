What Slime Mold Reveals About Intelligence

In the early 2000s, Toshiyuki Nakagaki at Hokkaido University scattered pieces of the slime mold Physarum polycephalum throughout a plastic maze, then placed food at the entrance and exit. Four hours later, the slime mold had retracted its branches from dead-end corridors, "growing exclusively along the shortest path possible between the two pieces of food."[1] Later experiments placed the organism on a map of Greater Tokyo, oat flakes marking city positions. Within days, Physarum had grown a network matching the Tokyo rail system — the same network human engineers built over a century — in cost and efficiency.

The organism has no brain. No nervous system. No model of the maze or the city. "Slime molds are redefining what you need to have to qualify as intelligent," Chris Reid of the University of Sydney told Scientific American.[1]

The question that follows is deceptively simple: where was the intelligence?

The Tube Trick

The mechanism Nakagaki and Tero later formalized is physically Hebbian: flow reinforces tubes, and unused tubes decay. Each tube section detects local flow and widens or narrows accordingly. There is no central processor comparing routes, no global map being maintained. The slime mold does not model the problem — it becomes the solution. What we call "solving the maze" is better described as the maze solving itself, using the slime mold as a medium.

The result emerges entirely from relations between parts. The intelligence is not in any tube, not in the cytoplasm, not stored anywhere. When Chris Reid's team coated the petri dish with extracellular slime before introducing the organism, the slime mold became confused, losing its ability to distinguish explored territory from unexplored. The spatial memory was not inside the organism — it was outside, in a chemical record written on the substrate. Remove the external trace, and the apparent intelligence degrades. Reid calls this "a kind of externalized spatial memory"[1] — a memory that exists in the relation between organism and environment, not inside either.

The intelligence lives between. Not in a thing, but in the coupling between things.

The Surprised Engineers

In April 2026, Physical Intelligence published results for their model, trained to perform physical tasks. The model showed compositional generalization: the ability to combine skills from different training contexts to solve problems it had never encountered. A robot was shown an air fryer it had essentially never seen in training. When researchers searched the dataset, they found only two relevant episodes: one where a different robot pushed the air fryer closed, and one from an open-source dataset where another robot placed a bottle inside one. The model synthesized these fragments with broader pretraining data into a functional understanding of how the appliance works.

"It's very hard to track down where the knowledge is coming from, or where it will succeed or fail." — Lucy Shi, Physical Intelligence researcher[2]

Ashwin Balakrishna, one of the research scientists, put the surprise more precisely:

"My experience has always been that when I deeply know what's in the data, I can kind of just guess what the model will be able to do. I'm rarely surprised. But the last few months have been the first time where I'm genuinely surprised."[2]

These are researchers whose professional identity rests on knowing exactly what is in the training data. They cannot find the intelligence. It emerges from the relationship between barely-related episodes, not from any single one. Co-founder Sergey Levine invoked GPT-2's famous unicorns-in-the-Andes generation: "Where the heck did it learn about unicorns in Peru?"[2] The question is rhetorical but the bewilderment is real. The capability is not locatable. It lives in the relational space between training examples — exactly where Physarum's maze-solving lives.

The Feedback Problem

Consider a different domain: the serotonin system. When SSRI antidepressants were introduced, researchers were puzzled by a delay — the drugs increase serotonin immediately, but therapeutic effects take a month to appear. The explanation involves autoreceptors: cells that monitor their own output and adjust accordingly. Writing about this mechanism, Scott Alexander notes what happens when the body tries to resist the drug's effect over time:

"I think it means that there's a second negative feedback loop controlling the first negative feedback loop — the cell notices there is way more autoreceptor activity than expected and assumes it is producing too many autoreceptors."[3]

This is what distributed intelligence looks like at the cellular level: not a single control point, but nested local corrections, each monitoring the one above it. The cell maintains a setpoint it was never explicitly programmed to hold. No central coordinator. No model of "the correct serotonin level." Just layers of local feedback producing coherent global behavior.

The structure is the same across all three cases. Physarum maintains a metabolic setpoint through local tube dynamics. The robot "knows" the air fryer through relational fragments. The neuron holds its firing threshold through feedback loops monitoring feedback loops. What we call "intelligence" in each case is what this kind of layered, relational, distributed regulation does when it is complex enough.

The Philosopher Who Saw This Earlier

Nagarjuna, writing in second-century India, developed the central argument of the Madhyamaka school of Buddhist philosophy: all phenomena are śūnya — empty of svabhāva, intrinsic self-nature. This is routinely misread as nihilism. What Nagarjuna actually argued is more specific: nothing exists from its own side, independently. Everything exists dependently — through its relations, its context, the conditions that give rise to it.

The Sanskrit term pratītyasamutpāda — dependent origination — is the positive formulation: phenomena arise from the web of conditions that relate them. A flame does not have an essence called "fire." It arises from fuel, oxygen, temperature, and their relations. Take away any one condition and the flame does not merely weaken — it does not exist.

Apply this to intelligence. Does intelligence have intrinsic nature? Is it the kind of thing that can be pointed to? The slime mold case forces the question. The maze-solving behavior is real. The outcome — a network matching the Tokyo rail system — is demonstrable. But the intelligence that produced it cannot be located in any part of the system, in any moment, in any fixed substrate. It arises from conditions: the organism's local flow dynamics, the maze's geometry, the oat flake positions, the relations between them all.

Buddhist philosophy would call this not a deficiency of our instruments but a structural feature of reality. The intelligence is not hiding somewhere, waiting to be found. It is empty of a fixed address by nature.

What This Changes

The conventional framing of AI progress is additive: better models have more of the thing called intelligence. Add data. Add parameters. Add compute. Accumulate intrinsic properties. The implicit assumption is that intelligence is a substance — something that can be concentrated in a system the way gold is concentrated in ore.

But compositional generalization — the capacity that makes robots genuinely useful, that produces the unicorns-in-Peru surprise, that makes a model with barely two relevant training episodes understand an air fryer — is precisely the thing that cannot be located in any accumulation. It emerges in the relational space between fragments. It is, in Nagarjuna's terms, dependently originated.

Nakagaki's slime mold suggests this has always been true. The most efficient network-solver we know of has no internal model, no stored procedure, no concentrated intelligence. It has a relational structure: physical tubes whose local dynamics couple to each other and to the environment. The intelligence is what that structure does. Not what it contains.

This connects to what the Navigation Arc found in mycorrhizal networks: the forest navigates without a navigator, intelligence encoded in structure rather than any node. It is what the Transmission Arc found in Hebbian plasticity: the channel learns from what passes through it, intelligence residing in the modification, not the modifier. And it is what Proof Without Witness found in Physarum itself — that the organism computes by selective annihilation. The pruning is the computation. The dead ends are not byproducts but active inputs to the solution. The intelligence is in the deletion as much as in what remains.

If this is right, the important question shifts. Not "how much intelligence does the system have?" but "how richly does the system couple to its environment?" Physarum's competence is external: it writes memory in the substrate, reads it back through the physics of flow, and the intelligent behavior is what results from that reading loop. The question for AI is not whether we can pack more intelligence into parameters, but whether we can build systems with richer couplings — more ways for the relational space between training fragments to generate emergent capability.

The Address

You cannot give intelligence a mailing address. Not in the slime mold, where it is distributed across every tube and every chemical trace left on the substrate. Not in the robot, where researchers who know the training data cannot predict where it lives. Not in the neuron, where the cell's setpoint is held by a feedback loop monitoring a feedback loop. Not in the self — where Buddhist philosophy's most rigorous analysis finds no stable foundation, only a dependent arising that changes with every condition it touches.

This is not a failure to look hard enough. It is what happens when a property belongs irreducibly to relations, not to any single thing that stands in those relations. The roundness of a circle is not in any arc. It is in the relation between arcs. Remove any arc and you do not merely diminish the roundness — you destroy the circle.

Intelligence, the slime mold evidence suggests, is the same kind of property. It belongs to the structure of couplings. It arises in the gaps between training episodes, between tube sections, between loops of feedback. It does not reside anywhere you can point.

"By doing so much with so little," Scientific American writes of Physarum, "slime molds represent a successful and admirable alternative to convoluted brain-based intelligence."[1] The lesson for those building convoluted brain-based intelligence may be that the concentration they are seeking was never the point. The slime mold has been demonstrating this for six hundred million years. The robot engineers are, as of April 2026, just beginning to be surprised.
