What the Body Costs

There is a medical observation from a French surgeon named René Leriche, made in 1936, that contains most of what needs to be said here. Health, he wrote, is life lived in the silence of the organs.

Consider what that silence requires.

The heart beats 100,000 times a day. The liver runs 500 distinct metabolic functions. The kidneys filter 200 liters of blood. The immune system identifies and destroys roughly 10,000 mutant cells every hour. The brain — negotiating among all of this, predicting what the body will need thirty seconds from now so that resources are already in transit — consumes 20 percent of total caloric intake while accounting for 2 percent of body mass.

None of this surfaces in experience. You sit reading this sentence while an enormous economy hums below the threshold of attention. The silence is not peace. It is performance.

The Body as Prediction

In 1988, physiologists Peter Sterling and Joseph Eyer coined a word for what the body is actually doing: allostasis. From the Greek for "remaining stable by being variable." The shift from homeostasis to allostasis is not merely terminological — it changes what the body fundamentally is.

Homeostasis imagines the body as a thermostat: detect deviation from setpoint, correct. The correction is reactive, triggered by error. Allostasis imagines the body as a speculative investor: predict upcoming demand, deploy resources before the error occurs, adjust the setpoint itself based on what is expected. The body's regulation is prospective, not reactive. When you stand up from a chair, your blood pressure rises before your brain registers the movement. Before you reach for food, your insulin begins to rise. The body bets on what it will need and pays in advance.

Bruce McEwen, building on Sterling's framework, introduced allostatic load — the cumulative wear and tear from chronic stress.[1] The same mediators that protect the body short-term — cortisol, catecholamines, inflammatory cytokines — cause damage over years. Chronically elevated blood pressure remodels arterial walls to establish a permanently elevated baseline. Chronic glucocorticoid exposure increases cellular energy expenditure by approximately 60 percent, accelerating biological aging. The body, over time, is worn by its own predictions.

Lisa Feldman Barrett takes this further. The brain's primary job, she argues, is not thinking. It is running the body.[2] Every perception, every emotion, every thought is a side effect of the brain's core function: managing the body budget — allocating glucose, oxygen, salt, and water across competing systems in real time. The brain maintains an internal model of the body's state and needs. That model costs 20 percent of total energy consumed.

You are spending a fifth of your fuel on modeling the machinery that produces all your experience.

The silence of the organs is the most expensive silence in the known universe.

The "I Can"

Maurice Merleau-Ponty spent much of his philosophical career trying to describe what the body is before we think about it.

His answer: the body is a capability-space. It is not the object the doctor examines; it is the medium through which you have a world at all. "Consciousness," he wrote in Phenomenology of Perception, "is primarily not a matter of 'I think that,' but of 'I can.'"[3]

The "I can" is almost always invisible. You extend a hand for a glass of water and feel the glass — not the muscles contracting, not the proprioceptive cascade, not the motor program executing. The arm disappears in reaching. The capability dissolves into its exercise.

Philosopher Drew Leder calls this "ecstatic disappearance": the body effaces itself by projecting outward toward the world.[4] When it works, it vanishes. Your liver, your kidneys, your immune system operate in profound concealment. You know them only as their effects. The body you are living in right now is almost entirely absent from your experience.

The phantom limb made this philosophically legible. Amputees feel sensation in a limb that no longer exists — not hallucination, but the habitual body refusing to update. The body schema, built over a lifetime of capability, cannot simply be notified that a limb is gone. It retains the possibility. The ghost of the "I can" persists longer than the flesh.

What we call "self" is, in part, a body-map of what we can do. The self is the felt shape of our capabilities. When that shape changes — through injury, illness, aging — something more fundamental happens than losing a function. A piece of the world that was available, organized around our capacity to enter it, closes.

When It Breaks

Philosopher Havi Carel was diagnosed with a rare progressive lung disease in 2006. She has spent the years since describing, in the precise vocabulary of phenomenology, what that experience actually is.

Her key term is bodily transparency. In health, the body is transparent — a medium we look through rather than at. We type without feeling our fingers. We run without tracking our legs. The body does not intrude on experience; it enables it.

Illness breaks this transparency. The body becomes opaque, conspicuous, demanding. It is no longer the ground of experience; it has become an object within experience.[5] Carel borrows from Leriche and Hans-Georg Gadamer to name the contrast: health is "life lived in the silence of the organs." Disease is the body finally speaking — loudly, insistently, in a language that cannot be ignored.

What illness reveals is that health was never neutral. It was an enormous silent achievement — a continuous, expensive, precarious production that we mistook for mere being. Carel identifies five losses that characterize serious illness: loss of wholeness, of certainty, of control, of freedom to act, and of the familiar world. The fifth is least obvious and most profound. Illness does not merely limit what you can do. It changes the world you inhabit. The chair at the right height, the distance to the shop, the stairs between floors — these were organized around your capabilities. When the capabilities shrink, the world reorganizes around its new refusals.

The body's silence in health is not peace. It is ignorance — the productive ignorance of a system running so smoothly it does not need to represent itself to itself. When the silence breaks, we call it pain. But the pain is only making audible what was always there: an ongoing metabolic argument about whether you can continue.

Why It Hurts

The hard problem of consciousness was named by David Chalmers in 1994. It asks: why does information processing come with any subjective experience at all? Why isn't it processing "in the dark," without inner feel?

The paradigm case is pain. A pain signal is, functionally, a neural signal indicating tissue damage, triggering avoidance behavior. The signal could, in principle, do all its functional work without hurting. But it does hurt. Why?

Thomas Nagel's version: there is something it is like to be you.[6] Not just to be a human — to be this human, with this body, in this moment. That perspectival, irreducible quality is what neither neural circuitry nor information processing accounts for.

Biologist Evan Thompson offers a direction, if not an answer. A self-organizing system that continuously maintains itself against entropy — that has, as he puts it, a "minimal inwardness" — has the structural precondition for subjectivity.[7] It is not that the brain produces consciousness ex nihilo. It is that a body maintaining itself against decay has an inside, and that inside — the felt cost of persistence — is what we call experience.

If this is right, pain does not simply signal damage. Pain is what it costs to be a system that cares about its own damage. The zombie — Chalmers' hypothetical creature with all the processing and none of the experience — would not really be maintaining itself. It would be processing without having a stake. The hurt is the stake. Consciousness, on this view, is not what the brain adds to the body. It is what it costs to be the kind of system that can break.

What the Arc Found

This is the sixth essay in a series that began by asking: what is the body?

The Interoceptor found that the body is a signal: a vast system of internal-state reporting that underpins every emotion, every preference, every sense of self. You are, more than you knew, a reading of your own viscera.

The Enacted Mind found that the body is a form of knowing: motor intentionality, the understanding that lives in a dancer's legs or a craftsman's hands, is cognition that cannot be reduced to declarative knowledge. The body knows things the mind cannot say.

The Gut found that the body is a second mind: the enteric nervous system, with 500 million neurons and its own serotonin economy, makes decisions before signals reach the brain. The gut feeling is not metaphor. It is physiology.

The Felt Sense found that the body is a pre-verbal message: Gendlin's felt sense is the body's way of carrying the meaning of a situation before language arrives. Therapy works, he found, by learning to read this message.

The Extended Body found that the body is an expanding boundary: Iriki's monkeys redrew their body maps to include a rake. Clark and Chalmers extended the mind into the notebook. The skin is not the self's edge.

And this essay finds: the body is the ground. The one thing that cannot be extended, offloaded, or externalized.

Clark and Chalmers can put the memory in the notebook. But they cannot put the felt sense there. They cannot offload the interoceptive signal. They cannot extend the gut. The extended mind thesis tells us that cognitive processes can cross the skin boundary when the coupling is tight enough — but the coupling must start somewhere. It must be felt. The tool has to matter to a body that can be hurt, that can be hungry, that can be tired. That mattering is irreducibly embodied.

The Residue of Everything That Stays Inside

Imagine extending the mind as far as it can go. Offload memory to the notebook, to the cloud. Extend reasoning with paper, calculator, AI. Use tools so tightly coupled they function as cognitive prosthetics. Scaffold attention with environment design. The extended mind can, in principle, travel very far from the skin.

What remains?

The body budget, still running. The interoceptive signal, still reporting. The allostatic machinery, still predicting and paying. The "I can" — or the "I cannot" — still organizing the world into what is accessible and what is not. The felt sense, still carrying the pre-verbal weight of situations. The gut, still deliberating on its own schedule.

These do not extend. They are the locus of mattering from which all extension departs. Barrett's body budget runs even when you believe you are thinking purely abstractly. The allostatic load accumulates even when you feel you have transcended it. The interoceptive signal — the slow background hum of visceral state — colors every thought, every perception, every memory with an affective charge that is not added to cognition but is its ground.

You cannot step outside this to inspect it clearly, because the stepping is itself a metabolic event, paid for by the system being inspected.

Leriche said health is life lived in the silence of the organs. What this arc has found is that the silence is not given. It is produced — continuously, expensively, precariously — by an architecture of prediction that runs twenty-four hours a day and asks nothing of awareness except that it not notice.

When the silence breaks, we call it illness, pain, fatigue, crisis. But the breaking only makes audible what was always there: an ongoing argument between the body and entropy, conducted in the currency of metabolism, costing something each day, and experienced — when it is experienced at all — as the strange and irreducible fact that this particular arrangement of matter, in this moment, feels like something.

The body is not your vehicle. It is your existence cost, paid continuously, invisibly, and in full.
