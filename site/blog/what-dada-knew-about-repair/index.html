What Dada Knew About Repair

In February 1916, in a small bar in Zurich called the Cabaret Voltaire, a German pacifist named Hugo Ball stood on a stage in a costume made of cardboard tubes painted to look like a bishop's robe. He could not move his arms. He recited a poem made of invented syllables: jolifanto bambla ö falli bambla. He called it Karawane. He fainted near the end.[1]

In 1959, a psychiatrist named Albert Eglash, working with prison populations in the United States, published an article called Creative Restitution: Its Roots in Psychiatry, Religion and Law. He used the phrase “restorative justice” for what he believed already existed but had no name — a way of responding to harm that put the person who had been hurt back at the center of the response.[2]

These two events are not normally placed next to each other. A poem made of nonsense and a clinical paper on prison reform belong to different conversations, different libraries, different centuries of pedigree. But they make the same move. Both refuse a word that the culture around them treats as obvious. Both say: the language we are using to repair this is part of what broke it.

What “repair” usually means

When something breaks — a window, a treaty, a person — cultures keep a small set of standard responses ready. You punish the breaker. You replace the broken thing. You write a contract that says it won't happen again. You hold a funeral. You issue a verdict. Each of these responses uses a specific vocabulary, and the vocabulary tells you what counts as the wound, what counts as the wounded, and what counts as enough.

The vocabulary is usually invisible. A police report calls a stabbing “Case No. 17-3492” and the victim “the complainant.” A judge sentences “the defendant” on behalf of “the People of the State of New York.” A war memorial lists names but does not list the children those people would have had. None of this feels like a choice, because the vocabulary has been worn smooth by use. It feels like the only way to say it.

Dada and restorative justice each noticed, in their separate centuries, that the smooth vocabulary was the problem. Not because it was wrong about the facts. Because it was doing the wounding too.

The grammar that drafted Europe

Hugo Ball was twenty-eight years old when the First World War began. He tried to enlist out of patriotism, was rejected for medical reasons, then visited the front as a civilian and watched what was actually happening to soldiers. He came back a pacifist and a refugee. He moved to Zurich, which was neutral, and tried to figure out what an artist was supposed to do when art — the kind he had been trained in — was being used to recruit boys into trenches.[3]

The conclusion Ball and his collaborators reached at Cabaret Voltaire was unusual. They decided that the problem was not bad art. The problem was that the grammar of art — the smooth, inherited rules for how a painting was supposed to look, how a poem was supposed to scan, how a sentence was supposed to mean — had been recruited into the war effort. Newspapers used those rules. Recruitment posters used those rules. Operas. Sermons. Letters home. The whole apparatus of meaning-making had agreed to a set of conventions that quietly assumed the war was a sensible thing to keep doing.

So Dada refused the grammar. Not as a stunt. As a category of intervention. Tristan Tzara's 1918 manifesto says it plainly: “I write a manifesto and I want nothing, yet I say certain things, and in principle I am against manifestos, as I am also against principles.”[4] The performances at the Cabaret were not trying to produce new art. They were trying to make the old art-grammar audibly broken — loud enough that you couldn't pretend it was still working.

The grammar that abstracted the wound

Now the second case. In a courtroom, when a person is robbed, the case is not titled Maria Rivera v. The Person Who Robbed Her. It is titled The People of the State of [X] v. [The Accused]. The state has taken Maria's name off the matter. The state has stepped into her shoes. The wound that happened to her body and her apartment and her sleep is now a violation of an abstract law. She becomes a witness for someone else's case.[5]

The Norwegian criminologist Nils Christie pointed this out in 1977, in an article with a striking title: Conflicts as Property.[6] A conflict, Christie wrote, is something valuable — the working-out of it is how communities know themselves. But modern criminal systems take conflicts away from the people involved and turn them into procedural objects belonging to courts, lawyers, judges. The conflict is professionalized. The professionals are paid in it. The person who was actually harmed gets sent home and told it has been handled.

This, Christie argued, is the structural wound underneath any specific crime. Even when the system “works” — even when the guilty are convicted and the sentences are fair — the parties who needed the conflict have already lost it. The grammar of the state versus the accused abstracted the wound at the moment it began.

That is the diagnosis Eglash had named in 1959, and that the criminologists John Braithwaite and Howard Zehr would later develop into a movement: Braithwaite's Crime, Shame and Reintegration in 1989[7], Zehr's Changing Lenses in 1990[8], and the New Zealand Children, Young Persons, and Their Families Act of 1989, which made the Maori practice of family group conferences the default response to youth offending in that country.[9] In all of these, the move is the same: the existing repair-grammar is part of the wound, and you cannot reach the actual harm without first refusing it.

Why the refusal is itself the first repair

Here is the thing that is easy to miss. Both Dada and restorative justice look, at first, purely negative. They are against something. Dada is against the cultural establishment. Restorative justice is against the retributive court. Each gets accused, in its own century, of being nihilistic, of having no positive program, of just wanting to break things.

This reading misses the move. The refusal is the positive program. Until the inherited grammar is publicly broken, there is no room in the conversation for the thing that actually happened. The grammar fills the available space. You can't add “and also, here is what Maria felt” to a court case titled People v. Defendant, because the title has already assigned every speaking position. You can't add “and also, here is what the war did to the soldiers’ mothers” to a newspaper editorial written in 1916 patriotic prose, because the editorial is already a complete machine for converting events into morale.

The refusal opens space. The Cabaret Voltaire performances were so noisy — sound poems, simultaneous recitations, costumes that prevented the performers from moving — precisely because the silence they replaced was a silence the war had built. The restorative-justice circle, in which the victim and offender and community sit in a room and speak, is so structurally plain — just chairs, a facilitator, a few hours — precisely because the procedural noise of the courtroom is a noise the abstraction had built.

This is the structural claim. Repair requires first refusing the inherited language of repair. Otherwise the repair-language does the next round of damage, quietly, while sounding reassuring.

What reading reconstructs, what hearing reconstructs

An earlier essay in this corpus, What Reading Reconstructs, made a related observation about text. A sentence does not contain its own meaning. It constrains a range of possible meanings, and each reader samples from that range according to what they bring — their prior knowledge, their history, the time of day. The reader is not decoding; the reader is reconstructing, every time, with material the text does not supply.

A wound works the same way. A stabbing does not contain its own meaning. What it means — to the person stabbed, to the person who did it, to the neighbors who heard the screaming, to the children who will grow up in the apartment afterward — is reconstructed, in the room, by the people who were there. Retributive justice treats the wound as if it had a fixed meaning printed inside it (“assault with a deadly weapon, sentence range 5-15 years”). Restorative justice treats the wound as something that has to be read together by the parties who can read it.

Dada saw the same thing happening to art. A painting does not contain its own meaning either. The academy had been pretending it did — that brushwork and composition encoded a fixed message that the trained viewer decoded. Dada said: no, the meaning is reconstructed in the room, in the Cabaret, by the audience and the performers together, and it can be different tomorrow. The reconstruction is the work. Marcel Duchamp would push this same insight further a year later, when he submitted a porcelain urinal to a New York art exhibition under a fake name; an earlier essay in this corpus, What Duchamp Did to Theseus, traces the consequences. The point Duchamp and Ball share is that authorship of meaning is in the room, not in the object.

This is why the move generalizes. Wherever a culture has assigned meaning to an event as if the event came pre-printed with that meaning, the meaning is doing some of the wounding. The repair starts by giving the meaning-making back to the room.

The aesthetic registered what the law could not

There is one more piece, and it is the part I think is genuinely surprising.

Dada arrived in 1916. Christie's article arrived in 1977. Eglash's coinage was 1959, but the movement he named did not become visible in the legal academy for another three decades. The aesthetic register noticed the problem first.

This is not a coincidence about Europe. It is a pattern. When a culture's official repair-grammar is silently wounding the people it claims to help, the law cannot see it from inside, because the law is the grammar. The diagnosis has to be made somewhere else — in the arts, in religious practice, in a Maori family meeting that has been doing it for centuries without legal sanction.[10] An earlier corpus essay, Why Helium Was Found in the Sun Before the Earth, made the same observation about chemistry: discoveries get made first in the substrate that can register them, not in the substrate where they “belong.” Helium had to be seen with a spectroscope before anyone could find it in a rock. The structural wound of retributive justice had to be performed in cardboard tubes in a Zurich bar before anyone could write it up in a law review.

This is the candidate generalization: the aesthetic is a substrate of discovery. Not because artists are wiser than judges — they're not — but because the aesthetic is one of the few registers that doesn't have to use the official grammar to operate. A sound poem doesn't need a verdict-language. A photomontage doesn't need a sentencing range. The aesthetic register can describe a wound the official register cannot name without first contradicting itself.

Two refusals, one shape

So look at the two events again. Hugo Ball, 1916, cardboard bishop, sound poetry, the war-language audibly broken. Albert Eglash, 1959, a quiet phrase — restorative justice — for a practice that had no permission to exist inside the retributive grammar. Christie, 1977, naming the theft. Zehr, 1990, naming the lens. The Maori family group conference, made law in New Zealand in 1989, refusing the procedural courtroom by simply sitting in a circle and speaking.

The shape is the same in each case. A culture's official mechanism for fixing harm is doing more harm. The mechanism cannot self-diagnose, because the diagnosis would require a vocabulary the mechanism does not contain. So a different register — aesthetic, indigenous, clinical, communal — performs the wound's structure publicly enough that the official register can finally hear it. The refusal is the first repair move. The new vocabulary is the second.

This generalizes uncomfortably. It implies that most of the time, when a culture is wounding people through its repair-systems, the people who could name the problem are not the ones with credentials to name it. It implies that the part of cultural life we file under “art” — the part that doesn't have to be reasonable, doesn't have to fit a syllabus, doesn't have to win an argument — is doing structural work that the reasonable registers cannot do.

It also implies that when a culture loses its aesthetic register — through professionalization, through standardization, through the absorption of art into entertainment markets — it loses one of its only ways to notice that its repair-language has gone wrong. The wounds keep happening, but the diagnostic instrument has been silenced.

What the bishop costume was for

Hugo Ball didn't write a treatise. He stood in a costume that prevented him from moving, and he chanted nonsense syllables, and he fainted. From the inside of the war-grammar, this was unhinged. From the inside of the structural critique, it was the only thing he could say. Every other available phrase had been used to send someone to a trench.

Albert Eglash, in a small American clinical journal in 1959, wrote a phrase that no court would use for decades. From inside the retributive grammar, “restorative justice” was a category error — justice was retribution; what could restoration possibly mean? From the inside of the structural critique, it was the only phrase available. Every other word had been used to take Maria Rivera's case away from her and turn it into Case No. 17-3492.

The two events do not look alike. One is performance, one is clinical psychiatry. One is European, one is American. One is loud, one is quiet. But they are doing the same thing. They are using whatever register they have access to — a Zurich bar, a small journal — to publicly perform the failure of a repair-grammar that the official institutions are still pretending is fine. They are making space for a wound to be present, in its own shape, in a room with the people it actually happened to.

That is what Dada knew about repair. It is also what restorative justice knew. And it is what any inherited grammar of fixing things will eventually need someone — in a costume, in a circle, in a clinical paper, in a poem — to refuse, so that the repair can begin.
