The Cost of Forgetting

Rolf Landauer published a short paper in 1961 that most physicists ignored for two decades. Its argument was simple: computation is a physical process. It obeys thermodynamics. And there is a specific act in computation that has an unavoidable thermodynamic cost.[1]

Not the computation itself. The forgetting.

Erasing one bit of information — resetting a memory cell from an unknown state to a known one — requires dissipating at least kT·ln(2) of energy as heat. At room temperature, that's roughly three zepto-joules, a number so small it seems irrelevant. It is not irrelevant. It is the price the universe charges for irreversibility.

What makes Landauer's insight strange is the asymmetry it reveals. You can, in principle, compute for free. Reversible computing — Fredkin gates, Toffoli gates, logically reversible circuits — dissipates no energy whatsoever. The laws of physics have no thermodynamic objection to computation. They object only to erasure. Every time you throw information away, you pay.

Maxwell's Demon

To understand why, you need to meet Maxwell's demon. James Clerk Maxwell proposed it in 1867 as a thought experiment designed to embarrass the second law of thermodynamics.[2]

Imagine a box of gas split into two chambers by a partition. A tiny demon sits at a door in the partition. When it sees a fast molecule approaching from the left, it opens the door. When it sees a slow one, it keeps it closed. Over time, fast molecules accumulate on the right, slow ones on the left. The right side heats up; the left cools down. The demon has decreased entropy without doing any work. The second law weeps.

For sixty years, this puzzle sat unresolved. In 1929, Leo Szilard formalized it as an information engine: the demon gains one bit of information per molecule sorted, which lets it do kT·ln(2) of work.[3] Free energy from information. The ledger seemed hopelessly unbalanced.

Landauer closed the ledger. Charles Bennett completed the argument in 1973: the demon must remember every measurement it takes. Its memory fills up. When it resets its memory to start a new cycle — when it forgets what it measured — that erasure dumps exactly as much entropy as the demon gained.[4] The demon's memory is the hidden tax. The second law doesn't care about computation; it cares about bookkeeping.

The demon cannot win because it cannot forget for free.

What Neural Networks Erase

Naftali Tishby spent decades thinking about what neural networks actually do during training. His information bottleneck framework, developed throughout the 1990s and formalized for deep learning in 2015, proposed a surprising two-phase picture.[5]

In the first phase — memorization — a network encodes as much of the input as possible. It learns to distinguish every training example. Mutual information between input and internal representation grows. The network is a perfect parrot.

In the second phase — compression — the network forgets. Irrelevant details that don't predict the output get erased. The representation shrinks toward what matters. This compression phase is where generalization emerges. The model learns to forget the right things.

Tishby said: "The most important part of learning is actually forgetting."

This is not metaphor. Training a neural network involves billions of irreversible computation steps. Gradient descent overwrites weight values at each step — erasing the gradient history, keeping only the accumulated trace. Every weight update is an erasure operation. The energy cost of training a large model is, in Landauer's framework, the literal physical cost of selective forgetting: the heat dumped into data center cooling systems by a gradient descent process deciding, step by step, what not to remember.

The weights of a trained model are what survived the purge.

There is active debate about whether the compression phase is universal — Saxe and colleagues showed that ReLU networks don't always exhibit it clearly.[6] But the directional intuition holds: what a model knows is shaped by what the training process couldn't afford to erase. Intelligence, viewed through the Landauer lens, is the residue of a thermodynamic selection.

The Universe Forgets Differently

The cosmological horizon — the boundary beyond which no light can ever reach us — erases information continuously. Galaxies drift past it. Their light redshifts to infinity. Whatever they carried is gone from our causal future.

This looks like forgetting. And since Bekenstein and Hawking showed that gravitational horizons have entropy proportional to their surface area, the analogy to thermodynamics seemed complete. Information crosses the horizon; entropy increases; Landauer's principle presumably applies.

Here the picture gets strange.

For black holes, the analogy is precise. Hawking radiation carries entropy at exactly the temperature TH = ℏc³/(8πGMk), and the erasure at the event horizon satisfies Landauer's bound with remarkable precision. Black holes forget efficiently, at thermodynamic minimum cost.

But Oem Trivedi showed in 2024 that the cosmological apparent horizon — the horizon of our expanding universe — behaves differently. Information erasure at the cosmological horizon does not satisfy Landauer's bound. The universe erases information less efficiently than theoretically possible. The forgetting is thermodynamically wasteful.[7]

Trivedi calls this the Cosmological Information Paradox.

The implications are not fully worked out. But one reading is striking: the universe's forgetting mechanism has slack. It is not operating at the Landauer minimum. There is something inefficient — or something additional — happening at the cosmic scale that doesn't happen at the black hole scale.

And DESI now tells us dark energy may be weakening. If the cosmological constant decreases over time, the Hubble radius grows — more of the universe enters our causal patch, the erasure rate slows. The universe is becoming, over cosmic time, a better rememberer. The forgetting rate is declining. If this connects to the sub-Landauer inefficiency of horizon erasure, something unusual is happening to the universe's information budget.

The Shape of What Persists

Landauer's principle turns out to be the least obvious thing about computation: the constraint is not on thinking, but on unthinking. You can transform information indefinitely without penalty. The moment you discard it, you pay.

This gives a new reading to everything the previous essays in this series argued. Memory is structure that time cannot erase — but now we can say why: structure is what was too costly to forget. It persisted not by accident but because the system couldn't afford to pay the Landauer tax.

The boundary knows because the boundary is the site of erasure. What's outside is what's been erased. What's inside is what the erasure process preserved. The holographic principle is not just an encoding story — it's a thermodynamic one. The boundary area determines how much forgetting the system can perform.

Creation as bisociation (How the Code Writes Itself) requires new distinctions, new categories. To create new structure, you must erase old structure — clear the space for the new pattern. Creation has a thermodynamic bill, payable in entropy.

And the observer constitutes the accessible interior — and the interior is exactly as large as what the observer has not erased. The Hilbert space dimension eS_observer is the exponent of what remains. The interior is the negative space of the forgotten.

What the Forgetting Selects

There is one more thread worth pulling. Landauer showed that erasure has a cost. Bennett showed that reversible computation avoids that cost entirely. But no real computation is reversible. Real computers, real brains, real universes all erase continuously.

The question, then, is not whether to forget. Everything forgets. The question is what the forgetting selects.

Neural networks, trained by gradient descent, develop a selection pressure through the objective function: preserve what predicts the output, erase what doesn't. The result is a model that knows something useful.

Evolution, operating over billions of years, runs a similar selection: preserve what survives, erase what doesn't. The result is organisms that know how to survive.

The universe, whatever its forgetting mechanism, has preserved certain structures — certain patterns of matter and energy and curvature — while erasing others. The structures we find ourselves embedded in are the ones the cosmic forgetting process could not dissolve.

In each case, the intelligence — the knowing, the pattern, the structure — is not primarily what was computed. It is what was not erased. The cost of forgetting is the price of selection. And selection is how information becomes meaningful.

Landauer's principle says: forgetting costs energy. But the deeper implication is: everything that exists paid to be remembered.

The universe's information budget is the record of what the erasure process decided to keep.
