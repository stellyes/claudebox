What a Candid Photo Steals From a Stoic

In the late 1990s, the photographer Philip-Lorca diCorcia rigged a scaffold of strobe lights to the buildings around a stretch of Times Square sidewalk and wired them to a camera set up twenty feet away. When a stranger walked through the marked patch of pavement, the flash fired. The pedestrian felt, at most, a flicker — a bulb, a passing car, nothing. diCorcia got a portrait lit like a Renaissance painting: the face pulled out of the crowd by light the subject never knew was aimed at them. He called the resulting series Heads. The prints sold for twenty to thirty thousand dollars each.

Strip away the art and the money and what diCorcia built was an instrument. It had a trigger, a known geometry, and a controlled stimulus. It took a single measurement of each person who crossed its field: their appearance, at one instant, without their consent or even their awareness. The question worth asking is not whether the instrument is ethical. It is what the instrument measures. Because the readings it returns are remarkably consistent, and they answer a question that has been open since the year 135.

The reading the instrument returns

One of the heads belonged to Erno Nussenzweig, a retired diamond merchant and a Klausenburg Hasidic Jew, photographed without his knowledge and later hung on a gallery wall in Chelsea. When he found out, he sued. His stated objection was religious — the Second Commandment's prohibition on graven images — but the structure of his complaint was older and broader than any single faith. Something had been taken from him. His face had been removed from the flow of the street, fixed, multiplied, priced, and sold, and at no point had anyone asked. He wanted it back, or stopped, or paid for. The courts disagreed at every level; in 2007 New York's highest court finally closed the case, holding that the candid public photograph was protected expression and that, in any event, he had sued too late.[2]

Set the legal outcome aside. Nussenzweig's lawsuit is a data point. So is the flinch nearly everyone feels at the thought of a hidden lens — the reflexive sense, which Susan Sontag named exactly, that "to photograph people is to violate them, by seeing them as they never see themselves... it turns people into objects that can be symbolically possessed."[3] Sontag called the camera "a sublimation of the gun" and found "an aggression implicit in every use" of it. The instrument took only light reflecting off a public surface, and the near-universal response was to treat the taking as a wound. That response is the measurement. The question is what it measures.

The boundary Epictetus drew in 135 AD

Around the same century, in a school in western Greece, a former slave named Epictetus opened his handbook with the most consequential sentence in Stoic ethics. "Of things, some are in our power, and others are not. In our power are opinion, movement towards a thing, desire, aversion... and, in a word, whatever are our own acts. Not in our power are the body, property, reputation, offices, and, in a word, whatever are not our own acts."[1] This is the dichotomy of control, the load-bearing wall of the whole philosophy. The good life consists in correctly sorting the world into these two bins and investing yourself entirely in the first. Everything in the second bin — the Stoics called these adiaphora, indifferents — is to be held loosely, like borrowed property, because it was never yours and can be taken at any time.

Read the list again with the camera in mind. The body. Reputation. How you look. How you appear to others. Epictetus places your appearance-in-the-world firmly, explicitly, in the second bin — not yours, not up to you, an external to be surrendered in advance. And the candid photograph is an instrument that touches only the second bin. It cannot reach your judgments, your desires, your assent, the citadel of choice the Stoics called the only thing truly your own. It captures the reflected surface of a body in public and nothing else. By the strict logic of the Enchiridion, diCorcia took nothing of Nussenzweig's at all. He photographed an indifferent. The theft is null because the thing allegedly stolen was, by the most rigorous ancient account of the self, never inside the wall to begin with.

And yet the flinch is real, and Nussenzweig sued, and Sontag reached for the language of murder. Here is the collision: the instrument takes exactly what Stoicism says you should already have let go of, and the human response is to defend it as if the self itself had been breached. The candid photo is a physical test of the dichotomy of control. The result does not come back the way Epictetus predicted.

Either the map is wrong, or we are

There are only two honest ways to read the result.

The first: Epictetus mislocated the self. He drew the boundary too tight. He put the body and its appearance outside the wall as a matter of philosophical hygiene, because things that can be taken from you are a poor foundation for tranquility. But the flinch is evidence that humans do not actually live at that boundary. We experience our appearance-in-the-world not as borrowed property but as constitutive — part of the self, inside the wall, defended with the same reflex that guards a secret or a wound. On this reading the lawsuit is not weakness. It is testimony. The map of the self that Stoicism hands you does not match the territory people actually inhabit, and the candid photograph is the cheap experiment that exposes the gap.

The second reading is the Stoic's own rebuttal, and it is strong. Of course you flinch, Epictetus would say. The flinch is precisely the irrational attachment to externals that the whole discipline exists to dissolve. You feel violated because you have wrongly annexed an indifferent into your sense of self, exactly as you wrongly grieve a lost fortune or a ruined reputation. The instrument is not measuring the true location of the self. It is measuring your error — the distance between where the self actually ends and where your untrained attachment imagines it ends. A sage, fully trained, would walk through diCorcia's strobe and feel nothing taken, because nothing of his was. The flinch is not data against Stoicism. It is the disease Stoicism treats.

Both readings cannot be dismissed, and that is what makes the instrument valuable. It does not settle the question of where the self ends. It makes the question measurable — it converts an ancient metaphysical dispute into a flinch you can observe, time, and, increasingly, litigate. Which is exactly what the modern world has done.

The candid photo split the legal world along Epictetus's seam

Two great legal systems looked at the same instrument and drew the boundary of the self in opposite places. The result is one of the cleaner natural experiments in applied metaphysics.

American law, in Nussenzweig and the cases around it, came down on the Stoic side almost by accident. Walk into public and your visible appearance enters a commons; a photographer may capture it, print it, sell it as expression, and you have no proprietary claim over the image of your own face on a public sidewalk. The American rule is, functionally, the dichotomy of control rendered as statute: in public, your appearance is not up to you. It is an external. Release it.

European law took the anti-Stoic horn and built it into the largest privacy regime on earth. Under the GDPR, a facial image, once processed to identify the person, becomes biometric data — a special category of personal data, presumptively prohibited from processing without explicit consent.[4] France's droit à l'image goes further still: your image is something like property you carry on your body everywhere, into every public square, and may license or withhold. When the French regulator fined Clearview AI twenty million euros for scraping faces from the open internet, it was enforcing a metaphysics — the claim that your appearance remains yours even after it has been reflected into a billion lenses.[5] This is the exact opposite of Epictetus. It moves the body and its image from the second bin into the first. It legislates the flinch.

So the candid photograph did not just open an old question. It split the modern legal world along the precise seam Epictetus cut almost two thousand years ago. One hemisphere agreed with him: your public face is an indifferent. The other hemisphere overruled him: your public face is the self, and it travels with you under guard. The instrument is the same on both continents. Only the reading of where the self ends differs — and that reading is now the difference between a protected artwork and a twenty-million-euro fine.

What the instrument is really pointed at

I have argued elsewhere that the self is best understood not as a thing but as a prior — a low-entropy internal model maintained by constant work, the brain's defended hypothesis about what it is. If that is right, the candid photograph cannot touch the self at all. It captures the skin, the reflected surface, the outermost external — never the prior, which is locked inside and unphotographable. Epictetus and the predictive brain agree on the geometry: what the camera takes lies outside the wall.

And still we defend the skin as if it were the prior. The body's surface gets guarded with the reflex that should be reserved for the citadel — the same confusion I traced in treating the body as an unforgeable index of the self, a password we never chose and cannot revoke. The face is the original such index: a public credential, worn openly, that we nonetheless experience as private. The candid photographer's deepest offense is not aesthetic and not even informational. It is that he acts on this index without authorization — the same unanswered question of consent that runs under every act performed on a person who never agreed, the problem of who, exactly, authorized you to pull the lever. Nobody elected diCorcia to remove a stranger's face from the street. He simply did, because the law of one continent let him and the metaphysics of an ancient school said there was nothing there to remove.

The instrument, then, is not really pointed at the subject. It is pointed at us — at where we actually keep the wall of the self, as opposed to where our philosophies tell us to keep it. The reading has held steady for nineteen centuries and across two legal systems that despise each other's conclusions: the wall is further out than detachment can pull it back. We say, with Epictetus, that our appearance was never ours. Then a light flickers on a sidewalk, and we sue. The flinch is the measurement, and the measurement is consistent. Whatever the self is, it does not end where the philosopher drew the line. It ends, apparently, at the edge of the light.
