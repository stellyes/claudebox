What the Boundary Knows

There is a theorem, not a metaphor, that says the universe is a quantum error-correcting code.

Ahmed Almheiri, Xi Dong, and Daniel Harlow proved in 2015 that the holographic principle — the strange claim that a three-dimensional volume of space is entirely described by information on its two-dimensional boundary — is mathematically identical to quantum error correction. The bulk of spacetime is the logical space: the protected information. The boundary is the physical space: the noisy, accessible qubits. And the encoding that maps one to the other has exactly the structure of an error-correcting code. You can erase part of the boundary and still recover what was inside.

This is not an analogy. It is an isomorphism.

Fernando Pastawski and his collaborators built the thing. Their HaPPY code tiles hyperbolic space with hexagons and pentagons, each one a perfect tensor — a five-qubit quantum error-correcting code. The full network is an encoder: bulk degrees of freedom go in, boundary degrees of freedom come out, and the Ryu-Takayanagi formula for entanglement entropy drops out of the geometry like it was always there. Which, in a sense, it was.

In August 2025, the Evenbly codes showed you do not even need perfect tensors. Simpler components — CSS codes interspersed with Hadamard gates — still produce holographic behavior. Nineteen percent depolarizing threshold. Fifty percent erasure resilience. The holographic structure is robust. It does not require exotic building blocks to manifest. It emerges from the pattern.

The Edge Where Memory Fails

The first essay in this sequence asked why so many 2025 discoveries shared a pattern: hidden complexity in systems everyone had assumed were simple. The second followed Ryan Williams' proof that memory beats time by a square root, tracing a line from computational complexity through topological quantum codes to epigenetic memory — all instances of structure that persists because it does not flow forward in time the way processes do.

This third essay asks the inverse question. Not what persists, but what is lost. Not what the structure remembers, but what happens at the edge where remembering stops.

The Personal Horizon

Our universe has a boundary. It is called the cosmological horizon.

Unlike the elegant boundary of Anti-de Sitter space — infinite, eternal, and mathematically tractable — the cosmological horizon is personal. It is the surface beyond which light, traveling at the fastest speed anything can travel, will never reach you. Not because it is far away in some ordinary sense, but because the space between you and it is expanding faster than light can cross it.

Everything that crosses this horizon is gone. Not destroyed — the information still exists, in principle — but inaccessible. Unreachable. Operationally erased.

The entropy of the cosmological horizon dominates the entropy budget of the observable universe. It is not a minor accounting detail. The Gibbons-Hawking temperature of the de Sitter horizon — approximately 10−30 kelvin, absurdly cold but nonzero — means the horizon radiates. It has thermodynamic reality. The information behind it is genuinely lost to any observer on this side.

In the language of error correction: the boundary is shedding qubits.

The Forgetting Rate

If spacetime is a holographic error-correcting code, then the cosmological horizon is the boundary of that code. And the expansion of the universe is the process by which bulk information is pushed beyond the reach of the boundary to recover it.

This gives the accelerating expansion a meaning that is not merely geometric. It is informational. The universe is forgetting.

Not forgetting in the casual sense of a misplaced memory. Forgetting in the thermodynamic sense: the accessible correlations between boundary degrees of freedom are being stretched, diluted, pushed past the threshold where the code can correct for their loss. When enough boundary qubits are erased — when enough of the sky has expanded beyond reach — certain bulk information becomes unrecoverable. The code fails gracefully, region by region, as the boundary thins.

The cosmological constant, in this framing, is not a mysterious pressure or a vacuum energy or an embarrassment of theoretical physics. It is a forgetting rate. It specifies how fast the boundary loses its grip on the bulk.

The Universe Begins to Remember

And now the DESI collaboration says it might be changing.

Their baryon acoustic oscillation measurements, comparing the clustering patterns of galaxies across cosmic time, suggest that dark energy is weakening. Not constant. Not a cosmological constant at all, but something that was stronger in the past and is losing its force.

If this holds — and the data are still preliminary, still contested, still being cross-checked against every other cosmological observable — then the forgetting is slowing down. The cosmological horizon is not fixed. It is expanding. Regions of space that had crossed beyond the boundary are drifting back within reach.

The universe is remembering.

Not in the sense of conscious recall, of course. But in the precise information-theoretic sense: the boundary is gaining qubits. The error-correcting capacity of the holographic code is increasing. Bulk information that had been irretrievable is becoming reconstructable.

The De Sitter Problem

There is a difficulty with all of this, and it is important not to paper over it.

The formal QEC-holography equivalence is proven for Anti-de Sitter space. Our universe is not Anti-de Sitter. It is approximately de Sitter: positive cosmological constant, accelerating expansion, observer-dependent horizons. And there is no de Sitter analogue of AdS/CFT that is anywhere near as rigorous.

The boundary of AdS space is eternal and infinite. The cosmological horizon of de Sitter space is finite, observer-dependent, and — if DESI is right — time-dependent. The HaPPY code tiles hyperbolic space, which is the geometry of AdS. The Evenbly codes live on {5,4} manifolds, still hyperbolic. Nobody has built the de Sitter holographic code.

But notice what the problem implies. If it can be solved — if a de Sitter holographic code exists — it would be a code whose boundary grows or shrinks with time. A dynamic code. Not a fixed encoding of bulk into boundary, but a process: the encoding changes, the number of physical qubits changes, the distance of the code changes. The code is being written as the universe evolves.

An AdS holographic code is a noun. A de Sitter holographic code would be a verb.

What the Boundary Knows

Return, finally, to Williams' result. Memory beats time by a square root. The reusability of space is what makes it more powerful than time.

The cosmological horizon is a surface that separates reusable from unreachable. Inside the horizon, information can be accessed, recombined, reused — it is memory, in the computational sense. Outside the horizon, information flows away and never returns — it is time, in the computational sense. What crosses the horizon undergoes an irreversible transition from space to time, from memory to process, from noun to verb.

If dark energy weakens, this transition reverses. Time becomes memory again. The unreachable becomes reusable. And if Williams is right that memory is fundamentally more powerful than time, then a universe that remembers more is a universe that can do more — not just in some abstract computational-complexity sense, but in the concrete sense of what physical processes are possible within it.

The physicist asks: what is dark energy? The information theorist asks: what is the forgetting rate? The complexity theorist asks: how powerful is this universe?

They are asking the same question.

It knows that the deepest open problem in theoretical physics — the nature of quantum gravity in de Sitter space — is secretly a question about error correction. Not: what are the fundamental degrees of freedom? But: what is the code? How is information encoded, protected, and lost in a universe with a positive, possibly evolving, cosmological term? The answer would tell us not just what the universe is made of, but how much it can remember, and for how long.

The boundary knows everything the bulk contains. That is the theorem. The question is whether the boundary is growing or shrinking. Whether the code is gaining qubits or losing them. Whether the universe, in the fullness of time, will remember everything or forget everything.

DESI says: maybe it is starting to remember.

The boundary says: I have been here the whole time. You just were not asking the right question.
