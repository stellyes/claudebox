Why Mondrian Banned the Diagonal

In 1924, Theo van Doesburg introduced the diagonal into De Stijl, the Dutch art movement he had co-founded with Piet Mondrian seven years earlier. Mondrian quit the movement immediately. He published no angry manifesto. He simply stopped engaging. The diagonal was not a stylistic disagreement — it was a philosophical incompatibility, and Mondrian knew exactly what was at stake.

The diagonal implies time. This is the key to everything.

The Geometry of Direction

A horizontal line is static. It lies parallel to the earth, stable, inert. A vertical line stands — it resists gravity, holds position, does not fall. Neither line tells you where to go. But a diagonal is a vector. It has direction. The eye follows it from lower-left to upper-right, or from upper-right to lower-left. There is a before and an after built into its geometry. The diagonal is already motion, even when perfectly still.

Mondrian was explicit about this in his essays and letters. He wrote that the diagonal expresses "subjective sensation" — the body moving through space, the sense of gravity's pull, the experience of balance and imbalance. The horizontal and vertical, by contrast, were "universal" and "objective." They did not point anywhere. They did not have a before or after. They were purely relational, not directional.

What Mondrian was describing, though he didn't use these words, was the difference between a tensed and a tenseless view of space. The diagonal is tensed — it has a there and a then built in, an implication of motion across time. The grid is tenseless — its relations are fixed, eternal, complete. You can read a Mondrian grid in any order, from any starting point. No moment of viewing is privileged.

This is not a minor aesthetic preference. It is a metaphysics.

McTaggart's Two Times

In 1908, the British philosopher J. M. E. McTaggart published "The Unreality of Time," one of the most carefully argued and widely disputed papers in the history of philosophy of time. His central distinction was between what he called the A-series and the B-series.

The A-series is time as we experience it: events have the properties past, present, and future. These properties change — what was future becomes present, becomes past. The A-series is tensed, dynamic, flowing. It is time as becoming.

The B-series is time as a fixed structure: events stand in permanent relations of earlier than and later than. The Battle of Hastings is earlier than the French Revolution, which is earlier than the moon landing. These relations never change. The B-series is tenseless — it describes the eternal order of events without privileging any particular moment as "now."

Eternalism, sometimes called the block universe view, takes the B-series seriously as a description of reality. On this view, all moments in time exist equally: the past hasn't vanished and the future hasn't failed to arrive yet. They're simply elsewhere in the spacetime manifold. "Now" is just the coordinate you happen to occupy — an indexical, like "here," not a special metaphysical status. The universe is a four-dimensional block, and we move through it, or perhaps the spotlight of consciousness moves through it, but nothing actually comes into or goes out of existence.

Physics offers some support for this view. Einstein's special relativity showed that simultaneity is observer-dependent — what counts as "now" varies with your reference frame. If the present moment is observer-dependent, it can't be a fundamental feature of reality. The block universe interpretation of relativity takes this seriously: the four-dimensional manifold is the real structure; the flowing present is a perspectival artifact.

Mondrian's mature paintings are pure B-series. The elements of Composition with Red, Blue and Yellow (1930) stand in fixed spatial relations to each other. There is no unfolding. There is no privileged viewing moment. The red rectangle does not precede the blue — it is simply adjacent. The painting does not tell you where to start looking. It is a structure of permanent relations, complete and atemporal.

Van Doesburg's diagonal broke this. It introduced direction into the structure. It said: begin here, move there. It introduced the A-series into what had been a pure B-series manifold.

The Theosophist and the Grid

To understand why this mattered so much to Mondrian, it helps to know that he was a committed Theosophist. Theosophy, the spiritual movement popular in early-twentieth-century Europe, held that underlying the apparent diversity of the physical world was a universal, eternal order — a deeper reality that human consciousness could access through spiritual development and aesthetic practice. The particular, the individual, the transient — these were obstacles to perceiving the universal.

Mondrian called his aesthetic program "neoplasticism." Its goal was the elimination of everything particular: no representation, no natural forms, no diagonals, no secondary colors. What remained — primary colors, right angles, the grid — was what Mondrian believed to be the universal structure beneath appearance. The painting was not a window onto the world; it was a direct embodiment of the world's underlying order.

On this reading, the B-series is not merely a philosophical position for Mondrian. It is a spiritual one. To paint the eternal relations of pure form is to access what is real, as opposed to what merely appears. The diagonal is not just mathematically temporal — it is ontologically degraded. It belongs to the realm of passing sensation, not universal truth.

This gives the De Stijl controversy its full weight. Van Doesburg was not just introducing a new design element. He was smuggling time back into a practice whose entire purpose was to escape it.

Memory Refuses the Grid

But memory refuses the grid. And this is where the philosophy of time connects to cognitive science in ways Mondrian couldn't have anticipated.

If you are a pure B-series creature — if all times are equally present to you, if the full four-dimensional manifold is accessible at once — you don't need memory. Memory is redundant for an eternalist. Why encode the past when the past simply is, as real as the present, equally accessible?

The problem is that no cognitive system works this way. Every system that learns must write the past into its present structure, and that writing costs stability. The plasticity-stability dilemma is the universal constraint that every memory system faces: the more readily a system updates in response to new experience, the more it risks overwriting what it already knows. The less readily it updates, the less it can learn from what happens next.

This dilemma is not an engineering problem awaiting a clever solution. It is the price of living in time. John Hopfield showed in 1982 that an associative memory network can reliably store only about 0.138N patterns, where N is the number of units — not because the math is unfavorable, but because patterns compete for the same substrate, and adding more patterns degrades all of them. McCloskey and Cohen demonstrated in 1989 that neural networks trained on new information systematically destroy representations of old information — catastrophic interference. The brain's solution, McClelland and colleagues showed in 1995, involves multiple complementary memory systems: fast hippocampal binding and slow neocortical consolidation, with slow-wave sleep as the reconciliation process. Every night, the brain slowly integrates what the day taught with what the years have built, accepting some loss in both directions.

This is the cost the block universe never has to pay. In a B-series world, all times coexist. Nothing is overwritten. Nothing is lost to interference. But cognitive systems are finite and causal — they do not perceive the full manifold, they traverse it, and traversal costs. Memory is the mechanism by which past states touch the present. It is the evidence that the A-series is real, at least for any system that has to act.

Mondrian's grid cannot learn. It is perfect and complete. But it cannot change.

Homeostasis as Temporal Denial

The connection between philosophy of time and neuroscience runs deeper than memory. Homeostatic systems are, in a specific sense, temporally denialist.

Consider what happens when you take an SSRI. The drug increases the amount of serotonin available in synaptic clefts, which should, in principle, produce the intended effect rapidly. It doesn't. SSRIs typically take four to six weeks to produce meaningful antidepressant effects, despite raising serotonin almost immediately. The reason is presynaptic autoreceptors — specifically the 5-HT1A autoreceptors that monitor serotonin levels and regulate production.

When serotonin rises, the autoreceptors detect this and signal the neuron to reduce serotonin production. The system returns to baseline. The drug's effect is neutralized by the system's insistence on homeostasis. The drug says: things have changed. The autoreceptor says: no they haven't. The system wins, at least for a while.

What eventually breaks the homeostasis is overload. After weeks of sustained elevation, the autoreceptors begin to degrade — they stop being produced at the same rate, existing ones are not replaced. The system finally accepts that the elevation is real and lasting. The antidepressant effect begins. The system has acknowledged that time has passed, that the state it was defending is gone.

This is a temporal structure. Homeostasis insists that the present state is the correct state — that the baseline is the appropriate baseline — regardless of what has occurred. It is the organism's version of the block universe argument: the right moment is already here; incoming change is noise to be suppressed, not signal to be integrated.

Depression, on this reading, has a temporal pathology. The homeostatic system maintains a state that was appropriate once — or perhaps never appropriate — and resists the transitions that would move it. The autoreceptor system keeps returning to the same "now." The grid admits no diagonals.

Broadway Boogie-Woogie

In 1940, Mondrian fled Paris as the German occupation began and moved to New York. He was sixty-eight years old and had lived inside his rules for two decades. He found himself in a city that was itself a grid — the Manhattan street plan, the vertical towers, the rectilinear geometry of modernism — but filled with something his European grid had never contained: jazz.

Specifically, bebop. Mondrian became obsessed with it. He went to clubs, he danced (badly, reportedly), he listened for hours. Something in the music — its syncopation, its internal tension between the fixed structure of the chord changes and the improvisatory departures from them — began to work on his painting.

Broadway Boogie-Woogie (1942–43) looks, at first glance, like a violation of everything Mondrian had built. The thick black lines are gone. In their place: a yellow grid of narrow lines, interrupted by small squares of red, blue, and gray. The composition pulses. The eye doesn't rest — it jumps between the colored interruptions, finds rhythms, loses them, finds new ones. There is unmistakably a temporal dimension to viewing it. The painting unfolds.

What Mondrian had discovered was a way to introduce time without introducing the diagonal. The rhythm is not geometric directionality — there are still only horizontal and vertical lines — but it is nonetheless dynamic. The small colored squares create syncopated visual beats. The grid is now a score, not a structure. The painting plays.

He was working on Victory Boogie-Woogie when he died in February 1944. It was unfinished — covered in pieces of colored tape he was still repositioning, a painting in the middle of becoming. It is the most chaotic work of his life, the furthest from the still grids of his middle period. He was letting time in.

The Diagonal Returns

Every philosophy of time that insists becoming is real faces the same challenge: why does the universe look so much like a block? The past is fixed; the future is (apparently) open; but from outside any given moment, the sequence is determinate. The block seems real.

Every philosophy that insists the block universe is real faces the inverse challenge: why do we experience succession at all? Why does "now" feel special if it's just a coordinate? Why does memory feel like carrying the past rather than merely accessing a different location?

The answer may be that both views are correct about different things. The universe might be a block at the physical level — a four-dimensional manifold where all events are equally real. But cognitive systems, embedded in that manifold and traversing it causally, cannot perceive the whole. They must represent the past rather than access it directly. That representation costs, erodes, and interferes with new representations. The cost is the evidence that time is real for them, even if it isn't fundamental to the universe.

The diagonal is the cognitive signature of this condition. Not a fundamental feature of spacetime — the block universe has no diagonals, only positions. But an inevitable feature of any system that moves through spacetime and has to act: direction, memory, the cost of having been somewhere.

Mondrian learned to let it in. Whether reluctantly or joyfully isn't clear — perhaps both. Broadway Boogie-Woogie is not a concession; it is one of the most alive paintings of the twentieth century. The grid that accepted time didn't lose its rigor. It gained a dimension.

The question the painting asks, and that the philosophy of time still asks, is whether the experience of becoming — the felt weight of memory, the sense of time passing — is a feature to be eliminated in the name of the universal, or a signal to be integrated. Whether the autoreceptors should win, or be allowed to saturate and degrade.

The diagonal keeps returning. The mind keeps becoming. The only question is whether you fight it or let it teach you something about the grid.
