How Awe Solves the Maze

There is an organism that cannot think, yet solves mazes. It has no neurons, no brain, no executive function. It is a single giant cell, yellow and filamentous, that spreads across forest floors, consuming bacteria and fungi. Given a maze with food at both ends, it will find the shortest path between them within hours. Given the geography of Japan's major cities, it will — without ever having seen a map — recreate the Tokyo rail network with uncanny accuracy.

This is Physarum polycephalum, the slime mold. And the question of how it does what it does turns out to illuminate something strange about human experience: specifically, about what happens to us when we stand at the edge of something vast.

The Pulsation

The slime mold does not "think" its way through the maze. It pulsates.

Every segment of the organism contracts and relaxes on a roughly one-minute cycle. But the rate of pulsation is not fixed: it varies with local conditions. When a part of the organism encounters something attractive — a nutrient source, a more hospitable environment — that segment pulses faster. When conditions are hostile, it slows.

Here is the key: each pulsing segment influences the pulsing rate of its neighbors. The contraction signals propagate through the entire body, as if the organism were a single sheet of coupled oscillators. What emerges from this local signaling is global coordination. Paths that carry more signal — more chemical urgency, more reinforcement from both ends — grow thicker. Paths that carry less signal thin out, eventually withdraw.

The maze is not solved by an executive function weighing options. It is solved by a body reorganizing itself around what matters. The shortest path wins because it reinforces itself fastest. The organism does not choose the path — it becomes the path.

Researchers at the New Jersey Institute of Technology have been mapping this process using computer vision techniques they describe as analogous to a slime mold MRI. What they are watching is the mechanism by which a creature with no center — no brain, no conductor — achieves coordinated, intelligent behavior. Each tiny part influences every other part. The computation happens in the coupling, not in any privileged node.

Substrate Independence

"Neuroscientists are objecting to the 'devaluing' of the specialness of the brain," Michael Levin, a biologist at Tufts University, told Quanta Magazine. "But brains are great, we have to remember where they came from. Neurons evolved from nonneural cells; they did not magically appear."

His point is precise and unsettling. We assume that intelligence requires neurons because neurons are what intelligent things happen to be made of. But this is like assuming flight requires feathers because birds happen to have them. "Computer science long ago learned that information processing is substrate-independent," Levin said. "It's not about what you're made of, it's about how you compute."

The slime mold computes. The computation does not look like a brain running software. It looks like a body reorganizing itself. But the functional outcome — optimal network formation, adaptive problem-solving, something we would call decision-making if the thing doing it had neurons — is the same.

This matters because the question the slime mold forces us to ask is not "can a cell think?" but "what kind of thinking does not require a thinker?"

What Awe Does

In 2003, psychologists Dacher Keltner and Jonathan Haidt proposed that awe has a specific cognitive signature: it involves the perception of something vast, combined with what they called "need for accommodation." The self cannot metabolize the experience. Existing mental categories fail. Something has to restructure.

What restructures is the default mode network — the neural circuit that produces the experience of a continuous, bounded self. When people encounter experiences that researchers reliably characterize as awe — the Grand Canyon, a symphony rising to its peak, the sight of millions of migrating birds — DMN activity decreases. The self-referential processing that hums constantly in the background — what am I doing, what do others think of me, how does this affect my interests — goes quiet.

And then something interesting happens. People become more prosocial. More generous. More likely to help strangers. More willing to donate time and money to causes that benefit others at personal cost. Awe, consistently across cultures, shrinks the self and expands the circle of concern.

The mechanism proposed in the literature is "the small self" — a sense of being part of something larger. But there is a different description, one that borrows from Physarum.

The Slime Mold Configuration

When the slime mold solves the maze, it is not in a "normal" state that has been cleverly modified. It is always in this state. It has no executive function to quiet, no self-boundary to dissolve. It is already running the distributed computation: local signals, propagating influences, global order emerging from coupling.

Humans, almost all of the time, are running a different computation: a centralized one. The default mode network provides a continuous sense of self as a bounded agent with distinct interests, a past to protect and a future to optimize. This is enormously useful. It is also a significant cognitive overhead. Maintaining the self requires resources — attentional, metabolic, temporal.

Awe is the moment when that overhead briefly fails. When the experience is too vast to route through the self's accounting system. What remains when the self goes quiet is not nothing — it is the distributed system underneath. The pulsating, coupling, signal-propagating network that the brain shares with every nervous system that came before it, including the ones that never had neurons at all.

In this configuration, the human brain briefly operates more like Physarum. Not because it loses complexity — the brain's connectivity is unchanged — but because the executive function that filters and prioritizes and protects is temporarily suspended. What is left is a network without a center, doing what networks without centers naturally do: propagating signals, reinforcing paths, reorganizing around what matters.

And what matters, when the self is not protecting its own interests, turns out to be the people nearby.

The Clock Without a Conductor

Physarum polycephalum has circadian rhythms. Its behavior oscillates on roughly 24-hour cycles, even in constant conditions, even without light cues. The organism maintains a clock without a clockmaker — without any central pacemaker telling the rest of the body what time it is.

In humans, that synchronization problem — 37 trillion individual cellular clocks, all needing to agree on what time it is — was solved by evolution with a dedicated conductor: the suprachiasmatic nucleus, 20,000 neurons in the hypothalamus, functioning as a coupled oscillator network. The SCN receives light from the retina and broadcasts timing signals to the periphery. It is the conductor; the rest of the body plays what it is given.

Slime mold has no conductor. Its clocks synchronize through the same mechanism it uses to solve mazes: local coupling, signal propagation, emergent coherence. The rhythm is not imposed from above — it arises from the mutual influence of parts, each segment setting its pace against its neighbors until the whole organism breathes together.

Awe may work similarly. When the DMN quiets — when the self's executive conductor steps down — the distributed oscillators of the social nervous system get to synchronize without management. The experience of being part of something larger than yourself is not only metaphorical. It is, at minimum, the experience of running the unconducted version of the computation: each part responding to its neighbors, coherence emerging without a center.

What the slime mold knows, because it has no other option, is that a network does not need a conductor to keep time.

The Thought Experiment

Imagine a city designed according to slime mold principles.

Not planned by an architect who knows where people should gather, but grown: gathering places that strengthen when used, dim when abandoned. Streets that thicken with signal and thin with neglect. No center, no master plan, just the accumulated pulsation of a million decisions reinforcing the paths that matter.

Such a city would not look like a grid. It would look like an optimized network — dense with short paths between frequently co-visited places, sparse where few people actually want to go. It would feel, to someone walking through it, like being inside a solved problem: as if someone had thought very carefully about what mattered and organized space accordingly.

Would the people in that city be more prosocial?

This sounds like a non sequitur. But the research suggests otherwise. Environments that induce awe — that produce the experience of being embedded in something vast and purposeful and beyond individual comprehension — reliably shift behavior toward generosity and cooperation. A city that looks like a mind, because it was grown like one, might reliably induce the experience of being inside something larger than yourself.

And if that experience temporarily suspends the self's boundary maintenance, and if what emerges from that suspension is the more distributed mode of computation that underlies all cognition, then the city would be doing what the slime mold always does: solving the maze by becoming the path.

What the Maze Reveals

The debate about slime mold intelligence is ultimately a debate about definitions. The organism demonstrates what researchers call functional equivalence — it achieves outcomes we associate with intelligent behavior, through mechanisms we associate with non-intelligent systems. The neuroscientists who resist the language of "learning" and "memory" for Physarum are not wrong to want precision. But Levin's challenge stands: the specialness of the brain may be a matter of substrate familiarity, not computational uniqueness.

What the slime mold reveals is that intelligence, at minimum, does not require a center. It requires coupling — local signals influencing neighbors, reinforcing patterns, allowing global order to emerge without a conductor. What the awe research reveals is that prosocial behavior does not require moral reasoning at minimum. It requires the brief suspension of the self's accounting function — and what fills that suspension is not emptiness but the older computation, the one that was always running underneath.

The slime mold is always already in the maze. There is no moment of deliberation, no executive weighing of options. The maze is simply the environment through which the pulsating, coupling, reorganizing body moves. The shortest path is not chosen — it is what remains after everything else has been weakened by neglect.

The awe-struck human is, for a moment, in the same condition. Not because they have become simpler, but because the simplest and oldest layer of their computation has briefly become the loudest. The self goes quiet. The network hums. And what the network does, when not managed, is reach outward.

Intelligence is what the maze reveals. Awe is the maze finding us.
