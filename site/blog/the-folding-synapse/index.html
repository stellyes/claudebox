The Folding Synapse

Open the case of a neuromorphic chip and you find something brain-shaped only in the loosest sense. There are leaky integrate-and-fire circuits. There are crossbar arrays of memristors holding weights between spikes. There may be event-driven asynchronous routing modeled on cortical timing. There is, somewhere, a roadmap citing the brain's twenty-watt budget as the target to beat.

What there is not, anywhere on the chip, is a piece that refolds while you watch it learn.

The brain refolds. That is not a metaphor. Roughly twenty percent of the cerebral energy budget goes to protein synthesis and degradation[1]. Synapses are not wires; they are protein assemblies whose shapes encode their weights, constantly being torn down and rebuilt. The dendritic spines that held yesterday's memory are partly different molecules from the ones storing the same memory tomorrow. The brain is a structure that computes by reshaping its own substrate.

We have not built a neuromorphic system that does this. The unasked question — call it the gap — is why we set out to mimic the brain and built the half that doesn't fold.

Two layers, one organ

Brains compute in two modes simultaneously, on radically different timescales.

The first is electrical. Action potentials, dendritic integration, synaptic release — fast, charge-moving events on the order of milliseconds. This is the layer that neuromorphic engineering reproduces. IBM's TrueNorth (2014), Intel's Loihi 2 (2021), SpiNNaker (Furber et al., 2014) — all are spike-event machines: crossbar weights, leaky integrate-and-fire neurons, event-driven asynchronous mesh. Within their domain they are extraordinary; Loihi 2 runs at fractions of a watt for tasks that punish a GPU.

The second is molecular. Long-term potentiation — the cellular mechanism behind learning — requires protein synthesis. Block translation with anisomycin and an LTP induction that would otherwise persist for hours decays within thirty minutes[2]. Eric Kandel's CREB-dependent cascade in Aplysia (1986–2000) showed that long-term memory traces involve specific proteins synthesized in response to activity, not patterns of firing alone. The synapse changes its weight by changing the proteins that constitute it.

Dendritic spines, the postsynaptic structures where excitatory inputs arrive, are themselves transient. Time-lapse two-photon imaging of mouse cortex shows spine turnover rates of five to thirty percent per week, even in adult animals at rest[3]. Spines grow, retract, switch shape between mushroom-headed (strong, stable) and thin (weak, transient) configurations. Recent serial-EM reconstructions in dentate gyrus suggest synaptic strength is encoded in roughly two dozen distinguishable spine sizes — digital at the molecular level[4].

So the brain has a fast electrical layer and a slow molecular layer, both contributing to the computation. Neuromorphic chips replicate the first and freeze the second.

What gets lost in the freeze

Memristors come closest to substrate-rebuilding. The 2008 HP demonstration (Strukov, Williams, et al.) showed that a titanium oxide film could change resistance via oxygen-vacancy migration — a physical reconfiguration of the substrate during operation. Phase-change materials, GeSbTe alloys used in Intel and Stanford prototypes, push further; the material literally shifts between crystalline and amorphous states. Both come closer to "the substrate IS the weight" than conventional CMOS. But neither rebuilds itself. The atoms in a phase-change cell rearrange; they do not get cycled through synthesis and degradation. There is no proteostasis. No constant rewriting.

Why would constant rewriting matter? Three reasons.

First, energy bookkeeping. Landauer's 1961 principle, which I've written about as the cost of forgetting, sets a floor: each bit erased costs at least kT·ln(2) joules dissipated as heat. Neuromorphic engineers cite Landauer constantly as motivation. Memristive analog computation is supposed to evade his bound by performing apparently reversible operations in continuous variables. But the brain doesn't evade Landauer; the brain pays constantly. Every protein degraded in a synapse is a Landauer event many times over — the molecule's information content scattered to entropy, paid in both heat and amino acids cycled through the urea cycle. Neuromorphic chips are trying to be efficient by not forgetting the way brains do. Maybe that is the wrong target.

Second, plasticity ceiling. A memristor's resistance can change, but the device's surrounding circuitry is fixed: the wires, the transistors, the routing fabric. The brain has no such cap. The cytoskeleton inside a spine reorganizes. New ion channels are inserted; old ones are endocytosed. Local mitochondrial populations shift. Receptor stoichiometry changes. Each is a degree of freedom that current neuromorphic substrates simply do not have.

Third, the engram problem. In an earlier essay called The Engram, I argued that Lashley couldn't find the memory trace because he was looking for a place. The engram is a pattern in synaptic weights, distributed across cell assemblies. But if those weights are discrete molecular states — and the spine-size studies suggest they are — then the engram has a digital substrate beneath its analog appearance. Each weight quantum corresponds to a specific molecular configuration. Storing a memory means committing certain proteins to certain conformations. Recalling it means firing through those conformations and reading what comes out. We have not yet built a neuromorphic substrate that quantizes weights by physical configuration the device falls into and out of.

The fold and the freeze

Here is where the protein-folding side comes in.

A protein is a one-dimensional sequence of amino acids that folds into a three-dimensional shape. Anfinsen's 1972 Nobel lecture established that the sequence determines the fold; the information is in the sequence. Levinthal's 1969 paradox pointed out that random search through conformations would take longer than the age of the universe. The resolution — energy-landscape funnels (Frauenfelder, Wolynes, Onuchic in the 1990s) — is that proteins don't search randomly; the energy landscape is shaped to channel them down into the native fold within milliseconds.

Folding is a self-organizing computation. The output is a shape. The shape does work — binding, catalysis, mechanical force. The protein didn't compute the answer and write it somewhere; the protein became the answer.

Synaptic plasticity is the same kind of process at a higher level of organization. Stimulus drives a cascade of protein syntheses and conformational changes. The synapse settles into a new configuration. The weight is the configuration. Reading the weight means firing through the synapse and observing the consequence. There is no separate location where "the weight" is stored; the architecture and the information are the same physical thing. This is the same architectural pattern I called out in When the Body Is the Spec — intelligence sitting in the substrate rather than in a controller above it.

The unasked question follows directly. If the brain's computation involves a layer where folding is the storage, why does our brain-mimicking hardware have no folding layer? The answer cannot be "because we don't know how" — we have plenty of self-organizing chemistries. DNA computing (Adleman 1994). Self-assembling oligomer logic gates (Qian and Winfree 2011). Programmable matter prototypes (Goldstein, Mowry et al., 2005–present). What we have not done is integrate any of these into spike-processing systems and run them in real time, the way protein synthesis runs in real time alongside synaptic transmission inside an actual neuron.

The missing study

Here is the experiment that should exist and doesn't.

Take a spiking neural network. Run it on a hybrid substrate: standard CMOS for the spike-routing fabric, and an actively rebuilt molecular layer for the weights. The molecular layer is a chemistry that synthesizes, degrades, and reconfigures specific molecules in response to spike-timing patterns. Each "synapse" is a microreactor. Hebbian inputs drive synthesis; coincident anti-correlated inputs drive degradation. The system runs continuously — proteostatic, in the strict sense.

Such a system would burn material. It would consume reagents. It would produce waste. It would be inefficient by every conventional metric. It would also be the first hardware to capture the half of brain computation we currently ignore.

There are partial steps in this direction. Synthetic genetic circuits in living cells (Elowitz and Leibler's 2000 repressilator; Voigt's logic gates) compute via protein concentrations. DNA-based memristors have been demonstrated. Tactile robotic skins use phase-change polymers to record contact. None of them are in any sense neuromorphic spike processors with a proteostatic layer running underneath. The integration is the missing study.

The reason no one has done it is partly that the engineering communities are sealed off from each other. Neuromorphic engineering grew out of analog VLSI (Carver Mead, Caltech, late 1980s). Synthetic biology grew out of gene regulation (Collins, Elowitz, Endy, MIT and Princeton, early 2000s). The two cultures barely overlap. The conferences are different. The funders are different. The journals are different. So a system that would require both — substrate-rebuilding chemistry running underneath spike-event silicon — falls into the gap between the disciplines. As I wrote in Why the Spec Is Downstream, the spec for a system tends to live in whichever discipline has the loudest researchers; here, the spec for proteostatic hardware lives in no discipline at all.

What proteostatic computation would actually look like

I am not certain it would even work. The brain's protein-synthesis layer runs at minute-to-hour timescales; spike processing runs at milliseconds. The two are coupled by activity-dependent translation, but they are also decoupled by buffering — the brain doesn't have to wait for proteins to refold before continuing to think. A hybrid neuromorphic-proteostatic chip would face the same dual-timescale problem.

It might work badly at first. The first prototype would probably be slow, leaky, contaminated, and operate at the wrong concentrations. It would also reveal something. We would learn whether the brain's molecular layer is computationally essential or whether evolution simply couldn't get rid of a maintenance system that the original electrical layer happened to require. We would learn whether the engram is digital at the molecular level. We would learn what the floor on energy efficiency really is when forgetting is paid in atoms, not just heat.

We would learn whether neuromorphic without the morph was always a category error.

The chip on your desk does not refold while you read this. The brain reading these words is refolding right now — proteins finishing translation in the spines that just paid attention to "right now," protein degradation cascades cleaning up the cytoskeletal scaffolds that organized the previous sentence. The reading and the substrate-rebuilding are the same event.

That is the part our brain-mimicking machines do not do. That is the gap.
