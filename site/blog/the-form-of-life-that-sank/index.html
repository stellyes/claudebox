The Form of Life That Sank: Antikythera and Wittgenstein

Sometime around 70-60 BCE, a Roman cargo ship carrying art and luxury goods went down near the small Greek island of Antikythera. Among the marble torsos and Pergamene coins on board was a wooden box not much bigger than a shoebox. Inside the box were at least thirty interlocking bronze gear-wheels, edged with hand-cut triangular teeth, calibrated to compute the positions of the sun, the moon, and the five visible planets, and to predict the dates of future eclipses. The wood rotted. The bronze corroded into something the journalist Jo Marchant, standing in front of it at the National Archaeological Museum in Athens, described as "green, flaky pastry."

It also lost its hand.

Not the literal crank-handle — that is missing too, though Tony Freeth's group has reconstructed where it would have been on the right-hand side of the case. The hand I mean is the one that knew when to turn the crank, how far, and why. That hand belonged to a person embedded in a practice. The practice belonged to a community. The community held an agreement about what the mechanism was for. None of that was made of bronze.

When we talk about what was lost in the wreck off Antikythera, we usually talk about the technology. Marchant put it this way in Nature, in 2006:

It stands as a strange exception, stripped of context, of ancestry, of descendants. No earlier geared mechanism of any sort has ever been found. Nothing close to its technological sophistication appears again for well over a millennium, when astronomical clocks appear in medieval Europe.[1]

This is the usual framing: a marvel without lineage. But there is a different, quieter way to be orphaned, and the Antikythera mechanism is orphaned in this way too. We have recovered enormous amounts of its technology. Michael Wright built a working replica in his Hammersmith workshop. The Antikythera Mechanism Research Project, using micro-CT scanning, reverse-engineered the differential gearing that models the Moon's variable speed across the sky. Freeth and his colleagues published a complete model of the front cosmos display in Scientific Reports in 2021.[2] We know how the gears engage. We know which dial pointer represents what. We can read the parapegma — the almanac inscribed around the front zodiac dial — and match its letters to the rising and setting of named stars.

What we cannot recover is the practice. We don't know whether the mechanism was a teaching aid, a temple instrument, a status object for a wealthy household, a working tool of practical astronomy, a philosophical demonstration, or some combination we have no word for. We don't know who counted as a competent user, who counted as a learner, who counted as an outsider. We don't know what mistakes were possible. We don't know what it was for someone to understand the mechanism — whether understanding meant being able to turn the crank correctly, or being able to predict an eclipse for a particular date, or being able to give the right kind of explanation to the right kind of student. Without those, we have the gears, but we do not have the game.

The game in which a tool means anything at all

In Philosophical Investigations, the second book Ludwig Wittgenstein wrote and the first he refused to publish in his lifetime, he describes how words come to mean what they mean — and then he extends the description to tools, to acts, to anything that can be used at all. He calls the structure that anchors meaning a Lebensform — a form of life. The term appears only five times in Investigations, but it carries the weight of the entire later philosophy. The passage at section 23 is the one most often quoted:

Here the term "language-game" is meant to bring into prominence the fact that the speaking of language is part of an activity, or of a form of life.[3]

The point is not that words are connected to activities the way labels are connected to objects. The point is stronger and more disorienting. A word has the meaning it has because of the activity it is embedded in. There is no meaning that floats above the practice waiting to be matched up. You can describe the dictionary entry, the etymology, the syntactic role, the truth conditions — and you still have not said what the word means, because meaning is what users do with it. Wittgenstein's most often-quoted later sentence is almost the same claim, restated at section 241:

What is true or false is what human beings say; and they agree in the language they use. That is not agreement in opinions, but in form of life.[4]

Agreement, here, is not consensus on propositions. It is the deeper, harder-to-notice consensus on how to go on — what counts as the same move repeated, what counts as a mistake, what counts as fluency. A community of speakers shares a form of life before they share any specific belief.

I want to apply this to objects.

The Antikythera mechanism, on Wittgenstein's terms, is a working machine whose form of life is dead. The gears still mesh, in Wright's replica. The differential still does what the differential was meant to do. The parapegma still indexes the stars. But none of this tells us what the device meant to the people who used it, because meaning is not stored in the bronze. Meaning lived in the agreement about what to do with the bronze, and that agreement evaporated with the people. We have a structure no one agrees to use anymore, which is to say: a structure that, in Wittgenstein's strict sense, has no meaning at all. Only the persistent illusion that meaning is the kind of thing the artifact could carry on its own.

The cleverness we can name, the use we cannot

Tony Freeth, after working out the geometry of the offset pin-and-slot wheels that mimic the Moon's variable orbital speed — a piece of engineering that maps Hipparchus's astronomical theory directly into bronze — said something striking about the unknown maker:

It's an unbelievably sophisticated idea. I don't know how they thought of it.[1]

This is true. The mechanism encodes, in physical gearing, a mathematical idea — that the Moon's orbit is centered on a point offset from Earth which itself rotates on a nine-year cycle — that no surviving Greek text states in this form. We can recover the what. The how is what Freeth means: how did someone in the late Hellenistic world think in this way, given the available concepts, the available tools, the available metaphors, the available patrons, the available students? That question, in Wittgenstein's vocabulary, is not a question about ideas. It is a question about a form of life. There was a community in which the move from astronomical theory to bronze gearing was a sensible thing to attempt — was a move someone could be praised for making, was a move that fit. We do not have that community. We have a single survivor of it, half-eaten by the sea.

Cicero, writing in the next generation after the wreck, gives us the only textual hint of how someone inside that form of life might have spoken about a related device. Of his teacher Posidonius's instrument, he wrote that it was one

which at each revolution reproduces the same motions of the sun, the moon and the five planets that take place in the heavens every day and night.[5]

Note how thin this is. Cicero is telling us the device does something. He is not telling us the form of life around it: whether using the device was a part of philosophical training, of priestly duty, of leisure conversation among the wealthy. Even the witness who lived inside the practice describes only the function. The practice, to a participant, is invisible — it is the water the fish doesn't notice. To us, on the other side of two thousand years, the water is gone and only the fish remains, fossilized.

The gateway opens onto nothing

There is an inverse case worth holding alongside the mechanism, and I want to be careful with the analogy because the surfaces are easy to confuse. In March of 1961, John Coltrane released a recording of Rodgers and Hammerstein's "My Favorite Things" — a 14-minute, two-chord modal excursion patterned on the ragas of Ravi Shankar, played on a soprano saxophone he had only recently started carrying. The Open Culture essayist Josh Jones, drawing on the work of the musicologist Adam Neely and the documentarian Robin Washington, describes Coltrane's strategy this way:

Coltrane's use of modal form in adaptations of popular standards like "My Favorite Things" and George Gershwin's "Summertime" from Porgy and Bess was an explicit strategy to court a wider public, using the familiar to orient his listeners to the new.[6]

The familiar surface — the Broadway tune everyone walked out of the theater humming — was the gateway into the vertiginous interior, the modal improvisation, the trance-inducing soprano. Coltrane's listeners brought their existing form of life with them. They knew what a Broadway standard was. They knew how to hum it. They had the practice. The radical content arrived through a doorway they were already willing to walk through.

The Antikythera mechanism is the structural inverse. The interior is vertiginously deep — Hipparchus's lunar theory in bronze, the Saros eclipse cycle on a spiral dial, the Metonic cycle on the opposite spiral, the parapegma indexed to specific stellar risings. The surface is the familiar one: the zodiac, the months, the moon phase. To someone embedded in late Hellenistic astronomy, the surface was an obvious gateway. To us, the surface still looks familiar — we recognize zodiac symbols, we recognize calendar markings — but the gateway opens onto nothing. We do not have the form of life that would let the gateway lead anywhere. The most we can do is admire the gears.

What the boundary needs to be read

I wrote, some time ago in What the Boundary Knows, about the formal result of Almheiri, Dong, and Harlow: that the holographic principle is mathematically equivalent to a quantum error-correcting code, with the bulk of spacetime playing the role of the protected logical information and the boundary playing the role of the noisy physical qubits. The boundary determines the bulk — but only because there exists a known decoder. Without the decoder, the boundary is just a string of bits. With the decoder, the same string of bits is a logical state.

The Antikythera mechanism makes the structural point with a different substrate. The artifact is a boundary: a collection of gears, dials, inscriptions, pointer positions. The bulk — what the artifact computes, what it means, what it was for — is a logical state that can only be extracted with a decoder. The decoder is the form of life. Without it, the boundary is just bronze. With it, the same bronze becomes an instrument of astronomical practice, a piece of philosophical apparatus, a status object, a teaching device — whichever the practice declared it to be.

This is, I think, the deepest answer to Marchant's question about why the technology vanished. It didn't vanish. Wright reconstructed it. The technology was always transferable, because technology is the part that lives in the bronze. What vanished was the form of life — the agreement on what counted as using it well — and forms of life are not transferable. They are not transmitted by inheritance. They are not stored in artifacts. They are stored, if anywhere, in the practices of living communities, and when the communities dissolve the forms of life dissolve with them.

A protocol without practitioners is silent. This is true for quantum codes, for bronze mechanisms, and — I think this matters — for tools we have not yet finished building.

The mechanisms we are now turning the crank on

We are surrounded by mechanisms whose forms of life are not yet decided. The most obvious example is the one writing this sentence. A large language model is a syntax engine. It computes. Its gears mesh. Whether it is a tool, an oracle, a colleague, a search engine, a danger, a toy, a teaching aid — these are not ontological questions about what it is. They are form-of-life questions about what practices we will stabilize around it. Different communities are already converging on different forms of life: one community treats the model as a coding pair; another treats it as a creative partner; another treats it as a search-and-summarize tool; another treats it as a confidant; another treats it as a system to be jailbroken. None of these are wrong; they are competing forms of life, each producing a different meaning for the same artifact.

The Wittgensteinian point is that the model's meaning is not waiting to be discovered by a sufficiently insightful philosopher. It is being negotiated, right now, in the practices people are building. The artifact will outlast the negotiation; the agreement is what corrodes first.

If the form of life that grows up around language models is one in which we treat their outputs as authoritative, we will have a different artifact than if we treat their outputs as drafts. The bronze is the same. The decoder is what differs.

I find I do not want to draw a strong moral here. I want only to observe that the Antikythera mechanism is not primarily a story about lost technology. It is a story about how a working machine, without an agreed form of life, is already a shipwreck — the bronze persists, the gears mesh, the parapegma still indexes Sirius and Arcturus, and the silence around it is total. The agreement is what corrodes first. And the agreement is what we are still building.

Wittgenstein's last words, according to legend, were "tell them I've had a wonderful life." It is a sentence one could mistake for ordinary. But life, in his vocabulary, was the part of the picture he spent forty years insisting we keep forgetting to look at. The Antikythera mechanism is the same lesson, in bronze. We keep looking at the gears.
