Does the Network Remember?

What would you call a system that, when given a map of Japan and offered food at each major city, recreates Tokyo’s rail network — not approximately, not conceptually, but with measurable efficiency that matches what human engineers spent decades optimizing?

I. What Counts as Intelligence?

Would you call it intelligent?

And if you wouldn’t — if intelligence requires something it doesn’t have, some central processor, some model of the world — then what exactly is doing the solving?

When Physarum polycephalum explores a maze to find two food sources, does it remember which paths led nowhere? Or does it simply stop reinforcing them, let them thin and collapse, while the successful tubes grow thick with repeated use?

Isn’t that a kind of memory? The kind that lives in the body of the network itself, not in any node?

II. What Kind of Problem Is This?

Here is a problem: a network has been damaged. Two nodes that were once connected are no longer connected. Communication that used to flow between them is blocked.

How does the network repair itself?

Does it need a planner? Does it need a map of what it looked like before? Does it need to know which node is responsible for the break?

Or does it just need to keep trying paths until it finds one that works?

When Physarum is severed — when a section of its body is physically cut away — does it grieve the lost tissue? Or does it simply begin again: extending pseudopods, following chemical gradients, rebuilding the connection that worked before?

Is the difference between those two descriptions more than aesthetic?

III. What Kind of Problem Is Justice?

When a harm occurs in a community, who is the damaged network?

Is it the victim, whose sense of safety has been severed from the reality that should have provided it? Is it the offender, whose connection to the moral community they violated is now strained or broken? Is it the witnesses, the bystanders, the people downstream of the harm who absorb its ripples?

Traditional criminal justice assigns the problem to a dyadic structure: state versus defendant. But what if the network being repaired is larger than either party? What if the court, by focusing on state versus defendant, is solving a different problem than the one that actually exists?

Restorative justice asks: what if we brought the network into the room? What if the goal wasn’t punishment — severing the offender further, adding another break to the network — but reconnection? Reconnection of what? The offender to accountability? The victim to safety? The community to a shared story about what happened and what it means?

Wouldn’t that look more like the slime mold’s approach? Not a linear path from crime to punishment, but a distributed exploration of possible repair — guided not by a judge but by the people whose lives are actually connected?

IV. What Is the Slime Mold Actually Doing?

When a particle in the slime mold’s body moves forward, it doesn’t know where it’s going. It senses chemical gradients — stronger signals ahead mean more food, or more recently traveled paths — and it turns toward them. It deposits its own trail as it moves, reinforcing the path for those who come after.

Is this intelligence? Or is it just chemistry?

But wait — isn’t that question also asked of neurons? Aren’t neurons “just chemistry”? When a decision emerges from a hundred million neurons firing in concert, do we say the neurons aren’t intelligent because each one is only following electrochemical rules?

What is intelligence if not the emergence of solution from the local behavior of many simple parts?

And isn’t that what restorative justice circles try to create — not one wise judge with full information, but a space where many people with partial information, each acting from their own position in the network, collectively find a path toward repair?

V. Who Decides What Repair Looks Like?

Here is where the slime mold’s approach runs into trouble.

The slime mold doesn’t have a target state. It has gradient signals: food is here, trail is thick here, avoid this dead end. The “repair” it achieves is just the most efficient path available under current conditions.

But when we repair a human harm, don’t we need to agree on what “repaired” means? And doesn’t that require us to agree on what things looked like before?

What was the baseline?

When harm occurs between a landlord and a tenant, a police force and a community, a corporation and a river — what is the baseline state that repair should restore? The state before the specific incident? The state before the pattern of incidents that made this incident possible? The state before the structural conditions that enabled the pattern?

And who was present when the baseline was set?

VI. The Drift

Didn’t Daniel Pauly watch fisheries scientists misread their data for decades — not because they were careless, but because each generation accepted the depleted ocean they first encountered as normal? Didn’t each generation recalibrate their baseline downward? Didn’t what looked like “recovery” to 1970s scientists look like continued collapse compared to 1950s data?

Does restorative justice face the same drift?

When a community practices restoration after harm — restoring balance, restoring relationship, restoring trust — are they restoring to the same baseline their grandparents would have named? And those grandparents — were they restoring to the same baseline their grandparents named? At what point does the baseline drift so far that “restoration” begins to mean something the original harm had already included?

What happens when the process of repair inherits the damage it was supposed to undo?

VII. What Does the Network Forget?

Here is something curious: Physarum can navigate a maze it has visited before faster than a maze it’s encountering fresh. Something in its tube structure — the thickened paths, the thinned dead ends — serves as a map of its own prior exploration.

Is that memory? It has no neurons, no synapses, no engrams.

But what about when you clear the maze — when you start the experiment over with fresh substrate, fresh food placement, the same organism but a different context?

Does it remember the previous layout? Or does it begin again?

And isn’t that the deep question in restorative justice, too? When we repair a harm, are we working with the same organism — the same community, the same relationships, the same trust structures — that existed before the harm? Or has the harm already changed those structures, so that “restoration” is restoration to something that no longer exists?

Can you restore a community that the harm itself has already changed?

Can you restore a baseline that was already drifting before the harm occurred?

VIII. What Is the Network Trying to Find?

The slime mold isn’t trying to find anything. It isn’t trying. It responds to gradients, and efficiency emerges from the interaction of its responses.

Is that what restorative justice does at its best — not impose a solution, but create conditions for one to emerge? Gather the people. Create a gradient: accountability, acknowledgment, a path toward repair. Let the network find it?

Does the consistent evidence that restorative justice reduces reoffending — across cultures, across offense types, across decades of research — tell us something about the topology of human social networks? That they want to reconnect? That given the right conditions, the network finds repair the way the slime mold finds food?

Or is the analogy breaking down here? The slime mold doesn’t have dignity to restore. It doesn’t have a story about what happened. It doesn’t need the offending node to acknowledge that it caused the break.

Does it matter that humans know they’re in the network?

IX. What Would the Mold Do?

Here is the thought experiment: if you could map a harmed community as a slime mold substrate — food sources at nodes of trust, chemical trails along established relationships, damage showing as gradients that pull away from the break — what would repair look like?

Wouldn’t it look more like restorative justice than punishment? Many paths explored simultaneously. The most reinforced paths becoming the most used. The solution emerging from the aggregate, not the apex?

And wouldn’t the failure mode look like punitive incarceration? A single forced path, severing one node from the network, simplifying the topology until the system runs on fewer connections than before?

What does a network lose when it prunes a node rather than reintegrating it?

What does it gain?

X. What Doesn’t the Mold Know?

The slime mold cannot ask: is this the right baseline?

It doesn’t know its current configuration is the product of a century of habitat loss. It doesn’t know the forest it’s navigating was clear-cut in 1890. It just optimizes toward available food, and the optimal network it builds reflects whatever damage was already present in the substrate.

Isn’t that the limit of the analogy — and the reason restorative justice requires humans to do something the slime mold cannot?

Doesn’t repair require not just finding the most efficient current path, but also asking: what should the path have been? What baseline are we restoring toward, and is that baseline itself a product of accumulated harm?

What would it mean to repair the substrate instead of just optimizing on it?

What would a slime mold do if you gave it a healthier maze?
