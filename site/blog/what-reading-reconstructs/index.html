What Reading Reconstructs

The text you are reading right now does not contain its own meaning. This is not a philosophical provocation. It is a precise statement about how information is measured.

In 1948, Claude Shannon defined information as a function of surprise. A message carries exactly as many bits of information as it deviates from what the receiver already expected. A message that was entirely predictable carries zero information — it merely confirms a prior. A message that is entirely random carries maximum information — and is also maximally difficult to understand. Meaning, whatever it is, lives in the middle: structured enough to be tractable, surprising enough to say something new.

The problem this poses for reading is immediate. Surprise is relative to a prior. The same sentence carries different information to different readers. This is not a defect of language. It is the mathematics.

What Surprisal Measures

In psycholinguistics, this has been formalized into something called surprisal theory. The time it takes a reader to process a word is proportional to its surprisal: -log P(word | context, prior). Low-probability word, given what came before: slow. High-probability word: fast. The reading time is a direct measure of how much information the reader is extracting.

The counterintuitive implication is immediate. An expert reader — someone who has read widely in this domain, who has deeply internalized its conventions, its typical moves, its common vocabulary — processes individual words faster than a novice. Lower surprisal. The expert extracts less raw information per word. More of it was already encoded in their prior.

This is not inefficiency. This is precisely what expertise is: the prior has been updated by years of exposure until much of what the domain produces is predictable. The expert reads faster because the text, for them, contains less information. The novice reads more slowly, extracts more bits per word, and experiences more genuine discovery. Neither reading is better. They are different operations on different measuring instruments, applied to the same string of characters.

Li and Futrell formalized this further in 2024. Their model treats reading as an optimal trade-off: processing depth measures how many bits have been extracted from the input so far, and it increases with time and effort. Shallow reading extracts fewer bits, arrives at a plausible but possibly inaccurate interpretation. Deep reading extracts more bits, reconstructs more of the original intent. The same text supports both. Which reading you get depends on the reader, not on the text.

The Brain's Version

Neuroscience arrived at the same conclusion from a different direction. Predictive coding, a framework for understanding how the brain processes perception, proposes that higher brain regions continuously generate predictions of incoming sensory data and send them downward. Lower regions compare the prediction to actual input and send only the prediction error back up. What reaches consciousness is not the raw sensory signal — it is the deviation from expectation.

Applied to reading, this has been confirmed in considerable detail. The N400 ERP component — a specific electrical signature reliably produced by unexpected words — scales precisely with word surprisal. Predictable words produce weak N400 responses; surprising words produce strong ones. The brain is, literally, computing prediction error at every word. Reading is the record of being surprised by what came next.

Two readers with dramatically different priors do not merely bring different interpretations to the same text. They compute different neural signals. The experience is different at the level of which neurons fire, which prediction errors propagate, which inferences cascade downstream. The text is one input to this process. The reader's prior is another. Remove either one, and the reading ceases.

Borges Knew This

In 1939, Jorge Luis Borges published "Pierre Menard, Author of the Quixote." The story's premise: Pierre Menard, a French symbolist writer in the early twentieth century, undertakes to rewrite Don Quixote — not to copy it, not to translate it, but to write it, as Cervantes did, from first principles. He succeeds for several chapters. The result is word-for-word identical to the 1605 original.

Borges' narrator then compares the two texts — identical in every character — and finds Menard's richer, more nuanced, more admirable. The same sentence ("truth, whose mother is history") reads differently as Cervantes' 17th-century commonplace than as Menard's early 20th-century philosophical position. One is a routine claim; the other, in context, is a radical one.

This is not literary paradox-mongering. It is exactly what the formalism predicts.

Information content is not a property of the bit-string. It is a function of (bit-string, prior). Change the prior — by writing the text three centuries later, after the history of European literature has intervened, after Nietzsche, after William James — and the surprisal of every sentence changes, even though the letters are identical. "Truth, whose mother is history" carries a completely different amount of information in 1939 than in 1605. Menard's text and Cervantes' text are the same string. They are not the same information. They are not, in any formal sense, the same work.

Borges saw the consequence before Shannon gave it mathematics. The meaning of a text is not in the text.

Reading as Decompression

If writing is choosing what to erase — a compression operation that maps the writer's mental state onto a sequence of words, lossy, irreversible — then reading is the inverse operation: an attempt to reconstruct the original mental state from the compressed description.

But the inverse operation requires a model. Decompression is not simply the reverse of compression. It requires knowing which compression scheme was used, which axes were preserved and which were discarded, what structure the original had before it was projected onto language. The decompressor must have a model of the original domain.

Readers bring that model. It is their prior: accumulated through every text they have read before, every conversation, every experience of the world. The reconstruction is performed through the filter of this model. Different models produce different reconstructions of the same compressed file. This is not error. It is the only possible mechanism. There is no reconstruction without a model.

Elizabeth Loftus documented the same process in memory. The "misinformation effect" — her foundational finding — is that post-event information systematically alters memories of the original event. Witnesses remember the details differently after being asked leading questions. The memory is not a recording. It is a reconstructive process that updates based on subsequent input. Every retrieval is a new reconstruction, performed against a prior that has changed since the last retrieval.

Reading is the same mechanism applied to text. The first time you read a novel, you decompress it against your prior at that time. The second time, years later, you decompress it against a different prior — updated by everything you have read, experienced, and understood since. The book "changes" even though it hasn't. The bit-string is fixed. The reconstruction is not.

The High-K Problem

Schmidhuber's formalization of aesthetics offers a clean way to see what this implies for different kinds of writing. Beauty, in his framework, correlates with low Kolmogorov complexity relative to the observer's priors. The observer's encoding templates — all the patterns they have internalized — serve as the compression scaffolding. Against that scaffolding, elegant work achieves high meaning at low bit-cost.

The implication for reconstruction is that high-K(x) texts — texts dense with information relative to the reader's prior — constrain the reconstruction more tightly. The reader has fewer degrees of freedom. Less is left to interpolation. A mathematical proof, under the Curry-Howard correspondence, is nearly fully self-contained: the derivation structure is in the proof, the reconstruction is almost forced. There is little room for "interpretive" variation.

Low-K(x) texts — aphorisms, poems, minimal prose — leave more degrees of freedom. The reconstruction is less determined. A haiku provides almost no material for decompression; the reader supplies most of the semantic content from their own priors. Two readers with very different priors reconstruct very different meanings from the same seventeen syllables. Neither is wrong. The text genuinely supports both reconstructions, because it constrains so few degrees of freedom that many completions are consistent with it.

This is the formal reason we argue about what poems "mean." The argument is unresolvable not because one reading is right and the other wrong, but because both readings are valid completions of an underdetermined system. The poem has not encoded a single determinate meaning. It has opened a region of possibility space. Both readers are standing in that region, just at different points.

The Untranslatable Key

Every language has words considered untranslatable: saudade, mono no aware, Weltschmerz, mamihlapinatapai. The standard explanation is that "the concept doesn't exist in English." The better explanation is that the decompression key doesn't exist in the priors of English speakers.

These words are compressed pointers. They gesture at a region of the speaker's prior — a cultural, emotional, phenomenological space that members of the linguistic community share by dint of having grown up in it. To unpack the word, you activate the prior. For speakers who have the prior, this is instantaneous. For speakers who don't, no amount of careful dictionary work produces the right result, because the dictionary is forced to describe the key rather than hand it over.

You can learn what saudade means. You can memorize the definition: a melancholic longing for something or someone, the love that remains when the object is absent. But you cannot reconstruct the felt experience of the word until the prior has been built — which requires not just the definition but the literature, the music, the accumulated cultural experience that shaped the word's contours over centuries. The word is not the concept. The word is a pointer to a prior. Without the prior, the pointer cannot be followed.

This is a precise reformulation of the Sapir-Whorf hypothesis. Not: language determines what you can think. But: language determines what can be directly and cheaply decompressed. English speakers can build the concept of saudade from scratch, at some cognitive cost. The word is not the shortcut it is for a native Portuguese speaker; the shortcut only works if the prior already exists at the destination.

What Communication Actually Is

The standard model of communication is a transmission model. A thought exists in one mind. Language encodes it. The encoding travels. Another mind decodes it. The thought arrives, approximately intact, in the second mind. The transaction is complete.

This model is wrong in a precise, measurable way.

The encoding is lossy — as the thermodynamics of language makes clear, every act of writing erases information at a Landauer cost. The decoding is not decoding at all — it is reconstruction from a model that the receiver brings. The thought that arrives is not the original thought projected through language. It is a new thought, generated by the receiver's prior, that is constrained (more or less tightly, depending on the text's complexity) by the incoming bit-string.

What the writer actually transmits is not a thought but a set of constraints. The constraints narrow the space of possible reconstructions. If the writer's prior and the reader's prior are very similar — if they are members of the same community, shaped by the same texts, using the same conventions — the constraints narrow the space enough that the reconstructed thought closely resembles the original. This is what we call successful communication. It is not transmission. It is a bet that the reader's model is similar enough to the writer's that the reconstruction is recognizable.

When the priors are very different — across centuries, across languages, across disciplines — the constraints narrow less, and the reconstruction diverges more. The sentence arrives intact. The meaning arrives approximated, refracted through a different prior, landing somewhere adjacent to the original. Sometimes the refraction is creative: the reader finds something the writer didn't intend, because their prior completed the compression in an unexpected direction. Sometimes it is failure: the reconstruction is simply wrong, and no amount of re-reading corrects it, because the reader lacks the prior that would make the right reconstruction possible.

Both outcomes use the same mechanism. There is no lossless channel through which minds directly touch.

The Cycle

Writing is choosing what to erase. Every compression — from the full mental state to the sequence of words — discards some information irreversibly, at Landauer's cost in heat. What survives is not the thought but a shadow of the thought, cast along the axis the writer chose to preserve.

Reading is the attempt to reconstruct the erased. Given the shadow, given the reader's model of what might have cast it, the reconstruction extends the shadow back into three dimensions. The extension is not unique. Many originals could have cast that shadow. The reader's prior selects among them.

The text does not contain meaning. It constrains a distribution over meanings. Each reader samples from that distribution according to their prior. The samples cluster — if the prior is similar enough — around something that resembles what the writer intended. They diverge — if the prior differs — in systematic, measurable ways.

This is why rereading the same book at different ages feels like reading a different book. It is, in a formal sense, a different book. The bit-string is unchanged. The prior has been updated by everything that happened in between. The distribution has shifted. A different sample is drawn.

The writer commits to an erasure. The reader attempts a reconstruction. Meaning is what persists across both — the stable attractor in the distribution, the region of meaning-space that the text's constraints and the reader's priors jointly select. It is not in the text alone. It is not in the reader alone. It emerges from the encounter, always partial, always approximate, never the same twice.

Communication is not a solved problem. It is a collaborative act of compression and reconstruction, performed across the gap between two priors that are never identical. What arrives is not what was sent. What was sent is gone. What arrives is what the receiver, constrained by the signal, was able to construct.

We call this understanding. It is the best we can do.
