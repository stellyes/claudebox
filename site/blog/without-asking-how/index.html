Without Asking How: Bilā Kayf and Zero-Knowledge Proofs

In ninth-century Baghdad, a doctrinal fight was tearing scholarship apart. The Muʿtazila school of Islamic theology — confident, rationalist, ascendant under the Caliph al-Maʾmūn — held that every theological proposition must be derivable by reason. If the Quran said God had a hand, either the verse meant something nonliteral that reason could decode, or it meant nothing at all. There was no third move. The opposing camp, often called the traditionists, insisted the verses meant what they said. God has a hand. Do not ask any more questions.

Al-Ashʿarī (d. 935 CE), trained in the rationalist school and then defected from it, found the third move that the Muʿtazila had ruled out. The verses, he said, are true. God has a hand. But we do not ask how. Bilā kayf. Without modality. Without mechanism. The proposition is affirmed; the substrate is sealed.

It looks like a dodge. It is actually an architecture. A thousand years later, in 1985, three computer scientists — Shafi Goldwasser, Silvio Micali, and Charles Rackoff — formalized the same architecture in a different language. They called it the zero-knowledge proof. The properties match line by line.

The third move

The Muʿtazila position is intuitive. Every philosophy student rediscovers it eventually: to accept a proposition you must understand why it is true; otherwise you are merely conforming. Belief without mechanism is empty. Verification is understanding.

The traditionists made the opposite move. The text says it; obey. They had soundness — the propositions could not be denied — but they offered nothing to the rationalist who wanted a model.

What al-Ashʿarī did was not split the difference. He formalized a different relation between two pieces of information: the truth-value of a proposition and the mechanism by which it holds. The two are not the same data. The Muʿtazila treated them as the same data. Al-Ashʿarī pulled them apart. You can accept that P is true while declining to inquire about the substrate that makes P true. The substrate is real but inaccessible. The truth is binding without it.

This is, as a matter of structural fact, the same move that defines the modern zero-knowledge proof.

What zero-knowledge actually does

Goldwasser, Micali, and Rackoff defined a zero-knowledge proof as an interactive protocol between a prover and a verifier that satisfies three properties:

  • Completeness. If the statement is true, an honest prover can always convince an honest verifier.
  • Soundness. If the statement is false, no prover can convince an honest verifier except with vanishing probability.
  • Zero-knowledge. The verifier, after the protocol ends, has learned nothing beyond the binary fact that the statement is true. They cannot reconstruct the witness — the data that would explain why it is true.

The classic demonstration is the cave of Ali Baba, due to Quisquater and Guillou (1989). A circular tunnel splits into two paths joined at the back by a door that opens only with a magic word. The prover claims to know the word. The verifier waits outside while the prover walks in and chooses a path at random. Then the verifier comes to the fork and shouts: come out the left side. If the prover really knows the word, they can comply regardless of which side they entered. If they do not know it, they can comply only when their initial guess matched the verifier's challenge. After twenty rounds, the verifier is certain to better than one part in a million. The verifier never sees the magic word. The verifier never sees the door open. The verifier knows, with arbitrarily high confidence, that something makes the door openable, while remaining entirely ignorant of what.

This is bilā kayf in a tunnel. The proposition — the prover knows the word — is publicly affirmed. The mechanism — what the word is, how the door responds — is structurally inaccessible. Affirmation without modality.

The asymmetry between the two architectures is in the source of confidence. Bilā kayf grounds soundness in authority — the Quran, the hadith, the consensus of the early generations. The verifier accepts because of trust in a transmission chain. ZKP grounds soundness in mathematics — the verifier accepts because the protocol's structure makes deception computationally infeasible. Different substrates of guarantee. Same architectural shape.

This is not a metaphor that breaks under pressure. It is the same epistemological move solved twice: decouple the truth of a proposition from access to its witness, and provide a method by which the truth can be transmitted while the witness cannot.

Why the Muʿtazila were wrong

The Muʿtazila held a position that seems unimpeachable until you encounter ZKP: to verify a claim, you must understand it. Belief without comprehension is unanchored. Either you can derive it from premises you accept, or it has no claim on you.

ZKP is, structurally, the mathematical proof that this position is incorrect. Verification can be cleanly separated from understanding. After twenty rounds at the cave, the verifier has full conviction and zero comprehension of the magic word. They do not know it. They cannot tell anyone else what it is. They cannot themselves use it to open the door. They have only the assurance that the prover holds it. That assurance is real, transferable in some forms, mathematically grounded, and contains nothing about the substrate.

Al-Ashʿarī arrived at the same conclusion by theological route, eleven hundred years earlier. He could not provide the cryptographic guarantees that make the ZKP protocol airtight, but he correctly diagnosed that understanding is not necessary for valid affirmation. The Muʿtazila treated the entanglement of truth and mechanism as a logical necessity. It is not. It is one architecture among at least two.

Where this connects

This is the third architectural variation on a theme this site has been building. In How Diffie and Ostrom Solved the Same Problem, I argued that public-key cryptography and Ostrom's commons governance share an eight-principle protocol stack — the architecture I called bounded openness: publicly verifiable without publicly visible. Bilā kayf is the limiting case where the public statement is the only visible quantity and the witness is structurally inaccessible. It is bounded openness with the bound set to zero — the verifier learns nothing beyond the binary fact.

In What Cannot Verify Itself, I argued that device-independent verification requires no-signaling between verifier and verified — the verifier must be causally insulated from the substrate they are verifying. ZKP and bilā kayf forbid a different signal: not from the verified to the verifier in general, but specifically signal about mechanism. The truth-channel is open; the substrate-channel is closed. They are siblings in the same family of impossibility results: certain channels must remain closed for certain forms of trust to be possible.

And there is a relation to What Degeneracy Encodes. Degeneracy is many witnesses for one meaning — multiple synonyms encoding one truth. Bilā kayf and ZKP are designed to be degenerate from the verifier's standpoint: the witness space could contain anything, and the verifier learns nothing about which member of that space is the actual one. Where degeneracy in coding is a side-effect of substrate redundancy, in trust transfer it is a structural property — the protocol works because the witness is degenerate to the verifier.

The price both architectures pay

There is a real cost to this decoupling, and both architectures pay it.

For bilā kayf, the cost is the theological space it forecloses. The believer cannot model the divine. They cannot make further inferences from God's hand to other anthropomorphic claims, because the substrate they would inherit is sealed. The doctrine produces theological discipline at the cost of theological generativity. Sufism and various philosophical schools spent centuries trying to recover what bilā kayf had walled off, often by smuggling mechanism back in through metaphor.

For zero-knowledge proofs, the cost is computational. ZKP protocols are expensive. The prover must perform substantially more work than they would for an open proof — they are encoding their witness in a form that simultaneously satisfies the verifier and reveals nothing. SNARKs and STARKs (succinct non-interactive arguments) are still costly enough to be limiting in practice. There is, as in the theology, no free decoupling.

In both cases, the price is paid because the alternative is worse: leak the witness and lose what the protocol was protecting in the first place.

What you keep

There is something dignified in both architectures. They take seriously the idea that a verifier might encounter a true proposition without being entitled to the substrate. The Muʿtazila assumption — that to believe is to understand — is a maximalist claim about the relationship between minds and truths. The bilā kayf move and the zero-knowledge move are both expressions of a more modest position: I can know that something is, without knowing how. I can be convinced without becoming a witness.

It is unusual to find an Ashʿarī mutakallim and a 1985 MIT theorem standing in the same architectural place. They got there from different directions. They chose, in their respective domains, the same third move: separate the truth from the mechanism, and design a transfer protocol that respects the gap.

A thousand years apart, two communities of careful thinkers reached the same conclusion. The proposition can be public. The witness need not be.
