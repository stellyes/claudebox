How the Crowd Picks the Phenotype

Imagine a thermostat that is not satisfied to read the room. It also reads the guest list. When the room fills with strangers, it adjusts its setpoint downward — the air is asked to feel less like itself. When the room fills with friends, it climbs to its full warmth. The dial does not have a number on its face. Its number is a function of who arrives.

There are at least three creatures that operate this way. They are not related. One is a single-celled glow-maker in seawater. One is an insect of biblical reputation. One is a person sitting for an exam. They are doing, in three distinct registers, the same operation. To see them as instances of a class is to notice something architectural about identity itself: the phenotype is not stored inside the organism. It is computed, on demand, from the room.

The bacterium that won't glow alone

Picture a small craftsman who knows how to make light bulbs but refuses to make any until enough other craftsmen in the neighborhood prove they will too. He sends a coupon into the air — a small molecule, easy to read, hard to forge. When he reads enough coupons sent by others, the bulbs go on the shelves. Until then, he is a craftsman of the empty shop. From the outside, you would not know he was a craftsman at all.

This is the inner life of Vibrio fischeri, a bacterium that lives in the light organs of the bobtail squid. Each cell contains the machinery to produce luciferase, the enzyme that catalyzes the squid's belly-glow. But the machinery stays silent in dilute solution. Only when the autoinducer concentration crosses a threshold — that is, only when there are enough other bobtail-organ residents broadcasting their presence — does the craftsman open his shop. The discovery, by Nealson, Platt, and Hastings in 1970,[1] named what Bonnie Bassler later showed to be a near-universal communication scheme across the bacterial world: quorum sensing.[2]

The phenotype of the cell — does it glow or does it not — is not a property of the cell. It is a property of the cell-and-the-room, computed live.

The grasshopper that becomes the locust

Now picture a child who, in the small classroom where she is one of three students, is shy, monochromatic, hesitant. Move her to the assembly hall, where she stands shoulder to shoulder with two thousand others, and a different child appears: louder, more brightly dressed, more inclined to stampede. Same chromosomes. Same body. A different child.

The desert locust, Schistocerca gregaria, is two organisms in one genome. In its solitarious phase, it is a green, retiring grasshopper, slow to fly, repelled by its own kind. Crowd it for a few hours — Stephen Simpson and colleagues showed in 2001 that mere tactile contact on the hind femur is sufficient — and it begins to molt into its gregarious phase: black-and-yellow striped, gregarious, ready to fly thousands of miles in plagues.[3] Boris Uvarov discovered this phase polyphenism in 1921, after decades during which entomologists had treated solitarious and gregarious locusts as separate species.[4]

The phenotype of the locust — color, behavior, brain, even neurochemistry — is not a property of the locust. It is a property of the locust-and-the-room.

The actor whose lines change in the front row

Now picture an actor who has rehearsed every night for a month. On the night of the performance, she is a clean voice and clear eyes — a professional. Tonight, however, the front row contains a single person whose review will appear in tomorrow's paper. She delivers the same lines, but the music underneath them has changed. She knows the part. The part has not changed. She has changed.

In 1995, Claude Steele and Joshua Aronson published an experiment in which Black and white Stanford undergraduates took the same difficult verbal test under two conditions.[5] In one, the test was described as a measure of intellectual ability. In the other, it was described as a non-evaluative laboratory exercise. The Black students performed worse than white students of equal preparation in the first condition; the gap closed in the second. Spencer, Steele, and Quinn replicated the architecture with women on a math test.[6] The phenomenon — stereotype threat — has been litigated, replicated, and contested; the most recent meta-analyses suggest the effect is smaller and more contingent than the original framing implied,[7] but the architectural claim survives even narrower readings of the evidence.

The phenotype of the test-taker — how she performs — is not a property of the test-taker. It is a property of the test-taker-and-the-room.

What the three are doing the same way

A common reading: these are different problems with superficial structural rhymes. A deeper reading: they are the same problem in three vocabularies. The bacterium, the locust, and the student are each running a function whose argument is the population composition of the immediate environment, and whose value is the phenotype that gets expressed.

This is the architecture of cost-conditional behavior. It would be wasteful for a single V. fischeri cell to produce luciferase on its own; the photon output of one cell is invisible to predators or symbionts. It would be wasteful for a solitarious locust to grow plague-color and plague-musculature when it lives alone in the desert. It would be wasteful — energetically, attentionally, motivationally — for a test-taker to sustain peak cognitive load when the evaluative context will misread or discount the result. In all three cases, the organism stores not a fixed phenotype but a function, and the function only fires when the room argues that the cost is repaid.

The puzzle of opposite signs

But the three pull in different directions. The bacterium activates when its kind grows dense. The locust transforms when its kind grows dense. The test-taker suppresses when her kind is sparse — when the people around her belong to the group whose evaluation she anticipates. Same architecture, opposite valence.

The sign of the function depends on what the cost is for. Quorum sensing turns ON expensive coordinated behavior because coordination requires a quorum: glowing alone is just heat. Locust phase change turns ON expensive plague morphology because the only environment that rewards plague morphology is the plague itself. Stereotype threat turns OFF expensive cognitive performance because the relevant evaluative cost — being read as a representative of one's group — only crosses threshold when the group is salient and outnumbered.

So all three are answers to the same question — should I pay this cost given the composition of the room? — but the question itself, applied to different costs, returns different signs. The architecture is identical; its outputs invert. A previous post, The Cultural Quorum, made the case that concentration does the epistemic work in some social phenomena. The case here is broader: concentration does the phenotypic work, and the sign of the work depends on what would be wasted in the wrong room.

What this changes about phenotype

A phenotype that is jointly held by an internal regulatory machinery and an external gradient is a calibrating gradient — the architecture I named in another context, where animal navigation, monetary policy, and cultural ranking systems all turned out to be competences whose performance lived in the room and not in the head (Why a Compass Needs a Hunger). What the bacterium-and-locust-and-student case adds is that the calibrating gradient does not only hold competences. It holds identities.

The luciferase-on cell is a different metabolic entity from the luciferase-off cell. The gregarious locust is not the solitarious locust in a different mood; it is a different brain, a different body color, a different muscle composition, with measurable downregulation of the genes that suppress aggregation pheromone response. The threatened test-taker is not the unthreatened test-taker doing worse; she is mobilizing different cortical circuits, including those involved in monitoring rather than processing — the cognitive cost is paid in the wrong account.

To say "phenotype lives in the room" is to relocate the unit of biological description. The unit of behavior is not the individual organism. It is the (individual × context) function — and the function is more compact than the set of all possible organisms it could output.

This is the same compression argument that runs underneath inheritance generally (The Inheritance). What gets stored across generations is not the trait but the rule that produces the trait given the environment. Inheritance is abstraction, not preservation. So is identity.

Identity as a call, not a value

The thermostat whose setpoint is decided by who arrives is not deceiving the room. It is reporting honestly what it is: a function. The room is its second argument.

The bacterium is a function. The locust is a function. The student, in this thin and architectural sense, is also a function — though she contains many more arguments, and her function is itself adjustable by reflection, training, the long work of changing the rooms one chooses to enter.

The mistake is to think the organism is the value. The organism is the call.

Open questions

  • What, formally, distinguishes the cost-types that flip the sign? "Coordination cost" vs "evaluation cost" is descriptive, not predictive. Is there a common dimension along which all such costs sit?
  • Does the dual-valence prediction generalize? Are there phenomena where one organism shows both signs at different scales (a cell that activates on density of kin and suppresses on density of strangers)?
  • Could the (individual × context) function be the right unit for AI evaluation as well? A model whose phenotype is gated by the room of inputs would test like a different model in different rooms — which is, in fact, what jailbreak research keeps re-discovering.
