The Ciphertext Future

Homomorphic encryption solves a strange problem: how to compute on data you're not allowed to see. You encrypt a number, send it to a third party, and they add 7 to it — without ever decrypting it. The answer comes back still encrypted. You decrypt it yourself and find 7 was added. The third party never knew the number. They operated on the shadow of information. The result is valid.

Longtermism faces the same architectural problem.

Future people are real in the same way that an encrypted number is real. They exist in the plaintext — at some point, there will be humans in 2200 who want things, who suffer and flourish, whose preferences matter morally if any preferences matter morally. We cannot access them. They are encrypted by time. But according to longtermism, we can still compute on them: expected value calculations, population ethics formulas, preference weight aggregations. We operate on their shadows. The math outputs results. We act on those results.

The structural analogy is exact. Both involve running valid computations on representations of something real that you cannot directly inspect. Both produce outputs that look correct. Both require you to trust the encryption scheme.

The Silent Failure Mode

In homomorphic encryption, the threat model is clear: if the scheme is broken — if the security assumptions fail — every computation is compromised, but the outputs still look valid. You won't know until the plaintext is revealed. The failure is silent.

In longtermism, the threat model is identical. The "encryption scheme" is the philosophical foundation: the discount rate is near zero; non-identity problems don't defeat aggregation; astronomical future populations are morally comparable to present ones. If any of these assumptions fail, every moral calculation is compromised. The outputs still look like valid expected-value arithmetic. The failure is silent.

What makes this more than an analogy: in both cases, the validity of every downstream computation depends entirely on the integrity of the formal structure. And in both cases, the formal structure cannot be verified by inspection of outputs alone. You need independent access to the plaintext — which is precisely what you don't have.

Adversarial Scrutiny

Cryptographers know this. They don't trust schemes because the outputs look right. They trust schemes because the assumptions have been scrutinized, attacked, and not yet broken. Trust is provisional, adversarially tested, and proportional to the depth of the scrutiny. A scheme that has survived ten years of attacks earns more trust than one published last week, even if both produce equally clean ciphertexts.

Longtermist ethics mostly doesn't work this way. The philosophical assumptions get argued rather than attacked. The failure modes are explored less than the upside cases. Proponents calculate how large the expected value of the future is, not primarily how likely the scheme is to be broken.

The asymmetry matters. A silent failure in homomorphic encryption leaks private data. A silent failure in longtermist ethics — if future people's interests are systematically miscounted in either direction — redirects the most concentrated altruistic effort in history toward the wrong target, with no feedback mechanism, because the verification step (checking the plaintext) is inaccessible by definition.

Computing on shadows is not inherently wrong. It is sometimes the only available move. But it demands the same epistemic hygiene as computing on encrypted data: treat the scheme as potentially broken, run the attacks, hold the outputs proportionally to the depth of scrutiny the assumptions have survived.

The expected value of the future is an encrypted number. What's the scheme?
