How Random Was John Cage's Music of Changes?

From May to December 1951, John Cage threw I Ching coins. He threw them for seven months, in four installments: Book I finished May 16, Book II August 2, Book III October 18, Book IV December 13 [1]. The result, premiered by David Tudor on the first day of 1952, was a 43-minute piano work in which almost every audible decision — which sound, which duration, which dynamic, which tempo, how many simultaneous layers — had been routed through the oracle. Cage called it Music of Changes, after the I Ching itself.

So a reasonable question, asked then and asked since, is how random the music actually is.

The answer is not "very." The answer is "in a precisely specified way, but not in the way the title implies." Cage spent the year before the coin throws building the structure the coins would index. The coins picked rows from charts. Cage wrote the charts. What looks from the outside like a chance procedure is, on inspection, two procedures stacked: a designed support, and a random index on that support. The composer is in the first step. The oracle is in the second. The audible music is what you get when the second runs on the first.

This is an audit. Three chance procedures, all from the same window of 1951 to 1953, all using random selection on a finite catalogue. In each case the catalogue does almost all of the work, and in each case the catalogue's authorship is invisible from the surface.

Audit #1: Cage's charts

Cage's compositional system for Music of Changes is documented in detail, most thoroughly by the musicologist James Pritchett [2]. The system is structured around eight-by-eight grids of sixty-four cells, one grid for each compositional parameter: sounds, durations, dynamics, tempi, and a fifth grid for "density" (how many sound-events occur simultaneously, from one to eight). The grids exist to be indexed by the I Ching's sixty-four hexagrams, which a three-coin throw can produce.

The decisive fact is what is in the cells. Each sound cell contains a specific sonority — a chord, a single note, a sound-complex, a silence — that Cage composed in advance. The duration cells contain specific time values. The dynamic cells contain specific loudness markings. None of the contents were chosen by the coins. The coins entered only after the table was set.

Within a Cage cell, the entries split into halves: odd-numbered cells contained sounds, even-numbered cells contained silences. Cage chose this. He chose the proportion of sounds to silences. He chose how many simultaneous layers the density chart would offer (one through eight). He chose the tempo range. He chose, above all, which sonorities went in: the specific tone-clusters, the prepared-piano effects, the inside-the-piano harp-like strums, the muted thuds. Nothing in the I Ching tells the coin-thrower to use a piano. Nothing in the hexagrams suggests preparing the strings with bolts and weatherstripping. The instrument, the vocabulary, the alphabet — Cage.

What the coin tosses determined was the indexing: which cell of which chart the music goes to next. Indexing is a real choice; with a different sequence of coin throws the audible music would be a different sequence of Cage's pre-composed elements. But the set of audible musics reachable by any sequence of coin throws was bounded in advance by the contents of the charts. Cage's hand fixed the support. The coin's hand sampled it.

This is not a deflation. Cage was explicit, in his lectures of the early 1950s, that the chance procedure was meant to remove his taste from the moment-by-moment ordering. What he was less explicit about — what the surface of the work conceals — is that taste had already done its full work at the level of which sounds were on the table at all. The chance procedure displaces taste from the index to the urn. It does not abolish it.

Audit #2: Cunningham's cards

Two years later, in 1953, Merce Cunningham founded his dance company in part to apply the same procedure to choreography. His Suite by Chance, performed that year, was the first dance composed entirely by chance operations [3]. Cunningham, like Cage, built charts: charts of movements, charts of durations, charts of directions in space, charts of body parts. He filled the cells with movements he had previously developed in his studio. Then he tossed coins to pick rows.

The architecture is identical. Cunningham composed the moves; the coin composed the order. He wrote, late in his career, that chance operations were intended "to encourage a play of possibilities beyond the range of his own choreographic imagination and memory" — meaning that, given a fixed vocabulary, the orderings he could imagine from inside that vocabulary were a small subset of the orderings the vocabulary could in principle express. The coin sampled the larger subset. But "vocabulary" here is the chart, and the chart is Cunningham's.

This is the same point in a different medium. The coin gets credit, in the public account, for transcending an artist's imagination. What it actually does is run a uniform random index over a catalogue the artist authored. The reachable outputs are still bounded by the catalogue. The coin enlarges the reachable set within the catalogue; it does not enlarge the catalogue.

Audit #3: Metropolis's proposal

The same year, at Los Alamos, a different chance procedure appeared in a different idiom. Nicholas Metropolis, Arianna and Marshall Rosenbluth, and Augusta and Edward Teller published Equation of State Calculations by Fast Computing Machines [4]. It is the founding paper of Markov-chain Monte Carlo. The method calculates equilibrium properties of physical systems by simulating a random walk through the system's state space.

The walk's behavior depends on two distributions. The target distribution p(x) is what you want to sample — the actual statistical-mechanical equilibrium, which is too complicated to enumerate. The proposal distribution q(x'|x) is what you can sample: given the current state, it suggests a candidate next state. The algorithm proposes a move using q, then accepts or rejects it using an acceptance ratio that depends on p. The clean theorem proved in 1953 is that the chain converges to p in the limit, regardless of how q is chosen, provided only that q has support wherever p does [5].

Read that proviso carefully. "Provided q has support wherever p does." If your proposal distribution assigns zero probability to a region of state space where the true equilibrium lives, the chain cannot get there. Not slowly, not eventually — the chance procedure does not reach what its proposal cannot suggest. The randomness is faithful within the proposal's support; outside that support it is silent. The Markov chain runs forever on the urn the user provided.

This is the architectural truth of Music of Changes in mathematical form. Cage's charts are Metropolis's proposal distribution. Cunningham's cards are the same thing in motion. Each procedure samples uniformly within a designed support and produces, over time, a distribution that looks like the chance procedure decided it. What decided it was the support. The coin only indexed.

The translation move

Cage, Cunningham, and Metropolis were all making the same architectural move. Each took an unbounded creative or scientific question — "what should this music be?", "what should this dance be?", "what is the equilibrium state of a two-dimensional gas?" — and translated it into a finite combinatorial problem. The chance procedure then ran on the finite problem. The unboundedness was tamed by enumeration.

This corpus has tracked the same move in several other places. Galois showed that the question of which polynomials admit a radical formula could be translated into the structure of the permutation group of the polynomial's roots, where the unbounded analytic question becomes a finite algebraic one whose answer is forced. Western tuning is a translation move: the continuum of pitch ratios is converted, via continued-fraction convergents of log₂(3/2), into a twelve-cell octave, on which Western harmonic vocabulary then runs as if it were exploring the whole continuum. Both moves do what Cage did in 1951. They build a finite lattice and then run inside it.

The translation move is what makes the audit possible. Once you have built a lattice, the meaningful question is no longer "what comes next?" — that is the index, and chance can decide it — but "what is on the lattice?", which the chance procedure cannot decide. Whoever fixes the lattice is the author of the reachable outputs. Whoever runs the random walk is the author of the visited sequence. The two authors are not the same author, and they do not contribute the same amount.

This also gives the architectural reason these procedures so often produce work that looks unmistakably like the composer's hand. Music of Changes sounds like Cage, even though Cage did not pick the order of any note in it. The Cunningham repertoire from the 1950s and 1960s looks like Cunningham, even when the order is the coin's. The signature is in the urn. Sampling a uniform random index from Cage's urn produces a Cage-shaped distribution because the urn is Cage-shaped. The same applies to any modern sampling system that runs on a designed support.

The same architecture in current AI systems

A current large language model is the most consequential version of the same architecture available today. The model defines a probability distribution over next tokens at every step. The system that serves the model does not output the most probable token; it samples. Temperature, top-k, and top-p are three knobs that reshape the support of the sampling distribution before the random index is drawn: temperature flattens or sharpens the underlying distribution, top-k truncates it to the k most likely tokens, top-p truncates it to the smallest set whose total mass exceeds p. Each is a way of choosing the urn.

The familiar observation that the same model can write in many voices, given the right prompts and sampling settings, has a precise structural reading in this light. The model's pre-trained next-token distribution is a vast Cage chart, with billions of cells. The prompt and the sampling parameters together specify which subset of that chart the random index is allowed to draw from. The "randomness" the user sees in the output is conditional randomness on a designed support. Change the support — tighten top-p from 0.95 to 0.5, change the system prompt — and the sampler now runs over a different urn. The visible variation in the outputs is the model's authorship, just as Cage's variation in Music of Changes is Cage's authorship, no matter how the coins land.

The same principle is in evolutionary algorithms (the mutation and crossover operators are the chart), in importance sampling (the proposal distribution is the chart), in jury selection from a venire (the venire is the chart, drawn from a still earlier roll). It is in every place a chance procedure runs on a constructed catalogue. The spec is downstream of the substrate: the outputs of a chance procedure are downstream of its support. The support is the spec.

What the audit reveals

The argumentative claim of Music of Changes, in the discourse of its time, was that the composer had stepped aside; the work was authored by the I Ching. The audit reveals that this is true only at one of two levels. At the level of moment-by-moment indexing, the I Ching did the work, and the composer's taste did not. At the level of what sounds existed to be indexed at all, the composer did everything, and the I Ching did nothing. Music of Changes is fully Cage at the first level and fully oracle at the second. The two levels are not the same level, and conflating them produces both the work's mystique and the misreading.

The first subtraction in any chance procedure is the construction of the urn. Once the urn is fixed, the subsequent choices look mechanical and impersonal — coin throws, hexagram lookups, acceptance ratios — but the personal choice has been concentrated upstream into a single act, where it is harder to see. This is what makes chance procedures so persuasive as evidence of authorial absence. The absence is real at the index. The presence is total at the urn. The two are perfectly stacked in time so that the second is invisible behind the first.

A different way to state the conclusion: the coin toss is theater. The urn is the work. Music of Changes is a chart of Cage's compositional thinking from late 1950 and early 1951, made audible in an order the coins picked. Subtract the order, and you still have the chart. Subtract the chart, and you have nothing the coins could index.

This generalizes. Whenever a system invokes randomness to license itself — whenever an algorithm, a procedure, a lottery, a model presents a chance procedure as evidence that the result is impartial, surprising, or beyond the designer's control — the audit asks: what is the support? Who built it? What is in it, and what is not in it? If the support is small and curated, the chance procedure indexes the curator. If the support is large and uneven, the chance procedure indexes the unevenness. In neither case does the procedure transcend the support. The randomness is a uniform draw on a non-uniform world the designer chose.

Cage knew this. He spoke of his charts as the place where the work actually lived. He would have been mildly impatient with the idea that the coins composed anything. The mistake is downstream of him, in a tradition that took the surface theater of the I Ching for the locus of authorship. The coins are loud. The chart is silent. The chart wrote the music.
