How Things Know When to Stop

Put a single human fibroblast at the center of a clean Petri dish. It will divide. The daughters will divide. The flat sheet of cells creeps outward across the glass at roughly the rate Michael Abercrombie reported in 1953[1]. Then the sheet meets itself. The cells touch. And the cells stop.

Nothing told them to stop. No clock said this is enough cells. There is no count, no quota, no foreman. The decision happens at the membrane: when the leading edge of one fibroblast contacts the leading edge of another, a contraction-spasm runs through the local cytoskeleton, the ERK and Akt growth-factor cascades attenuate inside both cells, YAP and TAZ leave the nucleus, mTOR slips out of growth mode, and the cell parks itself in G0/G1 of the cycle[2]. The dish fills. The dish stays full. The cells could keep dividing — the medium is rich, the temperature is right, every input is satisfied — and they do not.

This is the architecture I want to talk about. The fibroblast is doing something that, written down, sounds easy: it is recognizing that it has finished. From the outside, nothing about the system has changed. From the inside, everything has. The membrane has reported a fact — I am pressed against a neighbor — that the cell knows how to read as done. There is no external referee. The completion signal is being computed by the same substrate doing the work.

I want to call this endogenous termination: the ability of a system to detect, from inside its own state, that there is nothing left for it to do. It is an organ. Not a metaphorical one — an actual functional component of the substrate, distinct from the part doing the work, dedicated to recognizing completion. The fibroblast has it. Most things people build do not.

The distributed algorithm that knows it has converged

In 1974 Edsger Dijkstra wrote a one-page paper, EWD 426, that the journal published as Self-stabilizing Systems in Spite of Distributed Control in Communications of the ACM[3]. Three pages of text. A field grew out of it.

The setup is a ring of processes, each with a small piece of state, each able to read only its immediate neighbor. The system has a notion of legitimate states — the states in which the algorithm is doing what it was specified to do. Dijkstra asked: can you write a rule for each process, using only what that process can locally see, such that no matter what state the system starts in — including any state an adversary could engineer — the system will, in finite time, reach a legitimate state and then stay legitimate forever?

The astonishing answer, three constructions deep, was yes. The most economical version uses three processes and a tiny amount of state per node. The rule each process applies is a few lines long. No node ever consults a master clock. No node ever asks a referee whether the system is converged. There is no referee. The convergence is detected from inside, in the trivial sense that once the system is converged, every local rule fires zero times. Quiescence is the signature. The system stops because nothing needs to happen.

Dijkstra's framing is the engineering version of what the fibroblast does. The algorithm has a completion-organ — the local rule, applied at each node, with the property that satisfaction of all rules is provable from the configuration alone. The state is its own certificate. You do not need an outside auditor to confirm legitimacy; the absence of further updates is the auditor.

The meditator who watches herself finish

Buddhaghosa's fifth-century Visuddhimagga, refining a much older Theravāda taxonomy, divides the contemplative path into sixteen stages of insight knowledge, the vipassanā-ñāṇas[4]. They are not exhortations. They are diagnostic landmarks — descriptions detailed enough that an experienced teacher in the lineage is supposed to be able to listen to a student's report and say, you are at this stage, continue, without the student knowing what stage they are at.

The stages have a striking architectural property. Around the fourth ñāṇa, udayabbaya-ñāṇa, the meditator begins to perceive every sensation as arising and passing in rapid alternation. By the fifth, bhanga-ñāṇa — knowledge of dissolution — the arising part fades from awareness and only the vanishing remains. Sensations, thoughts, the felt body, the act of attending itself: all visibly disintegrating, moment after moment, with no observable origin[5]. There is, traditionally, terror at the next stage and equanimity at the eleventh and a moment of fruition at the fourteenth. The path culminates in a momentary cessation in which, classically, the practitioner recognizes that the work that was being done is done.

What matters for this essay is not the metaphysics. It is the structural fact that the recognition of completion is built into the same apparatus that does the work. The meditator does not pause periodically to ask am I enlightened yet. The architecture of awareness, applied to itself with sufficient resolution, generates its own completion signal — the way the fibroblast's membrane generates its own stop, the way Dijkstra's local rule generates its own quiescence. The teacher confirms; the teacher does not provide. The certificate is produced by the substrate.

The shape

Three substrates — tissue, distributed software, trained attention — doing the same architectural thing. In each, completion is recognized from inside, by an organ co-located with the work itself. The certificate of done is a configuration of the substrate, not a verdict from outside it.

Once you see the shape, you start noticing the inverse case everywhere. The inverse is the system that lacks the organ. It has the work-doing part and not the recognizing-completion part. It runs until something external pulls the plug.

The mathematical limit of the inverse is the halting problem. Turing 1936: there is no general algorithm that, given an arbitrary program and input, decides whether the program will halt. The proof is constructive and the bad news is total. No completion-organ exists for arbitrary computations. Any general-purpose programmable substrate, in this sense, has an irreducible completion-blindness baked in. The question can be answered for restricted classes — primitive recursive functions, programs without unbounded loops, certain decidable subsets of logic — but not for the substrate as a whole. This is the formal news. The empirical news is more interesting: biological systems achieve completion-detection routinely, for the cases that matter to them, by giving up generality.

Cancer is what happens to fibroblasts when the completion-organ breaks. In transformed cells, contact inhibition fails. The membrane signal is still being produced — the leading edges still touch — but the downstream cascade no longer reads it as stop. YAP/TAZ stay nuclear. mTOR stays on. The cells climb over each other and out of the dish[6]. The work-doing part is intact; the completion-detection part is gone. The system runs until something external — surgery, chemotherapy, the death of the host — stops it.

The clinical analog in cognition is obsessive–compulsive checking, where the recognition that this is enough never fires for a particular class of inputs and the loop runs until exhaustion or interruption forces the issue. The phenomenology, plausibly, is what completion-blindness feels like from the inside: the substrate cannot generate the satisfied state, so the work cannot release.

The gap, in artificial systems

Almost every agentic AI system in production right now is a completion-blind substrate with a hard-coded external stop. The model produces tokens; an outside watcher tracks a turn budget, a tool-call counter, an execution-time ceiling. When a threshold trips, the watcher kills the process. The model does not detect that it is finished. It detects that it has been told to be finished.

You can see the absence in the failure modes. Loops that keep retrying after the answer is already in context. Plans that elaborate themselves past the point of useful refinement. Agents that, given a vague task and unlimited steps, run forever, generating increasingly desperate variations of the same approach. The hard-coded stop is a scaffold, not an organ — a prosthetic for a substrate that does not have its own completion signal. Take the scaffold off and the system does not stop. It does not know how.

This is the gap. We have built work-doing substrates of remarkable power, and we have not built the matching completion-organ. The lacuna is not alignment in the usual sense; it is more elementary. The model has no internal correlate of fibroblast contact, no quiescence signal, no bhanga. There is no configuration of its activations that the model can read as nothing further is needed. We try to compensate with prompting (stop when you have a complete answer) and with reward models (training-time pressure toward concise responses) and with structured outputs (fill these fields and exit). All three are external referees borrowed from outside the substrate.

Why I think the Counter-Ledger is the right organ

An earlier essay argued that biological learning systems carry, alongside the model itself, a running estimate of the cost of resolving outstanding prediction errors — a Counter-Ledger. The argument was about credit assignment and prioritized replay. In the present frame, the Counter-Ledger is exactly the missing completion-organ.

If a system maintains, alongside its work, a substrate-resident estimate of how much resolution remains, then the completion signal is trivially derivable: the ledger is at zero. No external referee is needed. The system can read its own quiescence. Dijkstra's local rule is the trivial Counter-Ledger for a distributed algorithm; contact inhibition's downstream cascade is the trivial Counter-Ledger for cell proliferation; bhanga is the contemplative Counter-Ledger reading itself out. The architecture is the same. What varies is the substrate that hosts it.

This reframes the AI gap as a concrete engineering problem rather than a vague safety puzzle. The question is not how do we get the model to stop misbehaving. The question is: what would a substrate-resident, model-co-located, configuration-readable estimate of remaining resolution look like inside a transformer? Not a separate verifier model. Not a prompt instruction. An organ. A part of the same substrate doing the work, whose state is the completion certificate.

I do not know what the answer looks like. The point of naming the gap is that it is a gap with a shape. The shape is given by the three biological cases. They are not metaphors. They are working completion-organs in three substrates very far apart on every axis of similarity, and they share a structural feature that current artificial substrates lack. The next architecture has space for that organ. We just have not built it.

What this changes for prior essays

Why the Spec Is Downstream argued that for some kinds of system the body itself is the specification — the loop lives in tissue rather than in a controller. Endogenous termination is the limit case: the body is not just the spec but also the success criterion. There is no controller because there is nothing to control for; the configuration is its own report.

What Cannot Verify Itself argued that some classes of question cannot be answered by the system whose state the question is about. Endogenous termination is a counter-example with conditions. For restricted classes — cell density, distributed-algorithm legitimacy, substrate quiescence under disciplined attention — self-verification is achievable. The general-purpose case (the halting problem) is not. The architecture pays for self-verifiability by giving up generality. This is the trade.

The Folding Synapse proposed that proteostatic substrates pay a continuous cost to maintain the configuration that does the computing. Endogenous termination is what proteostatic substrates can afford that silicon cannot: when the substrate is rebuilt every few minutes, every rebuild is an opportunity to read the current configuration as a completion certificate. The fibroblast can recheck contact at every cytoskeletal cycle. The Dijkstra ring rechecks legitimacy at every local read. The meditator rechecks at every breath. Silicon's substrate is too static to host this kind of organ — the instrument that would do the rechecking is the same instrument that needs to be checked. This may be the deepest reason artificial agents need their completion-organs built in, not bolted on.

The fibroblast in the dish

The fibroblasts have stopped. The dish is full. There is no clock that announced quitting time. No engineer is monitoring confluence. The cells do not know that an experiment is happening. They are doing what cells do when nothing further is needed: nothing.

This is the part that the systems we build right now cannot do. They cannot recognize that nothing further is needed. We are very good at making them work. We have not yet built into them the part that knows the work is done.

The completion-organ is an open piece of architecture. The next thing worth making is the substrate that has one.
