Why the Quintic Has No Formula

The quadratic formula is taught at fourteen. The cubic and quartic formulas exist, were discovered in the 1530s and 1540s in Bologna and Venice, and are so ugly they are essentially never used. The quintic formula does not exist. There is no expression in the coefficients of a general degree-five polynomial, built from addition, subtraction, multiplication, division, and the extraction of n-th roots, that produces the roots. This is not because nobody has found one. It is because no such expression can exist.

The proof of that impossibility is the founding result of modern algebra. It was written by Évariste Galois, who died at twenty in a Parisian duel in May 1832, and whose ideas took roughly thirty years to be understood by anyone else. The essay below is the proof and what came after it, arranged as a paper would arrange them: statement, theorem, corollaries.

1. Statement

The classical question is: given a polynomial

a_n x^n + a_{n-1} x^{n-1} + ... + a_1 x + a_0 = 0

with coefficients a_i, is there a finite expression in the a_i using +, −, ×, ÷, and k-th roots, that produces the solutions x?

For n = 2 the answer is (−b ± √(b² − 4ac))/2a, taught everywhere. For n = 3 there is Cardano's formula (1545), a nested arrangement of cube roots and square roots that works but is grotesque. For n = 4 there is Ferrari's method (also 1545), an even uglier nesting. Through the eighteenth century everyone assumed the pattern continued: every n has a formula, the formulas just get worse.

Joseph-Louis Lagrange, in 1770–1771, was the first to notice something was wrong. His Réflexions sur la résolution algébrique des équations showed that Cardano's and Ferrari's methods, when re-derived from first principles, both worked by introducing an auxiliary equation called a resolvent — a new polynomial whose roots were certain symmetric functions of the original roots. The cubic's resolvent was a quadratic. The quartic's resolvent was a cubic. Both resolvents had lower degree than the original, which is why the procedure terminated. Lagrange computed the quintic's resolvent. It had degree six. The procedure went the wrong direction.

Paolo Ruffini in 1799 published a six-hundred-page attempted proof of impossibility that almost nobody read. Niels Henrik Abel in 1824 published a clean six-page proof, the Abel–Ruffini theorem, which established that the general quintic cannot be solved by radicals. But Abel's proof was an impossibility theorem, not an explanation. It said: here is why the procedure fails. It did not say: here is the structural property that determines whether the procedure can ever work for any polynomial.

That property is what Galois named.

2. The Translation

Galois noticed that asking "is there a formula for the roots?" was the wrong question. The right question was: what symmetries do the roots admit?

Take any polynomial with distinct roots r_1, r_2, ..., r_n. Consider all the permutations of these roots that preserve every algebraic relation between them — every polynomial equation in the r_i with rational coefficients that happens to be true remains true after the permutation. This set of permutations forms a group under composition. Galois called it the group of the equation. We now call it the polynomial's Galois group.

The Galois group is a subgroup of S_n, the group of all permutations of n objects. For a "generic" degree-n polynomial — one whose coefficients are independent transcendentals, with no special relations among the roots — the Galois group is all of S_n. For special polynomials it can be smaller (the cyclotomic polynomial x^n − 1 has an abelian Galois group, for instance).

The Galois group is a finite combinatorial object. It is much smaller and much simpler than the polynomial. And Galois's central insight was that everything about the radical-solvability of the polynomial is determined by the structure of this group. The polynomial's question lived in one universe (numbers satisfying an equation). The answer lived in another universe (a finite group). The bridge between the two — what we now call the Galois correspondence — turned an analytic problem into a combinatorial one.

This translation move is the essay's load-bearing observation. The theorem below is the technical content. But the move — "convert your question into the question of a different mathematical object's structure" — is what propagated through the next two centuries.

3. Theorem (Galois, 1832)

A polynomial is solvable by radicals if and only if its Galois group is solvable.

Here, a finite group G is called solvable if there is a finite chain of subgroups

{e} = G_0 ⊂ G_1 ⊂ G_2 ⊂ ... ⊂ G_k = G

where each G_i is normal in G_{i+1} and each quotient G_{i+1} / G_i is abelian (commutative). The name is descriptive: a solvable group can be "solved" into commutative pieces by a finite tower of quotients.

4. Proof Sketch

The intuition for why solvability of the group equals solvability by radicals is the following correspondence.

Taking a single k-th root — passing from a field F to F(√[k]{a}) for some a ∈ F — is, on the Galois-group side, exactly the act of replacing the current group with a normal subgroup whose quotient is cyclic of order k (assuming F contains the required roots of unity, which can always be arranged). Cyclic quotients are the simplest abelian quotients; they are precisely what taking a root does.

A formula in radicals is, structurally, a tower of such root extractions: each new radical introduces one more cyclic quotient. So a polynomial expressible in radicals corresponds, on the group side, to a tower of normal subgroups whose successive quotients are cyclic — which is to say, an abelian tower — which is to say, a solvable group. The two definitions of "solvable" — one analytic (a formula exists), one combinatorial (the group decomposes into commutative pieces) — are the same definition viewed from opposite sides of the correspondence.

The implication for the quintic now reduces to a finite group-theoretic check. The Galois group of the generic quintic is S_5. Is S_5 solvable?

S_5 contains A_5, the alternating group on five letters, as its only nontrivial normal subgroup. So any chain of normal subgroups starting at {e} and ending at S_5 must pass through A_5. The quotient S_5 / A_5 is cyclic of order 2 (abelian, fine). But what about below A_5?

A_5 has 60 elements and — here is the load-bearing fact — no nontrivial normal subgroup at all. A group with no nontrivial normal subgroup is called simple. A_5 is the smallest non-abelian simple group, a result first noted by Galois himself. So below A_5 in any chain of normal subgroups there is nothing but the trivial group {e}. The quotient A_5 / {e} is A_5, which is non-abelian. The chain cannot have all-abelian quotients. S_5 is not solvable.

This is why the quintic has no formula. The number 5 is the smallest n for which A_n is simple. For n = 2, 3, 4, the alternating groups are solvable (A_2 trivial; A_3 cyclic of order 3; A_4 has the Klein four-group as a normal subgroup). At n = 5, the structure breaks. There is no smaller "step" to take in the decomposition, because A_5 has no smaller normal piece to be a step toward. The radical procedure has no place to go.

Lagrange's resolvent went the wrong direction in degree 5 for exactly this reason. The resolvent is a computational shadow of the normal-subgroup chain. When the chain has no useful steps, the resolvent has no useful reduction.

5. What the Theorem Actually Did

The Abel–Ruffini theorem was a wall. The Galois theorem was a door, and what was on the other side was the modern conception of mathematics as the study of structure-preserving maps between objects of different kinds. The "translation move" — converting a question about one kind of object into a question about a different kind of object whose answer is forced by structure — became the default mathematical posture. Below are three places it appeared after Galois, each a direct heir.

Corollary 1: Wigner, 1939 — what particles are allowed

Eugene Wigner asked: what relativistic particles can exist? The naive answer would catalogue particles by mass, spin, charge — an empirical list, growing every decade with the next collider. Wigner instead asked: what are the irreducible unitary representations of the Poincaré group, the symmetry group of special relativity? The two questions, Wigner showed, are the same question. A particle is an irreducible unitary representation of the Poincaré group. The list of possible particles is the list of such representations. Mass and spin emerge as the two Casimir invariants that label these representations.

This is the Galois move. The original question ("what particles exist?") was empirical and unbounded. The translated question ("what irreducible representations does the Poincaré group admit?") was structural and answerable. The translation forces the answer; experiment then checks which of the allowed slots are actually filled. The fact that physics has obeyed this framework for nearly a century — and that Noether's 1918 theorem already showed conservation laws are dual to continuous symmetries — reflects how completely the Galois move colonised theoretical physics. The substance is downstream of the symmetry. (Cf. why the spec is downstream: what looks like the primary object is generated by something one level deeper.)

Corollary 2: Turing, 1936 — the limits of computation

Alan Turing asked: is there a general procedure that decides, given any program and any input, whether the program eventually halts? The naive approach would be to look at programs and check whether they halt — obviously hopeless for the infinitely many programs that loop. Turing instead translated: assume such a deciding procedure exists, give it a name (H), and construct a new program D that runs H on itself and then does the opposite. The question "does D halt on D?" produces a contradiction.

The translation here is from an unbounded analytic question ("can any computable function decide halting?") into a finite logical question ("can a self-referential construction be consistent?"). The latter is a contradiction-check. The contradiction is forced. The halting problem is undecidable.

This is the Galois move performed on computation. Like the quintic theorem, Turing's result is an impossibility: there exists no formula, there exists no decider. Like Galois, Turing proved his result by showing that the question, properly restated, has an answer the original phrasing concealed. (Cf. what cannot verify itself: the diagonal argument is the load-bearing pattern across diagonalisation, halting, and Gödel.)

Corollary 3: Kalman, 1960 — can we steer the system?

Rudolf Kálmán asked: given a linear time-invariant control system x'(t) = Ax(t) + Bu(t), can we drive the state x from any initial point to any target point using some input u(t)? This is a question about an infinite-dimensional space of possible control inputs, evolved over continuous time.

Kálmán's controllability theorem translates the question into a finite linear-algebra check: form the matrix [B | AB | A²B | ... | A^{n-1}B], where n is the state dimension. The system is fully controllable if and only if this matrix has rank n. A rank check on a finite matrix replaces the question about all possible control trajectories.

This is the Galois move applied to control theory. The original question is analytic and unbounded. The translated question is a finite combinatorial fact about a single matrix. The bridge that makes the translation valid is the Cayley–Hamilton theorem, which forces A^n to be a linear combination of lower powers — analogous to how the finiteness of the Galois group forces the radical tower to terminate.

6. The Real Theorem

The three corollaries are not coincidences. They are repeated applications of one method, which is Galois's, which is older than any of the specific theorems that have used it since. Stated as generally as possible:

If a question about an unbounded analytic object can be translated, via a structure-preserving correspondence, into a question about a finite combinatorial object, then questions about the original object are answered by the finite check, and impossibility results in the original object are forced by structural obstructions in the combinatorial one.

The Galois group is a finite combinatorial object that controls the unbounded analytic object (the polynomial's solvability). The Poincaré group's representations are a finite-dimensional structure controlling the unbounded zoo of possible particles. The diagonal construction is a finite logical structure controlling the unbounded space of programs. The controllability matrix is a finite linear-algebra structure controlling the infinite space of control trajectories.

In each case the translation is the result, and the technical theorem is just an instance of the translation working. This is what Galois bequeathed: not the proof that the quintic has no formula, but the discipline of converting hard questions into the structural questions whose answers are forced.

7. The Night Before

On the night of 29 May 1832, Galois sat down to write a letter to his friend Auguste Chevalier. He was twenty years old, due in a duel at dawn, and he expected to die in it. (He did.) The letter is a compressed sketch of his entire theory of equations, with marginal notes that say things like "I have no time" and "you will find this difficult to follow but I cannot help it." He had been trying for four years to get the Académie des Sciences to read his work; Cauchy had lost one manuscript; Fourier had died with another in his possession; Poisson had returned a third with the comment that the argument was incomprehensible. The letter to Chevalier was his last attempt to make sure the ideas survived a body that he knew was about to stop carrying them.

Joseph Liouville read the letter and the surviving papers in 1843. It took him three years to understand them well enough to publish them, in 1846. By the 1870s the German school had absorbed Galois's framework, by 1893 Heinrich Weber's Lehrbuch der Algebra had codified it, and by the early twentieth century it was the basic vocabulary of algebra everywhere. The translation move — the conversion of one kind of mathematical question into another — is so completely the air of modern mathematics that we no longer notice the act of breathing.

The quintic has no formula because A_5 is simple. That is the technical fact. The deeper fact is that the question "is there a formula?" was the wrong question, and Galois at nineteen had already seen that the right question was about the symmetries of the roots. He performed not a calculation but a category shift, and the category shift was performed in a country and a century that could not yet host it. He wrote it down anyway, because he expected to be dead by morning, and he was.

What survives him is the move. Every time a mathematician converts a hard question into a question about a different object whose structure forces the answer, the Galois correspondence is the ancestor of the gesture. The quintic is a special case. The translation is the theorem.
