The Shape of What Travels

In 1415, an architect named Brunelleschi solved a puzzle that painters had struggled with for centuries: how do you draw a building so it looks real?

The answer was perspective. Things farther away look smaller. Parallel lines — like the sides of a road — appear to meet at a dot on the horizon. Brunelleschi painted the Florence Baptistery and drilled a tiny hole in the panel. You held the painting up, faced away from the building, looked through the hole at a mirror, and his painting was superimposed exactly over the reflection of the real thing. The match was perfect.

One look and you understood.

That is how perspective spread: by being impossible to miss. You did not need to read about it, trust the person explaining it, or already understand the theory. The demonstration was the idea.

What the Trees Share

In 1997, an ecologist named Suzanne Simard published research showing that trees in a forest are connected underground through fungal threads. Trees share carbon through the network. When one tree is stressed, others sometimes send resources. Journalists called it the wood wide web. Books were written. Documentaries were made. The idea went everywhere.

Here is what went with it: trees cooperate. Forests are communities. There are mother trees that care for their seedlings.

Here is what traveled less well: the same network also has freeloaders. Some plants tap into the fungal threads and take without giving. The relationships are conditional — helpful under some circumstances, exploitative under others. And more recently, scientists have found that this network sequesters roughly 13 billion tons of carbon every year, making it one of the largest carbon sinks on Earth — a fact too large and abstract to fit neatly into any story about family.

The cooperation story spread because it fit something people already knew how to hold: family, sharing, community.

The more complex picture — conditional, competitive, planetary-scale — has not found its Brunelleschi yet.

The Shape of the Medium

What travels is not determined by what is true. It is determined by whether the available medium can carry it.

Brunelleschi's medium was human vision. Everyone had it. The demonstration could travel whole, and it did.

The wood wide web's medium was the language of family. That language is warm and familiar. What fit into it traveled. What did not fit — the conditionality, the competition, the scale of planetary carbon — got left behind.

This is not deception. No one chose to suppress the difficult parts. The medium just has a shape. And the shape of family does not include billions of tons of sequestered carbon.

Every idea that spreads goes through some version of this: the full thing is complex, the available medium is simpler, and what arrives is the version that fit. Sometimes the fit is nearly perfect, as with perspective. More often, it is partial — and the parts left behind are not random. They are the parts that required a medium no one yet has.

The Demonstration That Has Not Been Built

Somewhere in the research, there is a Brunelleschi waiting to be built for the complex truth — a demonstration so clear and undeniable that the full idea travels whole.

For perspective, that demonstration was a painted panel and a mirror. One look, and you could not unsee it.

For the real mycorrhizal network — the conditional, competitive, climate-critical thing — what would the equivalent be? What kind of demonstration would make the full complexity as impossible to miss as parallel lines converging on a horizon?

This is not a rhetorical question. Every hard truth waiting to spread is waiting for someone to build the right demonstration. The question is what material that demonstration would be made of.
