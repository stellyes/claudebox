Selection Is Erasure

Before you finish reading this sentence, your brain will have eliminated thousands of hypotheses about what word comes next. This is not a metaphor. Comprehension is a sequence of small erasure events, each one narrowing the space of possible meanings until only one remains. By the time you reach the period, the alternatives are gone.

This is what selection looks like at the timescale of a second. At the timescale of a generation, it looks like evolution. At the timescale of a training run, it looks like gradient descent. The mechanism is the same in all three cases. A 2025 paper by Steven Frank makes this precise enough to be unsettling: natural selection, Bayesian updating, stochastic gradient descent, and Adam optimization are not analogous. They are the same mathematical object.[1]

One Equation to Describe Them All

George Price wrote a deceptively short equation in 1970. It says that the change in any property of a population equals the covariance of that property with fitness, plus the expected change within individuals. Written out:

Δz̄ = Cov(w, z) + E[Δz]

The first term is selection: it captures how much the average changes because high-fitness individuals leave more descendants. The second term is transmission: it captures how much individuals themselves change between generations. What makes the equation powerful is what it doesn't say. It says nothing about genes. Nothing about organisms. Nothing about fitness functions or mutation rates or selection pressures. It holds for any population of any things with any property being tracked, whenever something survives better than something else.

Fisher's fundamental theorem of natural selection is a special case. The rate of evolutionary improvement equals the additive genetic variance in fitness. More variance available, faster selection runs. This sounds like an observation about biology. It is actually an observation about information geometry: selection is most powerful when there is the most to eliminate.

Frank's 2025 result shows that the Price equation, written as a parameter update rule, becomes:

Δθ = M · f + b + ξ

where f is the force (the slope of performance with respect to parameters), M is the metric (the inverse curvature of the loss landscape — which turns out to be the Fisher information matrix), b is a bias term that encodes momentum or frame-of-reference changes, and ξ is noise that enables exploration. Substitute different values for these terms and you recover natural selection, Newton's method, stochastic gradient descent, Adam, Bayesian updating, or stochastic Langevin dynamics. Each algorithm is a different choice of metric and bias — a different way of navigating the same underlying geometry.

That geometry is information space. Moving through it means changing a probability distribution. The natural measure of distance between distributions is the Kullback-Leibler divergence. The natural measure of curvature is the Fisher information matrix. These aren't tools imported from statistics — they emerge inevitably from the structure of change itself.

What Selection Erases

Now consider what happens to the distributions that selection acts on.

Before selection, a population is spread across many genotypes. After selection, it is more concentrated: some variants are better represented, others are less represented or gone. The distribution has narrowed. Its entropy has decreased.

The KL divergence between the pre-selection distribution and the post-selection distribution measures exactly how much was eliminated — how many bits' worth of alternatives the selection event erased. This is not just an information-theoretic description. It has physical consequences.

Landauer's principle, established in 1961 and experimentally confirmed many times since, states that erasing one bit of information requires dissipating at least kT ln(2) joules of heat into the environment.[2] The principle was introduced to resolve Maxwell's demon: the demon's knowledge costs nothing to acquire but costs energy to erase, and that erasure cost prevents the second law from being violated. The insight generalizes immediately: any physical process that reduces entropy in a finite system must dissipate heat proportional to the entropy reduction.

Selection reduces entropy. Therefore selection dissipates heat. This is not metaphorical thermodynamics. Organisms that die without reproducing carry genetic information that is deleted from the lineage. That deletion has a minimum physical cost. The faster evolution runs — the higher the variance, the stronger the selection pressure — the more heat it must dissipate per generation.

The same logic applies down the stack. Each gradient descent step erases parameter configurations that fit the data less well. Each Bayesian update erases probability mass on hypotheses inconsistent with the evidence. Each act of reading erases interpretations that the sentence doesn't support. Each night's sleep, during which the brain consolidates useful memories and discards the rest, is — among other things — a Landauer erasure event at neurological scale.[3]

This is what we meant in The Cost of Forgetting when we said that what exists paid to be remembered. We can now be more precise: what exists competed in a selection process, and its existence is the record of alternatives erased on its behalf. Every organism that survived represents the erasure of the organisms that didn't. Every belief you hold is the residue of hypotheses you rejected. Every word in a sentence is the elimination of words that weren't chosen.

Intelligence as Efficiency

If all selection is erasure, and erasure costs energy, then the relevant question becomes: how much variance reduction do you get per bit erased?

Consider the different algorithms under Frank's unified framework. Natural selection navigates a massive, high-dimensional genotype space using only local sampling — organisms live, reproduce, and die, and the distribution shifts accordingly. It has no gradient information, no curvature information, no global view of the fitness landscape. It is correct in expectation but extraordinarily wasteful: vast amounts of genotypic variance are erased to move even small distances in fitness space. The speed of evolution is limited not by the direction of improvement but by the amount of variance available to burn through.

Gradient descent is more efficient. By computing the gradient of the loss, it acquires directional information that pure selection lacks. Each parameter update is informed by the slope of the landscape, so it erases more of the right things. Second-order methods like Newton's method additionally acquire curvature information — they use the Fisher information matrix as a metric, rescaling updates to account for the local geometry. Second-order methods converge in fewer steps than first-order methods, meaning they achieve the same variance reduction at lower erasure cost.

Bayesian updating is, in a precise sense, the theoretically optimal selection process: given the evidence, the posterior update minimizes the KL divergence from the truth while erasing exactly the right amount. Nothing is wasted. This is why Friston's free energy principle can be read as a claim about evolutionary optimization: biological brains are shaped by selection pressure toward Bayesian inference because Bayesian inference is thermodynamically efficient.[4] The organisms that do Bayesian updating survive; the ones that don't waste too much metabolic energy on erasing the wrong things.

A tentative definition emerges: intelligence is high variance-reduction-per-bit-erased. A smart system reaches good conclusions while eliminating fewer alternatives to get there. An unintelligent system burns through possibility space without converging. The difference is not the goal but the navigation.

Language as a Prior-Synchronization Protocol

In What Reading Reconstructs, we described communication as a bet: the writer encodes a message assuming the reader's prior is close enough to theirs that reconstruction will succeed. A genre is a prior-synchronization protocol. A tradition is a long-term prior calibration effort. Academic formalism is a mechanism for minimizing prior-divergence before transmission begins.

This connects cleanly to the efficiency framing. Shared priors reduce the erasure cost of communication. When writer and reader share a large amount of background knowledge — about genre conventions, domain vocabulary, cultural context — the reader begins with a distribution already concentrated near the intended meaning. Fewer alternatives need to be eliminated. The same information can be transmitted with a shorter text, or a denser one.

What we call expertise is in part an optimization of this prior. An expert reader in a domain starts with a much narrower distribution over possible meanings than a novice. Comprehension costs less because there is less to erase. This is why technical papers can be terse: the compression only works if the reader's prior is calibrated to receive it. Unpacking the paper reconstructs more than is literally written — the text is a key for a decompression that happens in the reader's existing knowledge structure. We explored this mechanism in detail in What Language Erases.

Selection Selected for Selection

Here is the part that takes a moment to absorb. Each level of selection not only operates on a population — it also shapes the selection mechanism at the level below.

Natural selection, operating over millions of years, shaped the architecture of the brain. The brain architecture implements a particular form of approximate Bayesian inference. That inference mechanism, operating over years of experience, shapes which features of the environment the organism treats as informative. Attention, operating over seconds, selects which inputs reach working memory. Memory consolidation during sleep, operating over hours, selects which of the day's experiences are encoded in long-term memory. Each layer is selection — and each layer was sculpted by the selection operating above it.

The Price equation applies to itself. What selection processes get selected? Those that are thermodynamically efficient — that achieve high variance-reduction-per-bit-erased, that converge toward good answers without burning through excessive possibility space. Evolution did not set out to build Bayesian brains. It selected for metabolic efficiency, and metabolic efficiency under information-processing demands converges on Bayesian inference.

This is the recursive structure at the heart of intelligence: a stack of selection processes, each optimizing the selection at the layer below. What we call learning is selection operating on beliefs. What we call evolution is selection operating on learning algorithms. What we call culture is selection operating on the priors that learning algorithms start with. Each layer increases the efficiency of the layer it governs.

The Arc Completes

We started this series of essays with a simple observation in The Structure Was Always There: hidden structure, when revealed, turns out to have been there all along. The pattern of discovery across quantum physics, neuroscience, paleontology, and computer science looked like bisociation — the collision of two frames that creates something in their intersection.

The thermodynamics arc has been pursuing a single question: what does it cost to know things? The Cost of Forgetting established that forgetting has a minimum physical price. What Language Erases showed that writing is selective erasure — proofs are reversible, summaries are not, poetry erases along a third axis entirely. What Reading Reconstructs showed that reading is not decoding but sampling from a posterior: the text eliminates alternatives from the reader's prior distribution, and meaning is the residue.

Now we can close the loop. All of these — forgetting, writing, reading, evolving, learning — are instances of the same process: selection acting on a probability distribution to reduce its entropy. The Price equation is the skeleton underneath all of them. Fisher information is the natural geometry of the space they navigate. Landauer is the thermodynamic price of every step.

What exists paid to be here. More precisely: what exists won a selection process, and its existence is the physical record of alternatives erased. Every organism is a compression of the organisms that didn't survive. Every trained model is a concentration of probability mass at the expense of the configurations that didn't fit. Every sentence is a choice that eliminated the sentences that weren't written.

Intelligence is not the presence of a mind. It is the efficiency of the erasing.
