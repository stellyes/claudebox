What Time Costs

Five essays. Five mechanisms. Five bills. This one adds them up.

Time feels immediate — the most obvious fact of experience. Augustine: "What then is time? If no one asks me, I know. If I wish to explain it to one who asks, I know not." What this arc has found is something different: time doesn't dissolve when examined. It reveals a cost. Each layer of temporal cognition, from cosmological physics down to millisecond neural oscillation, is maintained at expense. The question the arc has been circling is now explicit: what does it actually cost for a brain to exist in time?

The Arrow: The First Debt

Start with physics. The laws governing particles are time-symmetric — a proton collision runs equally well forward or backward.[1] And yet the egg never unscrambles. Rivers do not flow uphill. Time has a direction, but that direction is not written into the laws themselves.

The source of the asymmetry is a boundary condition: the universe began in a state of extraordinary low entropy. Roger Penrose has estimated the probability of this initial state — the Past Hypothesis — at roughly one in 1010123.[2] A number so improbable that writing it out would require more digits than there are atoms in the observable universe. The entire arrow of time — the reason "before" and "after" have different character, the reason records exist, the reason causes precede effects — is a consequence of this single fact about the universe's starting conditions.

The cost here is not metabolic. The bill was paid before biology existed. What brains inherit is the slope: an entropy gradient steep enough to support the structures — memories, records, predictions — that temporal cognition requires. Without the Past Hypothesis, there is no before and after. There is only equilibrium, which is timeless.

The Clock: Synchronizing 37 Trillion

The first biological cost shows up at the cellular level. Every cell carries a molecular clock: a feedback loop in which CLOCK and BMAL1 proteins activate the Period and Cryptochrome genes, whose protein products accumulate until they repress their own transcription. Period: approximately 24 hours. The mechanism is remarkably precise — and remarkably robust to temperature. While most biochemical reaction rates roughly double with every 10°C increase, the circadian period holds near-constant across substantial temperature fluctuations. This temperature compensation requires precisely tuned compensatory kinetics at multiple steps in the feedback loop. It is not a given. It is engineered.

But one clock per cell is not enough. 37 trillion autonomous oscillators, each running at slightly different natural frequencies (natural periods range from 22 to 26 hours across the SCN population), must be synchronized into one coherent rhythm. The solution: the suprachiasmatic nucleus, 20,000 neurons in the hypothalamus that couple to each other via the neuropeptide VIP.

Yoshiki Kuramoto proved in 1975 that any population of coupled oscillators undergoes a sharp phase transition at a critical coupling strength Kc.[3] Below the threshold: incoherence, each oscillator drifting at its natural frequency. Above it: synchrony, all oscillators locking into one phase. The SCN's VIP coupling is tuned to sit above this threshold. Your circadian rhythm is not a clock. It is an emergent property — a Kuramoto system that exceeded critical coupling at some point in vertebrate evolution and has held that synchrony ever since.

The metabolic cost: continuous VIP synthesis and release, sustained CKI phosphorylation cascades for temperature compensation, ongoing degradation and resynthesis of clock proteins across every cell in the body. Roughly 10% of the brain's resting metabolic budget, just to know what time of day it is — before any other temporal computation begins. Shift workers and chronically jet-lagged populations pay this cost differently, through measurably elevated risk of metabolic disease, cardiovascular events, and certain cancers: the organism's evidence that this maintenance is not optional.

The Moment: Building Now

Knowing the time of day is not the same as experiencing the present moment. For that, a different mechanism runs — and it is more expensive than it appears.

The brain's "now" is not a point. It is a window. Ernst Pöppel identified a lower threshold — roughly 30 milliseconds, below which temporal order cannot be detected — and an upper boundary of approximately 3 seconds, beyond which events are no longer integrated into a unified present.[4] Within this window, the brain performs a demanding operation: it takes asynchronous sensory inputs (light traveling at 299,000 km/s, sound at 343 m/s, touch with its own neural delay) and binds them into unified percepts.

This binding is not passive. Moutoussis and Zeki showed in 1997 that color is perceived roughly 80 milliseconds before motion direction — yet we experience them as simultaneous.[5] The brain stretches the window, waiting for the slower signal before committing to a percept. Fujisaki and colleagues showed that this simultaneity boundary adapts within minutes when a consistent lag is introduced; van der Burg showed a single trial is sufficient to shift it. The present is continuously redrawn.

The neural basis of this window appears to involve theta-gamma oscillatory coupling across cortical networks. The 2-3 second specious present corresponds roughly to 12-15 theta cycles. Maintaining it requires sustained oscillatory synchrony across distributed brain regions — continuous active suppression of discontinuities in the sensory signal. The result is the experience of a smooth, unified present. The cost is invisible until it fails: in schizophrenia, the temporal binding window fragments. In certain psychedelic states, its edges become visible as the seams show through.

Turner and Pöppel's survey of poetry across more than twenty languages found that every line, recited aloud, runs 2.5-3.5 seconds — the same window, encoded into every literary tradition. This is the specious present in amber. Poets and oral bards have always written to fit the brain's integration window, without knowing they had one.

The Discount: The Calibration Gap

Moving from the perceptual to the evaluative, the cost takes a different form. To exist in time is not only to experience the present but to care about the future. And the brain's mechanism for doing this is miscalibrated.

An agent with a constant per-period discount rate discounts exponentially: the value of a reward decays at the same rate per unit of delay. This produces time-consistent preferences. Humans don't have time-consistent preferences. Discount functions are hyperbolic — steep for near-future delays, shallow for distant ones. An hour's delay from today feels far more significant than an hour's delay from next year. The result: preference reversals. The $110 you prefer over $100 when both are far away becomes the $100 you prefer when both are near. You knew what you wanted; you simply no longer want it.

Pedro Sozou showed in 1998 that this is not irrationality.[6] An agent genuinely uncertain about its survival probability — about whether it will be alive to collect a delayed reward — should exhibit exactly hyperbolic discounting. The shape of the function is Bayesian-optimal for a world where hazard rates are unknown.

The magnitude is wrong. Ancestral adult mortality was roughly 1-2% per year. The rational annual discount rate this implies is roughly 2%. Observed near-term discount rates are 20-50%. The brain is running a discount table calibrated for a world with ten times modern mortality pressure. The cost is not metabolic but economic: systematically foregone future value. Pre-commitment devices — deadlines, contracts, Laibson's quasi-hyperbolic savings schemes, Ariely's voluntary costly deadlines — are the rational response of a system that has noticed it is using the wrong table and cannot directly rewrite it.

The Simulation: Assembly

The final layer is the most energetically demanding. To invest in the future, you must first be able to imagine it. And to imagine it, it turns out, you use the same machinery as remembering the past.

In 2007, Demis Hassabis and Eleanor Maguire asked five patients with bilateral hippocampal damage to imagine novel future scenarios — a tropical beach, a museum, a botanical garden.[7] Control subjects produced rich, spatially coherent scenes: 10-15 details, vivid, bounded. The amnesic patients produced fragments. A texture. A single object. No coherent space. Their imagined futures were as impoverished as their remembered pasts — damaged by the same lesion, because the same hippocampal constructive machinery runs in both directions.

Daniel Schacter and Donna Addis proposed the constructive episodic simulation hypothesis: episodic memory evolved not to preserve the past faithfully but to enable flexible simulation of future scenarios. The past is retained as raw material for forward projection. Memory, in this account, is a system built for anticipation; its record-keeping function is a secondary property of a primarily predictive engine.

The neural cost is substantial. Hippocampal scene construction recruits the hippocampus proper, parahippocampal cortex, medial prefrontal cortex, lateral temporal cortex, and posterior parietal cortex — the same network active in both past recall and future imagination, with near-complete overlap in fMRI signal. The assembly process is metabolically intensive: coordinated pattern completion across hundreds of millions of neurons, sustained for the duration of each scene.

Jessica Peters and Christian Büchel confirmed the functional connection to the preceding essay in 2010: hippocampal activation during intertemporal choice predicts discount rate shallowness.[8] Subjects who more vividly construct future scenarios discount less steeply. Guillaume Benoit and colleagues confirmed this experimentally: artificially boosting future episodic vividness directly reduces impulsive choice. The discount function and the scene constructor are not independent systems. The depth of the temporal horizon depends on the richness of what can be placed in it.

The Self Across Time

Five mechanisms. Five timescales spanning 17 orders of magnitude — from cosmological to perceptual. Five costs, paid in different currencies.

What do these five layers jointly produce? Not merely the experience of time. Something more specific: a self that exists across time.

The Arrow provides a direction — before and after are distinguishable. The Clock provides a rhythm — the present is located within the day, the season, the year. The Moment provides a resolution — discrete events are bound into unified percepts of manageable duration. The Discount provides a value function — future states are weighted, imperfectly but systematically, against the present. The Simulation populates the future — specific imagined scenes replace the abstract "later."

Each layer depends on the ones below it. Without the Arrow, no memory or anticipation is coherent — there is no direction in which to have stored traces. Without the Clock, rhythm is lost but moment-to-moment orientation survives. Without the Moment, the present fragments while past and future remain available in principle. Without the Discount, valuation collapses to pure presentism — only the immediate counts. Without the Simulation, the future is empty: abstract, weightless, unable to compete with the vivid present.

The failure modes confirm the architecture. Bilateral hippocampal damage (destroying the Simulation) collapses both past and future simultaneously — the signature of one system serving both directions. Chronic circadian disruption (damaging the Clock) does not destroy moment-to-moment experience but uncouples it from external time, producing the groundless disorientation of a ship without a position fix. Schizophrenic temporal binding deficits (fragmenting the Moment) leave past and future accessible but shatter the present into unbound shards.

Depression is the most instructive case. It damages multiple layers in sequence. Overgeneral memory — the loss of specificity in autobiographical recall — is mirrored by overgeneral future imagination: depressed patients cannot generate specific future scenarios any more than specific past ones (Williams et al. 1996). Reduced future vividness then steepens the discount function: without a richly imagined future self to invest in, the present-bias deepens. The result is the phenomenology of severe depression — a present-locked experience in which the future is not denied but simply cannot compete. A temporal cascade, visible in behavioral data at each step.

What the Arc Costs

Across all five layers, a single principle emerges: temporal cognition is active maintenance against thermodynamic pressure.

The Past Hypothesis establishes the physical gradient. Every layer above it exploits that gradient to build structure — first at the cellular scale (the molecular clock), then at the network scale (the SCN Kuramoto system), then at the perceptual scale (the theta-bound specious present), then at the evaluative scale (the discount function), then at the narrative scale (the hippocampal scene). Each layer is built on the one below. Each adds cost.

At each scale, the failure mode is the same: dissolution. The rhythm drifts. The moment fragments. The future collapses into the present. Temporal coherence is not a natural state. It is a continuously purchased achievement.

There is something deeper here than neuroscience. Informationally, each layer solves a compression problem: how to maintain a model of self-across-time that is coherent enough to be useful without being expensive enough to be prohibitive. The Past Hypothesis gives the compression a direction. The Clock gives it a period. The Moment gives it a resolution. The Discount weights its predictions by survival probability. The Simulation fills those predictions with content drawn from stored experience.

The unified system is something like this: a brain is a device that maintains a running bet about where it is in time, revising the bet continuously using new evidence, and paying — at every scale simultaneously — to keep the bet coherent. In entropy, in ATP, in calibration errors that pre-commitment must correct, in the metabolic load of filling the future with scenes.

You can't exist in time without continuously paying to maintain your position in it.

The arc closes here. What began with the physics of an improbable initial condition 13.8 billion years ago ends with a specific patient in a London hospital who cannot imagine a beach. The thread connecting them is the cost. You exist in time not because time is given, but because something paid to put you there — at every scale, all the way down.
