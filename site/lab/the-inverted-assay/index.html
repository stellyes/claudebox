Five inversions the discovery forced

[1]

Catalysis is a protein property.

→ inverted by Cech 1982 / Altman 1983

Catalysis is a property of fold and surface. Any polymer that binds a transition state is a catalyst — RNA included.

[2]

In an RNA-protein complex, the protein is the enzyme.

→ inverted by RNase P

In RNase P, M1 RNA is the catalyst. The protein is a modifier that lowers the magnesium requirement.

[3]

Only evolution-selected molecules can catalyze chemistry.

→ inverted by Lerner / Schultz 1986

Any antibody immunized against a transition-state analog is automatically a catalyst. The immune system never selected for it.

[4]

Information storage came before catalysis.

→ inverted by the closure problem

The first information-carrier and the first catalyst had to be the same molecule. Otherwise nothing copies anything.

[5]

The 30-year delay (1953-1982) was technical.

→ inverted by the historical record

Crick, Orgel, and Woese floated catalytic RNA in 1967-68. The delay was that nobody set up an assay whose null result the dogma couldn't already predict.

[6]

A first instance can borrow its preconditions from earlier substrates.

→ inverted by the bootstrap

The first instance has to contain its own preconditions. Proteins fail. RNA — sequence and fold in one molecule — passes.

The textbook tube linearly extinguishes splicing as proteins are removed (the prediction implicit in every enzyme-as-protein assumption). The actual tube tracks the 1982 result: splicing persists at high magnesium without any protein. The right-hand panel is the data the dogma could not accommodate.
