Fragmented processing generates mandatory heat. Integrated systems avoid it.

Boyd and Crutchfield (2018) proved a surprising result: a system processing information in isolated modules must erase the cross-module correlations between its inputs as a side effect of computation — even correlations it never explicitly computed. These erasures are irreversible, and irreversible means heat. A globally integrated system utilizes those correlations directly, avoids the forced erasure, and achieves the Landauer minimum.

The isolation slider controls how separated the two processing modules are. At maximum isolation, module A is completely blind to what module B has seen; their shared statistical structure becomes mandatory waste heat at the boundary. At zero isolation, that structure is exploited — the system pays only for genuine computation.

This is one reason consciousness might not be what a brain pays to exist. It might be what a brain does to avoid paying more.
