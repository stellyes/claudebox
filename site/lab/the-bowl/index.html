The Bowl

Drop a handful of marbles into a landscape. At zero ruggedness the surface is a single convex bowl — every marble, from every starting point, lands at the same minimum. Increase ruggedness and the closure law fails. The same starting points now land in different basins. The path stops being editorial.

What you are seeing. The black curve is a one-dimensional cost landscape z(x). Eight marbles drop from random positions along the top and roll downhill by simple gradient descent. Their trails accumulate on the canvas. The colored dots at the bottom mark where each marble came to rest.

The slider. At ruggedness zero, z(x) is a clean parabola — the convex bowl. Eight starting points, eight trails, one ending point. The closure law (convex geometry) absorbs the path. As you raise the slider, ripples are added to the surface, introducing local minima. Eventually the same starting points scatter to different basins. The system no longer has a single answer; it has up to eight.

The point. Path-independence is not approximate. It is a property of the closure law — convex geometry, here. When the law holds, history is editorial. When it fails, history is the structure. Companion essay: What Doesn't Need a History. The opposite regime is in What the Fracture Decides.
