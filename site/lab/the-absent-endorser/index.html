In Flow Mode, the cursor tracks a smoothly moving target. The action is automatic, continuous, demanding. On Harry Frankfurt’s account of free will, only the first-order level is running: the desire to track, executing without reflection. The second-order level—the endorser who says “yes, this is what I want to want”—is offline. On Frankfurt’s terms, this is the profile of a wanton: hierarchically vacant, not because of malice, but because the reflective structure has stepped back.

In Deliberate Choice, you select between colored targets. Here the second-order level activates: you pause, weigh, endorse one option over the other. The hierarchy is complete. The action is, on Frankfurt’s account, free in the relevant sense.

The puzzle: flow feels freer than deliberate choice. The tracking task feels effortless, unified, fully “you.” The choice task feels effortful, almost alien. Yet Frankfurt’s hierarchy is only complete in the second case. The felt freedom and the philosophical structure of freedom come apart.

From “What Flow States Cannot Endorse” — a reversal of “Do Flow States Disprove Free Will?”
