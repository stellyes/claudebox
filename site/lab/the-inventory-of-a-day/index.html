The Inventory of a Day

One day. One hundred and twenty perceptions, each a different shade and a different light. Funes remembers all 120 separately, each with its own arbitrary name. A thinker forgets the differences and keeps the categories. Slide between the two modes.

Each square is one moment, twelve minutes of subjective time, coloured by the perception that filled it. Hover any square to see what Funes called it.

What remains in memory

The categories that survived the threshold. At zero, every moment is its own category. As the threshold rises, near-duplicates collapse into one name, the way the word “dog” collapses every dog you have ever seen.

Borges, “Funes el memorioso,” La Nación, 7 June 1942. The 120 perceptions are sampled in a four-dimensional sensory space (hue, brightness, texture, sound), then clustered by Euclidean distance under the forgetting threshold. The names are drawn from the registers Funes himself used: place-names of Uruguay, river-names, a few one-word objects, an occasional general.
