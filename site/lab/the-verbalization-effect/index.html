What This Demonstrates

In 2001, psychologist Sian Beilock found that asking expert performers to consciously attend to the mechanics of their skill reliably degrades performance. The effect is strongest for people who type quickly — because fast typing runs on a consolidated motor schema that operates below conscious access. Inserting verbal attention into the loop creates interference between two incompatible modes of knowing.

Philosopher Gilbert Ryle called this the difference between knowing how and knowing that. The fingers know how. The verbal mind knows that. They do not share the same storage format — and when you ask one to supervise the other, the knowing-how stumbles.
