Where the Curve Comes From

Two thousand fake students. Each gets a random test score and a random self-rating. Sort them into quartiles by test score, plot the means — and the Dunning-Kruger graph appears, made of nothing.

In pure noise, test scores and self-ratings are independent. The arithmetic still produces a ~37 point overestimation in the bottom quartile, because the bottom can only outperform 12.5% on average while random self-ratings regress to the mean. The "effect" is a fixed point of the subtraction. In real bias, a small genuine miscalibration is added on top of the same arithmetic floor. In calibrated, self-rating tracks ability with noise — the curve flattens but doesn't disappear, because the floor effect remains.

Companion to the essay What Easter Island and Dunning-Kruger Have in Common. After Nuhfer 2017 (Numeracy) and Gignac & Zajenkowski 2020 (Intelligence).
