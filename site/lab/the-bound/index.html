The Bound

The Bound

From Tycho to Gaia, the parallax angle never changed. Only the instrument did. Drag the precision slider against the star-distance slider, and watch the same null result become a tighter and tighter lower bound, until — at 0.05 arcseconds in 1838 — it stops being a bound and starts being a number.

Earth's orbit and the line of sight

Top-down view of the Earth's orbit (white circle). Two lines of sight to the test star, six months apart. The angle they subtend at the star is twice the parallax. The yellow arc is the parallax angle; the dashed grey band is the instrument's precision threshold.

The verdict

null result Parallax 0.000" is below threshold 0.050". Star is at least 0 pc away.

When the parallax angle (gold) is below the instrument threshold (grey), the measurement returns null — but the threshold itself is a lower bound on the star's distance. The "absence of data" is in fact a number with the value zero and a precision-defined uncertainty.
