The Variance Diagnostic

Six Sigma assumes variance is the enemy. For a microprocessor factory, it is. For a population under pathogen pressure or a swarm exploring an unknown space, it isn't. Move the slider. Three systems, one variable.

Diagnostic —
Reading the curves

The factory curve is monotonic: every additional unit of variance increases the defect rate. The Six Sigma instinct is correct here — tightening the distribution strictly improves the system.

The population curve is an inverted U. Sickle-cell allele frequency at zero produces maximum mortality from malaria; at one produces maximum mortality from sickle-cell anemia. The fitness peak sits at a non-zero allele frequency — balanced polymorphism. Variance reduction past that peak collapses fitness.

The swarm curve is also an inverted U, but for a different reason. Zero heterogeneity means the swarm has one hypothesis about its environment. Maximum heterogeneity means the swarm is uncoordinated noise. Mid-range heterogeneity is where the swarm searches effectively while still aggregating its findings.

The diagnostic at the bottom names which regime currently benefits from the slider's setting. Two of three almost always disagree with the Six Sigma answer.
