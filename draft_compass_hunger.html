Every fall, a young blackcap warbler hatched in a German garden takes its first flight south. It has never been to Spain. Its parents do not lead it. It has no map in any usual sense. But within its first weeks of life, it pulls itself onto a wire and points its body in a direction that, if held, will end three thousand kilometres later in a place it has never seen and that none of its siblings will see together. [1]

This is one of the most striking facts in biology, and it is taught to schoolchildren as an instance of "instinct," which is the word we use when we don't want to ask the next question. The next question is: what tells the bird where south is, and what tells it that south is the place to go? A compass and a reason. We will look at the compass first, because it is built out of physics. The reason is built out of need, and need is the more interesting part.

What the bird actually does

Birds appear to navigate with at least three independent systems: a sun compass that adjusts for the time of day, a star compass learned in the nest while looking up at night, and a magnetic compass that reads the inclination — the angle — of the Earth's field. The magnetic sense lives in special light-sensitive proteins called cryptochromes, which are concentrated in the retina, so the bird literally sees the field overlaid on the world. [2]

The most surprising piece is the inherited map. Loggerhead sea turtles hatch on a Florida beach, sprint into the water, and immediately begin a gyre — a long looping migration around the North Atlantic — that they have never been on. Ken Lohmann's lab has shown that they use the local magnetic signature of where they currently are as a kind of postcode, and that placing a young turtle in the magnetic signature of, say, the coast of Portugal will make it swim in the direction it should go from Portugal — even though the actual water surrounding it is in North Carolina. [3]

So the turtle inherits a lookup table from postcodes to directions. The bird inherits an inclination compass and the urge to fly along a particular bearing for a particular number of weeks. Each species has, written into its body, a rough version of the answer to: which way to go.

None of this is the interesting part.

The interesting part is the need

An inherited compass is useless without an inherited reason to use it. A turtle who hatches in a place with infinite food has no reason to swim a 14,000-kilometre loop. A blackcap raised in a garden with year-round insects has no reason to leave. We know this because some blackcaps have stopped migrating — over a few decades, a population in central Europe has begun wintering in British gardens, where bird feeders supply enough food. They still have the compass. They have stopped using it. [4]

The thing that makes the compass matter is hunger. More precisely: the predictable seasonal collapse of the food supply in the place the bird is currently in. The compass is calibrated, generation by generation, by a gradient — the difference between here-in-winter and there-in-winter — that selects birds who fly in the right direction at the right time. Birds who fly in the wrong direction starve. The gradient sharpens the compass. Remove the gradient, and the compass blunts.

This is what I want to call a calibrating gradient. A difference in the world that an organism has built a competence around. A mountain range, with snow on top and grass below. A coastline, with food at low tide and danger at high. A sky, with sun by day and cold by night. The competence is not stored in the body alone; it is stored in the body plus the gradient. Take away one and the other becomes ornamental.

The economist who saw it coming and didn't

In 1930, John Maynard Keynes published a short essay, "Economic Possibilities for our Grandchildren," in which he predicted that within a hundred years his grandchildren would only need to work fifteen hours a week. The math was simple: productivity was rising at about two percent a year. Compounded across a century, that meant material needs could be met with a tiny fraction of the labour they currently required. The remaining time, he thought, we would spend on art, gardening, and the cultivation of the soul. [5]

In productivity terms, he was almost exactly right. The economy of 2030 will have produced, per worker-hour, roughly what he predicted. In hours-of-work terms, he was completely wrong. We are not working fifteen hours a week. We are working forty, or sixty, and many of us would say we feel poorer than our grandparents did, even though by any material measure we are not.

The bird-and-the-magnet view explains why. Keynes assumed that need, like food in a Spanish field, was a resource you could simply have enough of. Material need is, mostly, like that. It can be satiated. The gradient of I am hungry, I am cold, I am at risk of dying this winter can be flattened. He was right that we have largely flattened it.

But human beings, like birds, do not run on a single gradient. The economist Fred Hirsch named the second one in 1976: positional goods. A positional good is one whose value comes from your having more of it than someone else does. A bigger house than your neighbour. A better school than your colleague's children. A faster car than the people on your road. Positional goods cannot be satiated, because their gradient is built into them: the moment everyone has them, no one does. [6]

The blackcap that stopped migrating because British bird feeders fixed its winter is not the same as the human being that did not stop working because GDP per capita was twenty times what it had been in 1930. The blackcap could redirect attention to other things — there was no positional version of "wintering." Humans cannot, because attention itself is a positional good (someone has to be watching you), and status is a positional good, and access to the best health care is a positional good. We flattened the gradient that we knew how to flatten and walked, blinking, into one we did not.

Two kinds of gradient

Let me try to draw the line cleanly. A flattenable gradient is a difference in the world that can, in principle, be reduced to zero by good engineering. Hunger is flattenable: more food removes it. Cold is flattenable: more shelter removes it. Disease is partly flattenable: vaccines, antibiotics, sanitation get most of the way there.

A non-flattenable gradient is one whose structure forbids satiation. Positional goods are non-flattenable. Mortality is non-flattenable: as the average lifespan rises, the worry about death does not disappear, because it is the comparison to a possible longer life, not the absolute number, that drives the worry. Attention is non-flattenable: you cannot give everyone everyone's attention. [7]

This matters because a competence calibrated on a flattenable gradient is perishable. When the gradient flattens, the competence has nothing to lean against, and within a generation or two it goes away. The blackcap's compass is rusting. The Cavendish banana, calibrated by selection against pests and then suddenly grown in plantations where pests were chemically suppressed, has no defence against the pests now coming back through resistance — because in the absence of the gradient, no defence was being maintained.[8]

A competence calibrated on a non-flattenable gradient is permanent. We will not, in any plausible future, stop competing for status, because the structure of status guarantees that gradient. We will not stop worrying about death, because no equilibrium in the relevant sense is reachable.

What this says about post-scarcity

The phrase post-scarcity usually means: a world in which material needs are met for everyone without struggle. Some version of this may be reachable. Probably it should be. But the migration-navigation view says something the optimistic version of the phrase does not: removing a calibrating gradient does not produce equilibrium. It produces drift.

Whatever competences we have built around the flattenable gradient — discipline, patience, the willingness to do hard work in the present for a payoff in the future, the entire moral economy of provision — those competences begin to erode the moment the gradient that produced them goes away. They cannot be re-derived from textbooks once they have decayed; the bird's compass cannot be restored by reading a book. Calibration requires the gradient that calibrates.

This is not an argument against ending hunger. It is an argument for being honest about what happens when hunger ends. The gradient does not vanish; it migrates. It moves from "I will starve this winter" to "my child will not get into the school the other children got into." That second gradient is non-flattenable, which is to say: we are migrating into a world structurally less satiable than the one we left. Hirsch saw it. Keynes did not.

This connects to a thread I have been following. In What the Map Won't Forget I argued that schemas can outlast their referents — phantom limbs, vestigial organs, cargo cults. That is the case where the map persists after the territory changes. The migration case is the inverse: the territory persists, but the map degrades because the calibration loop is broken. Both failure modes share an architecture: a competence held jointly by an internal structure and an external gradient, which fails when one or the other goes missing.

It also connects to Why the Spec Is Downstream, where I argued that the loop is the spec — once you stop running the loop, the specification of "what good behaviour looks like" drifts. Migration is a loop: hunger triggers flight, flight reaches abundance, abundance produces young, young inherit the loop. Stop the loop in one place — say, by feeding the blackcaps — and within a few generations the spec, which was held in the loop, is gone.

What might replace hunger

If post-scarcity is partial — material need flattened, positional need intact — then the question for the next century is which gradient calibrates which competence, and which of those we want to preserve. A child raised in a household with no flattenable scarcity may have no first-hand experience of the gradient that produced patience. They will encounter the non-flattenable gradients (status, attention, the search for meaning) and their competences will be calibrated by those instead. Some of those will be impressive. Many will be desperate, because non-flattenable gradients are the kind that produce arms races.

The honest version of the post-scarcity project, then, is not "remove all gradients." It is: which gradients do we want our children to be calibrated by? A society that wants to keep, say, the willingness to do hard slow work in the present for a payoff in the future will have to manufacture, deliberately, a flattenable gradient that requires it. The gradient cannot come from hunger if hunger is gone. It might come from learning a language, or growing food you don't strictly need, or making something with your hands. Those are scarcity gradients of a deliberately preserved kind. They are also the kinds of activity that both Keynes and the bird-feeder blackcaps somehow ended up not doing.

Sarah Perry, in an essay on what she calls the "consciousness monoculture," makes a related point about ritual: that the cultures preserving variant forms of human consciousness all required scarce, costly, group practices. [9] When the scarcity ended — when ritual became cheap or optional — the variants did not survive. Hunger is not the only flattenable gradient that we are flattening. We have been doing it to ritual, to attention, and to the difficulty of communication, for a few centuries.

The bird does not know where Spain is. It knows the angle of the field, the time of year, and that something is wrong with where it is. Calibration takes a difference. We will not run out of differences, because the world supplies the non-flattenable kind by default. But we may, if we are not careful, lose the competences that the flattenable kind taught us, and find ourselves with a fine compass and nothing it can find.

What this is not

This is not an argument that scarcity is good. Scarcity is mostly bad. Hunger should be ended. It is an argument that the metaphor of removing scarcity, as if scarcity were a layer of paint, is wrong. Scarcity is a gradient, and gradients are how living things steer. When you remove one, the steering does not become free; it begins to track whichever other gradient is left.

Two truths, then, and a speculation. The first truth is that material scarcity is mostly engineerable. The second truth is that organisms — including human ones — develop competences only against a calibrating gradient. The speculation is that the next century will spend its difficulty figuring out which gradients to manufacture deliberately, having flattened the ones that used to do the manufacturing for free.
