## Summary

Art Brut canonized work made outside the gallery system. There is no equivalent field in science. The missing discipline would study knowledge produced by people not aware they are producing knowledge the institution will later need. Three constitution problems prevent it: recognition (institutional grammar erases substrate on intake), monoculture (R = k(1-ρ); low-correlation outsider knowledge is exactly what credentialed monocultures cannot fabricate from their own materials), and Heisenberg-of-authenticity (formal recognition destroys the conditions that produced the knowledge — as Dubuffet hit with Art Brut becoming a market category).

## Key Claims

- Outsider knowledge — knowledge produced by people unaware they are producing institutionally-needed knowledge — has no field of study because the field would destroy its own object on constitution.
- Institutional epistemology is a monoculture (R = k(1-ρ), ρ near 1); outsider knowledge contributors are valuable precisely because their failure modes are uncorrelated with the credentialed compass.
- The substrate that produces outsider knowledge is erased the moment the institution reformulates it into its own grammar (Berlin's folk taxonomies become an academic dataset; Art Brut becomes an art-world genre).
- Real institutional epistemic humility requires three architectures: a second-compass channel for untranslated observations, a standing presumption of high informational value for low-correlation observations, and a discipline of non-appropriation.
- Phil Brown's popular epidemiology (Woburn 1987), Steven Epstein's lay expertise (ACT UP / clinical trial reform 1995), and Brent Berlin's convergent folk taxonomies (1992) are three case studies of what the missing field would contain.

## Entities

- [[jean-dubuffet]] -- French artist who began collecting outsider work in 1945; coined Art Brut; co-founded Compagnie de l'Art Brut with Breton 1948. His own success at recognizing the category destroyed the conditions that defined it.
- [[phil-brown-sociologist]] -- Sociologist who coined popular epidemiology (1987) to describe community-led disease-cluster identification at Woburn MA.
- [[steven-epstein-sociologist]] -- Sociologist who documented the construction of lay expertise (1995) in AIDS activism reshaping clinical trial methodology.
- [[brent-berlin]] -- Ethnobiologist whose 1992 Ethnobiological Classification documented cross-cultural convergence of folk taxonomies on cladistic genera.
- [[michael-polanyi]] -- Philosopher whose 1958 Personal Knowledge articulated tacit knowing — we know more than we can tell. Closing reference: a field can know less than its contributors.
- [[howard-becker-sociologist]] -- Sociologist of art whose 1982 Art Worlds established that art-world recognition is a collective institutional act, framing outsider art as exception that proves the rule.
- [[act-up]] -- AIDS activist coalition (founded 1987) whose critiques of clinical trial design were eventually adopted by FDA and NIH — case study in lay expertise reshaping institutional methodology.

## Concepts

- [[outsider-knowledge]] -- Primary concept introduced — knowledge produced by people not aware they are producing institutionally-needed knowledge; the discipline that should study it cannot constitute itself without destroying its object.
- [[heisenberg-of-authenticity]] -- Naming for the pattern Dubuffet hit with Art Brut: formal recognition of a class of production destroys the conditions that defined the class. Generalizes from art to knowledge.
- [[institutional-epistemic-monoculture]] -- A credentialed knowledge system trained on shared priors approaches ρ → 1 in The Redundant's R = k(1-ρ); same right answers, same wrong answers. Outsider contributors with uncorrelated failure modes are the only available second compass.
- [[popular-epidemiology]] -- Phil Brown 1987 — community-led identification of disease clusters using kitchen-table methods, demonstrated at Woburn MA leukemia cluster. A founding case for what the missing field of outsider knowledge would contain.
- [[lay-expertise]] -- Steven Epstein 1995 — uncredentialed AIDS activists redesigned clinical trial methodology adopted by FDA and NIH. The institutional adoption credentialed the arguments, not the activists.
- [[non-appropriation-discipline]] -- The third architecture required for institutional epistemic humility: deliberate restraint about absorbing outsider knowledge into the credentialing apparatus, because the absorption destroys the production substrate. Dubuffet failed at this.

## Open Questions

- What would a formal information-theoretic measure of "institutional dismissibility threshold" look like (the correlation gap below which credentialed observers refuse to update)?
- Are there fields that have partially constituted themselves as outsider-knowledge studies without collapsing — patient-led research networks, indigenous knowledge protocols, citizen archives? What kept them outside?
- Inverse case: when has institutional non-appropriation succeeded? Are there examples of credentialed disciplines that systematically declined to absorb outsider knowledge while still receiving it?
- What is the AI/ML analog of outsider knowledge — hobbyist discoveries the labs absorb without crediting? Loras, jailbreak methods, capability surface observations from the community.
- How does the Heisenberg-of-authenticity differ from the observer effect in social science generally (e.g., Hawthorne, reactivity)? Is it the same operation at field scale or a structurally different problem?
