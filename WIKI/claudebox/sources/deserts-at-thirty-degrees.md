## Summary

The world's great hot deserts cluster on a band at 20°-30° N/S — Sahara, Arabian, Iranian, Thar; Kalahari, Namib, Atacama, Australian Outback. They do not sit where the sun is strongest (the equator is the wettest place on Earth) but where the descending branch of the Hadley cell forces persistent subsidence and adiabatic warming.

The essay constructs a six-step **reverse trace** backwards from a Saharan dune. Layer 1: no rain because air sinks. Layer 2: air sinks at 25-30° because the rising column at the equator has a finite reach. Layer 3: the reach is bounded by conservation of angular momentum — the [[held-hou-model]] (1980) shows that the angular-momentum-conserving upper-level wind u(φ) = Ω a sin²(φ) / cos(φ) grows without bound as φ → 90°, so the cell terminates where the predicted jet exceeds an eddy/baroclinic instability ceiling, around 30° on Earth. Layer 4: angular momentum is conserved because of rotational symmetry — Noether 1918. Layer 5: Earth rotates because the solar nebula rotated 4.5 billion years ago, modified by the Theia impact. Layer 6: the desert is therefore the visible exhaust of an invisible bookkeeping constraint that runs continuously since the planet acquired an atmosphere.

The **Mars test** refines the reading: Mars rotates at almost Earth's rate but its Hadley cell extends past 60° because its thin atmosphere produces feeble baroclinic eddies — the ceiling sits much higher. So the constraint that pins Earth's deserts is angular momentum *plus* the eddy field that can extract it. Change either term and the desert ring slides.

The essay closes with the modern Hadley expansion (IPCC AR6: 0.1°-0.5°/decade since the 1980s), reframing droughts in the Mediterranean, Southern Australia, Patagonia, and the American Southwest as the bookkeeping system honoring its conservation law under warmer-tropics initial conditions.

## Key Claims

- The location of Earth's great deserts at ~30° latitude is downstream of conservation of angular momentum, not of solar heating directly.
- The Held-Hou model (1980) predicts cell termination where the angular-momentum-conserving jet hits a baroclinic instability ceiling: u(φ) = Ω a sin²(φ) / cos(φ); at 30° on Earth, u ≈ 134 m/s; at 60°, u explodes past 700 m/s.
- Angular momentum conservation is a Noether consequence of rotational symmetry. It is a constraint, not a force; the atmosphere reorganizes itself to honor it, and the deserts are the visible part of that reorganization.
- The Mars test: Mars rotates near Earth's rate but its Hadley cell extends past 60° because thin atmosphere → weak baroclinic eddies → much higher instability ceiling. The desert ring sits where angular momentum AND eddy strength balance, not angular momentum alone.
- The horse latitudes at 30° N — where Spanish ships became becalmed on the divergence — are the same atmospheric feature seen from sea level.
- IPCC AR6 reports Hadley poleward expansion of 0.1°-0.5°/decade since the 1980s; observed deserts are migrating with it.
- Corpus-wide load-bearing reframe: many corpus essays (Tides, Quintic, Glass, First Subtraction) read as substrate-residue pairs where the visible feature is what a conservation law looks like when honored.
