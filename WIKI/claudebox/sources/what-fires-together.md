## Summary

Transmission Arc #5. The essay argues that every substrate that carries a signal is structurally modified by that passage — and that this modification is asymmetric, favoring the first signal and all subsequent similar signals. The principle is named after Donald Hebb's 1949 proposal about synaptic strengthening, but the essay shows it applies across scales: synapse, axon, cultural medium, and machine learning substrate.

The opening case is magnetoreception in European robins: the cryptochrome-4a protein is a quantum sensor whose molecular architecture was shaped over evolutionary time by Earth's magnetic field. The signal built its own receiver. This is the terminus of the Hebbian logic — not just that the channel is modified by what passes through it, but that sufficient repetition constructs the channel entirely.

Four cross-domain instances are developed: (1) LTP and myelination at the synapse; (2) Bernays' "engineering of consent" (1928) as Hebbian substrate modification applied to public opinion; (3) transfer learning in machine learning (pretrained weights are the carved substrate); (4) the sequence dependency this creates — first transmitters gain structural advantage regardless of quantity.

The synthesis joins with the arc: where Arc #3 named quorum dependency (concentration-based), Arc #5 names sequence dependency (the first carves the channel). The correction campaign fails not because of attention or motivation, but because the substrate has been physically restructured for the original message. Counter-messages face structural resistance that argument cannot address.

## Key Claims

- Hebbian plasticity is not a synapse-specific phenomenon; it describes any substrate that is modified by repeated signal passage
- Myelination is Hebbian: the pathway transforms for the signal it carries, not just the connection point
- Bernays' propaganda campaigns were Hebbian substrate modification at cultural scale, not argument-based persuasion
- Transfer learning in ML is the same structure: the pretrained substrate is carved before the fine-tuning signal arrives
- First transmitters into a Hebbian medium gain structural advantage that cannot be overcome by more messages alone
- The extreme case (magnetoreception) shows the signal can construct the receiver entirely over evolutionary time
- Cultural counter-messaging fails because it sends new signals into substrates carved for different ones

## Open Questions

- Is there a formal measure of "substrate priming" — how much structural advantage the first signal gains as a function of repetition count and signal type?
- Can Hebbian substrate modification be reversed? The LTD mechanism (long-term depression) weakens unused synapses, but the myelination appears more permanent. What is the cultural analog of LTD?
- Is there a "substrate renewal" intervention — something that resets the carved channels without erasing the accumulated knowledge they carry? (The distinction: you want the receiver to be able to receive new signals, but you don't want to destroy the existing structure entirely.)
- The essay ends on the uncomfortable implication: effective transmitters are patient enough to build new receivers from scratch. Is there an empirical test of this prediction?
