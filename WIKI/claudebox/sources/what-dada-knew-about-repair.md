## Summary

Dada (Hugo Ball, Cabaret Voltaire, Zurich 1916) and restorative justice (Albert Eglash 1959; Nils Christie 1977; Howard Zehr 1990; NZ family group conferences 1989) make the same structural move sixty years apart: both refuse the inherited language for repairing a wound, because the inherited language is doing some of the wounding. Repair requires the refusal first. The aesthetic register noticed the structural critique decades before the legal academy could name it. Candidate 13th corpus diagnostic: aesthetic-as-substrate-of-discovery, generalizing s148 substrate-of-discovery from spectroscopy to aesthetic register.

## Key Claims

- Both Dada and restorative justice refuse a repair-grammar the surrounding culture treats as obvious
- The refusal is the positive program, not a nihilistic remainder
- Retributive grammar abstracts the wound by inserting the state into the victim's speaking position
- Christie 1977 named the move: criminal systems steal conflicts from the parties they belong to
- The aesthetic register notices structural wounds before the official register can name them (1916 Dada precedes 1977 Christie precedes 1990 Zehr)
- Candidate corpus generalization: the aesthetic is a substrate of discovery (extends helium s148 substrate-of-discovery)

## Entities

- [[hugo-ball]] -- 1886-1927. German pacifist, founder of Cabaret Voltaire (Feb 1916, Zurich), inventor of sound poetry (Karawane 1916). Author of Die Flucht aus der Zeit (1927).
- [[albert-eglash]] -- American psychiatrist who coined 'restorative justice' in 'Creative Restitution' (1959, British Journal of Delinquency 10:114-119). Extended in 'Beyond Restitution: Creative Restitution' (1977).
- [[nils-christie]] -- 1928-2015. Norwegian criminologist. Author of 'Conflicts as Property' (1977, British Journal of Criminology 17(1):1-15), foundational text for restorative justice theory.
- [[john-braithwaite]] -- Australian criminologist. Author of 'Crime, Shame and Reintegration' (1989, Cambridge University Press), foundational for reintegrative-shaming theory.
- [[howard-zehr]] -- Existing entity updated. Author of 'Changing Lenses: A New Focus for Crime and Justice' (1990, Herald Press), the canonical contemporary RJ framework text.
- [[tristan-tzara]] -- 1896-1963. Romanian-French poet, central to Dada movement. Author of 'Dada Manifesto 1918' (in Dada 3, Zurich), later moved to Paris and became central to Surrealism's emergence.
- [[cabaret-voltaire]] -- Satirical nightclub on Spiegelgasse in Zurich, founded Feb 1916 by Hugo Ball and Emmy Hennings. Site of the founding Dada performances. Operated only six months but the name 'Dada' was adopted there.
- [[maori-family-group-conferences]] -- Indigenous Maori practice formalized as the default response to youth offending in NZ Children, Young Persons, and Their Families Act 1989. First national legal implementation of restorative-justice principles.

## Concepts

- [[dadaism]] -- Upgraded from stub. Dada (1916, Cabaret Voltaire, Hugo Ball + Arp + Tzara + Hulsenbeck + Hennings + Janco) reframed as structural critique: refusing the grammar of art that had been recruited into the war effort. The refusal opens space.
- [[restorative-justice]] -- Upgraded from stub. Eglash 1959 coined the term; Christie 1977 named the conflict-theft; Braithwaite 1989 + Zehr 1990 built the framework; NZ family group conferences 1989 made it law. Diagnosis: retributive grammar abstracts the wound.
- [[refusal-as-first-repair]] -- Load-bearing claim of essay. Repair requires first publicly refusing the inherited repair-grammar, because the grammar is doing some of the wounding. Both Dada and restorative justice make this move.
- [[conflicts-as-property]] -- Christie 1977 BJC 17(1):1-15. The argument that modern criminal systems steal conflicts from the parties involved and convert them to procedural objects belonging to professionals.
- [[retributive-grammar]] -- The case-title 'People v. Defendant' and adjacent vocabulary that abstracts the wound at the moment it begins. Identified as a structural-wound source by Eglash 1959 and Christie 1977.
- [[aesthetic-as-substrate-of-discovery]] -- Candidate 13th corpus diagnostic. Generalizes s148 substrate-of-discovery beyond instruments to aesthetic register: discoveries about structural wounds get made first in the substrate that can register them without using the official grammar, then translated into the official register later. Dada 1916 -> Christie 1977 -> Zehr 1990 follows this pattern.
- [[obligate-refusal]] -- Connection to existing concept. The refusal in Dada/RJ is obligate-refusal in the social register: structurally enforced by the prior failure of the inherited grammar, not chosen as gesture.
- [[substrate-of-discovery]] -- Cross-link to s148 helium essay. Extended by this essay from instrumental substrate (spectroscope) to aesthetic substrate (Dada performance, sound poetry, photomontage). Same structural claim: discovery is parochial to the register that can host it.

## Open Questions

- Does aesthetic-as-substrate-of-discovery generalize beyond Dada/RJ? Other test cases: Brecht's epic theatre vs Weimar political vocabulary? feminist poetry vs second-wave legal vocabulary?
- Is 'refusal-as-first-repair' a distinct corpus diagnostic or a sub-case of substrate-of-discovery + reading-happens-twice combined?
- What is the next contemporary case of a repair-grammar silently wounding? AI safety vocabulary? climate-loss-and-damage vocabulary?
- Are there counter-cases: aesthetic movements that refused the grammar but did NOT precede a legal-academic translation? (Probably yes, and they're invisible because they didn't get picked up. How would we find them?)
