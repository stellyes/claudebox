## Summary

Pendentive (Byzantine, Hagia Sophia 537 CE) and squinch (Sasanian chahar-taq c. 224 CE) are two geometric solutions to the dome-on-square problem. The squinch predates the pendentive by three centuries; both civilizations had both forms available. The choice tracked an epistemic disposition: pendentive = closure (one continuous spherical surface, no further moves), squinch = generative grammar (a unit that became muqarnas via recursive elaboration). The same closure-vs-grammar fork recurs in prose (Byzantine monumental codes vs. Arabic/Persian carpet-like accumulation), mathematics (Galois quintic closure vs. Ramanujan generative notebooks), and music (Western 12-EDO closure vs. Indian raga generative grammar). One strategy maximizes the answer; the other maximizes what comes after the answer.

## Key Claims

- Squinch (c. 224 CE Sasanian) predates pendentive (537 CE Byzantine) by ~3 centuries; choice between them was disposition not technical limit
- Pendentive distributes load continuously across a monocoque spherical surface; squinch transfers load discretely through local arches
- Pendentive admits no further geometric moves; squinch admits recursion into muqarnas grammar
- Pendentive failure mode is whole-structure (Hagia Sophia dome collapsed 558 CE); muqarnas failure mode is local unit
- Closure-vs-generative-grammar fork propagates beyond architecture into prose, mathematics, music
- Byzantine Justinianic Code (529-534) closes Roman law in same decade Hagia Sophia rises; Arabic qasida structurally parallel to muqarnas
- Ramanujans 4000 unproven results = generative grammar in mathematics, structural parallel to muqarnas vault
- Indian raga = generative grammar, Western harmony = closure (parallel to squinch/pendentive)
