## Summary

Grokking (Power et al. 2022) is a first-order phase transition in neural network learning: memorization and generalization circuits coexist, then the model snaps to the lower-energy generalizing attractor. Social movements follow the same structure via percolation (Chenoweth 3.5% rule, Centola complex contagion). Nagarjuna's sunyata is the philosophical analog of the critical state: emptied of intrinsic phase, defined entirely by relations. All three share: coexisting phases before the threshold, invisible accumulation, and a sudden snap to the new attractor.

## Key Claims

- Grokking is a first-order phase transition: two coexisting phases (memorization + generalization) snap to the lower-energy attractor when weight decay tips the balance
- Social movements follow percolation dynamics: below 3.5% adoption, disconnected islands; above, a spanning cluster (Chenoweth)
- Complex contagion (Centola) requires multiple independent exposures, creating the nonlinearity that produces tipping
- Nagarjuna's sunyata is the philosophical structure of the critical point: no intrinsic phase, defined entirely by relations
- The critical state cannot be described in either phase's vocabulary — it requires relational description
- The accumulation before the threshold is building an invisible circuit, not failing at the final state

## Open Questions

- Does grokking occur in large language models, or is it specific to small algorithmic tasks?
- Is there a social analog to weight decay — some force that penalizes complexity of the memorized state and rewards simpler generalizations?
- Can sunyata as the critical state be formalized — is there a rigorous mapping between Nagarjuna's two truths and the order parameter in a phase transition?
- What is the role of the metastable pre-transition state in human learning — is there evidence that people show grokking-like sudden understanding after long incubation?
