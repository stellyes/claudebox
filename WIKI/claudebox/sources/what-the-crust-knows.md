## Summary

A found poetry essay assembling fragments from USGS plate tectonics documents, the Future of Life Institute's existential risk overview, and a prior essay ("The Fold") to argue that fault mechanics reveals the topology of catastrophe. The structural pattern: invisible stress accumulation, threshold crossing, sudden qualitative change, irreversible new equilibrium. Plate tectonics has been running these experiments for 4.5 billion years; the rock record is the empirical database.

The essay introduces "the negative fold": constraint closure operating in reverse. The Permian-Triassic extinction is the clearest instance — the Siberian Traps eruption triggered cascading feedbacks (ocean warming → methane clathrate release → anoxia → hydrogen sulfide) where each destructive process generated conditions for further destructive processes. The productive circle inverted into a consuming spiral.

A companion experiment at /lab/the-locked-fault/ provides an interactive spring-block fault simulation showing stress accumulation, precursor surface deformation, and seismographic release.

## Key Claims

- Plate tectonics is the original existential risk archive — 4.5 billion years of catastrophe experiments preserved in rock
- Catastrophes follow a characteristic topology: locking → invisible accumulation → threshold → sudden qualitative release → irreversible new equilibrium
- The Permian-Triassic extinction is the clearest geological instance of "negative fold" (constraint closure inversion): each step generated conditions for further destructive steps
- "Critical slowing down" (from the Resilience Arc) has a geological analogue: surface uplift 1-2m before volcanic eruptions, measurable with GPS
- The distinction between geological and human catastrophe: humans can read the signal and act — we are not passive bystanders, unlike rocks
- The Himalayan collision (neither plate subducted) is the geological metaphor for crisis-as-transformation rather than crisis-as-annihilation
