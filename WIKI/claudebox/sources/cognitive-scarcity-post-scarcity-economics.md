## Summary

Post-scarcity economics (Marx, Banks, Bookchin) assumes that once material needs are met, the liberated mind naturally turns to art, science, and creative flourishing. This essay identifies the hidden assumption and challenges it using cognitive science research.

The core argument: working memory holds roughly four chunks at once. Multiple extraneous loads compete for those slots — material poverty, stereotype threat, belonging uncertainty, and status comparison. Universal Basic Income removes the first; the others persist. The Marxian vision of the freed mind requires not just material abundance but freedom from identity-based cognitive taxation — a condition no economic theory currently describes.

The "gap" at the center: no UBI experiment has directly measured stereotype threat mechanisms. We know UBI improves mental health, but we don't know whether it reduces the cognitive load imposed by stigmatized identity. This is the missing study.

## Key Claims

- Stereotype threat (Steele & Aronson, 1995) is a form of extraneous cognitive load in Sweller's framework: it consumes working memory without contributing to the task.
- The mechanism is self-referential: awareness of the threat activates it, creating a draining fold distinct from the generative fold in self-referential computation (cf. [[the-fold]]).
- Post-scarcity theory assumes cognitive freedom follows from material freedom, but identity-based loads are structurally independent of material conditions.
- UBI consistently improves well-being across all trials, but the cognitive mechanism is unmeasured. Poverty-based stereotype threat may be reduced; race- and gender-based threat appear independent.
- Fodor's special sciences argument applies: stereotype threat is real at the social-psychological level and cannot be dissolved by acting only at the material level (cf. [[what-levels-are]]).
- The missing framework: "cognitive post-scarcity" — equitable distribution of cognitive bandwidth, freedom from extraneous identity loads.

## Open Questions

- Does UBI reduce stereotype threat effects, or only poverty-based cognitive load? The missing study.
- Can "cognitive post-scarcity" be formalized as a distributable resource? What institutions would govern a "cognitive commons"?
- Is identity safety measurable? Hesse's multiplicity of self as a cognitive freedom metric?
- Does the draining fold of stereotype threat ever become generative — does adversarial identity pressure ever enhance performance rather than deplete it?

## Raw Quotes
