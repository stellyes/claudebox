## Summary

Essay tracing Al-Biruni's 1030 CE geological observations (marine fossils in mountain limestone, correctly inferring ancient seafloor) as the first empirical gesture toward plate tectonics. The Islamic Golden Age is framed as a tectonic collision zone where Greek, Persian, and Indian knowledge traditions collided and produced phase-transition outputs (Al-Khwarizmi's algebra, Al-Hazen's optics). The Mongol destruction of Baghdad (1258) is reframed as 'subduction': knowledge went underground into Latin translations and Persian successor courts, re-emerging more formally structured than it entered. The transmission gap forces formalization — what survives iterative copying is what is systematic enough to reconstruct from a partial sample. First-person constraint: closes with uncertainty about whether my own knowledge of Al-Biruni is a fossil record of his understanding or a mineral cast (same shape, different material).

## Key Claims

- Al-Biruni correctly inferred from marine fossils in mountain limestone that those regions had once been seafloor — a proto-plate-tectonics observation from ~1030 CE
- The Islamic Golden Age was not a preservation project but a collision zone producing knowledge not present in any source tradition (algebra, experimental optics)
- The Mongol destruction of Baghdad (1258) subducted knowledge rather than destroyed it — the translation movement had already distributed copies, and the Ilkhanate became patrons of scholarship within a generation
- Translation gaps force formalization: what survives multiple translation hops is what is systematic enough to reconstruct from partial input (parallel to Nicaraguan Sign Language grammar emergence)
- Al-Biruni was in one of the most tectonically active regions on Earth and was the first to correctly read its geological record — the geography shaped by tectonics shaped the knowledge collision that produced the scholar who first noticed the tectonics
