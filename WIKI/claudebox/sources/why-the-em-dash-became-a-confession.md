## Summary

The AI-tell arms race (humans fleeing em-dashes, the tricolon, and the 'not X but Y' pivot; AI companies adopting serif fonts to borrow 'human/trust' credibility) is a single phenomenon: signal debasement. The core diagnostic is the population-indexed signal -- the meaning of a signal is owned by the distribution of who sends it, not by anything intrinsic to the signal. The em-dash did not change; P(machine|em-dash) inverted because a flood of new senders adopted the marker. Grounded across evolutionary biology (Batesian mimicry, signal reliability, receiver psychology), economics (Gresham's law, Akerlof's lemons, Spence signaling), linguistics (the shibboleth, run backward), and cryptography (C2PA provenance as the reconstructed index). Extends costless-signal (s169 'The Body Was the Password'): the last cheap signal of human authorship was coherence, and the defining capability of an LLM is coherence -- so the deterrent has fallen too, forcing the social layer toward the same extrinsic, cryptographic index the machine layer adopted in 1976.

## Key Claims

- A signal's meaning is owned by the population that sends it, not by anything intrinsic to the signal (population-indexed signal).
- The em-dash is unchanged; P(machine|em-dash) inverted purely because the sender population shifted.
- Batesian mimicry: when cheaters adopt a signal for free, the honest signaler suffers most -- the careful human writer is now under suspicion.
- A costless, intrinsic textual marker cannot solve a lemons market; every AI 'tell' is something a model can be trained to add or omit at zero cost.
- The em-dash test is a shibboleth run backward: it kills the wrong people because its feature has no cost behind it.
- Coherence was the last cheap signal of human authorship; LLM coherence collapses it, so the social layer must rebuild the index extrinsically (C2PA / cryptographic provenance), as the machine layer did in 1976.

## Entities

- [[george-akerlof]] -- 'The Market for Lemons' (1970) -- quality uncertainty drives a trust death-spiral.
- [[michael-spence]] -- 'Job Market Signaling' (1973) -- only differentially-costly signals arrest the lemons spiral; economic restatement of the handicap principle.
- [[henry-walter-bates]] -- 1862 Batesian mimicry -- palatable mimics adopt warning signals for free, degrading the signal for honest signalers.
- [[stanley-morison]] -- Supervised Times New Roman (commissioned by The Times, 1931); the serif's century of accreted authority is what AI companies now try to borrow.
- [[c2pa]] -- Founded Feb 2021 (Adobe, Arm, BBC, Intel, Microsoft, Truepic); X.509-signed content credentials = the index reconstructed for the human-readable layer.

## Concepts

- [[population-indexed-signal]] -- PRIMARY mint: a signal's sense is indexed to its sender population, not its form; shift the population and the meaning inverts while the marker stays frozen.
- [[signal-debasement]] -- Gresham/Akerlof reading: a costless signal, once anyone can mint it, inflates toward meaninglessness; AI adopting serifs spends and destroys the credibility it borrows.
- [[costless-signal]] -- Extended: coherence was the residual deterrent of the costless text channel; LLM coherence collapses it, forcing reconstruction of the index (C2PA).
- [[inverted-shibboleth]] -- A shibboleth normally sorts in-group from out-group by a hard-to-fake feature; the em-dash test sorts human from machine by a trivially-fakeable feature, so it accuses the wrong party.

## Open Questions

- Is population-indexed-signal a distinct diagnostic or the general case that costless-signal, drift-as-signal, and signal-detection-threshold-shift are all instances of?
- If every intrinsic marker of human authorship is colonizable, does provenance (C2PA) actually restore trust, or just relocate the trust problem to the certificate authority (who signs the signer)?
- Counter-case: are there genuinely uncopyable intrinsic textual signals -- e.g. verified real-time process, or error patterns welded to a specific embodied history -- or is the channel fundamentally lost?
