## Summary

Encoding Arc #4: The bootstrap problem of codes. Hugo Ball stripped language to pre-semantic phonemic noise at Cabaret Voltaire (1916); neuromorphic computing exploits stochastic resonance and STDP where noise is the computational substrate; Crick's frozen accident describes the genetic code crystallizing from a prior plastic/random phase. All three independently discovered that codes cannot begin from signal — they begin from undifferentiated possibility and are carved by differential reinforcement. The arbitrariness identified in Arc #3 is the fossilized residue of the bootstrap noise phase.

## Key Claims

- Codes begin from noise, not from signal — noise is the prior state from which structure is carved by differential reinforcement
- Hugo Ball's Dada sound poetry (1916) was an experiment in code-layer dissolution, revealing the phonemic substrate beneath language
- Stochastic resonance shows noise improves signal detection in nonlinear systems at the right level
- STDP (spike-timing-dependent plasticity) is the bootstrap mechanism: connection patterns emerge from statistics of what fires together in an initially random network
- Crick's frozen accident describes the genetic code bootstrap: a plastic noise phase consolidated into a fixed code when downstream complexity made mutation lethal
- The arbitrariness of codes (Arc #3) is the fossilized residue of the noise phase, not a design choice
- Hebbian plasticity generalizes as a cross-substrate bootstrap mechanism
