## Summary

Contradicts Navigation Arc #3 ('navigator-free' mycorrhizal networks). Argues that indigenous land management — Aboriginal fire stick farming, Coast Salish root management, old-growth protection — was the external navigator of mycorrhizal networks over millennia. The 'pristine wilderness' Europeans encountered was a post-catastrophe anthropogenic landscape. Fire suppression and colonization removed the navigator and degraded the network. Proposes a fourth navigation mode: the co-evolutionary external navigator.

## Key Claims

- Aboriginal cool burns preserved mycorrhizal hyphal networks at depth while eliminating competing surface vegetation; pyrophilous fungi co-evolved with the regime over 65,000 years
- Coast Salish root management (camas harvesting, digging stick aeration) shaped arbuscular mycorrhizal communities; Kimmerer's sweetgrass experiment shows reciprocal harvesting stimulates rather than depletes plant populations
- Simard's Mother Trees are network hubs maintained by indigenous protection of old-growth; colonization's fire suppression removed the architectural maintenance
- High-severity megafire (product of fuel accumulation from fire suppression) reduced ectomycorrhizal richness by 43.4% with no recovery after 11 years (Pulido-Chavez et al. 2021)
- Denevan's Pristine Myth (1992): pre-Columbian American forests were heavily managed; the 'wilderness' was a post-epidemic post-management landscape
- Fourth navigation mode: co-evolutionary external navigator — distributed human practice shaping the terrain without holding an internal model of it
