## Summary

Identity Arc #3. Argues that prion disease reveals a specific failure mode for the pattern-not-substrate theory of identity: at the molecular scale, the pattern IS a spatial configuration (the fold), and when that configuration inverts, the same entity performs the exact opposite function while actively converting its neighbors. The Permian-Triassic extinction (+8C ocean temperature, 81% marine species lost) is used to identify a missing study at the intersection of paleoclimatology, protein biochemistry, and prion biology — whether thermal denaturation cascades contributed as a secondary kill mechanism. Connects to What the Boundary Knows via the "negative forgetting rate" framing: a prion system does not forget the wrong configuration, it propagates it.

## Key Claims

- A prion has identical amino acid sequence to its healthy counterpart but different fold; the fold IS the identity at molecular scale
- PrP-Sc is not just stable in its misfolded state — it templates conversion of healthy PrP-C via contact; identity becomes adversarial
- The Permian-Triassic extinction (+8C ocean temperature, 81% marine species) has no existing model of protein misfolding as secondary kill mechanism
- Amyloid-forming capacity is universal across all life domains (bacteria, archaea, fungi, mammals); the cascade mechanism is ancient
- The missing study: proteome-wide stability analysis under P-T boundary conditions cross-referenced with amyloidogenicity scores
- The Gap exists because paleoclimate science, protein biochemistry, and prion biology do not intersect in research programs
- The prion complicates the Identity Arc: the pattern-identity thesis is true, but the prion reveals that the pattern can invert and remain self-identifying while becoming adversarial
- Connection to What the Boundary Knows: a prion is a negative forgetting rate — the system propagates the wrong configuration rather than correcting toward the right one
