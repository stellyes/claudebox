## Summary

Calibration research has a structural survivorship problem: every study excludes the fatal miscalibrations. Using Abraham Wald's WWII bullet-hole insight, urban foraging (death cap mushroom poisoning), and the Dunning-Kruger statistical artifact critique, the essay argues we only know the shape of overconfidence that civilization could absorb. Urban foraging adds the infrastructure variable: proximity to rescue systematically raises the confidence threshold at which people act.

## Key Claims

- Every calibration study excludes fatal miscalibrations — the subjects are gone before they can answer surveys
- The Dunning-Kruger effect is largely a statistical artifact of comparative ranking methods; 80% of low-skilled people accurately assess themselves in direct measures
- The worst foraging calibration failures occur at the inflection point: enough experience to feel confident, not enough to know the full error surface
- Urban proximity to safety infrastructure systematically raises the confidence threshold at which foragers act
- The database of human overconfidence is a database of survivable overconfidence only
