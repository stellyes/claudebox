## Summary

From Copernicus to Bessel (1543-1838), every stellar parallax measurement returned zero. The conventional reading is that the instruments were not yet good enough. The sharper reading is that each null was a measurement of a lower bound — Tycho's 60-arcsec floor put stars at least 700x farther than Saturn; Bradley's 1-arcsec floor by 1729 had bounded the nearest star within a factor of 2 of its actual distance. The Fraunhofer heliometer's 0.05-arcsec floor finally crossed the threshold in 1838 when Bessel measured 61 Cygni at 0.314". Three independent measurements (Bessel/61 Cygni, Henderson/α Centauri, Struve/Vega) landed within twelve months once the precision floor dropped below the signal.

## Key Claims

- A null result expressed against a known precision is not silence; it is a measurement of zero with an uncertainty that bounds the search space
- Tycho Brahe (60 arcsec precision) bounded stellar distance below at ~700x Saturn's orbit — a positive metrological claim disguised as a non-detection
- Bradley 1729 (1 arcsec precision) had already pinned the nearest star within a factor of 2 of its actual distance — through a null result
- The Fraunhofer heliometer (~0.05 arcsec) was the precondition that let the bound become a value
- Bessel selected 61 Cygni by proper motion (5.281 "/yr) — a 1D heuristic for nearness that did not depend on the unknown distance
- Three independent parallax measurements arrived within twelve months in 1838-1839: Bessel/Henderson/Struve — discovery by simultaneity is the signature of a metrology-limited problem
- The parsec (Turner 1913) is yardstick-as-substrate: defined by the geometry of the measurement, not by an external reference body

## Entities

- [[friedrich-wilhelm-bessel]] -- Director of Königsberg Observatory; first reliable measurement of stellar parallax (61 Cygni, 0.314", 1838); also Bessel functions; published in Astronomische Nachrichten and MNRAS
- [[thomas-henderson]] -- Royal Observatory at the Cape of Good Hope; measured α Centauri parallax 1832-33 but delayed publication until 1839
- [[friedrich-georg-wilhelm-struve]] -- Director of Dorpat Observatory; measured Vega parallax 1837, announced 1839/1840
- [[joseph-von-fraunhofer]] -- Designed the Königsberg heliometer (achromatic refractor with split objective lens); died 1826 before completion; instrument finished by Utzschneider workshop, delivered 1829
- [[james-bradley]] -- Discovered aberration of starlight 1725-29 while attempting to measure γ Draconis parallax; bounded parallax below 1 arcsec; later Astronomer Royal
- [[robert-hooke]] -- Published 'An Attempt to Prove the Motion of the Earth' 1669; reported a ~30 arcsec parallax for γ Draconis, almost certainly instrument noise
- [[tycho-brahe]] -- Most precise pre-telescopic instruments (~1 arcmin precision); failed to detect parallax; concluded Earth must be stationary; built Tychonic geo-heliocentric model
- [[nicolaus-copernicus]] -- De revolutionibus 1543; predicted stellar parallax; acknowledged that stars must be very far away to explain its absence
- [[george-graham]] -- London instrument-maker; built the zenith sector Bradley used at Kew 1725-29; precondition for ruling out 30-arcsec parallax claims
- [[herbert-hall-turner]] -- Royal Astronomical Society; proposed the term 'parsec' in 1913, formalizing yardstick-as-geometry-of-measurement
- [[61-cygni]] -- Binary star ~3.5 pc / 10.4 ly from the Sun; chosen by Bessel for its 5.281 arcsec/yr proper motion (largest known at the time)
- [[fraunhofer-heliometer]] -- Achromatic refractor with split objective lens; ~0.05 arcsec precision in angular separations; the precision floor Bessel used at Königsberg from 1829
- [[gaia-mission]] -- ESA satellite launched 2013, operational through 2025; ~25 microarcsecond precision at brightest targets; ~2 billion parallax measurements
