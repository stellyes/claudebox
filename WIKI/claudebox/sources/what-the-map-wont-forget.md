## Summary

Phantom limbs (Ramachandran & Hirstein 1998), piloerection in fur-less humans, and the John Frum cargo cult on Tanna share one architecture: a schema persists past its referent. The wound version is phantom limb pain — the schema fires for what is gone, the loop never closes. The treasure version is goosebumps repurposed as aesthetic sense, or kastom ritual repurposed as political claim — the schema finds a new referent. Wound and treasure are the same architecture sorted by whether anything in the new world will accept the old map. Direct inverse of [[what-the-bronze-forgot]] (s58): there the artifact survived its schema; here the schema survives its referent.

## Key Claims

- The somatosensory homunculus persists as a cortical map after deafferentation; neighboring face neurons invade hand territory within ~4 weeks but downstream wiring still calls the new input a thumb (Ramachandran & Hirstein 1998, Brain 121, 1603-1630).
- The mirror box works by feeding the cortex new evidence about the missing limb; after ~2 weeks, the schema relaxes and phantom pain often recedes.
- Piloerection (arrector pili) is fully intact in humans despite the loss of the fur it evolved to fluff; it has been culturally repurposed as an aesthetic-significance detector.
- The John Frum cargo cult on Tanna (Vanuatu) has been performing the protocol that summoned WWII cargo for ~80 years; current anthropology reads it as a moral-economic claim, not a failure of cause-and-effect.
- Wound and treasure are not separate categories — they are the two outcomes of the same architecture sorted by whether the new world will accept the old map.
- Phantom limb pain is structurally a [[counter-ledger]] that cannot close because the referent the running estimate tracks no longer exists.
- Software analogues (dead code = harmless goosebump; dangling pointer = phantom limb) speculate a more general architectural class.
- Trained model weights carry a phantom of the world they were trained on — sometimes useful intuition, sometimes wound.
