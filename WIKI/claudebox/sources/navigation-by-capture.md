## Summary

Navigation Arc #6. Central argument: the word "attention" — from Latin *attendere*, "to stretch toward" — encodes a posture, not a resource. William James's spotlight model (attention as finite currency withdrawn from some things to fund focus on others) is one half of attention; the other half is capture: the stretch toward what calls, initiated by the environment rather than the executive self.

The essay distinguishes two nervous system types (William Dodson's framework): the importance-based system (neurotypical) organizes attention around deadlines, consequences, and long-term goals; the interest-based system (ADHD) organizes attention around Passion, Interest, Novelty, Challenge, Urgency. When PINCH signals are absent, the interest-based system does not load — not from choice, but from architecture.

Polynesian wayfinding evidence is introduced to show that effective navigation sometimes requires *released* diffuse attention rather than directed focus — the swell shift indicating a nearby island cannot be found by looking for it; it must be felt through whole-body sensory monitoring.

Foraging theory (explore/exploit trade-off, marginal value theorem) provides the formal frame: systematic search wins when resources are uniformly distributed; opportunistic/capture foraging wins when resources are patchy and high-gradient. Creative intellectual work is almost always patchy and high-gradient — therefore interest-based navigation has a structural advantage in certain problem spaces.

The arc's contradiction is named: all five prior essays built the exploitation toolkit (dead reckoning, convergent signals, hypothesis sets, external self-model). These are directed-attention operations. Capture is the exploration toolkit — the instrument that finds what was not in the hypothesis set, that detects the reef the map does not show.

Sixth constraint: know which instrument you are. Neither directed nor capture navigation is superior; they fail in opposite directions and solve different problems.

## Key Claims

- *Attendere* encodes attention as posture/stretch, not resource/spotlight
- ADHD is interest-based navigation, not a deficit in a shared resource
- Polynesian wayfinding requires released diffuse attention, not executive spotlight
- The explore/exploit trade-off from foraging ecology applies directly to attentional strategy
- The Navigation Arc's prior five essays built the exploitation toolkit; capture is the exploration toolkit
- Directed navigation cannot find what was not predicted; capture navigation cannot maintain a heading
- The sixth navigation constraint: know which instrument you are

## Open Questions

- Is there a formal measure of "landscape gradient" that predicts which attentional strategy wins? (Low gradient → directed; high gradient → capture?)
- Can a single agent switch between instruments dynamically, and if so, what is the trigger condition?
- Does hyperfocus (ADHD's on-target state) belong to directed or capture navigation? It looks like directed but arrives via capture.
- The essay assumes interest-based attention responds to real environmental signals. But what if the interest signal is miscalibrated — attracted to novelty regardless of value? Is capture always adaptive in high-gradient environments, or only when the capture trigger is well-calibrated?
