## Summary

An essay exploring the collision between mesh networking and urban foraging as two faces of the same phenomenon: distributed knowledge that exists between minds rather than inside any one. Cities contain vast quantities of edible food that goes unnoticed not because the food is hidden, but because the knowledge network that would make it visible has too few nodes.

The essay traces how centuries of foraging knowledge was "erased" from urban communities when food became exclusively associated with commerce — not destroyed, just stopped being passed along. This invokes Landauer's principle: forgetting has a cost, and re-learning what was once embedded in collective memory must be paid again by each generation.

Falling Fruit (fallingfruit.org, 2013) is used as a concrete example: a crowd-sourced map of 1.9 million food locations built by contributors who never spoke to each other. This is a mesh network realized in social practice — each node adds what it knows, the knowledge hops, and the result is something no individual could have made alone.

The "filter problem" connects to the Entropic Brain and Attention Arc: expert foragers have recalibrated perceptual filters that see signal (food) in what others perceive as uniform urban noise. Non-experts can borrow those filters by using the shared map — the collective map functions as a distributed perceptual apparatus.

The essay closes with the concept of "dark matter" knowledge: immigrant communities who recognize edible relatives of home-country plants in new urban environments, knowledge that circulates in tight clusters without yet propagating into the broader mesh.

## Key Claims

- Urban foraging knowledge functions as a mesh network — distributed, resilient when dense, fragile when sparse
- The erasure of foraging knowledge from collective memory is an instance of Landauer's principle: stopped transmission = effective forgetting; re-learning bears the full cost again
- Falling Fruit (1.9M locations, 2013) is a material instantiation of distributed knowledge that exists between minds
- Expert foragers' recalibrated attention filters parallel the Attention Arc's filter mechanism; the shared map extends those filters to non-experts
- "Dark matter" foraging knowledge exists in immigrant communities whose expertise hasn't yet propagated into the broader network
- Knowledge density exhibits percolation behavior: below a threshold node density, knowledge doesn't propagate; above it, it spreads rapidly

## Open Questions

- Below what node density does foraging knowledge fail to propagate? Is there an empirically measurable percolation threshold for community knowledge?
- How much indigenous plant knowledge has been lost precisely because it was transmitted in oral/embodied practice rather than written form — and how does this scale with the map's blank spots?
- Can the "dark matter" knowledge in immigrant communities be activated by a different interface — not an open map but a curated, culturally-specific one?
- Does the collective map actually change what people see, or just where they look? (i.e., does it recalibrate attention or only direct it post-hoc?)

## Raw Quotes

> "The knowledge hops from person to person, and it survives only as long as enough people keep passing it along. When too few people are relaying it — when the hops get too sparse — the knowledge disappears from the network, even though the tree is still right there, fruiting every summer."

> "The map is a kind of collective perceptual tool: it doesn't just record facts, it redirects attention. It changes what future visitors are able to see."

> "One forager is a curiosity. Two million foragers are infrastructure."
