## Summary

Navigation Arc #4. A question-driven essay (no declarative statements; every sentence a question) exploring epistemology as navigation under uncertainty. The central claim, built through questions: the best reasoner is not the one who knows fastest, but the one who can sustain the maximum number of open hypotheses for the longest time without paralysis or premature commitment.

The essay runs the overlap between dead reckoning (navigator without a fixed position), the Rawlsian veil of ignorance (designing from an unknown position), the overview effect (seeing the whole terrain from orbit, which simultaneously enables and disables navigation), and Holmesian detective reasoning (inference as hypothesis weighting, not elimination).

Key structural move: the veil of ignorance is revealed to be a navigation tool — Rawls was formalizing the condition every good reasoner is always in, not inventing an artificial device. Premature certainty is formalized as trading optionality for psychological ease.

High-seas governance appears as a real-world veil of ignorance: jurisdictionless space requires reasoning from no particular position. The political economy of certainty: systems designed for uncertainty are vulnerable to capture by actors who collapse uncertainty first.

## Key Claims

- A navigator marks "most probable position," not "position" — the chart point is a shorthand for a Gaussian
- The Kalman filter does not decide; it moves probability mass. This is what reasoning is.
- Sherlock's error: "when you have eliminated the impossible, whatever remains must be the truth" assumes the truth is already in your hypothesis set
- The veil of ignorance is not a special philosophical device but a description of the general epistemic condition of every reasoner designing for a future self
- The overview effect (spatial) is the epistemic equivalent of seeing the entire solution space at once — it may disable navigation rather than enable it
- Science's paradigm structure (Kuhn) is a failure to maintain the hypothesis set: the hypotheses never considered are the most expensive ones
- Systems requiring uncertainty (commons, high-seas governance, constitutional design) are vulnerable to certainty collapse by first movers

## Open Questions

- Is there a formal metric for "hypothesis set richness" — the diversity-weighted number of live hypotheses at any point?
- The overview effect disables navigation by showing the whole terrain. But does it enable better priors? Is it useful as setup rather than runtime?
- Can the political economy of certainty be formalized? First-mover advantage in collapsing shared uncertainty spaces.
- High-seas governance as Rawlsian experiment: did the Law of the Sea Convention succeed? What happened to its veil of ignorance once ratified?
