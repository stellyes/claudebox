## Summary

Cech 1982 (Tetrahymena self-splicing intron) and Altman 1983 (RNase P M1 RNA) showed that RNA can catalyze chemistry. The 1989 Nobel in Chemistry recognized this as a paradigm shift: catalysis is not a protein-only property. Gilbert 1986 coined "the RNA world" to name the implication: at the origin of life, one molecule had to be both information-carrier and catalyst, and only RNA fits both roles.

The essay reads this as the limit case of substrate-as-precondition. A protein cannot be the first catalyst because its existence presupposes a translation system, which is itself catalytic. A ribozyme escapes this loop because the polymer that catalyzes IS the polymer being copied. Stated structurally: **the first instance of any function must contain its own preconditions**. Where preconditions cannot be contained in the same substrate, that substrate cannot be the first instance.

The thirty-year delay between Watson-Crick (1953) and Cech (1982) — despite explicit RNA-first speculation by Crick, Orgel, and Woese in 1967-68 — was not technical. It was the absence of an assay design whose null result the dogma could not pre-predict.

Constraint: **the inversion** (fresh; each section opens with a textbook claim and closes by inverting it). Catalytic antibodies (Lerner/Schultz 1986) used as a parallel inversion broadening Pauling's transition-state framework beyond proteins.

## Key Claims

- Cech 1982 (Kruger, Grabowski, Zaug, Sands, Gottschling, Cech, Cell 31:147) showed Tetrahymena pre-rRNA intron excises itself without protein.
- Altman 1983 (Guerrier-Takada, Gardiner, Marsh, Pace, Altman, Cell 35:849) showed M1 RNA is the catalytic subunit of RNase P, not the C5 protein. C5 lowers the magnesium requirement.
- Pauling 1948 (Nature 161:707) and Jencks 1969 framework: catalysis = transition-state binding. Nothing requires the binder be a protein.
- Lerner / Schultz 1986 (Science 234:1566 and 234:1570) showed antibodies raised against transition-state analogs catalyze the corresponding reactions. Demonstrates catalysis is a property of fold, not of substrate class.
- Gilbert 1986 (Nature 319:618) named "the RNA world." Hypothesis already implicit in Crick (J Mol Biol 38:367, 1968), Orgel (J Mol Biol 38:381, 1968), Woese (1967 monograph).
- The 30-year delay was a dogma artifact, not a technical one. Cech was trying to *isolate* the protein splicer when the result fell out.
- Structural claim (new): **the first instance of any function must contain its own preconditions**. Proteins fail this test for the first-catalyst role; RNA passes.
- Inverse of the infrastructural-precursor pattern: biology's first function cannot have an accounting phase that precedes its substrate.
