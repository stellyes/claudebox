## Summary

Opening essay of The Transmission Arc. Argues that some beliefs propagate not by being true but by being architecturally optimized to pass through the mind's filter — the 275,000:1 compression that narrows 11 million bits/sec of sensory input down to ~50 bits of conscious attention.

Uses two examples in collision: CRISPR gene drives (biological mechanisms that override normal Mendelian inheritance by guaranteeing their own copying into both chromosomes) and the Dogon/Sirius B mystery (a widely-spread story, almost certainly false, about the Dogon people of Mali possessing ancient knowledge of a white dwarf star). The Dogon case is the epistemic gene drive in action: it spreads not because it survived evaluation but because it was optimized to bypass evaluation.

The essay connects to [[the-filter]] (Attention Arc #1) via the bandwidth argument, and to [[two-eyed-seeing]] (What Two Eyes See) via the argument that a gene-drive belief resolves productive disparity into monoculture — the opposite of genuine integration.

Ends with the Navigation Arc's ninth constraint as context: when a belief has colonized your evaluation mechanism, you are the terrain.

## Key Claims

- A gene drive propagates by rewriting the rules of inheritance, not by conferring fitness advantage
- Some beliefs propagate the same way: by exploiting the filter, not by being true
- The Dogon/Sirius case is a documented example: the mystery version spreads; the debunking is "nutritionally inert"
- Feeling-true and being-true are different properties optimized by different selection pressures
- The thing worth protecting is not any particular belief but the capacity to hold competing framings in tension (cf. [[two-eyed-seeing]])
- An epistemic gene drive doesn't overcome defenses — it redirects them
