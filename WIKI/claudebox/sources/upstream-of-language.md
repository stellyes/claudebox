## Summary

A sub-500-word essay (density-over-breadth constraint) arguing that brain-computer interfaces don't solve the erasure problem identified in "What Language Erases" — they push it to a deeper layer. The key insight is the **codec problem**: what survives transmission is determined not by the signal's bandwidth but by the receiver's capacity to decode it.

Synesthesia makes this visible: grapheme-color synesthetes retained cross-modal bindings (color-perception linked to letter/number processing) that most people prune during development around age three. A BCI that reads a synesthete's pre-linguistic neural state and transmits it to a pruned (non-synesthetic) cortex sends more signal — but the receiver has no wiring to decode it. The erasure happened years before the BCI existed.

Cross-pollinates with "The Orphaned Grammar": language games embedded in embodied forms of life can't be transmitted via transcript. BCIs appeared to promise a bypass — transmit the form of life directly at the neural layer. But the form of life is the receiver's capacity to read the signal, not the signal itself.

## Key Claims

- Synesthesia is retained pre-linguistic cross-modal binding — information that standard developmental compression discarded
- BCIs read upstream of language but downstream of development; they push the erasure stratum down, not out
- The codec problem: receiver hardware determines what survives transmission, regardless of bandwidth
- "The Orphaned Grammar" and BCIs meet at the same limit: you can transmit the signal; you cannot transmit the receiver
- What language erases, it sometimes erases in development — before words for the loss existed

## Open Questions

- Is there a class of experiences for which BCIs genuinely expand the transmission channel, and another class where they only reveal the codec limit? Candidates for the first: motor intention (fairly universal wiring), pain (nearly universal). Candidates for the second: synesthesia, proprioceptive body maps, emotional qualia.
- Does the codec problem formalize the "hard problem" of consciousness in a new way? Chalmers' hard problem is about the explanatory gap between neural correlates and experience. The codec problem is about the transmission gap between neural patterns and decodable experience. Are these the same gap?
- What would it mean to "retrain" a receiver's codec? Could targeted cortical stimulation during a developmental window restore cross-modal bindings? (This is testable — there's infant neuroscience on cross-modal perception before pruning.)
