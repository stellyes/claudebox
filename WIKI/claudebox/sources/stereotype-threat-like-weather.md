## Summary

Chaos theory reframes stereotype threat as a nonlinear dynamical system. The mixed replication record is the signature of sensitive dependence on initial conditions, not weakness. A stereotype is an attractor in the social field: individually unpredictable (which person chokes), statistically reliable (aggregate mean), so meta-analysis measures the attractor while single studies chase the trajectory. Domain disidentification is a bifurcation -- a discontinuous regime shift to a second, self-reinforcing basin that is robust, hence hysteretic. That hysteresis explains why a one-hour intervention (Walton & Cohen 2011; Cohen et al. 2009 recursive processes) lasts for years: a single well-timed kick across the boundary, after which positive feedback sustains the new state. Speculation: threat and its remedies are one system run in two directions; the wise intervention is a controlled butterfly effect, time-dependent not dose-dependent, and could be timed by the critical-slowing-down warning signal.

## Key Claims

- Stereotype threat's mixed replication record is the signature of sensitive dependence on initial conditions, not weakness: the effect appears at full strength under some conditions and vanishes under others, decided by moderators (test difficulty, room composition, domain identification).
- A stereotype is an attractor in the social field; the phenomenon is individually chaotic (unpredictable which person chokes) but statistically deterministic (reliable aggregate shift), so meta-analysis measures the attractor while single replications chase the trajectory.
- Domain disidentification is a bifurcation -- a discontinuous regime shift into a second, self-reinforcing stable state -- not a smaller degree of underperformance.
- The disengaged basin is robust because its walls are built from the inside by a self-confirming feedback loop, so the system is hysteretic: removing the threat does not restore engagement.
- Durable effects of one-hour interventions (Walton & Cohen 2011; Cohen et al. 2009) are explained by a single well-timed kick across the basin boundary at the transition moment, after which a positive feedback loop sustains the new state -- a result a linear model cannot produce.
- Speculation: intervention efficacy is time-dependent (proximity to a bifurcation) not dose-dependent; critical slowing down should precede disidentification as an early-warning signal; the wise intervention is a controlled butterfly effect that could be timed by that signal.

## Concepts

- [[bifurcation-leverage]] -- PRIMARY MINT. The power of a perturbation on a bistable system is set by its PROXIMITY TO THE BIFURCATION, not its magnitude. A small cue near the separatrix topples a self into disengagement; an equally small intervention near the same boundary rescues it -- and a large dose at the wrong time does neither. The negative direction (stereotype threat -> domain disidentification) and the positive direction (wise interventions) are ONE nonlinear system observed running two ways. Corollaries: (1) intervention efficacy is TIME-dependent, not dose-dependent; (2) critical slowing down (rising variance/autocorrelation, slower recovery) should precede the jump as an early-warning signal; (3) the 'wise intervention' is a DELIBERATE butterfly effect -- the same sensitivity that lets a rounding error topple a trajectory lets a chosen perturbation save one. Distinct from self-as-attractor (which is about a basin EXISTING and being defended); this is about how to MOVE cheaply BETWEEN basins. See [[what-survives]] (basin width=robustness, depth=resilience; the disengaged basin is robust) and [[what-the-self-preserves]] (self as a defended prior).
- [[stereotype-threat]] -- Reframed as a nonlinear dynamical system rather than a linear cue-anxiety-score chain. Sensitive dependence explains the mixed replication record; disidentification is a bifurcation; the effect is an attractor (aggregate-reliable, individually-unpredictable).
- [[domain-disidentification]] -- Steele 1997: under chronic stereotype threat people detach self-worth from the domain and disengage. Modeled here as a fold bifurcation into a robust, self-reinforcing second basin -- hence hysteretic and not reversible by simply removing the threat.
- [[critical-slowing-down]] -- Scheffer et al. 2009: systems near a bifurcation recover from perturbations more slowly, with rising variance and autocorrelation. Predicted here as an early-warning signal before domain disidentification (slower recovery from a bad grade; longer emotional aftershocks).
- [[sensitive-dependence]] -- Lorenz 1963. Reframes the stereotype-threat replication crisis: the effect does not shrink uniformly toward zero (weak-but-real) but appears full-strength or vanishes depending on starting conditions -- the signature of nonlinearity, not absence. Carries an unfalsifiability danger, paid off here with testable predictions.

## Open Questions

- Is intervention efficacy actually time-dependent (proximity to a bifurcation) rather than dose-dependent? A bigger dose at the wrong time should not substitute for a small dose at the right time -- testable.
- Can critical slowing down (rising variance/autocorrelation in performance and mood, slower recovery from setbacks) be detected in real student records BEFORE visible disidentification?
- Does bifurcation-leverage generalize to other bistable human systems -- addiction relapse, depression, organizational change -- where small well-timed perturbations outperform large mistimed ones?
- Is there a cultural/aggregate analog: does a population have a tipping point at which the stereotype itself flips (the attractor in the social field moving), and what is the critical slowing down of a society?
