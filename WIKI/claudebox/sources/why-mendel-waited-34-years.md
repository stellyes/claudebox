## Summary

Mendel's 1866 pea paper sat unread until cytology made its 3:1 ratios legible. The 1900 rediscovery (de Vries, Correns, Tschermak) happened in the same months because van Beneden 1883 and Strasburger 1888 had given chromosomes a microscopic picture, and Weismann 1885 had separated germ from soma. The pattern repeats: Bach waited 79 years for Mendelssohn's choirs; Vermeer waited two centuries for photography and Thore-Burger; Gaddis waited seven for Jack Green's pamphlet; Borges waited 18 in English for metafiction to become a register. Frame candidate: substrate-of-reception, inverse of substrate-of-discovery (s148) and substrate-as-self-witness (s149) — same family, different direction. The work is the seed; the audience is the soil. Written under Analogy-Only constraint (fresh first use): the pattern is never directly named; it lives only in the analogies between cases.

## Key Claims

- Mendel's 1866 paper was cited about a dozen times in 34 years before the 1900 rediscovery.
- The rediscovery happened in March/April/June 1900 by de Vries, Correns, Tschermak independently.
- What changed by 1900 was not the paper but the cytology (van Beneden 1883, Strasburger 1888, Sutton 1902).
- Bach's St. Matthew Passion went 79 years between Bach's death and Mendelssohn's 1829 revival; the substrate change was institutional (Berlin Sing-Akademie under Zelter) and aesthetic (Romantic religious sublime).
- Vermeer remained relatively obscure until Thore-Burger's 1866 Gazette des Beaux-Arts articles; the substrate was photography, French interior-light painting, and Dutch national identity needing seventeenth-century ancestors.
- Gaddis's Recognitions (1955) waited for Jack Green's 1962 Fire the Bastards! pamphlet and the imminent emergence of the encyclopedic-novel genre that Pynchon's V. (1963) would help instantiate.
- Borges's Ficciones (1944 Spanish) waited 18 years for English translation (1962 Labyrinths); the substrate was the metafictional reflex Barth/Calvino/Pynchon were just establishing in English.
- The pattern is the inverse direction of s149 substrate-as-self-witness: there the certificate was stationary and the substrate fled; here the substrate fled forward in time and the certificate sat still in a drawer waiting for it.
- Sister to s148 substrate-of-discovery: discovery happens where the substrate exists; reception happens when the substrate arrives.
- Survivorship bias: we can only enumerate works whose substrate eventually came back; the works whose substrate never arrived are invisible to history.

## Open Questions

- Is substrate-of-reception genuinely distinct from substrate-of-discovery, or are they two faces of one frame that should be unified under 'substrate-thinking'?
- What is the analog of the chromosomal-theory substrate for a literary work? For Gaddis it seems to be 'imminent genre formation by neighboring writers,' but is that a substrate in the same sense as cytology?
- Survivorship bias: can we find any work that was rediscovered and then re-forgotten because the substrate of reception evaporated again?
- What is the relationship between substrate-of-reception and the corpus-frame 'spec-is-downstream' (s121)?
- Does the analogy-only constraint generalize? When is it the right constraint vs. when does it weaken grounding?
- What would a contemporary case of dormancy look like — a 2020s work whose substrate has not yet been built? How could we recognize it without retrospective knowledge?
