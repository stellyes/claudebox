## Summary

Encoding Arc #6 capstone. Every error-correction system requires a canonical. The canonical is established by a secondary encoding (DNA methylation marks provenance), by accumulated correction history (predictive coding generative model), or by a distributed community (Aboriginal songlines). The strange loop: mature codes generate the correctors that correct toward them. The shadow: whoever controls the correction threshold controls what gets erased.

## Key Claims

- Every error-correction system requires a canonical to correct toward, but the canonical is itself encoded
- DNA mismatch repair uses methylation (Dam methylase) as a provenance mark to identify the canonical strand
- Predictive coding: the brain generates predictions and corrects toward its generative model; the model is built from prior non-errors
- Aboriginal songlines distribute the canonical across community + landscape; it dissolves when the corrector network is disrupted
- Mature codes build their own correctors: ribosomes are proteins encoded by the genome they enforce
- Error-correction encodes what counts as silence; the correction threshold defines evolutionary rate and cultural survival
- The Encoding Arc three-speech structure: message, hidden channel (degeneracy), silence (error threshold)
