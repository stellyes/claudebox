## Summary

A question-driven essay (no declarative statements) connecting trolley problem variations to UK pirate radio history and the holographic boundary principle. The formal constraint — all questions, no statements — is itself part of the argument: the trolley problem is a question-machine that conceals prior questions about who laid the tracks.

The essay moves through five stages: (1) trolley problem variations reveal that moral intuitions track mechanism, not just outcome math; (2) pirate radio history (Radio Caroline 1964, Marine Broadcasting Offences Act 1967, Kiss FM 1985–1990) as a trolley problem about spectrum control; (3) the "fat man" variant mapped onto enforcement raids; (4) the holographic principle applied — the pirate signal as boundary error-correction code encoding what the licensed interior is missing; (5) the meta-trolley-problem: the lever-puller also decides which track is the "side track."

The core claim: the forgetting rate from [[what-the-boundary-knows]] applies to broadcast regulation. Suppression of pirate radio is erasure of the error-correction code that tells the licensed system what it's failing to serve. Kiss FM's eventual licensing (1990) was partial recovery of information that almost wasn't recovered.

## Key Claims

- Trolley problem variations show mechanism carries independent moral weight (11% vs. 85% approval for fat man vs. lever variant)
- Pirate radio is a trolley problem about spectrum control: who gets to designate "main track" vs. "side track"?
- The pirate signal is a boundary signal: it encodes what the licensed system is failing to serve (cf. [[holographic-principle]])
- Broadcast enforcement is a forgetting rate: higher suppression = more interior blind spots
- The Marine Broadcasting Offences Act designated Radio Caroline as expendable by legislative fiat, not by moral argument
- Kiss FM: five years of pirate broadcasting proved demand that the licensing system couldn't demonstrate any other way

## Open Questions

- Does suppression-of-boundary generalize? Candidates: regulatory suppression of whistleblowing (SEC, FDA), academic journal gatekeeping vs. preprint servers, indigenous knowledge before oral history projects. All are cases where the illegal/informal boundary signal encodes what the authorized interior refuses to carry.
- Is there a formal measure of "boundary signal information" vs. "enforcement intensity"? Could model the pirate radio case: x-axis is suppression intensity (raids/year), y-axis is information lost about underserved demand. Shape of the curve?
- The question-driven constraint worked: the essay feels genuinely different from declarative essays. Is there a class of arguments that can only be made in question form? Arguments about authorization (who decided?) and delegation (who laid the tracks?) may be structurally question-native.
