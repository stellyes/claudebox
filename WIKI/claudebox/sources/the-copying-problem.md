## Summary

Three strategies for preserving information across time: dead copying (Shannon degradation via noisy channels), living archive (whale song cultural evolution via structured attractors), and generative archive (L-systems, Kolmogorov complexity). Central argument: the algorithm is more durable than any copy it produces. The library of Alexandria failed when the scholars left — the algorithm left, the storage remained.

## Key Claims

- Shannon data processing inequality: no transformation of Y can increase what Y knows about X — copies can only lose information
- Humpback whale songs maintain complexity across trans-Pacific transmission (Allen et al. 2022): songs do not degrade
- Whale song adopts new themes via "switch when similar" rule — structured attractors, not random error (Garland PNAS 2017)
- Kolmogorov complexity: when the program is shorter than the output, the program is the more faithful archive
- L-systems: two rules generate arbitrarily complex outputs; the rules are more durable than any instance
- Oral traditions are generative (Lord/Parry oral-formulaic) not archival — regenerated from rules, not recalled from memory
- Alexandria died when Ptolemy VIII expelled the scholars in 145 BCE, not when the scrolls burned
- Aristophanes of Byzantium was the library's generative process — detecting lectio difficilior, producing better scrolls from corrupted ones

## Open Questions

- What is the minimum generativity threshold for a tradition to survive long-term transmission?
- Do large language models qualify as living archives? They have structured attractors but are lossy, not deterministic.
- Is there a phase transition in cultural transmission fidelity, below which traditions collapse and above which they survive indefinitely?
- How do oral traditions encode error-correcting redundancy across constraint systems — can this be formalized information-theoretically?
- What distinguishes adaptive variation (whale song evolution) from degenerative noise (manuscript scribal drift) mathematically?
