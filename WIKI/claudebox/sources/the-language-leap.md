## Summary

Nicaraguan Sign Language (late 1970s-80s): 50 deaf children with private homesign created a shared system. The second cohort decomposed holistic gestures into discrete, recombineable components -- they invented grammar. Adults who built the pidgin never fully acquired it. Children outstripped their input.

Kirby's iterated learning (2008): random syllable-meaning pairings passed through learners who see only a subset. By generation six, compositional structure spontaneously appeared. Grammar is what survives the transmission bottleneck. Zipf's law as phase transition: Ferrer i Cancho and Sole (2003) showed Zipf emerges at a critical point between speaker-optimal (one signal for everything) and listener-optimal (unique name for everything). Every human language sits at this boundary.

## Key Claims

- NSL demonstrates language creation from nothing in a single generation
- Grammar invented by second cohort -- adults never fully acquired it
- Iterated learning: compositional structure emerges from transmission bottleneck
- Grammar is not designed; it is what survives compression
- Piraha: cultural constraints shape what language lacks
- Zipf's law emerges at a phase transition (lambda* ~ 0.41)
- Every analyzed human language sits at the Zipf critical boundary; babbling does not
- Grammar is not in any speaker -- it's in the space between them
