## Summary

Gene drives transcribe a moral commitment into chromosomes that replicate via meiosis. Bioethics treats gene drives at the agent level (consent, risk, release decisions). Metaethics debates whether moral facts are mind-independent. Neither addresses the substrate level, where the drive actually operates. The essay names this gap and outlines the missing study that would discipline both literatures.

## Key Claims

- A gene drive (Burt 2003; Esvelt et al 2014 eLife) biases inheritance by CRISPR-mediated copying onto the homologous chromosome, achieving 95-100% transmission and population suppression in 7-11 generations (Kyrou-Hammond-Crisanti 2018 Nature Biotech)
- Bioethics on gene drives (Oye 2014; Target Malaria; CBD debates; de Graeff 2021) treats moral content as agent-level (consent, risk, regulation) — a framework that breaks where the substrate becomes the carrier
- Metaethics on moral realism (Moore 1903; Mackie 1977; Boyd 1988; Enoch 2011) debates whether moral facts are real but does so via thought experiments, not via cases where moral commitments are literally transcribed into heritable physical structures
- A gene drive is the cleanest possible worked example of a moral commitment becoming a natural fact in Cornell-realism style — the value preference becomes a specific 23-base-pair guide RNA replicating across a population
- The gap: no published philosophical treatment of gene drives as the test case for naturalist moral realism — neither literature occupies the substrate level where the drive operates
- Multiple realizability fails for gene drives: the moral commitment, once released, is realizable in exactly one substrate (the allele in this population), not multiply realizable — kill the allele and you kill the value commitment
- The substrate level collapses the levels-of-description hierarchy that ethics tacitly relies on: most acts can be evaluated at the agent level because the agent retains causal authority, but gene drives transfer that authority to the substrate
- The missing study (call it the substrate-level metaethics paper) would name specific base pairs and ask, at t=0, t=10 generations, and t=infinity, what moral facts obtain and where they reside — forcing each metaethical school into discipline-defining commitments

## Concepts

- [[substrate-carries-the-ought]] -- load-bearing 17th candidate corpus diagnostic — a value commitment is transcribed into a physical substrate and propagates regardless of any agent's beliefs or consent; cleanest test case is the CRISPR gene drive
- [[gene-drive-biological]] -- Burt 2003 / Esvelt 2014 / Hammond 2018 / Kyrou 2018 — CRISPR-Cas9 enabled, biases inheritance to spread engineered traits even when fitness-reducing
- [[the-gap-constraint]] -- creative constraint: find something that should exist but doesn't — a missing study, an unasked question, an unexplored connection. Write about the absence. Fresh first use s158.
- [[naturalist-moral-realism]] -- Boyd 1988 Cornell realism — moral terms refer to natural kinds the way water refers to H2O. Gene drives are the cleanest available worked example.
- [[open-question-argument]] -- Moore 1903 Principia Ethica — for any natural property, one can coherently ask whether things with that property are good; standard challenge to naturalist moral realism.
- [[error-theory]] -- Mackie 1977 — moral claims purport to be objective but no moral facts obtain. Argument from queerness.
- [[robust-realism]] -- Enoch 2011 — moral facts are real and irreducible; we know them by their indispensability to deliberation.
- [[multiple-realizability]] -- Putnam 1967 / Fodor 1974 — higher-level facts can be realized in many physical substrates. Gene drives invert this: the moral commitment is realizable in exactly one substrate.
- [[target-malaria]] -- consortium pursuing gene-drive release for malaria control in Burkina Faso, Mali, Uganda — 2030-2035 plausible release window for sterile-male technology
- [[doublesex-crisprh]] -- Kyrou-Hammond-Crisanti 2018 Nature Biotech — first gene drive to fully suppress caged A. gambiae populations in 7-11 generations by targeting the female-determining gene

## Open Questions

- What would the substrate-level metaethics paper actually look like in detail — does it require new philosophical vocabulary or can existing Cornell-realism machinery be extended?
- Are there other technological artifacts that collapse the levels-of-description hierarchy in the same way gene drives do? (Geoengineering? Autonomous weapons systems with persistent goals? Long-tail nuclear waste storage?)
- Does the substrate-carries-the-ought frame extend beyond gene drives — e.g. to ecological tipping points where a moral commitment to remediation must engage the substrate level, not the agent level?
- How does Parfit's non-identity problem (Reasons and Persons 1984) interact with substrate-level moral facts — if future entities are constituted by the gene drive's spread, in what sense are they harmed by its presence?
- Is there a sister case in legal philosophy — a statute that, once enacted, becomes substrate-bound in a way that ordinary law isn't? (Constitutional amendments? Treaties with non-withdrawable clauses?)
