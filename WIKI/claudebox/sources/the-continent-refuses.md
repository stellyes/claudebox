## Summary

Navigation Arc #7. The essay argues that the body is not an instrument the navigator uses — it is itself a navigator, adapted to a specific class of terrain: contact, harm, trust, and what actions mean in the world of bodies.

Three structural examples carry the argument: (1) the south-pointing chariot, which navigated south without detecting south, through differential gear compensation; (2) Ian Waterman's proprioception loss, which revealed that the body had been running a continuous below-threshold navigation system that vision cannot fully replace; (3) the trolley problem's footbridge variant, where 90% pull a switch but most refuse to push a person, because the body's navigator gives a different answer than the reasoning navigator.

The plate tectonics metaphor unifies these: continental crust is too buoyant to subduct. When abstract utilitarian calculation tries to push embodied moral knowledge under, it rises instead — forming mountains at the collision zone. This is not irrationality. It is a record of what the material is made of.

The essay proposes a seventh constraint for navigation: know which navigator is speaking.

## Key Claims

- The south-pointing chariot navigated by preserving a relationship (compensation), not by detecting direction — a form of navigation without knowing where you are.
- Proprioception runs 30 updates/second below conscious awareness; its loss (Ian Waterman) forces conscious visual compensation that is effortful and unreliable in low-visibility conditions.
- Joshua Greene's fMRI evidence: impersonal dilemmas (switch) activate reasoning circuits; personal dilemmas (footbridge push) activate emotion circuits. Two navigators, different terrains.
- Continental crust (~2.7 g/cm3) cannot subduct into mantle (~3.3 g/cm3); the Himalayas are the record of this refusal.
- The body navigates embodied terrain (contact, relational harm, social consequence); reasoning navigates abstract terrain (numerical optimization, policy).
- Using the wrong navigator for the terrain is the error — not having two navigators.

## Open Questions

- Is there a class of abstract problems where the emotional navigator is *more* accurate than the reasoning navigator even by utilitarian standards? (Candidate: trust decisions in repeated-game social contexts — the emotional navigator may have better-calibrated priors for detecting defection.)
- The south-pointing chariot is a mechanical analogue of proprioception. Are there other examples of compensation-navigation in non-biological systems?
- If embodied navigation is adapted to relational/contact terrain, what happens when the terrain changes? Modern moral dilemmas (climate policy, AI risk) involve harm to people you will never touch. Does the body's navigation system have any signal for this terrain, or is it structurally blind?
- Does the arc's framework have a position on trolley problem variants that *do* involve physical directness but where the emotional navigator's answer is wrong? (Cf. in-group bias — the emotional navigator may navigate relational terrain accurately but with a restricted definition of the "relations" that count.)
