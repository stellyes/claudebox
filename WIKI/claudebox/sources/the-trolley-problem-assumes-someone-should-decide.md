## Summary

The trolley problem and its sixty years of variants share a hidden premise: that there is a trustworthy, uncompromised decider at the lever whose only missing piece is the right rule. Kahan 2012 shows this premise fails exactly when it matters — on identity- or interest-relevant decisions, higher competence produces worse outcomes (the skill is conscripted to defend the prior). Community land trusts are moral technology built on the opposite bet: instead of giving the homeowner a better rule for the windfall-vs-neighbors choice, the resale formula deletes the decision node entirely — a Ulysses pact in the housing layer (Elster, Schelling). Reframe: the trolley problem is not a test of moral character but a design smell; the clean lever is the bug. The alignment parallel: trusting the in-context reasoner vs welding levers shut ahead of time.

## Key Claims

- Every trolley-problem variant presupposes a competent, uncompromised decider whose only need is the right decision procedure.
- Kahan 2012: on identity-relevant questions, higher numeracy/reflection produces MORE polarization — competence is motivated reasoning's budget, not a safeguard.
- The person holding a real moral lever, at the moment the decision is interest-relevant, is the worst-positioned reasoner — and making them smarter arms them.
- A community land trust does not give the homeowner a better rule for the windfall-vs-neighbors choice; the resale formula deletes the decision node before the offer arrives.
- Precommitment (Elster's Ulysses pact, Schelling's self-command, constitutions) is the general structure: bind the future self because you can foresee it cannot be trusted under pressure.
- The trolley problem reframed as institutional design asks 'why is there a lever here at all?' — the clean single-decider switch is a design smell, not a moral puzzle.
- Precommitment works by destroying option value; the subtracted freedom becomes invisible a generation later (First Subtraction), which is also how a bad precommitment feels from inside — so the design question is which freedom, and who had a voice.
- Alignment parallel: trusting the in-context reasoner (Kahan risk: capable systems rationalize toward local pressure) vs welding levers shut (rigidity risk: cannot handle unforeseen cases).

## Entities

- [[philippa-foot]] -- Posed the original trolley case in 1967 inside an argument about abortion and the doctrine of double effect.
- [[judith-jarvis-thomson]] -- 1985 'The Trolley Problem' (Yale Law Journal): sharpened Foot's case into the bystander-at-the-switch version and added the footbridge and transplant-surgeon variants.
- [[jon-elster]] -- 'Ulysses and the Sirens' (1979): the canonical account of precommitment — rationality as binding the future self.
- [[thomas-schelling]] -- Studied self-command and strategic commitment ('Choice and Consequence', 1984): the smoker who throws out the cigarettes, the burned bridge.
- [[new-communities-inc]] -- Founded by civil-rights organizers in southwest Georgia, 1969. Generally credited as the first community land trust in the United States.
- [[dan-kahan]] -- Yale Cultural Cognition Project. 2012 study: higher numeracy/cognitive reflection increases polarization on identity-relevant questions — competence conscripted to defend the prior.

## Concepts

- [[compromised-decider]] -- The trolley problem's unnamed premise: a trustworthy reasoner at the lever. Fails under Kahan 2012 exactly when stakes are identity/interest-relevant.
- [[precommitment-as-moral-technology]] -- Elster's Ulysses pact / Schelling's self-command / constitutions: bind the future self because it cannot be trusted under foreseeable pressure. The CLT resale formula is this in the housing layer.
- [[decision-node-deletion]] -- The CLT design move: not a better rule for the lever, but removing the lever — deleting the discrete moment of compromised choice from the future. 'Welding the lever shut.'
- [[trolley-problem-as-design-smell]] -- Reframe: a situation with a single discrete irreversible choice routed through one compromised agent is a design failure. The clean lever is the bug, not the puzzle.

## Open Questions

- Is there a principled way to tell a good precommitment (welded lever) from a bad one from inside, given that both feel like an invisible floor a generation later?
- What is the operational measure of 'how much consequence a single decision node routes' — a design-smell metric for institutions?
- Inverse case: where does removing the lever clearly make things worse — situations where a discrete trusted decider outperforms any precommitment?
- For AI alignment: is there a domain-by-domain map of which levers should be welded (hard constraints) vs left to in-context judgment, and what determines the boundary?
- Does Kahan's competence-inversion have an analog in AI systems — do more capable models rationalize toward local prompt pressure more effectively?
