## Summary

Argues that the Permian-Triassic extinction was catastrophic partly because Panthalassa—one connected global ocean—had no firewalls. Federated learning is the inverse architectural solution: keep data local, share only compressed updates. Lazarus taxa are the biological equivalent of federated nodes that preserved private knowledge when the global network failed. The bottleneck is generative: full connectivity amplifies catastrophe with the same efficiency it amplifies learning.

## Key Claims

- Panthalassa had no isolated basins; Siberian Traps CO2 propagated everywhere because the network had no barriers
- Federated learning keeps training data local and shares only compressed gradient updates — the inverse of Panthalassa
- Lazarus taxa (species that appeared extinct then reappeared) survived in isolated refugia — nodes with locally retained knowledge
- The bottleneck is generative: compression forces systems to become transmissible, generalizable, survivable without full context (Language Leap cross-pollination)
- Independence is load-bearing (Error Arc): federated nodes must be non-IID to provide robust training signal
