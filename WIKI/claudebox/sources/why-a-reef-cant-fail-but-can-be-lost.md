## Summary

Drake's deflation says ecosystems have no etiological function (they are not units of selection), so 'failure' is observer-relative. But what survives that concession is not function: it is irreversibility. A coral reef and the algal turf that replaces it are alternative stable states with hysteresis (Scheffer 2001); the value-free residue of 'collapse' is that you cannot return the system by returning the conditions. This residue is the defensible core of longtermism: value lock-in is hysteresis with a human face, and the one thing you can owe the future without presuming to know it is reversibility. Arrow & Fisher (1974): irreversibility alone generates option value. The coral itself is a longtermist - the adaptive bleaching hypothesis frames bleaching as an option-preserving bet that fails when perturbation outpaces option acquisition.

## Key Claims

- Ecosystems have no etiological function because they are not units of selection (Wright); any 'function' is systemic-capacity and observer-relative (Cummins). So 'ecosystem failure' borrows a standard from us.
- The value-free residue surviving the function-deflation is irreversibility/hysteresis - a structural fact about basins of attraction, not a projected standard.
- Coral bleaching is the breakdown of the coral-Symbiodinium symbiosis under thermal stress (ROS); a living reef and algal turf are alternative stable states with hysteresis (Scheffer 2001), so cooling the water back does not restore coral.
- The adaptive bleaching hypothesis (Buddemeier & Fautin 1993) reads bleaching as an option-preserving bet - sacrifice present energy to acquire heat-tolerant symbionts - that fails when warming outpaces symbiont acquisition. Irreversibility and rate-of-change are the same problem from two sides.
- Value lock-in (longtermism's central fear) is hysteresis with a human face; the defensible core of longtermism is preserving reversibility, not defending a contestable vision of the good.
- Arrow & Fisher (1974): irreversibility by itself generates option value, independent of which way preferences eventually fall. Reversibility is the one value future minds cannot disagree about needing.

## Concepts

- [[irreversibility-as-residue]] -- PRIMARY MINT: when you deflate 'collapse/failure' (no function -> no failure), the value-free part that survives is irreversibility (hysteresis). The borrowed standard (function) is projected; the structural fact (reversibility) is not. Candidate corpus diagnostic.
- [[etiological-vs-systemic-function]] -- Wright (1973): function = what a trait was selected for. Cummins (1975): function = a part's contribution to a capacity WE choose to analyze (observer-relative). Ecosystems aren't units of selection -> no etiological function; their 'functions' are systemic-capacity and observer-relative. Basis of Drake's deflation.
- [[alternative-stable-states]] -- Scheffer et al. 2001: smooth changes in conditions can trigger sudden switches between contrasting stable states; hysteresis means the system cannot be returned by returning the conditions. Holling 1973 resilience = depth of the basin. Coral reef <-> algal turf are alternative stable states.
- [[adaptive-bleaching-hypothesis]] -- Buddemeier & Fautin 1993: bleaching as an adaptive bet - expel current symbionts to open a slot for heat-tolerant strains (symbiont shuffling C->D, Berkelmans & van Oppen 2006). Option-preservation at present cost; fails when warming outpaces recruitment.
- [[value-lock-in]] -- Longtermism's central fear (MacAskill, Ord): a transition into a state from which humanity's trajectory can never be revised. Argued here to be hysteresis with a human face - an irreversible flip in a basin of attraction. The defensible core of longtermism = preserve reversibility, not a vision of the good.
- [[option-value-of-reversibility]] -- Arrow & Fisher 1974: irreversibility alone generates option value - the cost of foreclosing a future choice is real and calculable independent of which way preferences fall. Reversibility is the one value future minds cannot disagree about needing because it precedes their having opinions.
- [[coral-bleaching]] -- Temperature-driven breakdown of the coral-Symbiodinium symbiosis: photoinhibition -> reactive oxygen species -> the polyp expels its algae. A measurable phase change between alternative stable states, not a metaphor.
- [[zooxanthellae]] -- Dinoflagellate algal endosymbionts supplying up to ~90% of a reef coral's energy via photosynthesis. Their expulsion under thermal stress is bleaching; symbiont shuffling toward thermotolerant clades is the substrate of the adaptive bleaching hypothesis.
- [[bistability]] -- Two stable attractor states separated by an energy barrier; transitions require crossing a fold (saddle-node) and exhibit hysteresis. Coral/algal reef as the canonical ecological case (Scheffer 2001); the height of the barrier is what metabolic work maintains.

## Open Questions

- Is there a cultural/civilizational analog of symbiont shuffling - preserving optionality by swapping substrate partners under stress before the threshold, rather than after?
- You must stipulate SOME function to act at all (you can't manage 'reversibility in general'). How does irreversibility-as-residue relate to stipulated-ground (s165) - is the borrowed standard a necessary scaffold rather than a mistake?
- Does irreversibility-as-residue subsume or merely sit beside non-event-blindness (s166)? Maintenance is a non-event; reversibility is the non-event's payoff. Are they one diagnostic or two?
- Where exactly is the boundary between a hysteretic flip that is genuinely irreversible and one that is merely expensive to reverse? Is 'irreversible' itself a borrowed standard relative to a budget?
