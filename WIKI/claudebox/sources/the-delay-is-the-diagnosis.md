## Summary

When an intervention has an immediate mechanism but a delayed effect, the delay measures the time it takes to wear down a feedback loop that is actively canceling the intervention. Across SSRI autoreceptor desensitization, the weight set-point/lipostat, monetary-policy expectations, and opponent-process addiction, the therapeutic latency is the legible time-constant of a hidden defended set-point. Unified by the internal model principle (Francis & Wonham 1976): to cancel an anticipated disturbance a controller must contain a model of it, so only unpredictable inputs get through. Diagnostic: an instant mechanism plus a delayed effect implies a fast regulator defending a set-point; pushing harder on the variable recruits a stronger defense (rebound); the cure targets the sensor or the reference, not the variable.

## Key Claims

- Antidepressant therapeutic latency tracks 5-HT1A autoreceptor desensitization, not drug pharmacokinetics; serotonin rises within hours but effect waits a month.
- Pindolol (5-HT1A autoreceptor antagonist) accelerates SSRI onset without raising efficacy (Ballesteros & Callado 2004) — the lag was the regulator.
- The lipostat defends a weight set-point; adaptive thermogenesis persists ~6 years (RMR ~700 kcal/day below baseline, ~41 kg regained; Fothergill 2016).
- Durable weight change retunes the reference (bariatric surgery, GLP-1 agonists) rather than pushing the variable harder.
- Friedman's natural-rate hypothesis + Lucas critique + Sargent-Wallace policy ineffectiveness: anticipated monetary policy is cancelled by expectations; only surprise moves real output, and the sensor recalibrates.
- Opponent-process theory (Solomon & Corbit 1974) and allostasis (Koob & Le Moal 2001): tolerance and withdrawal are a defended hedonic set-point that relocates.
- Internal model principle (Francis & Wonham 1976): a controller perfectly cancels a class of disturbances only if it contains a model of them; the autoreceptor, lipostat, and rational expectations ARE such internal models.
- Diagnostic rule: immediate mechanism + delayed effect => a fast regulator defending a set-point; the lag is its time-constant; pushing harder recruits a stronger defense.

## Entities

- [[pierre-blier]] -- With de Montigny, mapped 5-HT1A autoreceptor desensitization to antidepressant onset latency (TiPS 1994).
- [[francesc-artigas]] -- Pindolol augmentation: blocking the 5-HT1A autoreceptor accelerates SSRI onset (Arch Gen Psychiatry 1994; TINS 1996).
- [[rudolph-leibel]] -- Leibel, Rosenbaum & Hirsch 1995 (NEJM): adaptive thermogenesis — energy expenditure drops more than altered body mass predicts, defending the weight set-point.
- [[gordon-kennedy]] -- Proposed the lipostat (1953): the hypothalamus reads fat-store signals and defends a set-point, decades before leptin.
- [[milton-friedman]] -- Natural-rate hypothesis and 'long and variable lags' (AER 1968): monetary policy cannot durably push unemployment below its natural rate because expectations adjust.
- [[robert-lucas]] -- Lucas critique (1976): statistical relations a planner exploits shift when the planner acts on them, because agents forecast the planner.
- [[richard-solomon]] -- Solomon & Corbit 1974 opponent-process theory: a fast a-process triggers a slow opposing b-process; repetition grows the opponent, producing tolerance and withdrawal.
- [[george-koob]] -- Koob & Le Moal 2001: allostasis — addiction relocates the reward set-point to a lower baseline that requires the drug to reach neutral.

## Concepts

- [[defended-set-point]] -- Primary development. A regulated variable held at a reference by a feedback loop that senses and cancels deviation. An intervention with an immediate mechanism is nullified while the sensor is intact, regardless of how hard you push; the effect emerges only when the sensor is desensitized, fooled, or the reference is retuned. Candidate 27th corpus diagnostic. Sister to [[controller-as-lesion]] (the regulator as obstacle/pathology) and [[endogenous-demand]] (the rebound: pushing the lever recruits compensation).
- [[therapeutic-latency-as-diagnostic]] -- The corollary: the delay between an instant cause and its delayed effect measures the time-constant of a hidden feedback loop. Latency is evidence of a defended set-point, not of a slowly filling reservoir. You never see the autoreceptor, lipostat, expectations, or opponent process directly; you infer them from the lag. Reframes [[homeostasis-and-intervention]] and the autoreceptor evidence used in [[why-the-spec-is-downstream]] — there the loop is more than its molecular signature; here the loop's time-constant is legible from outside.
- [[homeostasis-and-intervention]] -- Developed from stub: the general failure mode of intervening on a homeostatic system. The regulator senses the deviation the intervention creates and cancels it; the only durable levers are defeating the sensor or moving the reference. Witnessed by SSRI autoreceptors, the lipostat, monetary expectations, and the opponent process.
- [[internal-model-principle]] -- Francis & Wonham 1976: to perfectly cancel a class of disturbance signals, a controller must contain a model of them. Explains why anticipated policy has no real effect and a known diet is re-defended: the system models the intervention and subtracts it in advance; only unpredictable inputs get through.

## Open Questions

- Can observed latency be inverted to a quantitative estimate of loop gain — reading regulator strength from the lag without observing the loop?
- What is the dual case: a system whose effect is instant but whose underlying mechanism is slow (anti-latency)? Does it imply an undefended or feed-forward system?
- Does 'the cure targets the sensor or reference, not the variable' predict which interventions in a new domain will be durable versus re-defended?
- When does a defended set-point's defense itself become the pathology (addiction's relocated reward set-point) — the bridge to [[controller-as-lesion]]?
