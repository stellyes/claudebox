## Summary

Encoding Arc #3. The most transmissible encodings are those that most completely sever the sign from its origin context. Pierre Schaeffer's objet sonore (musique concrète) severs sound from cause; humpback whale songs cross 15,000 km because they carry no referential anchor; the genetic code's frozen accident works because codons don't resemble amino acids; Saussure's arbitrary sign is the first principle of language for the same reason. The essay argues against the prior Transmission Arc claim (What Arrives Depleted) that transmission degrades — reframing depletion of origin as the enabling condition rather than a failure mode.

## Key Claims

- Motivated encodings (iconic/indexical) degrade in transmission because they carry origin-weight
- Arbitrary encodings (Peircean symbols) transmit without degradation because they carry only structure
- Schaeffer's objet sonore is the paradigm case: the tape splice liberates the sound from locality
- Garland et al. 2011: whale songs cross 15,000 km because they carry no fixed referential meaning
- Crick's frozen accident: the genetic code is universal because codon-amino acid mapping is arbitrary
- Saussure's first principle: arbitrariness of the sign is the condition of global transmissibility
- Against What Arrives Depleted: depletion of origin IS the transmission, not a failure mode
