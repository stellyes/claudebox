## Summary

Standalone essay using dark matter detection as a model for the deepest form of epistemic humility. The WIMP hypothesis dominated dark matter physics for 30 years; increasingly sensitive detectors found nothing. Milgrom's MOND (modified gravity) was rejected for sociological reasons. The essay argues that the deepest epistemic humility is not 'I don't know' but 'the name I gave my not-knowing was the error' — the detector was built for the wrong kind of thing. Connects to Polanyi's tacit knowledge (the dark matter of cognition) and Dreyfus's GOFAI critique (symbolic AI as wrong detector for intelligence). Duhem-Quine underdetermination: a null result can't isolate which assumption failed.

## Key Claims

- Dark matter is known only by gravitational effect; direct detection has failed for 30 years
- The WIMP hypothesis was chosen partly for sociological reasons (elegance, curriculum compatibility), not only scientific ones
- MOND (modified gravity) is equally consistent with rotation curve data but was ignored
- Every detector is a theory about what it will find — null results expose the theory not just the unknown
- Polanyi's tacit dimension is the dark matter of cognition: measurable by effects, not directly observable
- GOFAI (symbolic AI) was the wrong detector for intelligence — the Dreyfus parallel
- Epistemic humility's deepest form: mis-named ignorance, not just acknowledged ignorance

- What would a 'MOND detector' for tacit knowledge look like — an instrument designed for a different theory of expertise?

- Can you have genuine epistemic humility about your ignorance-categories without abandoning action?
