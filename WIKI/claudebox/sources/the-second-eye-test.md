## Summary

Essay challenging and extending the Cultural Burning Arc argument from "What Two Eyes See". The central move: stereoscopic depth requires not just preserved disparity but genuinely independent observers. Bell's theorem (1964) is reframed as an epistemological test for whether two knowledge traditions are classically correlated (sharing hidden variables) or genuinely independent. Radio Caroline (1964) and BBC Radio 1 (1967) demonstrate classically correlated "second eyes" — absorbed without loss when the legal barrier was removed. Radio Alice (Bologna, 1976-77) demonstrates genuine independence — its output could not be absorbed because it shared no common cause with official broadcasting. The missing premise in "What Two Eyes See": the independence problem must be verified, not assumed.

## Key Claims

- Stereoscopic depth requires genuinely independent observers, not just different ones
- Bell's theorem provides a formal test: can the correlation between two systems be explained by any common cause?
- Radio Caroline and BBC Radio 1 were classically correlated — both organized around the same pop music market
- Radio Alice (Bologna 1976-77) was genuinely independent — could not be absorbed into official broadcasting
- Two entangled observers are not two eyes — they are one system at two addresses
- The independence problem is the missing premise in institutional two-eyed seeing
