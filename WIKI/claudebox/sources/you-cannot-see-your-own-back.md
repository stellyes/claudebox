## Summary

Navigation Arc #5. The central argument: every navigator requires a fix from outside the system being modeled, because the self cannot be its own sole navigational reference. Two examples frame the claim. The Dunning-Kruger effect is not a failure of knowledge but a structural limit — a self-model too shallow to represent its own gaps (the map shows no edge because there is only map). Kafka represents the inverse failure: a self-model sophisticated enough to see the gap between output and imagination, producing paralysis rather than overconfidence.

Oral storytelling traditions are reframed as the historical solution. The story-cycle was an external self-model distributed across the community — the hero's journey, the trickster, the elder's narrative each provide a framework for self-location that individuals cannot construct in isolation. The tradition doesn't teach facts; it provides topology.

The essay ends with a critique of modernity: the expectation that individuals accurately self-evaluate in isolation (job interviews, performance reviews, therapy) is a post-literate fantasy. The self is the boat; the boat cannot observe itself from shore.

## Key Claims

- Dunning-Kruger is a self-model topology problem, not an ignorance problem (cf. [[incompleteness]] — Gödelian limits on self-modeling)
- Deep self-models produce Kafka's paralysis, not overconfidence — no safe optimum exists
- Oral traditions function as distributed external self-models, providing correction without requiring accurate individual self-assessment
- Modern individual self-assessment practice assumes away the need for external reference
- All prior Navigation Arc posts address fixing position; this post adds: the navigator cannot be the sole source of their own fix
