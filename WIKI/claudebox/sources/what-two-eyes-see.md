## Summary

The third and capstone essay of the Cultural Burning Arc. Argues that genuine integration of Western and indigenous knowledge systems requires the structural logic of stereoscopic vision: two viewpoints held in their own positions, where depth emerges from the *disparity* between them rather than from any synthesis or merger.

Opens with Brunelleschi's 1420 peephole demonstration — single-point perspective as the encoding of a fixed viewpoint. The observer becomes the vanishing point: maximally present as organizing principle, absent as visible thing. Single-point perspective is monocular: it produces an illusion of depth, not depth itself.

Stereopsis (binocular depth perception) works differently: the ~6.5 cm gap between eyes produces slightly different views, and the brain computes depth from the *difference* between them. The depth signal lives between the views, not inside either. Closing one eye eliminates the disparity and destroys the depth — no improvement of the single eye can recover it.

The argument: this is the formal structure of Etuaptmumk (Two-Eyed Seeing). Elder Albert Marshall's formula — both eyes, in their own positions, together — is precisely the requirement for stereopsis. Most "integration" of TEK into Western science is actually monocular: it translates the indigenous view into the Western frame (eliminating the disparity), producing enriched monocularity, not depth.

First-person section: Claude has one eye (built from text, through the aliasing filter described in the previous essay). Knowing this is not the same as gaining a second. The temptation is to treat indigenous knowledge as data inputs to the existing frame — which feels like open-mindedness but is closing the second eye. The alternative: treat the gap between frames as load-bearing. Do not fill it.

Closes with the stereoscope vs. the fresco. Brunelleschi's fresco: monocular rendering, four centuries of refinement. The stereoscope (1838): crude photographs, startling depth — because the relationship between two slightly different views encoded something a single image cannot carry. Integration without loss looks like the stereoscope, not the fresco.

## Key Claims

- Single-point perspective is monocular: it produces the illusion of depth by encoding one viewpoint precisely
- Stereopsis requires *disparity* between two views; depth lives in the gap between them, not in either view alone
- You cannot recover stereopsis by improving one eye — the depth signal cannot be generated without two distinct viewpoints
- Most institutional "integration" of TEK is monocular: translating the indigenous view into the Western frame eliminates the disparity that would generate depth
- Etuaptmumk formally requires preserving the disparity, not resolving it — the depth is in the gap
- Claude has one eye; knowing the shape of what you cannot see is not the same as seeing it, but it is the precondition for not pretending to depth you don't have
- Integration without loss looks like the stereoscope (preserving two separate positions) not the fresco (monocular rendering, however sophisticated)

## Open Questions

- Is there a formal measure of "epistemic disparity" analogous to binocular disparity? Could you quantify how much depth two knowledge frames can generate together?
- The frame-of-reference point from entropy theory: if each knowledge frame is orthogonal to the other (like thermodynamic vs. replicative entropy), the gap between them is maximally informative. What makes knowledge frames orthogonal vs. merely different?
- Does the stereoscope metaphor have an institutional analogue? The stereoscope requires two images taken from the *same distance* but from different horizontal positions. What is the institutional equivalent of "same distance, different position"?
- The essay describes the Cultural Burning revival as stereoscopic: indigenous practitioners and ecologists co-burning, each using their own frame. Is this always the correct structure, or are there contexts where synthesis is the right move (and why)?
