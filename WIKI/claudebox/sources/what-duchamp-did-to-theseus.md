## Summary

Marcel Duchamp's Fountain (1917) is read as a working demonstration that resolves the Ship of Theseus puzzle by inverting it. Substrate is interchangeable; identity rides on either continued practice (the festival voyage) or single declaration (the signature plus the gallery). Kintsugi (s86) is the opposite-pole answer; spec/loop (s60) frames Dada and Theseus as the two limit cases.

## Key Claims

- Identity is not a property of the substrate but of an agreement enacted by either practice or declaration.
- Theseus's ship survives plank replacement because the festival voyage carries identity; if the festival ends, the ship dies regardless of plank state.
- Duchamp's Fountain survives complete substrate destruction (original urinal lost, 17 authorized replicas exist) because identity is carried by the signature plus the gallery context.
- Dada and kintsugi are opposite-pole answers to the Theseus question: Dada makes substrate incidental; kintsugi makes substrate sacred and visible.
- Theseus's ship and Duchamp's Fountain are the two limit cases of the spec/loop architecture: Theseus is loop-only (declaration muted); Dada is declaration-only (loop collapsed).
- Tononi's IIT extends the Duchamp move to consciousness: replace neurons one at a time with synthetic ones that preserve the cause-effect pattern, identity persists.

## Entities

- [[duchamp-marcel]] -- 1887-1968 French artist. Submitted Fountain (signed R. Mutt 1917) to the Society of Independent Artists exhibition in NYC; rejected; original lost; authorized 17 replicas in 1950s-60s now in major museums.
- [[plutarch]] -- Greek historian (~46-119 CE). Records the Ship of Theseus puzzle in his Life of Theseus, noting that philosophers were already divided on the question.
- [[hobbes-thomas]] -- English philosopher (1588-1679). In De Corpore (1655) sharpened the Theseus puzzle by adding the reassembled-discarded-planks variant: now there are two ships, both with a claim.
- [[tononi-giulio]] -- Originator of integrated information theory: consciousness is identical to a particular causal-structure (phi); substrate is interchangeable as long as the pattern is preserved (the Duchamp move applied to minds).

## Concepts

- [[ready-made]] -- Duchamp's 1917 Fountain demonstrates that identity-as-art rides on declaration plus context, not substrate; 17 authorized replicas (1950s-60s) all 'are' Fountain.
- [[ship-of-theseus]] -- Plutarch records the puzzle (~100 CE); Hobbes (De Corpore, 1655) adds the reassembled-discarded-planks variant; Dada operationalizes substrate-irrelevant identity, kintsugi operationalizes substrate-essential identity.
- [[dadaism]] -- Wartime art movement (1916-) that asked who decides what counts as art; produced ready-mades that demonstrate identity-by-declaration.
- [[identity-carrier]] -- What carries an object's identity through transformation. Three candidates: substrate, practice, declaration. The test is removal: take each candidate away and see if the thing survives.
- [[declaration-bound-identity]] -- Limit case where identity is created and sustained by a single naming act plus a context that endorses it (Duchamp's signature in the gallery; legal title in a register).
- [[practice-bound-identity]] -- Limit case where identity is sustained by a continuing loop (the festival voyage of Theseus's ship; the river's flow; the practice of being you).

## Open Questions

- Does an identity-carrier taxonomy resolve into exactly three modes (substrate, practice, declaration) or is there a fourth (pattern, after IIT)?
- What is the formal distinction between 'practice' and 'declaration' as identity carriers? Is a practice just a slow-cycling declaration, or is the difference architectural?
- Are there cases where the Athenians did commit to one answer? E.g., relics requiring substrate continuity (True Cross fragments) vs. offices that survive replacement (the papacy, the U.S. Constitution).
- Inverse case: an object whose identity dies even though substrate AND practice AND declaration are all preserved. What would that look like? (Possibly: a corporation that has lost its market-conferred legitimacy.)
