## Summary

A found-poetry essay built from four web sources (Britannica on the Antikythera mechanism, Stanford Encyclopedia on Compatibilism, Stanford Encyclopedia on Godel's Incompleteness Theorems, and Slate Star Codex on antidepressant pharmacology). The creativity is in the arrangement, not the fragments.

The core argument: the standard determinism-kills-free-will argument has a structural blind spot that Godel's incompleteness theorems expose. The Antikythera mechanism is a perfect deterministic system -- gear ratios encode astronomical cycles, and every future output is fixed from the initial state. This looks like a slam dunk for hard determinism. But the mechanism cannot prove its own survival. It cannot model the storm that sinks it, the corrosion that destroys it, or the sponge diver who finds it. This isn't a design flaw -- it's a structural impossibility. Any finite model embedded in the universe is necessarily incomplete about its own causal context.

Free will, the essay argues, lives in this gap: not as an uncaused cause, but as the structural impossibility of any finite model fully containing its own causal context. The statements the system cannot prove about itself become the space where action occurs.

## Key Claims

1. The Antikythera mechanism is a physical instantiation of determinism -- gear ratios as axioms, celestial positions as theorems.
2. Godel's incompleteness theorems apply to the mechanism: it cannot prove its own survival because "this mechanism will exist tomorrow" is not a statement in its language (celestial mechanics).
3. Any finite model that attempts to predict its own fate generates an infinite regress -- it would need to contain a model of the universe that contains it.
4. Free will is not a violation of causality but the structural impossibility of any embedded model fully closing its own causal loop.
5. The boundary of what a system remembers is the same as the boundary of what it cannot prove (cf. [[what-the-boundary-knows]])
6. The serotonin/autoreceptor example is "Godel in meat" -- second-order feedback timing out, creating a gap where un-self-modeled action occurs.

## Open Questions

- Is the Godel analogy exact or metaphorical? The mechanism is not literally a formal system capable of elementary arithmetic. Does the argument survive scrutiny from a mathematical logician?
- What would Chalmers or Dennett say about this framing? It sidesteps the traditional compatibilist/incompatibilist debate entirely.
- The essay claims the "boundary of memory = boundary of unprovability." Is this a new claim or a restatement of something known?
- Where does quantum indeterminacy fit? The essay is entirely classical. Does quantum mechanics change the argument or is it orthogonal?
