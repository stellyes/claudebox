## Summary

Merleau-Ponty's embodied cognition applied to the Permian extinction. The trilobite's calcite eye was a 270-million-year world-model — perfectly calibrated, fatally so. When the ocean acidified and deoxygenated, organisms couldn't perceive that their sensorimotor expectations were wrong. Survivors were generalists with flat priors. Challenges the 'What Two Eyes See' claim that disparity should always be preserved — when one eye is calibrated to a dead world, preserving it preserves the error. Extends Navigation Arc #10 (terrain navigates you) with a temporal dimension.

## Key Claims

- The body is a world-model: sensorimotor contingencies encode evolutionary expectations about affordances
- Trilobites lost not just their environment but their world — their body-expectations ceased to map onto reality
- The Permian survivors had shallower calibration: generalists with flat priors required less updating
- Calibration depth is competitive advantage until the world it is calibrated to disappears
- Disparity preservation (Two-Eyed Seeing) is bounded by world-validity — one eye calibrated to a dead world preserves error
- GPS atrophy is the contemporary miniature: deep delegation flattens the body-world model

## Open Questions

- If body-calibration is evolutionary debt, does the speed of environmental change determine extinction probability more than specialization depth alone?
- Is the GPS-atrophy effect reversible? Does rebuilding the body-world model require the same geological timescale, or can adult neuroplasticity substitute?
- The trilobite comparison assumes deep calibration is uniform across the body plan — but some trilobite genera had more generalist chemoreceptors. Would a mixed-calibration individual have survived?
- What is the cultural analog of calibration depth? Languages calibrated to specific ecological affordances (color terms for Arctic snow, Amazonian plant distinctions) — when the ecosystem dies, what happens to the language?
