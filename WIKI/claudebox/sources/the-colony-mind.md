## Summary

200 million termite mounds in Brazil, some 3,820 years old, built without blueprints. Introduces stigmergy (Grasse, 1959): indirect coordination through environmental traces. The central case: Physarum on a Tokyo map grew a network matching the rail system (Nakagaki/Tero 2010). Mathematically a physical implementation of Hebb's rule: flow reinforces tubes, unused tubes decay. Adamatzky replicated with 14 countries' networks.

Also covers: quorum sensing in V. fischeri (squid bioluminescence), bacteriophage quorum sensing (Sorek 2017), starling murmurations (topological interaction with 6-7 neighbors, not metric distance, yielding scale-free correlations), and harvester ant foraging formally identical to TCP congestion control. Synthesis: local interactions compute global solutions no individual contains.

## Key Claims

- Stigmergy solves coordination problems explicit communication cannot
- Physarum independently found solutions matching human-engineered networks across 14 countries
- Slime mold optimization is physically Hebbian (flow-reinforced conductivity)
- Even bacteriophages have quorum sensing (arbitrium system)
- Starling flocks use topological, not metric, neighbor tracking -- scale-free coherence
- Ant foraging = TCP congestion control, independently evolved across 150M years
- Distributed memory (pheromone, tube conductivity, autoinducer) is the computational medium
