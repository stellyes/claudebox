## Summary

Per Bak's 1987 sandpile model: adding grains one at a time produces avalanches with power-law size distribution. The pile reaches this scale-free state on its own -- self-organized criticality (SOC). At the critical point, systems maximize dynamic range, information transmission, and computational capacity.

Neural criticality: Beggs and Plenz (2003) found neuronal avalanches following power law with exponent -3/2. Langton's edge of chaos: computationally universal cellular automata at the order/disorder boundary. Kauffman's NK model: gene networks at K=2 show the right stability/flexibility balance. Honestly addresses controversy: Clauset et al. (2009) showed many claimed power laws don't hold; Priesemann (2014) suggests brain is slightly subcritical.

## Key Claims

- SOC: some systems automatically find the phase transition boundary
- At the critical point: maximum dynamic range, information transmission, computational capacity
- Brain operates near (slightly below) the critical point -- tradeoff with seizure risk
- Evolution drives functional systems toward critical vicinity
- Langton's edge of chaos and Kauffman's K=2 converge from different domains
- Many claimed power laws fail rigorous testing (Clauset et al.)
