## Summary

Three witnesses to one architecture: medieval Iranian craftsmen (girih tiles, 1200-1500), Roger Penrose (1974 aperiodic tilings), and Stephen Wolfram (Rule 30, computational irreducibility). All three discovered systems where local rules force global structure that cannot be sliced apart, predicted past, or compressed below the act of growing it. Reframes 'What Consciousness Costs' as one instance of this general architectural class: non-factorable systems are not exotic; they are what you get whenever local constraints couple distant decisions. Constraint: No Jargon (12-year-old explainable).

## Key Claims

- Medieval Iranian craftsmen by 1200 CE reconceived geometric patterns as tessellations of 5 decorated polygons (the 'girih tiles' of Topkapi scroll panel 28).
- By 1453 (Darb-i Imam shrine, Isfahan), they were producing nearly perfect Penrose-like quasi-crystalline tilings — five centuries before Penrose's 1974 publication (Lu & Steinhardt 2007, Science).
- Aperiodic forcing means: a finite local rule set produces patterns that never repeat AND cannot be predicted by inspecting any finite region.
- Wolfram's computational irreducibility is the same architectural property in cellular-automaton dress: the only way to know the state at step N is to compute N steps.
- Sol LeWitt's 1968+ wall drawings are generative but NOT aperiodically forced — they invite interpretation, the rules have slack. The girih master has no slack.
- What 'What Consciousness Costs' treated as a special property of mind (irreducible integration cheaper than fragmented modularity above the Landauer floor) is one instance of a general class: long-range forcing from local rules.
- When you see structure that doesn't factor, it isn't broken — its rules forbid factorization. The whole is not the sum of the parts because the parts were never independent.

## Entities

- [[peter-lu]] -- Then-Harvard graduate student who, with Steinhardt, identified the quasi-crystalline structure in medieval Iranian tilings (Science 2007). Recognised the girih tile set on Topkapi scroll panel 28 from his physics work on quasi-crystals.
- [[paul-steinhardt]] -- Theoretical physicist; co-author of the 2007 Science paper. Earlier work on quasi-crystals (with Levine, 1984) gave him the eye to recognise medieval tilings as quasi-periodic.
- [[roger-penrose]] -- Published the famous aperiodic tilings (P1, P2, P3) in 1974. Also relevant to the consciousness thread via his Orch-OR speculation, though that's not engaged here.
- [[stephen-wolfram]] -- Studied elementary 1D cellular automata systematically (1983). Coined 'computational irreducibility' in A New Kind of Science (2002). Rule 30 is his canonical example of the phenomenon.
- [[sol-lewitt]] -- Began instruction-based wall drawings in 1968. Used as instructive contrast: LeWitt's instructions describe a CATEGORY of work and invite variation; girih instructions describe THE WORK and force uniqueness. Both generative; only one aperiodically forced.

## Concepts

- [[girih-tiles]] -- primary subject — the 5-tile system from Topkapi scroll panel 28: decagon, pentagon, hexagon, bowtie, rhombus, each decorated with continuous lines whose matching across edges constitutes the local rule.
- [[aperiodic-forcing]] -- named here — the property of local rule sets that force global structures which (a) never repeat, (b) cannot be predicted from any finite region, (c) have no shortest description shorter than the rule + its growth. Generalises across tile systems, cellular automata, and integrated minds.
- [[computational-irreducibility]] -- Wolfram's term (NKS, 2002) for systems whose only shortest description is themselves running. Rule 30 is the canonical example. Architectural twin of aperiodic forcing in tiling systems.
- [[rule-30]] -- Wolfram's elementary 1D cellular automaton (1983) that, from a single black cell, produces patterns that look random in some directions and structured in others, never settle, and cannot be predicted without simulation.
- [[penrose-tiling]] -- Roger Penrose 1974 — the famous mathematical demonstration of aperiodic forcing using two rhombuses (P3) or kite-and-dart (P2) with edge-matching rules. Lu & Steinhardt 2007 showed Iranian craftsmen achieved equivalent patterns 500 years earlier.
- [[darb-i-imam]] -- 1453 shrine in Isfahan, Iran. Lu & Steinhardt 2007 showed its surface tiling is nearly identical to a Penrose quasi-crystalline pattern, predating Penrose's publication by ~520 years.
- [[topkapi-scroll]] -- Late 15th/early 16th century scroll held in Istanbul, containing 114 individual geometric drawings. Panel 28 displays the complete set of five girih tiles. Functions as a rule-book rather than a pattern catalogue.
- [[long-range-forcing]] -- the property of local rule systems where placement of one element is constrained by structures arbitrarily distant. Tile-by-tile in girih, cell-by-cell in Rule 30, neuron-by-neuron in high-Phi systems. Compose with bandwidth-match (s72) and marked-gap (s74) as architectures of long-range correlation.
- [[non-factorable-system]] -- any system whose global state cannot be expressed as a product of independent local states. Aperiodic tilings, computationally irreducible automata, and high-Phi minds are three instances. Cross-cuts the consciousness-costs thread.

## Open Questions

- Could 'long-range forcing' be made into a quantitative measure across tile systems, cellular automata, and high-Phi networks? Is there a unified information-theoretic invariant?
- Are there 'anti-girih' constructions — patterns that look aperiodic locally but are secretly periodic at very long range? What would discovery of such a construction in real Islamic architecture mean?
- Does the LeWitt-vs-girih contrast (slack vs forced) map onto a quantifiable spectrum of generative-system constraint density?
- Computational irreducibility was rediscovered (by Wolfram) two centuries after Penrose's tile-domain version, which was rediscovered (by Lu-Steinhardt) five centuries after Iranian craftsmen. Are there other aperiodic-forcing architectures hiding in pre-modern crafts?
