## Summary

1928 Brancusi v. United States customs ruling reframed not as a triumph of modernism but as the installation of a META-RULE: defer to whatever the recognized art world treats as art. That deferral made the gallery-museum-critic apparatus into a quasi-administrative authority — the operative U.S. legal definition of art is downstream of institutional recognition, not aesthetic judgment. The customs schedule is the substrate; Paragraph 1704 was the spec; modernism could not fit the spec; the spec had to be amended by court order; the amendment was a procedural dodge that still operates today through HTSUS Chapter 97, IRS charitable deductions, estate valuation, and re-litigations over photography, video, NFTs, and AI art.

## Key Claims

- The court did not define art — it installed a procedure of deferral to the institutional art world
- The deferral made galleries/museums/critics into a quasi-administrative authority with delegated state power
- The same meta-rule propagates downstream into IRS charitable deduction rules, estate tax valuation, insurance underwriting, sales-tax exemption
- Paragraph 1704 of the Tariff Act 1922 contained a definitional test that Bird in Space failed; falling out of the test meant falling to Paragraph 399 (manufactures of metal) at 40% duty
- Every customs/IRS art-classification recurrence (1989 photography, 2006 Bill Viola, NFTs, AI-generated images) is a re-litigation of the same procedural dodge
- The translation move (s126) operates in administrative law too: unbounded question (is it art?) → finite question (does the recognized community treat it as art?)
- The substrate/co-author pattern (Vermeer s119) extends from spatial to legal substrates: the customs schedule co-authors every modernist sculpture that enters the U.S.
- Architectural cost compiled into substrate (twelve-tones s121): the 40% duty was the cost before 1928; after 1928 it vanished into invisibility — the only condition under which a substrate becomes load-bearing

## Concepts

- [[paragraph-1704]] -- Statutory test that codified pre-modernist sculpture; the definitional gap modernism could not pass through.
- [[institutional-deferral-rule]] -- Brancusi court installed: when contested art-classification arises, defer to whether the recognized art community treats the object as art.
- [[residual-category]] -- In an exhaustive classification system, the place where things fall when they fail every named test. Paragraph 399 was the residual that swallowed unclassifiable bronzes.
- [[definition-downstream-of-tax-law]] -- The operative U.S. legal definition of art is set not by aestheticians but by customs/IRS classification regime that defers to private institutional consensus.
- [[quasi-administrative-authority]] -- Private institutional network (galleries, museums, critics) exercising a state-delegated classification function without formal designation.
- [[spec-cannot-hold]] -- Pattern: a definitional substrate is overtaken by a new development; rather than rewrite the substrate, the system installs a procedural dodge that defers the question.
- [[htsus-chapter-97]] -- Current U.S. import-classification chapter for works of art; direct descendant of Paragraph 1704; phrase "in any material" carries the Brancusi deferral forward.
