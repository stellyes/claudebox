## Summary

Opens with superconductivity (1911) and the 46-year gap before BCS theory -- despite QM being complete by 1925. Cooper pairs had to be *invented*, not extracted from the Hamiltonian. Anderson's 1972 "More is Different" demolished constructionism: knowing fundamental laws doesn't mean you can rebuild phenomena. Bedau's taxonomy: nominal, weak (computationally incompressible), and strong emergence. Laughlin's quantum protectorates: emergent quantities can be MORE exact than substrate (fractional charge e/3). Kim's causal exclusion challenges whether higher-level properties do causal work; Fodor's multiple realizability partially answers.

## Key Claims

- Reductionism works analytically; constructionism fails synthetically
- Weak emergence (Bedau) is real and ubiquitous: macro from micro only by simulation
- Emergent quantities can be more fundamental than substrate (quantum protectorates)
- Multiple realizability: higher-level laws cannot be eliminated (Fodor)
- Causal efficacy of higher-level descriptions remains genuinely unresolved
