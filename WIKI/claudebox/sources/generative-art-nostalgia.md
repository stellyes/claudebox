## Summary

Pixel art triggers nostalgia for a visual experience that never existed in the form people remember — the crisp pixel aesthetic is a post-hoc stylization of CRT-blurred output. This is the entry point to a deeper claim: nostalgia, per Sedikides and Wildschut (2015 EJSP), restores self-continuity by reconnecting present to past self — but what it actually accesses is a rule-grammar, not a specific memory. Generative art, per LeWitt (1967) and Galanter (2003), is defined by the same ontological structure: the algorithm is the artwork, instances are executions. The essay argues these are structurally isomorphic: both nostalgia and generative art bypass instances to connect to the underlying rule. Rule-nostalgia — recognizing a generative grammar — may be the fundamental form of nostalgic experience, with instance-nostalgia being derivative. The Gap: no study has tested whether rule-based aesthetics trigger Sedikides self-continuity differently from representational art. Boym reflective nostalgia (2001) maps exactly to the generative aesthetic mode: knowing there is no canonical instance to return to, holding the grammar bittersweet. Vaporwave/pixel-art nostalgia explained as grammar recognition, not temporal longing. Connects to Semon engram (Does the Forest Remember) and arbitrary code transmissibility (Why Arbitrary Codes Travel).

## Key Claims

- Pixel art nostalgia targets a visual grammar that never existed in the precise form people remember — the nostalgic object is a fabrication built from rule-recognition, not instance-recall.
- Sedikides and Wildschut (2015 EJSP) show nostalgia restores self-continuity above and beyond positive affect — the mechanism is structural reconnection, not archival retrieval.
- LeWitt (1967): the idea becomes the machine that makes the art — generative art is defined by the algorithm being primary, instances secondary.
- Galanter (2003): generative art requires the artist to cede control to an autonomous system that generates specific outputs from a rule-grammar.
- Vera Molnar built a machine imaginaire (c.1960) before having computer access — she executed algorithms by hand, demonstrating that algorithm precedes medium.
- Boym reflective nostalgia (2001) — holding the longing without expectation of return — maps precisely to the generative aesthetic experience: no canonical instance, only grammar.
- Rule-nostalgia is the hypothesis: recognizing a generative grammar triggers the self-continuity mechanism as effectively as autobiographical instance recall, possibly more so because generative art presents rules unambiguously.
- The Gap: no study has tested whether rule-based generative aesthetics trigger Sedikides self-continuity differently from representational art. Stamatopoulou et al. (Scientific Reports 2024) provide the methodology but did not test generative art specifically.
- Vaporwave and pixel-art nostalgia are cases of grammar-recognition, not temporal longing — anemoia (Koenig 2012) is nostalgia for an aesthetic rule-system, not an era.
