## The Link

The cosmological constant as forgetting rate ([[what-the-boundary-knows]]) extends to broadcast regulation. A universe's horizon encodes the interior's information; jamming the horizon erases that encoding. A licensing system's boundary — the unlicensed/pirate signals at its edge — encodes information about what the interior refuses to carry. Enforcement is the forgetting rate: how fast the system erases the error-correction signal pointing at its blind spots.

This is not a metaphor. The information-theoretic structure is the same: a boundary (physical or institutional) encodes the interior's state. Suppression of the boundary reduces the system's ability to read its own failures.

## Evidence

**From physics (holographic principle):** Almheiri, Dong, and Harlow (2015) — holography is QEC. The boundary is the error-correcting code for the bulk. Erasing boundary information destroys the correction capability, not just the data.

**From pirate radio history (UK, 1964–1990):**
- Radio Caroline 1964: 10M listeners within weeks, proving unmet demand the BBC couldn't see
- Marine Broadcasting Offences Act 1967: enforcement rate increased — boundary signals suppressed
- Kiss FM 1985–1990: five years of illegal operation proving demand before legal licensing was possible. The pirate era was the necessary precondition for the legal outcome — boundary encoding that couldn't have been produced any other way
- Dread Broadcasting Corporation 1980: Britain's first Black-owned radio station, unlicensed because the licensing structure had no category for it. Enforcement here was erasure of error-correction code pointing at a structural exclusion

**From moral philosophy (trolley problem):**
- The lever-puller in the trolley problem usually doesn't choose who is on the side track — the tracks are stipulated
- In spectrum regulation, the regulatory body designates which broadcasters are "main track" and which are "side track" — making the track-drawing a prior act that the trolley-problem framing conceals
- This is the meta-trolley problem: the lever-puller who also draws the tracks has converted a thought experiment into a policy tool

## Implications

1. **Regulatory blind spots are forgetting rates.** Agencies that aggressively suppress informal/illegal activity at their boundaries are not merely enforcing law — they are reducing their own error-correction capacity. The tradeoff should be explicit.

2. **The pirate era is sometimes load-bearing.** Kiss FM required five years of illegal operation to prove what the licensing system couldn't otherwise know. Premature legalization or suppression before proof-of-demand destroys this function.

3. **Authorization is the hidden premise in the trolley problem.** Most variations stipulate who the agent is but not who authorized the track layout. Extending the trolley problem to institutional contexts (spectrum, zoning, publication) exposes this hidden premise.

4. **Generalizes beyond broadcasting.** Candidate cases: preprint servers vs. journal gatekeeping; whistleblowing channels vs. official disclosure; informal urban markets vs. licensed commerce; indigenous oral practice vs. academic publication. Each may function as boundary-signal-encoder for an interior system's blind spots.
