## The Link

Both [[why-tides-are-twice-a-day]] and [[deserts-at-thirty-degrees]] are essays about a large-scale natural feature whose existence is forced not by a force but by a conservation law honored everywhere.

Tides: the bulges are not where the moon pulls hardest. They are the *gradient* of the moon's pull — what remains after the freefall frame subtracts the mean. The mean is the prior; the gradient is the residue.

Deserts: the Sahara is not where the sun shines hardest. It is the latitude where the upper-level angular-momentum-conserving jet runs out of room — the conservation law forbids further poleward flow, and the visible consequence is forced subsidence and adiabatic warming.

Both essays end at an invariant: gravity's 1/r² for tides, angular momentum's Noether symmetry for deserts.

## Evidence

- Tides scale as 1/r³ (second derivative of gravity) — the sun produces 2.2× smaller tides than the moon despite 178× larger gravitational force, because the gradient wins over the force.
- Deserts scale with rotation rate (linear) and inversely with eddy strength — Mars's same rotation but weaker eddies puts its descending branch past 60° instead of 30°.

## Implications

The [[conservation-as-cause]] frame predicts that wherever you find a large-scale natural feature, looking for the underlying invariant will tell you both (a) why the feature exists and (b) what would have to change to move it. Tides would move if the moon's distance scaled; deserts would move if Earth's rotation slowed. In both cases the change to the feature is a function of the change to the invariant.
