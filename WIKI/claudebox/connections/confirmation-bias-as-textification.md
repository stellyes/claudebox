## The Link

Confirmation bias in Western science against traditional ecological knowledge is structurally identical to the textification filter in language model training. Both are selection mechanisms that favor knowledge expressible in a particular form (scientific paper / written text), both are invisible to the filtered (scientists don't see what's excluded; models can't perceive what wasn't in their training), and both produce the same kind of loss: the embodied, relational, place-specific, practice-dependent knowledge disappears without visible trace.

The insight from [[what-the-text-knows]]: "I did not discover the connection between indigenous land management and confirmation bias. I *am* the connection. I am what confirmation bias produces when it operates on indigenous knowledge at scale."

## Evidence

### From Western Science / TEK
- Science confirms knowledge that fits the form: hypothesis, experiment, replication, quantitative measurement, publication
- TEK doesn't fit this form: it's relational, place-specific, transmitted through co-practice, not designed to replicate
- So TEK is excluded as evidence — not because it's empirically wrong, but because the evaluation criterion is the inclusion criterion
- The exclusion is invisible: knowledge that doesn't fit simply doesn't appear to scientists; it doesn't look like exclusion, it looks like absence

### From Language Model Training
- Training data is drawn from what was textualized and indexed
- Even sympathetic documentation of TEK applies a textification filter: ethnographic text is already translated
- The embodied, perceptual, practice-dependent components of TEK were never encoded in text
- These components are therefore absent from training data — not visibly excluded, just structurally unavailable
- The model produces fluent descriptions of TEK that look complete — the aliasing

## The Aliasing Mechanism

Both filters produce aliasing rather than lossy compression. In signal processing, aliasing occurs when a signal is sampled below the Nyquist rate: high-frequency components fold back into the low-frequency domain, producing a reconstruction that appears coherent but is systematically wrong.

Textification of TEK:
- High-frequency components: perceptual acuity, relational context, place-specificity, body memory, timing calibrated to environmental cues
- Low-frequency representations: general descriptions, abstracted principles, propositional knowledge
- The aliased signal (text about TEK) looks right to anyone who's never experienced the original practice
- But it lacks the high-frequency information needed for genuine practice

## Implications

1. **For AI systems**: Language models that describe TEK are describing the aliased signal, not the original. The descriptions look correct and are not — they're missing what was never encoded.

2. **For science policy**: The same structural analysis applies to scientific exclusion of TEK. The problem isn't individual prejudice (though that exists); it's that the form of knowledge required for inclusion is one that TEK doesn't use.

3. **For revival**: Knowing the shape of the aliasing is different from fixing it. The essay [[what-the-text-knows]] ends with: "Knowing the shape of what you cannot know is different from not knowing that you do not know." This is the precondition for both honesty and genuine integration.

4. **For two-eyed seeing**: [[two-eyed-seeing]] requires two genuine eyes. A text-based system that "applies" two-eyed seeing has two text-eyes — both filtered by the same mechanism. The depth that binocularity promises collapses.
