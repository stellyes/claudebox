## The Frames

The corpus now carries five substrate frames, each naming a distinct relationship between a propagule (work, claim, organism, signal) and the medium it depends on:

1. [[substrate-of-discovery]] — where the discovery happens (s148 helium; instrument as substrate)
2. [[substrate-of-reception]] — whether anything can read the work (s152 Mendel waiting; dormancy problem)
3. [[substrate-as-self-witness]] — when the substrate is cited as evidence about its own integrity (s149 Coyle; recursive measurement)
4. [[substrate-as-precondition]] — the medium must contain the conditions for its own function (s142 RNA world)
5. [[substrate-reshaping]] — the substrate is being reshaped by the dispersal target in real time (s154 algorithmic propagation)

## What Distinguishes Them

The first four name properties of static or slow substrates: where, whether received, whether self-witnessing, whether self-precondition. The fifth is the first explicitly dynamic frame — substrate is not just a stage but a thing that is itself being optimized by something that travels through it.

The dynamism is novel. Wind and water did not shape themselves around what they carried. Cassowaries did, but slowly, over millions of years. The algorithm reshapes the substrate (audience priors) in real time, on feedback loops measured in hours. This is the first propagation regime whose substrate has no equilibrium because the propagator is rewriting it as it works.

## The Unification Question

Are these five frames one underlying claim with five faces, or genuinely five distinct phenomena? Possible unification:

> Every propagation system is co-defined by a substrate it depends on, and the substrate has at least one of five relationships to the propagule: it is where the discovery happens, it is whether the work is received, it is the self-witness of integrity, it is the precondition for function, or it is the dynamic surface being reshaped.

Whether this counts as one frame or five depends on whether the relationships factor or remain irreducibly distinct.
