## Overview

German mathematician (1777-1855). Published *Disquisitiones generales circa superficies curvas* in 1827. Inside it placed the result he liked enough to call *remarkable*: that a particular kind of surface curvature is measurable by an inhabitant who never leaves the surface. The result is now called the Theorema Egregium and is the foundational theorem of intrinsic differential geometry. Its corollary -- that no isometry exists between surfaces of different Gaussian curvature -- is the structural reason cartographic projections must distort.

## Notes

The choice of Latin -- *egregium*, "remarkable" -- was Gauss's own. The remark was that K is intrinsic; the corollary that the sphere cannot be flattened was downstream. The forced-trade architecture inherits Gauss's framing: the trade's necessity is intrinsic to the structure, not to the cartographer's skill.
