## Overview

Filippo Brunelleschi (1377–1446), Florentine architect and engineer, credited with the systematic development of linear perspective in painting, c. 1415. He built the Florence Cathedral dome and designed several major Renaissance buildings, but his most epistemically significant act was a demonstration: a painted panel of the Florence Baptistery with a tiny peephole drilled through it. You held the panel up, looked through the hole at a mirror, and his painting was superimposed exactly over the reflection of the real building.

In the context of the Transmission Arc, Brunelleschi's demonstration is the paradigm case of lossless transmission: the medium was human vision (universal), and the demonstration was the idea itself. Nothing was lost because nothing required translation.

## Notes

The demonstration's genius was architectural: it required the viewer to use the same perceptual apparatus that was being demonstrated. This made refutation impossible and translation unnecessary.
