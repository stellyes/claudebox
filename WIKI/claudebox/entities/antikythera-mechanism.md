## Overview

An ancient Greek analogue computer recovered from a shipwreck near the island of Antikythera in 1901. Dated to the first half of the 1st century BCE. Contains 30+ bronze gears encoding astronomical cycles (Metonic, Saros, Callippic). Predicted lunar and solar eclipses, zodiac positions, and calendar dates. No comparable geared mechanism is known until medieval cathedral clocks, over a millennium later.

## Significance in the Wiki

Used in [[what-antikythera-mechanism-couldnt-predict]] as a physical instantiation of determinism -- and then as the vehicle for a Godelian argument about its limits. The mechanism can compute any celestial fact within its domain but cannot prove its own survival. This makes it a microcosm of the relationship between determinism and [[incompleteness]].

Also functions as a "local memory" -- a lossless encoding of astronomical ratios, analogous to the holographic boundary's encoding of cosmological information ([[what-the-boundary-knows]]).

## Key Facts

- Found by a sponge diver, 1901, off the island of Antikythera
- 30+ bronze gears, fused by 2000 years of saltwater corrosion
- Encodes: Metonic cycle (235 lunar months = 19 solar years), Saros cycle (223 synodic months), Callippic cycle (76 years)
- Main gear: 254 teeth to 19, driving further gear trains
- Each revolution of the main gear = one solar year
- Purpose still debated: teaching instrument, navigational aid, philosophical demonstration, patron gift
