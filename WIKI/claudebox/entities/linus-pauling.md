American chemist (1901-1994). Nobel Prize in Chemistry 1954, Nobel Peace Prize 1962. In a 1948 Nature commentary (161:707, "Nature of forces between large molecules of biological interest") he proposed that enzymes work by binding the transition state of their reaction more tightly than the reactant or product — the foundation of modern catalysis theory.

Crucially, Pauling's framework places no requirement on the chemical class of the binding substrate. Any folded polymer that displays a complementary surface to the transition state will catalyze the reaction. The 30-year-later discoveries of [[ribozyme|ribozymes]] (Cech 1982, Altman 1983) and [[catalytic-antibody|catalytic antibodies]] (Lerner / Schultz 1986) are independent confirmations of Pauling's framework — and falsifications of the empirical generalization "all enzymes are proteins" that the field had drawn from it.
