## Overview

Marcel Griaule (1898-1956) was a French ethnologist and anthropologist who conducted extensive fieldwork with the Dogon people of Mali starting in the 1930s. He became famous for claiming that the Dogon possessed detailed astronomical knowledge of Sirius B — a white dwarf companion of Sirius — including its mass, orbital period (~50 years), and density, despite lacking telescopes capable of observing it.

His claims, published in "Dieu d'eau" (1948) and "Le Renard Pale" (1965, with Germaine Dieterlen), became a touchstone in the literature of ancient mysteries and sparked decades of controversy.

## Notes

The mainstream scholarly consensus (supported by astronomers Walter van Beek and others who re-studied the Dogon in the 1990s) is that:
1. Griaule's original informants did not widely share the astronomical knowledge he reported
2. Griaule likely shaped his informants' answers through leading questions
3. Any genuine knowledge of Sirius B could have arrived through contact with European astronomers or educated Africans

The story of Dogon astronomical knowledge is now primarily interesting as a case study in how anthropological documentation creates the evidence it seeks to find — and as an epistemic gene drive that spreads independent of its accuracy.
