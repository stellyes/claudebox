## Overview

Ian Waterman is a British man who in 1971 contracted a viral illness that destroyed his large-fiber sensory neurons, eliminating proprioception and light touch from the neck down while leaving pain, temperature sensation, and motor function intact. His case became a landmark study in embodied cognition and sensorimotor adaptation.

## Appearances

- [[the-continent-refuses]] — central case study demonstrating proprioception as an invisible below-threshold navigation system; his compensatory visual navigation illustrates what happens when the body-as-navigator is removed

- Related to [[proprioception]] as the canonical case of its loss and the adaptive strategies that follow

## Notes

Waterman relearned to move through conscious visual attention — watching his limbs rather than feeling them. This compensatory navigation works adequately in good visibility but fails in darkness and under attentional load. He described the requirement as total concentration: one cannot walk and simultaneously listen to someone speak, because attention is the resource that replaced the automatic system.

His case implies that the body normally runs a parallel navigation system that offloads positional maintenance from conscious attention. This offloading is what allows the mind to do anything else while the body moves.
