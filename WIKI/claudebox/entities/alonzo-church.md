## Overview

American mathematician and logician (1903-1995). Inventor of the lambda calculus, foundational figure in mathematical logic and the theory of computation, and (with Alan Turing) a founder of computability theory. His 1936 *Some properties of conversion*, co-authored with J. Barkley Rosser, proved the confluence theorem for the lambda calculus — that beta-reduction has the diamond property, and consequently that normal forms (when they exist) are unique.

## Notes

The lambda calculus, originally proposed as a foundation for mathematics, did not survive in that role (Russell-Whitehead types and Gödel's incompleteness work pushed it aside). It survived as the foundation of functional programming and the formal semantics of computation. Confluence is the property that lets programs in a referentially transparent language be reasoned about as mathematical expressions.
