## Overview

The south-pointing chariot was an ancient Chinese mechanical device (attested from approximately the 3rd century BCE through the Song Dynasty, ~1100 CE) in which a figurehead mounted on a chariot always pointed south regardless of the chariot's direction of travel. The mechanism did not use magnets or any compass-based detection of south — it used a differential gear system that counter-rotated the figurehead by exactly the angle the chariot turned. South was preserved through compensation, not detection.

The exact mechanism was rediscovered in the 20th century; the original engineers left no surviving schematic.

## Appearances

- [[the-continent-refuses]] — opening example and central metaphor for compensation-navigation; the chariot demonstrates that a system can maintain orientation without knowing its absolute position

## Connections

- Related to [[proprioception]]: both are compensation-navigation systems that preserve orientation through continuous correction rather than periodic detection
- Related to [[dead-reckoning]]: both involve maintaining position without external reference; but the chariot preserves heading (not position), and uses mechanical rather than cumulative accounting
- Conceptual precursor to the differential gear, which appears in the [[antikythera-mechanism]] and modern automobile transmissions

## Notes

The chariot's figurehead has no information about where south is. It only "knows" the relationship between its current orientation and the chariot's current heading, and the differential mechanism ensures that as the heading rotates, the figurehead counter-rotates by the same amount. South is an invariant maintained through change, not a fixed fact detected anew.

This makes it a model of navigation-by-invariant-preservation, structurally distinct from both dead reckoning (position-by-accounting) and landmark navigation (position-by-detection). The Navigation Arc uses it to open the discussion of embodied navigation precisely because it demonstrates that a third navigation mode — relationship-preservation — exists and is ancient.
