## Definition

VO2max is the maximum rate at which an athlete can take in, transport, and consume oxygen, measured in millilitres per kilogram of body mass per minute. It is the conservation ceiling for aerobic energy: above it there is no aerobic metabolism, only anaerobic systems that cannot sustain endurance work.

By the Fick equation, VO2max equals cardiac output (heart rate × stroke volume) multiplied by the arterio-venous oxygen difference. Each factor has a physiological ceiling; together they bound the rate at which oxygen molecules can move from atmosphere to mitochondria.

## Tensions and Contradictions

- VO2max is highly trainable in untrained subjects (20-30% gains possible) but plateaus quickly in elite athletes (1-2% gains per year, then stable).
- Heritability of VO2max is high (estimates 40-60%), but the heritable fraction is not the dominant determinant of marathon performance among elites — running economy is.
- VO2max measurements vary by ~3% between modalities (treadmill vs cycle ergometer) and ~5% between labs; absolute comparisons across studies are noisy.

## Synthesis

VO2max is the most-publicized item in the marathon inventory because it sounds like the simplest answer to "how aerobic is this athlete?" In fact it is the conservation ceiling — necessary but not sufficient. Among elite marathoners VO2max varies less than the other two items, and the predictive power of the model comes from how the three multiply, not from any single number.
