## Definition

Saussure's first principle of linguistics: the link between signifier (sound-image) and signified (concept) is arbitrary — conventional rather than natural, with no resemblance or causal connection between them. The word "tree" doesn't look like a tree. "Arbre" and "Baum" refer to the same concept with different signifiers. This arbitrariness is what makes linguistic structure globally portable.

## Peirce's Typology

- **Icon**: works through resemblance (portrait, onomatopoeia) — substrate-dependent, non-translatable
- **Index**: works through causal/existential connection (smoke→fire) — medium-dependent
- **Symbol**: works through convention only (words, codons, musical notation) — universally transmissible

## Tensions and Contradictions

- Onomatopoeia appears to challenge arbitrariness, but Saussure dismissed it as marginal — and even onomatopoeia varies across languages (dogs "woof" in English, "wang wang" in Chinese)
- Is there a continuum from motivated to arbitrary, or a sharp distinction? The Peirce typology treats it as a spectrum
- Initial learning might favor motivated encodings — arbitrary signs require community agreement to bootstrap

## Synthesis

The arbitrary sign is the linguistic instance of a general principle: the most transmissible encodings are those that sever completely from origin context. Saussure presented this as a structural feature of language; Encoding Arc #3 shows it holds across molecular biology, marine biology, and acoustic art. The cut is infrastructure.
