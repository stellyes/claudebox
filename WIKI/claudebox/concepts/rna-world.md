## Definition

A hypothesized phase in the origin of life on Earth during which RNA performed both the information-storage role (replicated as sequence) and the catalytic role (folded into active sites), before the emergence of DNA-as-vault and protein-as-workforce.

Term coined by Walter Gilbert in a Nature commentary (Gilbert, Nature 319:618, February 1986) following the early-1980s discovery of [[ribozyme|ribozymes]] by Cech and Altman.

## Antecedents

Three independent voices proposed RNA priority fifteen years before there was empirical support:

- Carl Woese, *The Genetic Code* (Harper & Row, 1967).
- Francis Crick, "The origin of the genetic code," J Mol Biol 38:367 (1968).
- Leslie Orgel, "Evolution of the genetic apparatus," J Mol Biol 38:381 (1968).

All three reasoned by elimination: a primordial system needed both information and catalysis, but a system with two separate substrates faces a chicken-and-egg problem. The only molecule known at the time that could plausibly do both was RNA, since the cloverleaf secondary structure of tRNA showed nucleic acids could fold into intricate shapes.

## Empirical support

- [[ribozyme|Ribozymes]] -- RNA molecules can catalyze chemistry.
- The ribosome's catalytic core is entirely RNA (Steitz, Yonath, Ramakrishnan 2009 Nobel).
- *In vitro* selection has generated ribozymes for ligation, polymerization, and aminoacylation (Bartel, Szostak, Joyce labs from the 1990s onward).
- Prebiotically plausible synthesis of activated ribonucleotides (Powner, Gerland, Sutherland, Nature 459:239, 2009).

## Open problems

- Synthesis of long, sequence-specific RNA strands under prebiotic conditions remains unsolved.
- A fully self-replicating ribozyme — one that copies a strand long enough to copy itself — has not yet been demonstrated, though incremental progress continues.
- A simpler chemical ancestor (TNA, PNA, GNA) may have handed off to RNA later. If so, the [[substrate-as-precondition]] argument still applies but to that ancestor, not to RNA per se.
