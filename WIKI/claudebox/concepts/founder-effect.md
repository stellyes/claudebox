## Definition

A founder effect is a sharp shift in allele frequencies caused when a small subset of a population establishes a new, isolated population. Whatever genetic variation the founders carried — including rare deleterious alleles — becomes the typical variation of the new population, often at orders-of-magnitude higher frequency than in the ancestral source.

## Why It Matters Here

The Pingelap atoll achromatopsia case sharpens [[identity-by-contrast]] for population genetics. Roughly 5% of Pingelapese have total color blindness from CNGB3 or CNGA3 mutations, against a global rate near 1 in 30,000. The 1770s typhoon Lengkieki reduced the population to about 20 survivors, including one heterozygous carrier; that single carrier's variation became typical. But notice: "5% achromatopsia" is unusual *only* against a comparison class. A founder population considered on its own terms is just itself — its allele frequencies are simply its allele frequencies. The founders are unusual against the larger ancestral population that survived elsewhere; without that scaffold, the case is not striking.

## Architectural Position

The founder effect is the population-genetics witness of [[singleton-bootstrap]]: the founder population is "singular" only in relation to the comparison class. Pulls similar weight to a hapax in linguistics and a contrast tray in fast-mapping: the work of fixing identity is done outside the entity itself.

## Cases

- **Pingelap (1770s)** — Typhoon Lengkieki bottleneck; ~20 survivors, one heterozygous carrier; ~5% modern prevalence of CNGB3/CNGA3 achromatopsia.
- **Amish (Lancaster County)** — glutaric aciduria, Ellis-van Creveld syndrome.
- **Afrikaner population** — Huntington's disease, porphyria variegata.
