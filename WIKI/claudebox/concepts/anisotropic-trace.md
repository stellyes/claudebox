## Definition

The directional principle inside [[failure-defined-structure]]: a disturbance leaves a trace in the substrate IN THE DIRECTION OF the disturbance, not isotropically. Bone deposits along the principal stress direction (Wolff). Silk crystallizes along the shear gradient (Vollrath/Knight). Paxos protocol depth grows in proportion to partition rate. Without anisotropy, the substrate would average disturbances away; with it, the substrate accretes a record.

## Tensions and Contradictions

- Inverse case: isotropic substrates that respond uniformly to disturbance regardless of direction (homogeneous heating in a uniform medium). These cannot be failure-defined.
- Compatible with [[hebbian-plasticity]] — the Hebbian "fire together, wire together" rule is anisotropic in the direction of co-activation.

## Synthesis

Anisotropy is what distinguishes a plastic substrate that *records* disturbance from one that merely *resists* it. The structure you see in failure-defined systems is the anisotropic trace, accumulated over enough disturbance events to dominate the substrate's geometry.
