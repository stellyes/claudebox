## Definition

The tropical atmospheric circulation cell consisting of rising air near the equator, poleward flow aloft (~10-15 km), descending motion in the subtropics (annual mean around 25° N/S), and surface return flow toward the equator that appears as the trade winds. First described by George Hadley in 1735.

## Tensions and Contradictions

The axisymmetric Held-Hou theory predicts cell extent based on inviscid angular momentum conservation alone, but observed Earth Hadley cells are narrower than Held-Hou predicts. Comprehensive GCMs need explicit baroclinic eddies (Frierson 2007, Lu 2007) to match observations. The cell terminates where the angular-momentum-conserving jet exceeds whatever the actual eddy field can absorb — Earth has strong eddies and a narrow cell; Mars has weak eddies and a much wider cell.

## Synthesis

The Hadley cell is the visible result of two competing constraints: angular momentum wants to drive the upper-level jet ever faster as air moves poleward, and baroclinic instability wants to break up flows above a critical shear. Where they meet, the cell terminates. The deserts ride on the meeting line.
