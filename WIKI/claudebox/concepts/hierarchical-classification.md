## Definition

A classification scheme in which every item is assigned a single address in a tree — one parent category, optionally subdivided. The Dewey Decimal Classification (1876) is the canonical example: 500 is natural science, 510 is mathematics, 516 is geometry, 516.3 is analytic geometry. Each book sits on one shelf.

## Tensions and Contradictions

Items that pertain to multiple subjects are routed to one parent and lose the others. A book on medieval Arab transmission of Greek mathematics is filed under one parent and disappears from the others. The collision rate scales with subject diversity.

## Synthesis

Hierarchical classification is the high-altitude index: one number per book, one axis privileged, everything else discarded. Browseable, equality-queryable, lossy. Works well when items genuinely have one dominant axis; fails when they don't.
