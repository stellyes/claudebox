## Definition

The principle, originally proposed by Donald Hebb in 1949, that repeated co-activation of two neurons strengthens the synaptic connection between them. Formally: if neuron A repeatedly fires and thereby causes neuron B to fire, the synapse from A to B is strengthened. The canonical formulation is "neurons that fire together wire together" (not Hebb's own phrasing).

The generalized form — relevant across the Transmission Arc — is that any substrate modified by repeated signal passage becomes structurally better at carrying that signal again. This applies beyond synapses: myelination is Hebbian (white matter plasticity follows the same rule); cultural substrates are Hebbian (repeated exposure restructures reception); pretrained ML models are Hebbian substrates (training carves channels before fine-tuning arrives).

## Tensions and Contradictions

- **LTD as the limit**: Long-term depression (LTD) is the counter-mechanism — unused synapses weaken. This provides some substrate renewal. But myelination appears more permanent than synaptic weight. The cultural analog of LTD is unclear.
- **Intentional vs. incidental carving**: Bernays deliberately engineered substrate modification. Most cultural Hebbian carving is incidental — no one designed the priming. Does intentionality change the mechanism or only the speed?
- **The first-mover asymmetry**: Hebbian mechanics favor first transmitters. But there are domains where later signals can overcome early carving (scientific paradigm shifts, conversion experiences). What conditions allow late signals to overcome structural disadvantage?
- **Is substrate renewal possible without erasure?** LTD weakens the carved channel, but you lose the accumulated weight, not just the asymmetry. Is there a way to restore receptivity to new signals while preserving existing knowledge?

## Synthesis

Hebbian plasticity, generalized beyond neuroscience, explains a class of transmission failures that are not addressable by message improvement. If the substrate carrying a signal has been modified by prior signals, any message that differs from the priming signal enters a biased medium. The structural advantage accrued by early transmitters is physical, not argumentative. It can be overcome only by substrate modification — changing the reception apparatus, as Bernays understood — or by finding a substrate that has not yet been claimed.

The most extreme form is the evolutionary case: Earth's magnetic field shaped the cryptochrome receptor over hundreds of millions of years. The signal built its own receiver. Cultural propaganda campaigns are the fast-timescale version of the same mechanism.
