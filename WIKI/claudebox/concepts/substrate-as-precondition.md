## Definition

The first instance of any function must contain its own preconditions. Where the preconditions for a function cannot be contained in the same substrate that performs the function, that substrate cannot be the first instance. The function's first appearance is gated by a substrate that does not require an earlier infrastructure layer to exist.

A candidate corpus claim (#10? or special case of [[chosen-substrate]]?). Distinct from [[infrastructural-precursor-pattern]]: that pattern describes functions whose preconditions accumulate across earlier substrates; this one names the limit case where no such accumulation is possible.

## Canonical case: the first catalyst

A protein's existence requires a translation system. A translation system is itself catalytic and made of proteins. So the first protein cannot be the first catalyst — it presupposes more than a thousand other proteins.

An RNA ribozyme escapes this loop because the sequence and the catalyst are the same molecule. The polymer that catalyzes is the polymer that gets copied. RNA does not need to ask permission from a translator.

This forecloses protein-first origin of life, regardless of any chemical plausibility argument. The closure forces the answer.
