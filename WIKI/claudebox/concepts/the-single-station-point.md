## Definition

**The single station point** is the structural cost shared by every representation that makes a heterogeneous, multi-claimant reality commensurable and exchangeable: it does so by **projecting everything onto one privileged viewpoint**. That projection buys real coherence — legibility at a glance, tradability between strangers — at a fixed price: every stakeholder's relation *other than* the privileged one becomes an **anamorphic distortion** the representation cannot register. The discarded relations are not erased; they are present in the scene but legible only by stepping off the one spot the representation was built for.

The name is from linear perspective. A perspective painting is geometrically correct from exactly one point in space — the *station point* — and progressively wrong everywhere else. [[linear-perspective]]

## The two instances that prove it is one move

- **Perspective** projects continuous, qualitative space onto a flat surface ruled into commensurable units, correct from one eye. Alberti called the picture plane a *finestra aperta* (open window) — but a window shows what's there from anywhere you stand; a perspective construction only from its station point. Panofsky: it is a *symbolic form*, a worldview rendered as if it were optics. [[erwin-panofsky]] [[anamorphosis]]
- **Price** projects every stakeholder's relation to a thing onto one scalar, correct from the **marginal exchanger's** viewpoint (the next buyer/seller who would actually transact) and anamorphic from every other. The 30-year tenant is the skull in Holbein's *Ambassadors*: fully present, invisible from the seat the picture was built for.

That these are the *same* move is not analogy but history: Crosby's **pantometry** ([[pantometry]]) shows perspective painting and double-entry bookkeeping as two expressions of one 15th-century quantification revolution (Piero della Francesca + Pacioli, same town).

## The reform theorem (the sharp kernel)

If a price is a single-station-point projection, there are **exactly two** things you can do about whom it distorts:

1. **Move the eye** — redistribution *within* the projection (subsidy, voucher, tax credit). Relocates which stakeholder is crisp; **cannot zero the total distortion** (best case minimizes it, never eliminates it). Someone is always the skull. The displacement is conserved.
2. **Split the projection** — remove the thing from the surface the price is painted on. The **community land trust** ([[community-land-trust]]) holds land in perpetuity; the resident owns only buildings; ground-distortion goes to zero for everyone. It does not lower the price — it removes the land from the priced plane.

Confusing (1) with (2) is the central error of housing policy.

## Open questions

- Is there a **third reform** — a *multi-station* (polycentric) representation that stays commensurable without a single eye? Do prediction markets, participatory budgeting, or quadratic voting approximate it, or do they smuggle a station point back in?
- The **cost frontier**: how much commensurability must be surrendered per unit of distortion removed?
- **Generalize the station-point test**: which stakeholder is the skull of a credit score, a benchmark, GDP, a model eval?
