## Definition

The cluster of behaviors African elephants (and to a lesser extent Asian elephants) exhibit in response to the dead and to the bones / ivory / skulls of their conspecifics: approaches, touching with the trunk, lifting attempts, vocalizations, and prolonged stationary attendance, often spanning multiple visits across days, months, or years.

Two anchor sources:

1. **McComb, Baker, & Moss 2006** (Biology Letters): in a controlled choice test at Amboseli, elephants showed a steep species-specific gradient -- ivory > elephant skulls > rhino/buffalo skulls > wood -- with no metabolic explanation. Crucially, individuals did not show preferential interest in the personally-known matriarch's skull over unrelated females' skulls
2. **Goldenberg & Wittemyer 2020** (Primates): review of field observations across Samburu and other sites; behaviors persist across decay stages (fresh / scattered / sun-bleached) and across both bonded and unbonded individuals

## Tensions and Contradictions

- The "no preference for personal kin" finding complicates anthropomorphic readings of elephant grief. Is the behavior emotion-driven or category-driven? Memorial-cost reframes the question as receipt-keeping rather than emotional response, sidestepping the dispute
- Replication question: is McComb-2006 stable across populations? What would Samburu, Kruger, Botswana data show?

## Synthesis

Elephant mourning is the cleanest non-human witness for memorial-cost architecture. The behavior persists despite no metabolic payoff, persists after the personal kinship has been forgotten, and produces a herd-visible cost that flags the loss in the social fabric. The behavior is best read not as "elephants grieve like humans" but as "elephants and humans both pay memorial cost, in their respective substrates."
