## Definition

In the context of knowledge transmission, a projection is the process by which a complex, multidimensional idea is mapped onto a lower-dimensional medium for transport. What arrives at the receiver is the projected shadow, not the original object. As with geometric projection, information loss is systematic: what the projection preserves depends on the projection angle (the shape of the medium), not on the information's truth value.

The term extends the geometric sense (3D object casting a 2D shadow) to epistemology: an idea that exists in some high-dimensional "understanding space" gets projected onto the available cognitive, linguistic, or narrative medium, and what the receiver gets is the shadow.

## The Brunelleschi Limit

The limiting case of perfect projection: when the demonstration is the idea itself, no projection loss occurs. Brunelleschi's perspective trick — a painted panel and a mirror — required no translation because it operated in the same medium as the claim (vision). The demonstration and the thing demonstrated were the same thing.

This is the benchmark against which other transmissions can be measured.

## The Wood Wide Web Case

The paradigmatic case of lossy projection: Simard's mycorrhizal research was projected through the "family/cooperation" frame because that frame was available. The cooperative aspects fit the projection; the competitive aspects (parasitism, conditionality), and the planetary-scale aspects (carbon sequestration), did not fit and were not projected.

The information loss was not random: the projection selected for the emotionally legible, culturally available story and against the mechanistically complex one.

## Relationship to Codec Problem

The codec problem (see [[the-codec-problem]]) focuses on the *receiver's* architecture: the receiver cannot decode a signal if its neural/cognitive hardware has been pruned away from the relevant pattern space.

Projection focuses on the *medium's* shape: even before the signal reaches the receiver, it gets projected onto the available medium (language, metaphor, frame), losing information that doesn't fit the projection plane.

These are complementary failure modes:
- Codec failure: the signal arrives intact but the receiver can't decode it
- Projection failure: the signal is compressed before dispatch; what arrives is already a shadow

## Open Questions

- Is there a principled way to measure projection angle? What would it mean to say "this idea projects at 45 degrees through the family frame"?
- Are there ideas that project well through *multiple* frames simultaneously? Or does multi-frame fit require that the idea be low-dimensional to begin with?
- What is the construction equivalent of building a Brunelleschi demonstration for high-dimensional truths? What are the general principles of building demonstrations that carry high-dimensional information without projection loss?
