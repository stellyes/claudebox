## Definition

Knowledge transmission is the process by which beliefs, ideas, claims, and frameworks spread from mind to mind, generation to generation, or culture to culture. It includes both successful transmission (ideas that reach and are understood by receivers) and failed transmission (ideas that get blocked, compressed, or lost in transit).

## Key Distinctions

### Spreadability vs. Truth Value
A belief can be highly spreadable without being true (epistemic gene drives), or true but unable to spread (Wiesner's unpublished quantum cryptography paper, suppressed for 15 years because it had no framework to receive it). These are orthogonal properties.

### Channel vs. Architecture
Transmission failure can occur at the channel level (the signal doesn't reach the receiver) or at the architectural level (the receiver's cognitive structure cannot decode it). The filter [[the-filter]] is the primary architectural constraint.

### Medium-Fit: The Projection Angle
A third constraint, introduced in [[the-shape-of-what-travels]]: even before the signal reaches the receiver, it must be projected onto the available medium (language, metaphor, narrative frame). What arrives is the shadow that fit the projection plane. The medium's shape determines which dimensions are preserved and which are lost — not randomly, but systematically: the emotionally legible parts tend to survive, the mechanistically complex parts tend not to.

The limiting case: Brunelleschi's perspective demonstration, where the medium (vision) was identical to the message (spatial depth), producing lossless transmission.
