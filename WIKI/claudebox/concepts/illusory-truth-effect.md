## Definition

The illusory truth effect: repeated exposure to a statement increases its perceived truth rating, even when the statement is false. First demonstrated by Hasher, Goldstein, and Toppino (1977) in three-session studies at Villanova and Temple Universities. Average truth ratings for repeated statements rose: 4.2 → 4.6 → 4.7 (on 7-point scale). Non-repeated statements showed no change.

## Key Properties

**Logarithmic curve**: The relationship between repetition count and perceived truth follows a logarithmic function. The largest single jump occurs on the *second* exposure. Subsequent repetitions yield diminishing returns, with the curve plateauing around 9 exposures. (2021 study, PMC8116821; r = 0.52-0.56 correlation with log scale)

**Knowledge neglect** (Fazio et al. 2015): Prior knowledge does not protect against the effect. Participants who correctly answered a factual question still became more credulous toward repeated false alternatives. Multinomial modeling confirmed that people sometimes rely on fluency even when stored accurate knowledge is available. This is the most disturbing finding — the heuristic overrides deliberate evaluation.

**Single-exposure fake news effect** (Pennycook, Cannon & Rand 2018): Even one prior exposure to a fake news headline increased perceived accuracy — within the same session and after a week-long delay. Effect held even when headlines were labeled "contested by fact-checkers" and even when inconsistent with political ideology. Only entirely implausible claims were immune.

**Sleeper effect** (Begg, Anas & Farinacci 1992): Statements explicitly labeled FALSE in one session were later rated more true than entirely novel statements. Source memory decayed; processing fluency from prior exposure persisted.

## Mechanism: Processing Fluency

The underlying mechanism is processing fluency. Prior exposure makes a statement easier to process — the brain's perceptual and semantic machinery runs faster with familiar inputs. This ease generates a mild positive affect and a sense of "rightness." People cannot distinguish *why* something feels easy — they experience the ease and infer reliability.

Reber & Schwarz (1999) demonstrated fluency affects truth ratings *without repetition*: identical statements in higher visual contrast were rated as more true. McGlone & Tofighbakhsh (2000): rhyming aphorisms rated as truer than non-rhyming equivalents with identical meanings.

### Ecological Rationality

Reber & Unkelbach (2010) argue the fluency-truth heuristic is locally rational under cooperative communication. If (1) most statements encountered are true, (2) true statements cohere with background beliefs and thus receive more repetition, and (3) there is one true value per fact but infinite false alternatives — then fluency really does correlate with truth. Experimental confirmation: when 90% of fluent stimuli were actually true, accuracy using the fluency heuristic reached ~86%.

**The problem**: the heuristic cannot distinguish cooperative from adversarial communication environments. Under systematic misinformation (propaganda, viral false claims, ritual repetition of false cosmology), the quorum threshold is reached equally by false and true claims.

### Reversibility

Unkelbach (2007) showed the fluency-truth association is *learned*, not hardwired. Participants trained in an environment where fluent statements were systematically false reversed their judgments. Implication: cultural quorums are dissolvable in principle — but require sustained environmental retraining, not argument.
