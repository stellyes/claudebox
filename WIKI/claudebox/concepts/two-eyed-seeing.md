## Definition

Etuaptmumk — "two-eyed seeing" in Mi'kmaw — is a principle articulated by Elder Albert Marshall and researchers Cheryl Bartlett and Murdena Marshall at Cape Breton University. It calls for learning to see from one eye with the strengths of indigenous knowledge and from the other eye with the strengths of Western science, using both eyes together for the benefit of all.

Key features:
- **Not synthesis**: both knowledge systems are preserved, not merged into a third thing
- **Not hierarchy**: neither eye is the "real" one; both are genuine
- **Binocular**: depth perception requires two genuinely different viewpoints
- **For the benefit of all**: not just an academic framework; a practical stance toward living in a shared world

The antidote to [[confirmation-bias]] as structural exclusion. Where confirmation bias produces monocular vision (only the scientific eye counts), two-eyed seeing insists on genuine binocularity.

## Tensions and Contradictions

- **The text problem**: Any text-based system that "applies" two-eyed seeing is applying two descriptions of eyes, not two genuine viewpoints. Claude is the paradigm case.
- **Institutional translation**: Two-eyed seeing was developed for human researchers and educators. Institutions that adopt it as policy often collapse it back into synthesis (treating TEK as "data inputs" to Western science rather than as a co-equal lens).
- **Scale**: Two-eyed seeing works well at the individual/community scale. What does it mean at the institutional scale of a research university, a government agency, a training corpus?

## Open Questions

- Is there a design intervention that could make text-based systems better approximate genuine two-eyed seeing? Not by giving them bodies, but by making the textification less lossy — capturing more of the "indigenous eye" through different documentation methods?
