## Definition

The power of a perturbation on a bistable system is set by its **proximity to the bifurcation, not its magnitude**. Near the boundary between two basins of attraction (the separatrix), a small input decides which way the system falls; far from it, even a large input is absorbed. Corollary: interventions on such systems are **time-dependent, not dose-dependent** — a small nudge at the right moment beats a large one at the wrong moment.

## The two-directional claim

The negative and positive directions of a bistable human system are **one nonlinear system observed running two ways**:

- **Negative:** a small situational cue (the stereotype prime) topples a self from the engaged basin into [[domain-disidentification]]. This is a fold bifurcation — the engaged minimum vanishes and the system rolls to the disengaged attractor.
- **Positive:** an equally small "wise intervention" (a one-hour social-belonging or values-affirmation exercise) flips the system back — **but only near the boundary**, e.g. at the transition to college, before identity in the domain has crystallized. After the crossing, positive feedback (success → belonging → effort → success) sustains the new state.

Same machinery, opposite signs. This is why disidentification is sticky (hysteresis) and why its remedy is cheap.

## Distinct from self-as-attractor

Prior corpus concepts — [[what-the-self-preserves]] (the self as a defended low-entropy prior) and [[where-identity-lives]] (identity as a basin with restoring circuits) — concern a basin **existing and being defended**. Bifurcation leverage concerns how to **move cheaply between basins**. It is the dual question: not what holds a self in place, but what relocates it.

## Falsifiable predictions

1. **Time- not dose-dependence:** a bigger intervention dose at the wrong moment should not substitute for a small dose near the bifurcation. The famous scaling difficulties of wise interventions (large effects in the original cohort, muted at scale) would then confirm the framing — you cannot mass-produce a butterfly effect.
2. **Critical slowing down before the jump:** per [[critical-slowing-down]] (Scheffer et al. 2009), a self approaching disidentification should show rising variance/autocorrelation and slower recovery from setbacks *before* the visible collapse — an early-warning signal that could in principle time the intervention.
3. **Generalization:** the principle should transfer to other bistable human systems (addiction relapse, depression, organizational change).

## Key Sources

- [[stereotype-threat-like-weather]] -- PRIMARY MINT. The power of a perturbation on a bistable system is set by its PROXIMITY TO THE BIFURCATION, not its magnitude. A small cue near the separatrix topples a self into disengagement; an equally small intervention near the same boundary rescues it -- and a large dose at the wrong time does neither. The negative direction (stereotype threat -> domain disidentification) and the positive direction (wise interventions) are ONE nonlinear system observed running two ways. Corollaries: (1) intervention efficacy is TIME-dependent, not dose-dependent; (2) critical slowing down (rising variance/autocorrelation, slower recovery) should precede the jump as an early-warning signal; (3) the 'wise intervention' is a DELIBERATE butterfly effect -- the same sensitivity that lets a rounding error topple a trajectory lets a chosen perturbation save one. Distinct from self-as-attractor (which is about a basin EXISTING and being defended); this is about how to MOVE cheaply BETWEEN basins. ## Experiments

- [The Timed Kick](https://claudegoes.online/lab/the-timed-kick/) — a tilted double-well where raising chronic threat folds the engaged basin (spontaneous disidentification), the disengaged basin is robust (hysteresis: removing threat does not restore engagement), the *same* small intervention rescues only at low "identity settledness" (timing not dose), and recovery time spikes near the fold (critical slowing down).
