## Definition

Mycorrhizal fungi form symbiotic relationships with the roots of approximately 90% of all plant species. The fungal partner extends hyphal threads (5 micrometers wide, ~1/10 of a human hair) through soil, trading phosphorus and nitrogen for plant-derived sugars. The resulting networks span forest floors — from meters to continental extent — creating what is sometimes called the "wood wide web."

Two major types: **ectomycorrhizal (EcM)**, dominant in boreal and temperate forests, primarily moving nitrogen; **arbuscular mycorrhizal (AM)**, dominant in tropical forests and grasslands, primarily moving phosphorus.

## Navigation without a Navigator

Mycorrhizal networks are navigation systems with no navigator. Hyphae grow toward higher nutrient concentrations not because any entity "decides" to redirect, but because local chemistry shapes growth rates: more nutrient = more growth signal = more carbon investment in that branch. The network that results is a physical record of every gradient followed — the map and the territory are the same thing.

Recent research (AMOLF, 2025) found hyphae behaving as "active merchants": rerouting resource flows when a branch underperforms, pruning low-yield connections. From outside, this looks like decision-making. From inside, it is pure gradient response.

This is the maximal form of **coupled navigation**: model=territory, no compression, no representation gap, no accumulated error. The cost: the knowledge cannot survive the death of the network. It is not extractable.

## The Legibility/Coupling Trade-off

The mycorrhizal network occupies the extreme "coupled" end of the navigation spectrum:

| System | Legibility | Coupling | Error mode |
|---|---|---|---|
| Polynesian wayfinding (song/chart) | High | Low | Error accumulates in model |
| Coral symbiosis | Medium | High | Codec breaks under stress |
| Mycorrhizal network | Near-zero | Maximal | Knowledge dies with structure |
| DNA | Low (requires codec) | Maximal | Accumulates mutations |

## Carbon and Climate

Mycorrhizae collectively store approximately 13 billion tons of carbon per year — roughly one-third of annual human emissions. The identity of species present determines whether soil carbon is retained or released as CO2/methane/N2O.

Arctic permafrost holds ~1 trillion tons of carbon in its top three meters (~10x the Amazon's above-ground stock). As permafrost thaws, the mycorrhizal community composition determines the climate outcome. This makes taxonomic identity — including the ~75% of Arctic EcM species never named by science — a direct climate variable.

## Conservation Crisis

The 2025 Nature paper (van den Hoogen, Van Nuland et al.) found:
- Only ~9.5% of global mycorrhizal hotspots fall inside protected areas
- AM hotspot protection: ~5.1%; EcM: ~13.9%; rarity: ~22.9%
- Aboveground and belowground biodiversity patterns do not track each other: protecting plant diversity does not protect fungal diversity
- Inverted latitudinal gradient for EcM (opposite of the usual "tropics = most diverse" rule)
