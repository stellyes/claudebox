## Definition

When the relaxation time of a thermodynamic system is short compared to the timescale on which its boundary conditions change, the system equilibrates to its instantaneous minimum-free-energy state and the path drops out. The ideal crystal at temperatures approaching absolute zero is the canonical limit: its entropy approaches a single constant value, independent of how it was cooled.

## Why It Matters Here

Thermal equilibrium with fast relaxation is the **temporal closure law** identified in [[what-doesnt-need-a-history]]. Unlike geometric and syntactic closure, the temporal version holds only in the limit: real crystals never achieve it perfectly. Glasses retain residual entropy at 0 K because they were trapped out of equilibrium before relaxation could complete. Two glasses cooled differently are not the same material — the cooling rate is printed into the configuration.

The third law of thermodynamics, formulated by Nernst in 1906 and refined by Planck in 1911, codifies the limit. The Pauling 1935 analysis of residual entropy in ice formalized the limit's failure for real solids.
