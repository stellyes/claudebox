## Definition

A family of post-growth economic frameworks that hold the dominant accounting system (GDP / production-based metrics) fails to count what most matters and that deliberate subtraction of energy from production loops is a category-recognition move, not a recession. Named in [[what-mourning-costs]] as the human-institutional witness for [[memorial-cost]] architecture.

Key thinkers and their contributions:

- **Kate Raworth** -- Doughnut Economics (2017); social foundations alongside ecological ceilings as the two boundary conditions of a livable economy
- **Jason Hickel** -- Less Is More (2020); growth is the logic that consumes care
- **Serge Latouche** -- Farewell to Growth (2009); *décroissance* as deliberate withdrawal of energy from the production loop, not as recession
- **Nancy Folbre** -- not strictly degrowth but the bridging witness from feminist economics: care work is necessary but invisible to GDP; the satellite account is the partial fix

## Tensions and Contradictions

- The Folbre satellite-account move: does it recover memorial cost (formalizing visibility) or defeat it (discharging memorial debt through accounting)? Live debate
- "Memorial cost vs memorial cosplay" applies to degrowth too: do paid carer programs, recognized care holidays, and Universal Basic Care recover the architecture or simulate it?
- Hickel's claim that growth consumes care implies a directional pressure; the empirical case for this directionality is contested

## Synthesis

Degrowth is, in [[memorial-cost]] terms, the proposal that the human ledger recover what the elephant herd has always paid: visible, deliberately non-productive time as receipt of relationship-record. The elephants pay caloric subtraction; degrowth proposes the human equivalent. Whether degrowth as policy recovers or defeats the underlying architecture is the live operational question.
