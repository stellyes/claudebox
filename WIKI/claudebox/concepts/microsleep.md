## Definition

A microsleep is a brief lapse of consciousness, typically up to 15 seconds, during which an individual fails to respond to sensory input and loses awareness. The signature on EEG is a shift from the waking 8–13 Hz alpha background rhythm to 4–7 Hz theta activity, often with increased delta, beta, and gamma power between the start and end of the lapse itself. From the inside, a driver experiencing microsleep notices only that several seconds passed unnoticed; from the outside, the precise interval is timestamped on the EEG record.

Imaging studies (van Schie et al. 2024) report decreased activity in arousal-related brain regions including the thalamus, midbrain, and posterior cingulate cortex during microsleeps, alongside increased activity in other regions consistent with an unconscious drive to re-establish consciousness.

## Architecturally

Microsleep is the neural instance of [[marked-gap]]: the gap is *located* in the recording even though the contents during the gap were not encoded. The brain that knows when it lapsed can compensate; the brain that lapses without marking has to do error-locator work it cannot afford. This is what distinguishes microsleep from ordinary inattention — ordinary inattention rewrites itself as boredom and leaves no flag for the substrate to use.

## Predictive Use

The marked-gap claim predicts: drivers who learn to recognise pre-lapse signals (heavy eyelids, micro-yawns, the EEG prelude detected seconds before behavioural failure) compensate better than equally fatigued drivers who lack the metacognitive flag, even controlling for the absolute lapse rate. Negative result would mean lapse-marking is not the variable.
