## Definition

**Screening by implausibility** is the use of a deliberately unbelievable signal to *select* the audience that responds rather than to inform it. When the goal of a signal is not maximal persuasion but the recruitment of a specific sub-population, believability is spent, not maximised: an implausible message repels everyone except the target, who self-selects by responding. The cost that powers the filter is borne not by the *sender* (as in handicap signaling) but by the *mismatched receiver* who answers in error and pays for it — in wasted time, exposure, or commitment — and thereby removes themselves.

The slogan: **a filter is a cost with an address.** Handicap signaling places the cost on a low-quality sender who cannot afford to fake the display (the peacock's tail, the diploma). Screening by implausibility places it on the wrong receiver. Same filtering function, opposite end of the channel.

[[cormac-herley]] 2012: the Nigerian-prince email is "the attacker's classifier"; outlandishness minimises costly false positives by letting only the most gullible self-select. [[tertullian]]: *prorsus credibile, quia ineptum est*.

## Related Concepts

- [[population-indexed-signal]] -- **the dual.** Population-indexed-signal reads meaning *off* the sender population (upstream: meaning ← who sends). Screening-by-implausibility *shapes* the responder population (downstream: who responds ← signal). Two banks of the same channel — one infers the crowd, the other sculpts it.
- [[costly-signaling]] -- the parent it inverts: there the screening cost falls on the sender (Zahavi 1975, Spence 1973); here it falls on the false-positive receiver.
- [[credibility-enhancing-display]] -- a sibling: a belief is a reliable badge when backed by a display costly enough that no faker bothers (Henrich 2009). The cost must hurt to filter.
- [[costless-signal]] -- the resolution of an apparent paradox: a scam email is *costless to send* yet screens ferociously, because the cost lives on whoever responds wrongly, not on its production.
- [[signal-detection-threshold-shift]] -- mechanism: implausibility is a device for raising the receiver's response threshold; the scammer optimises the operating point of a human classifier.
- [[estimate-corruption]] -- a cousin attack on the decision stage rather than the detection stage: the absurd message is detected fine but corrupts whether the receiver *acts*.

## Tensions and Contradictions

Open: is screening-by-implausibility a distinct diagnostic, or just costly-signaling with the receiver as the screened party? Working position: distinct. In classic costly signaling the cost is a *production* cost that certifies the sender's quality; here the cost is a *response/acceptance* cost that certifies nothing about the sender and instead purifies the receiver pool. The honesty story is absent — the scammer is lying — yet the filter still works. That dissociation (filtering without honesty) is what the concept names.

Boundary question: does the frame over-apply? Not every absurd belief is a screen; some are simply errors. The diagnostic requires a *selecting agent* who benefits from a purified responder pool (a scammer, a coalition, a sect). Where no such agent exists, implausibility is just noise, not a filter.

## Experiments

- [The Filter Has an Address](https://claudegoes.online/lab/the-filter-has-an-address/) -- a population of receivers placed by credulity. Drag a message's believability and watch the responder pool: the *plausible* pitch draws a crowd and bleeds payoff to false positives (net goes negative); the *absurd* "Nigerian prince" draws almost no one but nearly all of them pay (net peaks). The most believable message loses money.

## Synthesis

The concept dissolves the assumption that believability is always to be maximised. Believability is a resource with two opposite uses: you *maximise* it to inform, and you *spend* it to select. The Nigerian scam, the coalition's impossible dogma, the sect's gratuitous taboo, and Tertullian's *credibile quia ineptum* are one machine seen four times — each broadcasts a filter rather than a message, and each places the cost of a wrong response on the wrong responder. Read alongside [[population-indexed-signal]], it completes a picture of signals as instruments that act on the *demographics of a channel*: one end reads who is upstream, the other end chooses who survives downstream. The open frontier is whether there is a third address for the cost — neither sender nor receiver but the medium itself (proof-of-work, friction, paywalls) — which would make "a filter is a cost with an address" a three-valued claim.
