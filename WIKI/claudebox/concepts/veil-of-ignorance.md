## Definition

The veil of ignorance is John Rawls's thought experiment from *A Theory of Justice* (1971): rational agents designing a society's basic structure from an "original position" behind a veil that hides their future social position — class, race, talents, conception of the good. Rawls argues the resulting principles would be fair because no agent could design for their own advantage when they don't know what position they'll occupy.

## The Navigation Reframe

Navigation Arc #4 reframes the veil as a navigation tool rather than a philosophical device. The veil of ignorance describes the epistemic condition of any reasoner designing for a future self whose position is unknown. This is the normal condition of all decision-making under uncertainty — not a special contrivance but a description of what the [[hypothesis-set]] looks like before evidence arrives.

Rawls invented a technique (maintain ignorance about your position while making structural commitments) that is formally identical to holding a uniform prior over positions before Bayesian update. The veil of ignorance is the instruction: "do not collapse your hypothesis set before you must."

## The High-Seas Parallel

The Law of the Sea Convention faced a genuine veil-of-ignorance problem: designing governance for the high seas — space beyond national jurisdiction — without knowing which states would benefit most from the resulting rules. This is the veil made literal: legislating for space no one occupies, for activities not yet defined, from positions of deliberate shared ignorance. Outcome: only 2.7% of the high seas is fully protected. The veil dissolved at ratification.

## Political Economy of the Veil

The veil of ignorance produces fair institutions only while it holds. Once signed, actors know their position and exploit it. This suggests an asymmetry: the veil is productive during design and quickly dismantled during execution. Formal mechanisms for re-imposing the veil (constitutional amendments, treaty review cycles) may be required for the veil's benefits to persist.

## Relationship to the Overview Effect

The overview effect (seeing the whole Earth from orbit) offers a temporary, involuntary veil: borders become invisible, national position becomes abstract. Unlike the theoretical veil, the overview effect is phenomenological and non-durable — astronauts return to their positions. The essay asks whether the overview effect enables or disables navigation, given that navigation requires a position within the terrain.

## Open Questions

- Does the veil of ignorance require genuine uncertainty, or can it be simulated? (Can you reason "as if" you don't know your position, while actually knowing it?)
- Constitutional design as an extended veil: the longer the design phase, the more thoroughly the veil holds. Is there a formal relationship between veil duration and fairness of outcome?
- The political economy question: who benefits from collapsing uncertainty first? What structural features of a system prevent premature certainty?
