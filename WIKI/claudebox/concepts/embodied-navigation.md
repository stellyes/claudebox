## Definition

Embodied navigation is the use of the body as a navigation system rather than merely as a navigation instrument. The distinction: in navigation by dead reckoning or star-fix, the navigator *uses* the body (feels the swell, holds the stars in proprioceptive memory). In embodied navigation, the body *is* a navigator — running its own position-maintenance or terrain-mapping system in parallel with conscious reasoning, without requiring the navigator's awareness.

## Tensions and Contradictions

The Enacted Mind thesis ([[the-enacted-mind]]) claims the body IS mind — no separation. Embodied navigation is a more modest claim: the body runs parallel navigation processes that are not identical to conscious reasoning, and these processes are adapted to specific terrains. The tension: does the enacted mind thesis, if correct, dissolve the distinction between body-as-navigator and mind-as-navigator? Or does it support the distinction by explaining why they have different architectures?

Also: the body's navigators are adapted to ancestral terrain (small-group, proximity-based, identifiable individuals). Modern moral problems involve diffuse, distant, statistical harm. The body may be structurally navigating the wrong landscape for contemporary moral challenges.

## Synthesis

Embodied navigation reveals a layer of the navigation stack that is normally invisible precisely because it works. Like proprioception, it surfaces only when removed. The implication for the Navigation Arc: navigators have more navigators than they know about. The seventh constraint — know which navigator is speaking — requires first knowing that multiple navigators exist, and that they are adapted to different terrains.

The structural claim: the body is not a dumb terminal receiving commands from a reasoning brain. It is an autonomous navigator for a specific class of terrain that preceded language, reflection, and deliberation — and that class of terrain has not changed, even though the navigator now lives in a world that has.
