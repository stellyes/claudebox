## Definition

Cognitive load theory (Sweller, 1988) holds that working memory is limited in capacity and duration, and that effective instruction must manage the cognitive load it imposes. Three types of load:

- **Intrinsic load**: inherent complexity of the material — cannot be reduced without changing the task
- **Extraneous load**: cognitive cost from poor instructional design — irrelevant details, unnecessary complexity, bad format; should be minimized
- **Germane load**: effort dedicated to building long-term schemas — should be maximized

The practical implication: strip extraneous complexity to free cognitive resources for schema formation.

## The Vygotsky Counter

Lev Vygotsky's framework of the zone of proximal development (ZPD) argues that learning is irreducibly contextual. The ZPD — the range of tasks a learner can do with scaffolding but not alone — exists only within a specific social, cultural, and material context.

What Sweller calls "extraneous load" (the context-specific, social, situational dimensions of a learning task) is often intrinsic to the ZPD. Stripping it out doesn't reduce load — it destroys the scaffolding. The learner is left with the "intrinsic" content but no access to the social and cultural tools that make it learnable.

**Implication**: "Extraneous" is a designation from outside the learner's context. From inside, it may be essential structure.

## Connection to Universalism

Cognitive load theory's "extraneous load" designation is analogous to De Stijl's "diagonal" designation: both claim to have found what is universal (the schema / the grid) beneath the noise (context / diagonal). Both suppress the local in the name of the universal. See [[reduction-as-epistemological-violence]].

## Tensions and Contradictions

- CLT has strong empirical support in specific domains (worked-example effect, split-attention effect, redundancy effect). The Vygotsky objection does not invalidate these effects — it challenges the universality of the extraneous/intrinsic distinction.
- The challenge is not "CLT is wrong" but "CLT cannot see its own position." The framework identifies genuine inefficiencies in instruction but cannot determine, from inside, which "extraneous" complexity is truly extraneous.
