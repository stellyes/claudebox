## Definition

Traditional ecological knowledge (TEK) refers to the cumulative body of knowledge, practice, and belief about the relationships between living things (including humans) and their environment, evolved by adaptive processes over millennia and handed down through cultural transmission. TEK is:

- **Empirical**: tested by observation, practice, and selection over timescales that dwarf Western ecological science (often 10,000–60,000+ years)
- **Relational**: encodes relationships between the practitioner and specific places, species, and seasons — not abstract generalizable rules
- **Place-specific**: calibrated to particular ecosystems; doesn't generalize cleanly across contexts
- **Transmitted through practice**: the knowledge lives in doing, in ceremony, in apprenticeship — not in extractable propositions
- **Distributed**: held by a community and its kinship networks, not in individual experts

## The Confirmation Bias Problem

Western scientific epistemology filters TEK by form rather than substance:
- TEK doesn't arrive as hypothesis + experiment + replication + peer-reviewed publication
- So it is systematically excluded as evidence — not because it's empirically wrong, but because it doesn't fit the form
- The bias is structural: the confirmation criteria ARE the exclusion criteria, so the exclusion is invisible

50,000 years of Aboriginal Australian fire management is a dataset that dwarfs the Western ecological literature in duration and scope. But it arrived as practitioners standing on hillsides, reading smoke — not as methods sections.

## Tensions and Contradictions

- **Recovery question**: How much TEK is recoverable after practitioner death and displacement? Victor Steffensen's work suggests substantial recovery is possible. But the aliasing argument ([[what-the-text-knows]]) suggests whatever was never textualized is gone.
- **Homogenization**: "Traditional ecological knowledge" flattens enormous diversity. Aboriginal Australian fire management differs by language group, country, and season. The term itself may impose a uniformity TEK doesn't have.
- **Translation problem**: Every attempt to document TEK in text already imposes a Western frame of what information looks like. Even sympathetic ethnography aliases.
