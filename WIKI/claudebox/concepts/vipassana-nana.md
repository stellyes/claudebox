## Definition

The sixteen "stages of insight knowledge" articulated in Theravāda Buddhist contemplative manuals (most prominently Buddhaghosa's 5th-century *Visuddhimagga*) as diagnostic landmarks on the path from beginning meditative attention to the realization of *nibbāna*. They are not exhortations but descriptive markers a teacher in the lineage is supposed to be able to recognize from a student's report.

## Stages (selected)

1. *namarupa-pariccheda* -- knowledge of mental and physical states
2. *paccaya-pariggaha* -- discerning conditionality
3. *sammasana* -- knowledge of the three characteristics (*anicca / dukkha / anatta*)
4. *udayabbaya* -- arising and passing away (rapid alternation visible)
5. *bhanga* -- knowledge of dissolution (only the passing-away remains visible)
6. *bhaya* -- knowledge of the fearful nature of phenomena
7-13. -- various refinements; equanimity stages
14. *phala* -- fruition (a moment in which the work is recognized as done)
15-16. -- review knowledges

## Tensions and Contradictions

- Empirical operationalization of the ñāṇa stages is contested. Inter-rater reliability across teachers is reasonable within Mahasi-style traditions, weaker across lineages.
- Some traditions (Zen, Dzogchen) do not enumerate stages and would deny that the path admits this kind of stage-by-stage diagnosis. The architectural claim here does not depend on the *count* of stages, only on the architectural fact that recognition is generated by the same apparatus that does the work.

## Synthesis

The Theravāda *vipassanā-ñāṇa* taxonomy is the most explicit cross-cultural example of an architecture in which the work-doing substrate generates its own completion signal. Trained meta-attention applied to itself produces visible markers; the practitioner is supposed to recognize fruition (*phala*) from inside, not be told they have arrived. This is structurally identical to the local-rule quiescence of self-stabilization and the YAP/TAZ exit of contact inhibition: the substrate produces the certificate.
