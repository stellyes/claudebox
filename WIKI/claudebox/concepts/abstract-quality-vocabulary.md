## Definition

A lexicon of source-independent quality terms used to describe perceptual experience. The Jahai (Malay Peninsula hunter-gatherers, Aslian language) have ~13 abstract olfactory verbs — *cŋɛs* (stinging, common to petrol/smoke/insects), *pl̃ɛŋ* (cooked food / blood-attracting-tigers), and others — that name source-independent qualities rather than typical emitters. Majid & Burenhult (2014) showed Jahai speakers name odours about as fluently as colours, where English speakers stall at source-descriptors.

## Tensions and Contradictions

The cross-cultural picture is more complex than the Jahai-vs-English binary suggests. Industrialised-society speakers can be trained on abstract olfactory tasks; the deficit is partially recoverable with effort. But the everyday ease gap — Jahai naming odours as easily as colours — is robust across replications.

## Synthesis

Abstract quality vocabularies are the rare middle ground inside a high-dimensional perceptual space — small public sets of axes that capture most discriminations without collapsing to source. They work because the speech community has shared experience that converges on which qualities matter. The Jahai rainforest selects for cross-source quality terms; the post-industrial city selects for source-bound terms because most smells are commercial and source-named.
