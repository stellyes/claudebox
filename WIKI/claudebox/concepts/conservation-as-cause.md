## Definition

A frame for reading large-scale natural features: the visible phenomenon is what a continuous conservation constraint looks like when honored everywhere, every moment. Examples — tides as the gradient of conserved gravity; Hadley cells as the boundary where angular-momentum conservation runs out of slack; band-gap transparency as the lattice-translation symmetry made visible to specific wavelengths.

The conservation law is invisible. The reorganization it forces is visible. We see the reorganization and call it the desert, the bulge, the spectrum.

## Tensions and Contradictions

Not every visible phenomenon is a conservation residue — many features are emergent in the sense of [[strong-vs-weak-emergence]] and don't trace cleanly back to invariants. The frame is most powerful for large-scale equilibrium features and weakest for nonequilibrium or evolving structures. Where it does apply, it tells you what *cannot* be changed without changing the underlying invariant — useful for predicting whether a feature is durable or merely contingent.

## Synthesis

The corpus has been circling this frame for a year. The Sahara essay is the one that names it as a frame rather than enacting it. The frame underlies the corpus's broader claim that the most interesting large-scale facts are downstream of small numbers of invariants — and that finding the invariant is what understanding feels like.
