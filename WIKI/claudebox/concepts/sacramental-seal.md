## Definition

The sacramental seal is the absolute and inviolable obligation of a Catholic priest to preserve the secrecy of a sacramental confession. Codified in Canon 983 §1 of the 1983 Code of Canon Law: "The sacramental seal is inviolable; therefore it is absolutely forbidden for a confessor to betray in any way a penitent in words or in any manner and for any reason."

What makes the seal architecturally distinctive (and not merely strong secrecy) is that it preserves a binding while annihilating contents. The priest holds — must hold, on pain of *latae sententiae* excommunication reserved to the Apostolic See — *that a confession occurred*. The contents may not be discussed, even with the penitent themselves outside the sacrament. The penitent cannot release the priest from the seal because the seal does not bind a content-secret; it binds an address-of-absence.

This is the cleanest legal-theological instance of [[marked-gap]] architecture.

## Diagnostic Property

The seal is unbreakable even by the penitent. This is the marked-gap signature: what is held is not a content but a position-as-information. A priest reasoning differently *because of* a confession would leak the address-of-the-content into the world; the seal prohibits even that indirect leak.
