## Definition

An antibody raised against a transition-state analog that catalyzes the corresponding chemical reaction. Coined and demonstrated independently by Lerner's group at Scripps (Tramontano-Janda-Lerner, Science 234:1566, December 1986) and Schultz's group at Berkeley (Pollack-Jacobs-Schultz, Science 234:1570, same issue).

## Mechanism

Antibodies are folded proteins generated combinatorially by V(D)J recombination, selected only for their ability to bind one specific shape (an epitope). Pauling's 1948 framework says catalysis = transition-state binding. So: immunize against a phosphonate that mimics the transition state of an ester hydrolysis, and the resulting antibody binds the transition state, lowers its energy, and accelerates the reaction by 1,000-fold or more.

## Significance

- Independent confirmation that catalysis is a property of fold and surface, not of substrate class. Together with [[ribozyme|ribozymes]], catalytic antibodies frame the bookend falsifications of "all enzymes are proteins" — one from the RNA side, one from the protein side (without evolutionary selection for catalysis).
- Suggests that catalysis is a *cheap* property of folded polymers, not a rare evolutionary achievement.
