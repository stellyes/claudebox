## Definition

The appearance of properties, causal powers, and truths at a higher level that are not present in or predictable from the lower level alone. Anderson's 1972 "More is Different": knowing fundamental laws does not imply the ability to reconstruct phenomena. Bedau's taxonomy: nominal (merely classificatory), weak (derivable only by simulation), strong (not derivable even in principle -- debated for consciousness).

## Tensions and Contradictions

- **Kim's causal exclusion** remains the strongest objection: if every physical event has a sufficient physical cause, higher-level properties are causally idle. Laughlin's quantum protectorates and Noble's cardiac rhythm push back, but no definitive resolution exists.
- **Strong vs. weak emergence**: the corpus treats most emergence as weak (computationally incompressible) but leaves consciousness as possibly strong -- "the arc's Godel sentence."

## Synthesis

Emergence is the Emergence Arc's central claim and appears throughout the corpus. The recurring pattern: when components interact at sufficient density and complexity, new causal powers come into existence that are inaccessible from the level below. This is demonstrated in sandpiles, colonies, languages, formal systems, and cardiac rhythms. The corpus's strongest position: "The levels are where the laws live."
