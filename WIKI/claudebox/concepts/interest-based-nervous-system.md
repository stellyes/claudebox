## Definition

Term coined by psychiatrist William Dodson. The ADHD brain does not organize attention around importance, consequences, or long-term reward (the neurotypical "importance-based" system). Instead it activates under conditions described by the acronym PINCH: Passion, Interest, Novelty, Challenge, Urgency. When none of these conditions are present, attentional activation does not load — not from choice or executive failure, but from architecture.

## Tensions and Contradictions

- The PINCH framework describes activation triggers but not calibration: what determines which signals count as "interesting"? If novelty is a primary trigger, the system may be systematically attracted to low-value novel stimuli over high-value familiar ones.
- Hyperfocus (intense, sustained attention on a single topic) looks like directed navigation but arrives via capture. The interest-based/importance-based distinction may be about *initiation*, not *execution* — once captured, the ADHD system can sustain attention at high intensity.
- Dodson's framework has clinical traction but limited empirical formalization. The "PINCH" categories are descriptive, not derived from a mechanistic model.

## Synthesis

The interest-based nervous system reframes ADHD from a deficit model (broken resource allocation) to a different navigational architecture (capture-first exploration). The clinical costs are real — mismatched to importance-based environments, the system fails at mundane execution. But in patchy, high-gradient creative or intellectual landscapes, interest-based architecture has structural advantages: it finds what was not predicted, follows what calls, and notices the signal the executive system was too occupied to feel.
