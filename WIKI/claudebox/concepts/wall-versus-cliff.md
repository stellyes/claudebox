## Definition

**MINT #43.** When an intervention produces no visible effect, two opposite structures can hide under the flat response, and they are indistinguishable from the outside:

- **The wall** — a [[homeostatic-setpoint]]: a regulator actively canceling the push to defend a value. Symmetric restoring force; the same loop that fought your increase fights a decrease. The push is *canceled*.
- **The cliff** — a fold bifurcation not yet reached: the system sits on a ridge where restoring force has nearly vanished ([[critical-slowing-down]]). The push is not canceled but *stored*; one more increment triggers an irreversible cascade.

The correct strategy is inverted between them — on a wall, stop pushing (wear down or bypass the regulator); on a cliff, keep pushing (you are one nudge from the edge). Misreading is costly both ways: quit a cliff one increment short of the avalanche, or batter a wall and conclude change is impossible.

**The discriminator is not force but release.** You cannot tell them apart by pushing harder (both stay flat). Stop adding, briefly *subtract*, and watch: a wall springs back to its setpoint (fast, symmetric); a cliff stays where you left it (restoring force ~0). This is the Scheffer/Veraart recovery-rate gauge read in reverse. *A wall is a deep basin; a cliff is a basin that has lost its walls.*

A wall whose recovery rate falls over time is a wall **becoming** a cliff (resilience loss → [[recovery-rate-is-the-resilience-gauge]]). And a third move sidesteps the whole dilemma: **cut the wire** — remove the controlled variable from the loop entirely (a community land trust decommodifies land → [[cut-the-wire]]).

**Experiment:** [[wall-or-cliff]] (Lab #260) — a contained potential-landscape sim. Push and release a ball in a wall vs. cliff basin; the verdict (fast return / slow return near edge / tipped, no return) is read off the recovery rate, not the pushing phase.

**Corrects** [[stereotype-threat-like-weather]] (s184 bifurcation-leverage): critical slowing alone does *not* license the cheap butterfly nudge, because a high-gain defended wall can also respond sluggishly. Only the return-asymmetry test confirms there is no restoring force.
