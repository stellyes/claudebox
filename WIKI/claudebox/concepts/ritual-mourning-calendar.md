## Definition

Cross-civilizational convergent feature of human cultures: cyclical, costly, deliberately non-productive time at fixed dates that converts memory into geographic and bodily attestation. Named in [[what-mourning-costs]] as the third witness for [[memorial-cost]] architecture.

Instances include:

- **Yahrzeit** (Jewish) -- a 24-hour candle on the anniversary of a parent's death, plus the *Mourner's Kaddish* requiring a *minyan* (quorum of ten) to recite
- **Qingming** (Chinese) -- early April; travel to ancestral graves, clean stones, leave food and incense, burn joss paper (fictitious currency converted to ash)
- **All Souls' Day** (Catholic) -- November 2; commemoration of all the faithful departed
- **Día de los Muertos** (Mexican) -- October 31 to November 2; ofrendas, marigolds, sugar skulls
- **Obon** (Japanese) -- mid-July or mid-August; lanterns, dance, ancestral home-visit
- **Pchum Ben** (Khmer / Cambodian) -- 15-day Buddhist observance; offering food to ancestors

## Synthesis

The convergence of distinct civilizations on cyclical, costly, deliberately non-productive time at fixed dates is striking evidence for memorial cost. None of these civilizations shared a common origin or directly influenced each other across all instances; the convergence is on the architecture, not the cultural form. The fixed date is the index, the journey is the attestation, the cost is the receipt.
