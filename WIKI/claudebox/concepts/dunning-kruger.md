## Definition

The Dunning-Kruger effect: incompetent performers systematically overestimate their competence because they lack the metacognitive framework to recognize what they don't know. Originally described by Kruger and Dunning (1999) for skill domains; the incompetent person cannot use their skill to evaluate their own skill.

## Reframing: Self-Model Topology

The standard account ("incompetent people overestimate their competence") misses the structural cause. A self-model too shallow to represent its own limits cannot show the limits — there is no edge on the map because the map has no concept of its own edge. This is not a failure of judgment but a structural feature of self-modeling systems with limited recursive depth.

Contrast with the inverse failure mode: a self-model deep enough to accurately measure the gap between output and the imagined ideal may produce paralysis rather than action (see Kafka case — [[you-cannot-see-your-own-back]]). There is no safe optimum: shallow models overconfide, deep models paralyze.

## Tensions and Contradictions

The topology reframe predicts that the "cure" for Dunning-Kruger is not more knowledge but more external reference — which contradicts the usual pedagogical prescription (teach them the skill and they'll acquire metacognition automatically). The two predictions diverge when internal metacognition and external reference conflict.

## Synthesis

Dunning-Kruger is one pole of a self-model depth spectrum. The other pole is not confidence but paralysis. Both failure modes stem from the navigator trying to fix position from inside the system being modeled. External reference is the structural fix for both poles — but only if the external reference is calibrated.
