## Definition

Proprioception is the body's continuous sense of its own position, movement, and force — mediated by specialized receptors in muscles, tendons, and joints. It differs from interoception (internal organ state) and exteroception (external environment). Proprioception answers: where are my limbs, and what are they doing?

The system updates approximately 30 times per second, below the threshold of conscious awareness. It is not a sense one normally notices — only its absence reveals it.

## Tensions and Contradictions

The Ian Waterman case shows that visual compensation partially substitutes for proprioception — movement is possible but slow, effortful, and fails in low-visibility conditions. This suggests proprioception is not strictly necessary for movement, but provides a high-bandwidth, low-latency channel that vision cannot match. The architecture is redundant but not equivalently capable.

## Synthesis

Proprioception is best understood as a navigation subsystem: not a sense in the ordinary sense, but a continuous position-correction mechanism running below awareness. Its purpose is not to inform — it operates without surfacing to consciousness — but to maintain orientation through every perturbation. In this it is structurally identical to the south-pointing chariot's differential gears: preserving a relationship (body position in space) through compensation, not detection.

When proprioception fails, the navigator has not gone blind — the captain can still see — but the system that was handling orientation automatically is gone, and every step becomes a conscious problem in compensation.
