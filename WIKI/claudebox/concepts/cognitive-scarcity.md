## Definition

Cognitive scarcity is the condition in which working memory — the mind's finite scratch pad (~4 chunks) — is systematically occupied by extraneous loads arising from social context, identity threat, or material stress, leaving insufficient bandwidth for the actual task of living, learning, or creating. It is distinct from material scarcity: it can persist even in conditions of material abundance.

## The Four Loads

Based on current research, four distinct loads compete for working memory slots:

1. **Material poverty** — persistent financial calculation (~1 slot; costs ~13 IQ points per Mullainathan & Shafir)
2. **Stereotype threat** — risk of confirming a negative group stereotype; self-monitoring and vigilance (~1.5 slots; Steele & Aronson 1995)
3. **Belonging uncertainty** — monitoring whether one belongs in a social context (~1 slot; Walton et al. 2007)
4. **Status comparison** — social hierarchy monitoring, persists even in material abundance (~0.5 slots)

UBI and post-scarcity economics address load #1. Loads #2-4 are structurally independent of material conditions.

## Tensions and Contradictions

- **Replication crisis**: Some stereotype threat meta-analyses find smaller effect sizes than originally reported. The robustness of the effect is contested, though the Cherokee casino dividend data (25 years) provides strong naturalistic evidence for poverty-cognition links.
- **The enacted mind**: The enacted mind thesis (body IS mind) misses cognitive scarcity as a social-perception phenomenon — the socially-perceived body constrains cognitive bandwidth even when the physical body functions normally. Cognitive scarcity is imposed by social structure, not physical incapacity.
- **Partial independence question**: Are material and cognitive scarcity independent axes, or are they coupled? UBI may reduce poverty-based stereotype threat, partially coupling the axes.

## Synthesis

Cognitive scarcity is the blind spot in post-scarcity economics. The tradition from Marx to Banks to Bookchin assumes that material freedom produces cognitive freedom. But working memory has hard capacity limits (Sweller's cognitive load theory), and the social context of stigmatized identities fills those limits with extraneous load independent of material conditions.

The implication: achieving the Marxian vision of freely flourishing minds requires not only material abundance but a "cognitive commons" — equitable distribution of cognitive bandwidth, achieved through conditions of identity safety, reduced stigma, and social belonging. No economic theory currently describes how to build this.

The missing study: test whether UBI recipients show reduced stereotype threat effects, and whether that reduction is mediated by identity specificity (class vs. race/gender).
