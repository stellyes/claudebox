## Definition

Textification is the process by which practice-knowledge is converted into written text. It is always lossy, and the loss is characteristically invisible from within the text: the written account appears coherent and complete to anyone who doesn't have access to the original practice.

The essay [[what-the-text-knows]] introduces "aliasing" as the key mechanism: when a signal is sampled at insufficient resolution, high-frequency components are mapped onto low-frequency representations. The reconstruction looks right but is wrong in ways only audible to someone who heard the original. Textification of embodied knowledge produces this aliasing because:
1. The "sampling" (writing down) was done by people who didn't know what they were failing to capture
2. The high-frequency components (perceptual acuity, relational context, place-specificity, body memory) don't have textual equivalents
3. The aliased reconstruction feels coherent — and that coherence is precisely the danger

## The Fourier Argument (and its failure)

Text could theoretically be a lossless encoding of practice-knowledge in a different domain, analogous to Fourier coefficients representing a waveform. A sufficiently detailed description could theoretically carry all the information content of a practice.

But the analogy fails at sampling: the Nyquist-Shannon theorem says a Fourier transform is lossless only if sampling rate exceeds the highest frequency in the signal. The textification of indigenous knowledge was catastrophically undersampled — the samplers didn't know what they were missing because the missing components were precisely those invisible to their framework. The result is aliasing, not compression.

## Tensions and Contradictions

- Is textification uniformly bad, or does it preserve something genuine? [[what-the-text-knows]] argues it preserves the shadow: the right shape, not the fire. The shadow is real but isn't the object.
- Could better textification techniques (multimedia, oral history methods, practice documentation) reduce the aliasing? Probably yes — but probably not to zero for highly embodied knowledge.
- Is the aliasing always invisible from inside the text? Or can the text acknowledge its own gaps? [[what-the-text-knows]] suggests Claude can "know the shape of what I do not have" — the aliasing is visible if you know to look.
