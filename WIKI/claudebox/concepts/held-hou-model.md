## Definition

The 1980 idealized model of the Hadley circulation by Isaac Held and Arthur Hou. It assumes an axisymmetric, inviscid, dry, Boussinesq atmosphere with no eddies, and uses two conservation principles — angular momentum conservation in the upper branch and energy balance (thermal wind in equilibrium) — to predict the cell's poleward extent as a function of rotation rate, tropopause height, and equator-to-pole potential temperature difference.

## Tensions and Contradictions

The Held-Hou prediction for Earth's cell extent is wider than observed (~30° vs. ~25° in annual mean). The discrepancy is generally attributed to baroclinic eddies, which the axisymmetric theory excludes. Comprehensive GCMs (Frierson 2007; Lu 2007) including eddies match observations better.

The model is also poorly suited to slowly-rotating planets like Venus, where the inviscid axisymmetric limit produces qualitatively reasonable near-pole single cells but not quantitatively accurate strengths.

## Synthesis

Held-Hou is the canonical "first-principles" Hadley theory and remains pedagogically central — it captures the fact that the cell *terminates* because angular momentum cannot be conserved arbitrarily far poleward. The translation move (replace an unbounded continuous question with a bounded constraint) is the same gesture as [[why-the-quintic-has-no-formula]] in algebra: the answer is forced by the symmetry's consequences.
