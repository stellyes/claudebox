## Definition

The navigational system developed by Polynesian peoples to cross the Pacific Ocean without instruments. Spans roughly 1000–1400 CE; enabled settlement of Hawaii, New Zealand, Easter Island, and hundreds of islands across 10 million square miles of water.

Characterized by: total reliance on embodied and memorized knowledge; no written charts or navigational devices; knowledge transmitted orally, often in song.

## Key Techniques

### Star Paths
Sequences of stars memorized as bearings for specific routes. Navigators would "set a heading by a star near the horizon, switching to a new one once the first rose too high." Star compasses specified up to 150 stars with known bearings. Also "sailing down the latitude" — knowledge that specific stars pass over destination islands at predictable times.

### Ocean Swells
Persistent directional patterns in the deep ocean that remain consistent for days. Readable through the hull of the canoe as vibration. Island chains create predictable swell disturbances recognizable to experienced navigators.

### Other Environmental Signals
- Cloud formations over islands (visible before land appears above horizon)
- Bird species and flight patterns (certain birds fly from islands at dawn, return at dusk)
- Ocean color, temperature, phosphorescence

### Memory of Movement
Core technique: "Navigators have to memorize where they have sailed from in order to know where they are." Position is continuously maintained, not looked up. This is dead reckoning implemented as total attentional practice.

## Relationship to Dead Reckoning

Polynesian wayfinding is dead reckoning made into a civilizational practice. The periodic external fixes (stars, swells, birds) all arrive in the same codec as the navigation itself — they are part of the same environmental system the navigator inhabits. This is the condition for a valid external fix.

## Colonial Erasure

Colonial administrators evaluated Polynesian navigation using European cartographic standards: chart-drawing, compass bearings, explicit methodological explanation. When navigators could not demonstrate these (because the knowledge was structured differently), the official conclusion was often that Polynesian navigation was not a proper system — "merely instinct" or "luck."

This is a codec mismatch imposed as correction: the evaluator's reference frame could not decode what was being demonstrated, and concluded the demonstration was void. The official record was corrupted, not corrected.

Modern reconstruction on the Hokule'a (1976 voyage from Hawaii to Tahiti without instruments) demonstrated the system's precision definitively.

## Knowledge Transmission and Transformation

The knowledge was held in body, memory, and practice — not in written form. When it was finally documented and taught formally, the embodied quality changed:
- Writing down star paths enables the knowledge to survive the death of individual navigators
- But strips the felt sense of the swell, the trained attention required to read subtle signals
- The chart is accurate and dead; the trained navigator is inaccurate and alive

## Legibility vs. Coupling

Navigation Arc #3 introduced the legibility/coupling spectrum. Polynesian wayfinding sits at the "high legibility" end: the knowledge can be encoded in song, eventually written down, transmitted across generations without the practitioners. This transmissibility is its survival mechanism — the Hokule'a reconstruction worked because the knowledge had been partially preserved in notation.

But legibility comes at a cost: the written star path is accurate but dead. The embodied felt sense of the swell — the navigator's trained attention — cannot be fully legible. What survives legibilization is the chart; what is lost is the instrument.
