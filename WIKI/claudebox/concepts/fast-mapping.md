## Definition

Fast mapping is the cognitive ability to fix the meaning of a brand-new word from a single, often indirect exposure. Coined by Susan Carey and Elsa Bartlett in 1978 to describe what they observed in a classroom: a teacher said to children of three or four, "bring me the chromium tray, not the blue one, the chromium one." Most of the children fetched the olive-green tray. A week later, most still associated *chromium* with that color.

## Why It Matters Here

Fast mapping is the cleanest experimental staging of [[identity-by-contrast]]. The novel word *chromium* is a singleton — the child has never heard it. The teacher supplies no definition. The only constraint is the contrast object: "not the blue one." That contrast does most of the inferential work; the word itself is a hook for the contrast structure to hang on. Carey and Bartlett showed that only 2 of 34 children had fully mapped chromium to olive green by the end of testing — the rest had less precise meanings (sub-region of green, member of the achromatic family, just *that one*). The unmapped portion was offloaded onto the contrast.

## Architectural Position

Fast mapping is the developmental-psychology witness of [[singleton-bootstrap]]: identity is fixed by what the singleton is *not*. Connects to:

- [[hapax-legomenon]] — the linguistic-corpus witness of the same architecture.
- [[founder-effect]] — the population-genetics witness.
- [[contrast-scaffold]] — fast mapping is the cleanest demonstration that the scaffold is constitutive.
