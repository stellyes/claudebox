## Definition

The latitude at which the angular-momentum-conserving upper-level zonal wind u(φ) = Ω a sin²(φ) / cos(φ) exceeds the threshold at which baroclinic eddies can extract the momentum, forcing the Hadley cell to terminate. On Earth this sits near 30°; on Mars (thin atmosphere, weak eddies, higher ceiling) it can extend past 60°.

## Tensions and Contradictions

The "ceiling" is not a single quantity but a balance between two: the angular-momentum-conserving u(φ), which depends only on rotation rate, and the eddy field's capacity to extract westerly momentum, which depends on atmospheric thickness, obliquity, ocean coupling, and seasonality. Earth's ceiling is much lower than Mars's — same rotation, very different desert ring.

## Synthesis

The ceiling is the place where a conservation law runs out of slack. The desert ring at 30° is the planet's response to the joint constraint of angular momentum (Noether) and eddy-driven instability (baroclinic). Both have to fail at once for the cell to extend further. Mars satisfies that — Earth does not.
