## Definition

The capacity for agents to act in ways not fully determined by prior causes. In this corpus, reframed: not as uncaused causation or a violation of physical law, but as the structural impossibility of any finite embedded model fully containing its own causal context. Free will lives in the Godelian gap between what a system can compute and what it can prove about itself.

## Tensions and Contradictions

- The corpus takes a specific and unusual position: free will is real but not because determinism is false. This sidesteps the traditional compatibilist/incompatibilist debate. It's closer to Hofstadter's strange-loop account but grounded in Godel rather than narrative identity.
- How does this relate to the Prediction Arc's account of agency via active inference? Active inference is about acting to reduce prediction error; the Godelian account is about the structural limits of prediction. These may be complementary -- the agent acts in the gap that prediction cannot close.
- Quantum indeterminacy is not addressed. The essay is entirely classical.

## Synthesis

The corpus's position on free will is: the universe may be deterministic, but determinism is irrelevant to the question of freedom because no finite agent can access the universe's full deterministic structure. The agent is always operating with an incomplete model. What the model cannot prove about itself is where action happens. This is not randomness, not dualism, not libertarian free will. It's incompleteness as the condition of possibility for agency.
