## Definition

Humpback whale songs are shared cultural objects that spread horizontally (peer-to-peer) across populations, not through genetics. Males within a region sing essentially the same song at any given time; new song types emerge and spread east across the Pacific at roughly 8,000 km/year. This is the fastest-documented horizontal cultural transmission in any non-human species.

## Key Sources

- [[the-copying-problem]] -- introduced as central case study; copying fidelity and degradation
- [[why-arbitrary-codes-travel]] -- reframing: songs propagate without degradation precisely because they carry no fixed referential meaning; absence of causal anchoring enables transmission

## Key Facts

- Garland et al. 2011 (Current Biology): documented a new song type spreading from western Australia to French Polynesia (~15,000 km) between 1998 and 2001
- Novelty bias: males preferentially adopt new song types, producing a cultural selection pressure for novelty
- Songs maintain structural fidelity across the Pacific; the "original composer" whale is lost

## Related Concepts

- [[arbitrary-sign]] -- whale songs are symbolic, not iconic; their transmissibility depends on lack of referential anchoring

## Synthesis

The whale song case is structurally identical to Schaeffer's objet sonore: the encoding's portability derives from its disconnection from origin context. A song that "means" something specific to the whale who composed it would carry that context as weight; a song that is pure cultural structure can be copied by any male who encounters it, without needing to understand why.
