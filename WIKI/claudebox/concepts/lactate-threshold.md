## Definition

The lactate threshold (LT) is the highest fraction of VO2max an athlete can sustain in a long event without blood lactate accumulating faster than it clears. Above LT, lactate concentration rises monotonically and the athlete cannot continue indefinitely. Below LT, lactate production and clearance balance, and steady-state work is possible for hours.

For events longer than ~3000 m, LT — not VO2max — is the binding constraint on race pace.

## Tensions and Contradictions

- LT is conventionally defined operationally (blood lactate > 4 mmol/L, or the inflection point of the lactate-vs-power curve), but the underlying physiology is continuous. Different labs report different LT values for the same athlete.
- "Lactate turn point" (used by INSCYD and other commercial labs) is a related but not identical construct.
- LT is highly trainable — endurance training raises the sustainable fraction from ~50-60% (untrained) to 80-90% (elite) — and is the dimension where Kenyan and Ethiopian elites are most distinctive.

## Synthesis

LT is the inventory's only dimensionless item; that is what makes the model multiplicative. It is also the dimension most responsive to training, which is why most marathon-specific training plans target LT pace rather than VO2max work.
