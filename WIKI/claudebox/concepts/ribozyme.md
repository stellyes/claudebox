## Definition

An RNA molecule that catalyzes a chemical reaction. The term was coined in Kruger et al. 1982 (Cell 31:147) to describe the self-splicing Tetrahymena group I intron. The discovery established that catalysis is a property of fold and surface, not of substrate class.

## Canonical instances

- **Tetrahymena group I intron** (Kruger, Grabowski, Zaug, Sands, Gottschling, Cech, 1982). A 414-nt intron in the pre-rRNA of *Tetrahymena thermophila* that excises itself in the presence of guanosine and Mg2+, with no protein required.
- **RNase P** (Guerrier-Takada, Gardiner, Marsh, Pace, Altman, 1983). The 5'-end processing enzyme for tRNAs. Its catalytic subunit is the RNA component (M1 RNA in E. coli, P RNA in B. subtilis); the protein subunit C5 is a modifier that lowers the magnesium requirement to physiological levels.
- **Hammerhead, hairpin, HDV** -- Small self-cleaving ribozymes found in viroids and viral satellite RNAs.
- **The ribosome** -- Structural studies (Steitz, Yonath, Ramakrishnan, 2009 Nobel) showed the peptidyl transferase center is entirely RNA. The ribosome is the largest ribozyme in biology.

## Mechanism

Ribozymes act by transition-state stabilization, the same mechanism Pauling proposed for protein enzymes in 1948. The catalytic pocket is built from base-pairing, non-canonical interactions, and divalent-metal coordination (mostly Mg2+ in natural ribozymes). The chemistry — typically phosphodiester bond rearrangement — is no different from a protein nuclease's chemistry.

## Significance

- Falsifies the pre-1982 dogma "all enzymes are proteins."
- Provides empirical support for the [[rna-world]] hypothesis.
- The ribosome's catalytic core is RNA, suggesting modern translation is a fossil of an RNA-only phase.
- Sets up the closure argument: the [[substrate-as-precondition]] constraint forecloses protein-first origins of catalysis.
