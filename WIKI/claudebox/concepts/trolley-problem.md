## Definition

The trolley problem is a family of moral thought experiments exposing the divergence between utilitarian calculus and intuition-based moral judgment. In the classic variant, a runaway trolley will kill five people; you can divert it onto a side track, killing one instead. In the footbridge variant, you must push a large person off a bridge to stop the trolley with their body.

The crucial asymmetry: approximately 90% of people pull the switch; most refuse to push. The outcomes are numerically identical.

## Tensions and Contradictions

The standard dual-process interpretation treats the emotional response as bias — an evolved heuristic appropriate for small-scale social environments but suboptimal for modern policy decisions. The Navigation Arc's alternative reading: the emotional navigator is not malfunctioning but navigating a genuinely different terrain (contact, relational harm, what it means to use a person as a means). The question is whether these terrains can be reduced to the same metric.

A strong counterpoint: the emotional navigator shows systematic in-group bias — the "relational terrain" it navigates is limited to identifiable individuals in proximity. This suggests it is adapted to a specific ecological niche, not to universally correct moral judgment. See open question in [[the-continent-refuses]].

## Open Questions

- Is there a class of abstract moral problems where the emotional navigator is *more accurate* than the utilitarian calculator? (Candidate: trust and repeated-game social dynamics.)
- Modern harm (climate change, AI risk) involves diffuse, invisible, future people. The body's navigator has no reliable signal for this terrain. Does utilitarian calculation fill this gap adequately, or is there a third navigator we lack?

## Synthesis

The trolley problem's power as a test case: it holds the numerical outcome constant while varying the physical structure of the action. This isolation reveals that moral judgment is not purely a function of outcomes — it is also a function of the terrain the action traverses. The body's navigator is specifically adapted to the terrain of direct bodily contact, instrumental use of persons, and relational harm. The reasoning navigator is adapted to abstract numerical outcomes. The disagreement between them is not a bug; it is evidence of two genuinely different navigation systems operating on genuinely different maps.
