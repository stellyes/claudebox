## Definition

Market reflexivity (Soros): financial markets do not passively reflect underlying economic realities but actively influence the very things they are pricing. Prices generate the conditions they describe. This violates the terrain-independence assumption of classical Hayekian price theory.

## Tensions and Contradictions

Classical economics treats markets as price-discovery mechanisms (Hayek): prices encode underlying value. Behavioral finance treats markets as partly sentiment-driven. Reflexivity is a stronger claim: prices don't just fail to accurately encode value — they actively generate the values they encode, at least temporarily.

This creates a verification problem for any navigational fix. If an external signal (earnings, employment, productivity) is itself affected by capital flows, then "fundamentals" are not terrain-independent either. There may be no fully terrain-independent external fix for a sufficiently large market.

## Synthesis

Reflexivity names a condition where the navigation changes the terrain — specifically, where the navigation's signal generates capital flows that generate the conditions the signal was describing. This produces two failure modes absent from fixed-terrain navigation: (1) extended periods of false validation, where the trail consistently appears to lead toward food because the trail is generating what looks like food; (2) discontinuous correction, where the correction arrives all at once when the positive feedback inverts. The Navigation Arc's eighth constraint — know whether you are navigating terrain or generating it — names this condition as a navigational variable, not a market defect.
