## Definition

Stereopsis is depth perception arising from the slight difference between what the left and right eyes see. Because eyes are approximately 6.5 cm apart, nearby objects produce a larger horizontal disparity (difference in apparent position) between the two retinal images than distant objects. The brain computes depth from this disparity gradient.

Stereopsis is categorically distinct from monocular depth cues (perspective, size gradient, atmospheric haze, occlusion). Closing one eye eliminates stereopsis while leaving all monocular cues intact. You can still see — but depth perception is degraded, especially for fine spatial discrimination at intermediate distances.

The crucial property: **depth from stereopsis lives in the relationship between two views, not inside either view alone.** No improvement of the single eye can generate the disparity signal.

## Scientific Details

- **Binocular disparity**: the horizontal angular difference between each eye's image of the same point. Crossed disparity for objects closer than fixation; uncrossed for farther objects.
- **Vergence**: the brain keeps corresponding points aligned by rotating eyes inward/outward; this maintains the disparity signal in a readable range.
- **Panum's fusion area**: a narrow disparity range where fusion occurs; beyond this, diplopia (double vision) results — the equivalent of "too much epistemic distance."
- **Stereoblindness**: ~4% of people lack stereopsis entirely despite normal monocular vision; caused by amblyopia or disrupted binocular development in infancy.

## Analogical Extensions

The structure of stereopsis — depth from disparity between two views rather than from either view alone — has been applied analogically to:

- **Epistemic binocularity** ([[two-eyed-seeing]]): Western science and indigenous knowledge as two eyes; the depth of understanding comes from the disparity between them
- **Frames of reference** ([[frames-of-reference]]): different frames of reference (thermodynamic entropy vs. replicative order) are "orthogonal eyes" whose apparent disagreements encode real structural information
- **The stereoscope**: the historical device that made the principle visible — two slightly different photographs, one to each eye, producing startling depth from crude images

## Tensions and Contradictions

- Stereopsis works best at intermediate distances and fails at very near or very far ranges. The epistemic analogue: two frames that are too similar (near) produce no useful disparity; two frames too dissimilar (far) cannot fuse at all.
- Stereopsis requires the two eyes to be aligned in the same horizontal plane (approximately). What is the epistemic analogue of this alignment constraint?
- The metaphor breaks down in that real stereopsis produces a *unified* depth percept — the brain fuses the two views. Two-eyed seeing explicitly does NOT fuse; it holds both views simultaneously. The better analogy might be: stereopsis shows the depth is real, two-eyed seeing is the practice of not collapsing it.
